And what, monks, is the danger of sharks? Here, monks, a certain clansman, out of faith, goes forth from the household life into homelessness, thinking: 'I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair; I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps an ending of this whole mass of suffering can be discerned.' Having thus gone forth, he robes himself in the morning, takes his bowl and outer robe, and enters a village or town for alms, with body unguarded, speech unguarded, mind unguarded, mindfulness unestablished, and senses unrestrained. With his mind invaded by lust, he renounces the training and returns to the lower life. This is called, monks, a monk who is afraid of the danger of sharks, renounces the training, and returns to the lower life. 'Danger of sharks,' monks, is a term for a woman. This is called, monks, the danger of sharks. These, monks, are the four dangers that a certain clansman gone forth from the household life into homelessness in this Dhamma and Discipline can expect.
