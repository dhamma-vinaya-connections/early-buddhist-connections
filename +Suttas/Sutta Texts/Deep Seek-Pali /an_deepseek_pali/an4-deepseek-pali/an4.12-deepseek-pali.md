# AN 4.12 Sīlasuttaṃ

## AN 4.12 The Discourse on Virtue

12. ‘‘Sampannasīlā, bhikkhave, viharatha sampannapātimokkhā, pātimokkhasaṃvarasaṃvutā viharatha ācāragocarasampannā aṇumattesu vajjesu bhayadassāvino. Samādāya sikkhatha sikkhāpadesu. Sampannasīlānaṃ vo, bhikkhave, viharataṃ sampannapātimokkhānaṃ pātimokkhasaṃvarasaṃvutānaṃ viharataṃ ācāragocarasampannānaṃ aṇumattesu vajjesu bhayadassāvīnaṃ samādāya sikkhataṃ sikkhāpadesu kimassa uttari karaṇīyaṃ?

12.“Monks, dwell with virtue perfected, with the Pātimokkha perfected, restrained by the restraint of the Pātimokkha, accomplished in conduct and livelihood, beholding danger in the slightest faults. Undertake and train yourselves in the training rules. For you, monks, dwelling with virtue perfected, with the Pātimokkha perfected, restrained by the restraint of the Pātimokkha, accomplished in conduct and livelihood, beholding danger in the slightest faults, undertaking and training yourselves in the training rules, what more is to be done?”

‘‘Carato cepi, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno abhijjhābyāpādo vigato hoti, thinamiddhaṃ… uddhaccakukkuccaṃ… vicikicchā pahīnā hoti, āraddhaṃ hoti vīriyaṃ asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ, carampi, bhikkhave, bhikkhu evaṃbhūto ‘ātāpī ottāpī satataṃ samitaṃ āraddhavīriyo pahitatto’ti vuccati.

“Even while walking, monks, if a monk has abandoned covetousness and ill will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, doubt is abandoned, energy is aroused and unflagging, mindfulness is established and unerring, the body is tranquil and unagitated, the mind is collected and one-pointed—even while walking, monks, such a monk is called ‘ardent, conscientious, constantly striving with aroused energy, resolute.’

‘‘Ṭhitassa cepi, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno abhijjhābyāpādo vigato hoti, thinamiddhaṃ… uddhaccakukkuccaṃ… vicikicchā pahīnā hoti, āraddhaṃ hoti vīriyaṃ asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ, ṭhitopi, bhikkhave, bhikkhu evaṃbhūto ‘ātāpī ottāpī satataṃ samitaṃ āraddhavīriyo pahitatto’ti vuccati.

“Even while standing, monks, if a monk has abandoned covetousness and ill will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, doubt is abandoned, energy is aroused and unflagging, mindfulness is established and unerring, the body is tranquil and unagitated, the mind is collected and one-pointed—even while standing, monks, such a monk is called ‘ardent, conscientious, constantly striving with aroused energy, resolute.’

‘‘Nisinnassa cepi, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno abhijjhābyāpādo vigato hoti, thinamiddhaṃ… uddhaccakukkuccaṃ… vicikicchā pahīnā hoti, āraddhaṃ hoti vīriyaṃ asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ, nisinnopi, bhikkhave, bhikkhu evaṃbhūto ‘ātāpī ottāpī satataṃ samitaṃ āraddhavīriyo pahitatto’ti vuccati.

“Even while sitting, monks, if a monk has abandoned covetousness and ill will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, doubt is abandoned, energy is aroused and unflagging, mindfulness is established and unerring, the body is tranquil and unagitated, the mind is collected and one-pointed—even while sitting, monks, such a monk is called ‘ardent, conscientious, constantly striving with aroused energy, resolute.’

‘‘Sayānassa cepi, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno jāgarassa abhijjhābyāpādo vigato hoti, thinamiddhaṃ… uddhaccakukkuccaṃ… vicikicchā pahīnā hoti, āraddhaṃ hoti vīriyaṃ asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ, sayānopi, bhikkhave, bhikkhu jāgaro evaṃbhūto ‘ātāpī ottāpī satataṃ samitaṃ āraddhavīriyo pahitatto’ti vuccatī’’ti.

“Even while lying down, monks, if a monk, being awake, has abandoned covetousness and ill will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and worry, and doubt; if energy has been aroused and is unflagging, mindfulness established and unerring, the body tranquil and unagitated, the mind collected and one-pointed—then, monks, such a monk, lying down awake, is called ‘ardent, conscientious, constantly striving with aroused energy, resolute.’”

‘‘Yataṃ [yathā (ka.) itivu. 111] care yataṃ [yathā (ka.) itivu. 111] tiṭṭhe, yataṃ [yathā (ka.) itivu. 111] acche yataṃ [yathā (ka.) itivu. 111] saye;Yataṃ [yathā (ka.) itivu. 111] samiñjaye [sammiñjaye (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] bhikkhu, yatamenaṃ [yatameva naṃ (sī.), yatametaṃ (syā. kaṃ.), yatameva (?)] pasāraye.

“A monk should be restrained when walking, when standing, when sitting, when lying down; when bending or stretching.

‘‘Uddhaṃ tiriyaṃ apācīnaṃ, yāvatā jagato gati;Samavekkhitā ca dhammānaṃ, khandhānaṃ udayabbayaṃ.

Upwards, across, and downwards, as far as the world extends; having closely examined the arising and passing away of phenomena and the aggregates.

‘‘Cetosamathasāmīciṃ, sikkhamānaṃ sadā sataṃ;Satataṃ pahitattoti, āhu bhikkhuṃ tathāvidha’’nti. dutiyaṃ;

One who trains in the practice appropriate for mental calm, ever mindful, continually resolute—such a monk they call.”
