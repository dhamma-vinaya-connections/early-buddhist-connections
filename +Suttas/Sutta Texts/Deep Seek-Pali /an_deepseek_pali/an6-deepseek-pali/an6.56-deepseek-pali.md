# AN 6.56 Phaggunasuttaṃ

## AN 6.56 The Phagguna Sutta

56. Tena kho pana samayena āyasmā phagguno [phegguno (ka.), phagguṇo (sī. syā.)] ābādhiko hoti dukkhito bāḷhagilāno. Atha kho āyasmā ānando yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho āyasmā ānando bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘āyasmā, bhante, phagguno ābādhiko dukkhito bāḷhagilāno. Sādhu, bhante, bhagavā yenāyasmā phagguno tenupasaṅkamatu anukampaṃ upādāyā’’ti. Adhivāsesi bhagavā tuṇhībhāvena. Atha kho bhagavā sāyanhasamayaṃ paṭisallānā vuṭṭhito yenāyasmā phagguno tenupasaṅkami. Addasā kho āyasmā phagguno bhagavantaṃ dūratova āgacchantaṃ. Disvāna mañcake samadhosi [samañcopi (sī. syā. pī.), saṃ + dhū + ī = samadhosi]. Atha kho bhagavā āyasmantaṃ phaggunaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘alaṃ, phagguna, mā tvaṃ mañcake samadhosi. Santimāni āsanāni parehi paññattāni, tatthāhaṃ nisīdissāmī’’ti. Nisīdi bhagavā paññatte āsane. Nisajja kho bhagavā āyasmantaṃ phaggunaṃ etadavoca –

56.Now at that time, the Venerable Phagguna was sick, suffering, gravely ill. Then the Venerable Ānanda approached the Blessed One; having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the Venerable Ānanda said to the Blessed One: “Venerable sir, the Venerable Phagguna is sick, suffering, gravely ill. It would be good, Venerable sir, if the Blessed One would approach the Venerable Phagguna out of compassion.” The Blessed One consented by remaining silent. Then, in the evening, having risen from seclusion, the Blessed One approached the Venerable Phagguna. The Venerable Phagguna saw the Blessed One coming from afar. Having seen him, he stirred on his bed. Then the Blessed One said to the Venerable Phagguna: “Enough, Phagguna, do not stir on your bed. There are seats prepared by others; I will sit there.” The Blessed One sat down on the prepared seat. Having sat down, the Blessed One said to the Venerable Phagguna:

‘‘Kacci te, phagguna, khamanīyaṃ kacci yāpanīyaṃ? Kacci te dukkhā vedanā paṭikkamanti, no abhikkamanti; paṭikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no abhikkamo’’ti? ‘‘Na me, bhante, khamanīyaṃ na yāpanīyaṃ. Bāḷhā me dukkhā vedanā abhikkamanti, no paṭikkamanti; abhikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no paṭikkamo.

“Phagguna, I hope you are bearing up; I hope you are getting along? Are your painful feelings decreasing, not increasing? Is a decrease apparent, not an increase?” “Venerable sir, I am not bearing up, I am not getting along. My painful feelings are increasing, not decreasing; an increase is apparent, not a decrease.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhante, balavā puriso tiṇhena sikharena muddhani [muddhānaṃ (sī. pī.)] abhimattheyya [abhimaṭṭheyya (syā.)]; evamevaṃ kho me, bhante, adhimattā vātā muddhani [hananti (sī. pī.), ohananti (syā.)] ūhananti. Na me, bhante, khamanīyaṃ na yāpanīyaṃ. Bāḷhā me dukkhā vedanā abhikkamanti, no paṭikkamanti; abhikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no paṭikkamo.

“Just as, venerable sir, a strong man might crush my head with a sharp blade; even so, venerable sir, exceedingly strong winds are pounding my head. I cannot bear it, venerable sir, I cannot endure it. Severe painful feelings are overwhelming me, not subsiding; their increase is evident, not their decrease.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhante, balavā puriso daḷhena varattakkhaṇḍena sīsaveṭhanaṃ dadeyya; evamevaṃ kho me, bhante, adhimattā sīse sīsavedanā. Na me, bhante, khamanīyaṃ na yāpanīyaṃ. Bāḷhā me dukkhā vedanā abhikkamanti, no paṭikkamanti; abhikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no paṭikkamo.

“Just as, venerable sir, a strong man might tie a tight leather strap around his head, so too, venerable sir, I have an intense headache. It is unbearable for me, venerable sir, and unsustainable. Severe painful feelings are overwhelming me, not subsiding; only their increase is apparent, not their decrease.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhante, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā tiṇhena govikantanena kucchiṃ parikanteyya; evamevaṃ kho me, bhante, adhimattā vātā kucchiṃ parikantanti. Na me, bhante, khamanīyaṃ na yāpanīyaṃ. Bāḷhā me dukkhā vedanā abhikkamanti, no paṭikkamanti; abhikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no paṭikkamo.

“Just as, venerable sir, a skilled butcher or a butcher's apprentice might cut open the belly of a cow with a sharp butcher's knife; just so, venerable sir, excessively strong winds are cutting open my belly. I cannot bear it, venerable sir, I cannot endure it. Intense painful feelings are overwhelming me, not subsiding; only an increase is apparent, not a decrease.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhante, dve balavanto purisā dubbalataraṃ purisaṃ nānābāhāsu gahetvā aṅgārakāsuyā santāpeyyuṃ samparitāpeyyuṃ; evamevaṃ kho me, bhante, adhimatto kāyasmiṃ ḍāho. Na me, bhante, khamanīyaṃ na yāpanīyaṃ. Bāḷhā me dukkhā vedanā abhikkamanti, no paṭikkamanti; abhikkamosānaṃ paññāyati, no paṭikkamo’’ti. Atha kho bhagavā āyasmantaṃ phaggunaṃ dhammiyā kathāya sandassetvā samādapetvā samuttejetvā sampahaṃsetvā uṭṭhāyāsanā pakkāmi.

“Just as, venerable sir, two strong men might seize a weaker man by the arms and burn and scorch him over a pit of glowing embers; just so, venerable sir, there is an excessive burning in my body. It is unbearable and unendurable for me. Severe painful feelings are increasing, not decreasing; their increase is evident, not their decrease.” Then the Blessed One, having instructed, inspired, gladdened, and encouraged Venerable Phagguna with a Dhamma talk, rose from his seat and departed.

Atha kho āyasmā phagguno acirapakkantassa bhagavato kālamakāsi. Tamhi cassa samaye maraṇakāle indriyāni vippasīdiṃsu. Atha kho āyasmā ānando yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho āyasmā ānando bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘āyasmā, bhante, phagguno acirapakkantassa bhagavato kālamakāsi. Tamhi cassa samaye maraṇakāle indriyāni vippasīdiṃsū’’ti.

Soon after the Blessed One had left, Venerable Phagguna passed away. And at the time of his death, his faculties became clear. Then Venerable Ānanda approached the Blessed One, paid homage to him, and sat down to one side. Seated to one side, Venerable Ānanda said to the Blessed One: “Venerable sir, Venerable Phagguna passed away soon after the Blessed One had left. And at the time of his death, his faculties became clear.”

‘‘Kiṃ hānanda, phaggunassa [pheggunassa ānanda (ka.)] bhikkhuno indriyāni na vippasīdissanti! Phaggunassa, ānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ avimuttaṃ ahosi. Tassa taṃ dhammadesanaṃ sutvā pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuttaṃ.

“Why, Ānanda, should the bhikkhu Phagguna's faculties not be clear? The bhikkhu Phagguna's mind was not freed from the five lower fetters. Having heard that Dhamma teaching, his mind was freed from the five lower fetters.

‘‘Chayime, ānanda, ānisaṃsā kālena dhammassavane [dhammasavaṇe (sī.)] kālena atthupaparikkhāya. Katame cha? Idhānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya. Tassa tathāgato dhammaṃ deseti ādikalyāṇaṃ majjhekalyāṇaṃ pariyosānakalyāṇaṃ sātthaṃ sabyañjanaṃ, kevalaparipuṇṇaṃ parisuddhaṃ brahmacariyaṃ pakāseti. Tassa taṃ dhammadesanaṃ sutvā pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, paṭhamo ānisaṃso kālena dhammassavane.

“There are, Ānanda, these six benefits of listening to the Dhamma at the right time and examining the meaning at the right time. What six? Here, Ānanda, a bhikkhu's mind is not freed from the five lower fetters. At that time, near the time of death, he gets to see the Tathāgata. The Tathāgata teaches him the Dhamma—good in the beginning, good in the middle, good in the end, with the meaning and phrasing; he reveals the holy life entirely perfect and pure. Having heard that Dhamma teaching, his mind is freed from the five lower fetters. This, Ānanda, is the first benefit of listening to the Dhamma at the right time.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle na heva kho [no ca kho (ka.)] labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya, api ca kho tathāgatasāvakaṃ labhati dassanāya. Tassa tathāgatasāvako dhammaṃ deseti ādikalyāṇaṃ majjhekalyāṇaṃ pariyosānakalyāṇaṃ sātthaṃ sabyañjanaṃ, kevalaparipuṇṇaṃ parisuddhaṃ brahmacariyaṃ pakāseti. Tassa taṃ dhammadesanaṃ sutvā pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, dutiyo ānisaṃso kālena dhammassavane.

“Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu whose mind is not freed from the five lower fetters. At the time of death, he does not get to see the Tathāgata, but he gets to see a disciple of the Tathāgata. That disciple of the Tathāgata teaches him the Dhamma—good in the beginning, good in the middle, good in the end, with the meaning and the letter, and reveals the perfectly complete and pure holy life. Having heard that Dhamma teaching, his mind is freed from the five lower fetters. This, Ānanda, is the second benefit of listening to the Dhamma at the right time.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle na heva kho labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya, napi tathāgatasāvakaṃ labhati dassanāya; api ca kho yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakketi anuvicāreti manasānupekkhati. Tassa yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakkayato anuvicārayato manasānupekkhato pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, tatiyo ānisaṃso kālena atthupaparikkhāya.

“Furthermore, Ānanda, the mind of a bhikkhu is not freed from the five lower fetters. At that time, at the moment of death, he does not get the opportunity to see the Tathāgata, nor does he get the opportunity to see a disciple of the Tathāgata. However, he reflects and examines the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it, attending to it in his mind. As he reflects and examines the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it, attending to it in his mind, his mind becomes freed from the five lower fetters. This, Ānanda, is the third benefit of examining the meaning at the right time.

‘‘Idhānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti, anuttare ca kho upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya. Tassa tathāgato dhammaṃ deseti ādikalyāṇaṃ majjhekalyāṇaṃ…pe… brahmacariyaṃ pakāseti. Tassa taṃ dhammadesanaṃ sutvā anuttare upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, catuttho ānisaṃso kālena dhammassavane.

"Here, Ānanda, the mind of a bhikkhu is freed from the five lower fetters, but his mind is not yet freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. At that time, at the moment of death, he gets to see the Tathāgata. The Tathāgata teaches him the Dhamma, which is good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end; he proclaims the spiritual life that is entirely fulfilled and purified. Having heard that teaching, his mind becomes freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. This, Ānanda, is the fourth advantage of listening to the Dhamma at the proper time."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti, anuttare ca kho upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle na heva kho labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya, api ca kho tathāgatasāvakaṃ labhati dassanāya. Tassa tathāgatasāvako dhammaṃ deseti ādikalyāṇaṃ…pe… parisuddhaṃ brahmacariyaṃ pakāseti. Tassa taṃ dhammadesanaṃ sutvā anuttare upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, pañcamo ānisaṃso kālena dhammassavane.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the mind of a bhikkhu is freed from the five lower fetters, but his mind is not yet freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. At that time, at the moment of death, he does not get to see the Tathāgata, but he does get to see a disciple of the Tathāgata. That disciple of the Tathāgata teaches him the Dhamma, which is good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end; he proclaims the spiritual life in its entirety and purity. Having heard that Dhamma teaching, his mind becomes freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. This, Ānanda, is the fifth advantage of listening to the Dhamma at the proper time."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno pañcahi orambhāgiyehi saṃyojanehi cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti, anuttare ca kho upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ avimuttaṃ hoti. So tamhi samaye maraṇakāle na heva kho labhati tathāgataṃ dassanāya, napi tathāgatasāvakaṃ labhati dassanāya; api ca kho yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakketi anuvicāreti manasānupekkhati. Tassa yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakkayato anuvicārayato manasānupekkhato anuttare upadhisaṅkhaye cittaṃ vimuccati. Ayaṃ, ānanda, chaṭṭho ānisaṃso kālena atthupaparikkhāya. Ime kho, ānanda, cha ānisaṃsā kālena dhammassavane kālena atthupaparikkhāyā’’ti. Dutiyaṃ.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, the mind of a bhikkhu is freed from the five lower fetters, but his mind is not yet freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. At that time, at the moment of death, he does not get to see the Tathāgata, nor does he get to see a disciple of the Tathāgata; however, he reflects, considers, and mentally examines the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it. As he reflects, considers, and mentally examines the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it, his mind becomes freed in the supreme destruction of acquisitions. This, Ānanda, is the sixth advantage of inquiry into the meaning at the proper time. These, Ānanda, are the six advantages of listening to the Dhamma at the proper time and inquiry into the meaning at the proper time." Second.
