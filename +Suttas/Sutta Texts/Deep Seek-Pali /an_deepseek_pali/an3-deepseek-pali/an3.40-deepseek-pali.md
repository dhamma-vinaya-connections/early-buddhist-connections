# AN 3.40 Ādhipateyyasuttaṃ

## AN 3.40 The Discourse on Masteries

40. ‘‘Tīṇimāni, bhikkhave, ādhipateyyāni. Katamāni tīṇi? Attādhipateyyaṃ, lokādhipateyyaṃ, dhammādhipateyyaṃ. Katamañca, bhikkhave, attādhipateyyaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu araññagato vā rukkhamūlagato vā suññāgāragato vā iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘na kho panāhaṃ cīvarahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Na piṇḍapātahetu, na senāsanahetu, na itibhavābhavahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Api ca khomhi otiṇṇo jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto. Appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethāti. Ahañceva kho pana yādisake [yādisake vā (sī. pī. ka.)] kāme ohāya agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito tādisake vā [ca (ka.)] kāme pariyeseyyaṃ tato vā [ca (ka.)] pāpiṭṭhatare, na metaṃ patirūpa’nti. So iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘āraddhaṃ kho pana me vīriyaṃ bhavissati asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekagga’nti. So attānaṃyeva adhipatiṃ karitvā akusalaṃ pajahati, kusalaṃ bhāveti, sāvajjaṃ pajahati, anavajjaṃ bhāveti, suddhaṃ attānaṃ pariharati. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, attādhipateyyaṃ.

40.“Monks, there are these three masteries. What three? Mastery of self, mastery of the world, and mastery of the Dhamma. And what, monks, is mastery of self? Here, monks, a monk who has gone to the wilderness, to the foot of a tree, or to an empty dwelling reflects thus: ‘I did not go forth from the household life into homelessness for the sake of robes, nor for the sake of almsfood, nor for the sake of lodging, nor for the sake of existence or non-existence. Rather, I am immersed in birth, aging, and death; in sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair. I am immersed in suffering, overcome by suffering. Perhaps an ending of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.’ Having abandoned such sensual pleasures to go forth into homelessness, if I were to seek those same or even worse sensual pleasures, that would not be fitting for me.’ He reflects thus: ‘My energy will be aroused and unflagging, mindfulness established and unconfused, the body calmed and untroubled, the mind collected and unified.’ Making himself the master, he abandons the unwholesome and develops the wholesome, abandons the blameworthy and develops the blameless, and maintains himself in purity. This, monks, is called mastery of self.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, lokādhipateyyaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu araññagato vā rukkhamūlagato vā suññāgāragato vā iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘na kho panāhaṃ cīvarahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Na piṇḍapātahetu, na senāsanahetu, na itibhavābhavahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Api ca khomhi otiṇṇo jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto. Appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. Ahañceva kho pana evaṃ pabbajito samāno kāmavitakkaṃ vā vitakkeyyaṃ, byāpādavitakkaṃ vā vitakkeyyaṃ, vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ vā vitakkeyyaṃ, mahā kho panāyaṃ lokasannivāso. Mahantasmiṃ kho pana lokasannivāse santi samaṇabrāhmaṇā iddhimanto dibbacakkhukā paracittaviduno. Te dūratopi passanti, āsannāpi na dissanti, cetasāpi cittaṃ pajānanti [jānanti (ka.)]. Tepi maṃ evaṃ jāneyyuṃ – ‘passatha, bho, imaṃ kulaputtaṃ saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito samāno vokiṇṇo viharati pāpakehi akusalehi dhammehī’ti. Devatāpi kho santi iddhimantiniyo dibbacakkhukā paracittaviduniyo. Tā dūratopi passanti, āsannāpi na dissanti, cetasāpi cittaṃ jānanti. Tāpi maṃ evaṃ jāneyyuṃ – ‘passatha, bho, imaṃ kulaputtaṃ saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito samāno vokiṇṇo viharati pāpakehi akusalehi dhammehī’ti. So iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘āraddhaṃ kho pana me vīriyaṃ bhavissati asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekagga’nti. So lokaṃyeva adhipatiṃ karitvā akusalaṃ pajahati, kusalaṃ bhāveti, sāvajjaṃ pajahati, anavajjaṃ bhāveti, suddhaṃ attānaṃ pariharati. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, lokādhipateyyaṃ.

"And what, monks, is world-governance? Here, a monk who has gone to the wilderness, to the foot of a tree, or to an empty dwelling reflects thus: ‘I did not go forth from the household life into homelessness for the sake of robes, nor for the sake of almsfood, nor for the sake of lodging, nor for the sake of becoming this or that. Rather, I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, and death; by sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair. I am overwhelmed by suffering, steeped in suffering. Perhaps an ending of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.’ Yet if I, having gone forth thus, were to think thoughts of sensual desire, or thoughts of ill will, or thoughts of harming, this world is indeed vast. In this vast world there are ascetics and brahmins endowed with psychic power, with the divine eye, who know others’ minds. They see from afar, though even when near they are not seen; they know others’ minds with their own minds. They would know me thus: ‘Look, friends, this clansman who has gone forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness dwells entangled in evil, unwholesome states.’ There are also deities endowed with psychic power, with the divine eye, who know others’ minds. They see from afar, though even when near they are not seen; they know others’ minds with their own minds. They too would know me thus: ‘Look, friends, this clansman who has gone forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness dwells entangled in evil, unwholesome states.’ He reflects thus: ‘My energy will be aroused and unflagging, mindfulness established and unconfused, the body calmed and untroubled, the mind collected and unified.’ Making the world his governing principle, he abandons the unwholesome and develops the wholesome, abandons the blameworthy and develops the blameless, and maintains himself in purity. This, monks, is called world-governance."

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, dhammādhipateyyaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu araññagato vā rukkhamūlagato vā suññāgāragato vā iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘na kho panāhaṃ cīvarahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Na piṇḍapātahetu, na senāsanahetu, na itibhavābhavahetu agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito. Api ca khomhi otiṇṇo jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto. Appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethāti. Svākkhāto bhagavatā dhammo sandiṭṭhiko akāliko ehipassiko opaneyyiko paccattaṃ veditabbo viññūhīti. Santi kho pana me sabrahmacārī jānaṃ passaṃ viharanti. Ahañceva kho pana evaṃ svākkhāte dhammavinaye pabbajito samāno kusīto vihareyyaṃ pamatto, na metaṃ assa patirūpa’nti. So iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘āraddhaṃ kho pana me vīriyaṃ bhavissati asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekagga’nti. So dhammaṃyeva adhipatiṃ karitvā akusalaṃ pajahati, kusalaṃ bhāveti, sāvajjaṃ pajahati, anavajjaṃ bhāveti, suddhaṃ attānaṃ pariharati. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, dhammādhipateyyaṃ. Imāni kho, bhikkhave, tīṇi ādhipateyyānī’’ti.

“And what, monks, is mastery of the Dhamma? Here, monks, a monk who has gone to the wilderness, to the foot of a tree, or to an empty dwelling reflects thus: ‘I did not go forth from the household life into homelessness for the sake of robes, nor for the sake of almsfood, nor for the sake of lodging, nor for the sake of existence or non-existence. Rather, I am immersed in birth, aging, and death; in sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair. I am immersed in suffering, overcome by suffering. Perhaps an ending of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.’ The Dhamma has been well proclaimed by the Blessed One, visible here and now, timeless, inviting investigation, leading onward, to be personally experienced by the wise. There are fellow monks who live knowing and seeing. And I too, having gone forth in such a well-proclaimed Dhamma and Discipline, might dwell lazy and heedless. That would not be fitting for me.’ He reflects thus: ‘My energy will be aroused and unflagging, mindfulness established and unconfused, the body calmed and untroubled, the mind collected and unified.’ Making the Dhamma his master, he abandons the unwholesome and develops the wholesome, abandons the blameworthy and develops the blameless, and maintains himself in purity. This, monks, is called mastery of the Dhamma. These, monks, are the three masteries.”

‘‘Natthi loke raho nāma, pāpakammaṃ pakubbato;Attā te purisa jānāti, saccaṃ vā yadi vā musā.

“There is no secret place in the world for one who does evil; your own self, O man, knows your deeds, whether true or false.

‘‘Kalyāṇaṃ vata bho sakkhi, attānaṃ atimaññasi;Yo santaṃ attani pāpaṃ, attānaṃ parigūhasi.

“Indeed, good sir, you overestimate yourself, concealing the evil that exists within you.

‘‘Passanti devā ca tathāgatā ca,Lokasmiṃ bālaṃ visamaṃ carantaṃ;Tasmā hi attādhipateyyako ca [attādhipako sako care (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)],Lokādhipo ca nipako ca jhāyī.

The devas and the Tathagatas see the fool behaving wrongly in the world; therefore, one should be self-mastering, world-mastering, wise, and meditative.

‘‘Dhammādhipo ca anudhammacārī,Na hīyati saccaparakkamo muni;Pasayha māraṃ abhibhuyya antakaṃ,Yo ca phusī jātikkhayaṃ padhānavā;So tādiso lokavidū sumedho,Sabbesu dhammesu atammayo munī’’ti. dasamaṃ;

The one who has the Dhamma as their master and lives in accordance with the Dhamma, the sage of steadfast effort does not decline. Having forcefully conquered Māra and the Ender, and having attained the destruction of birth through striving, such a one, a knower of the world, wise and discerning, is a sage unattached among all phenomena. Tenth.

Devadūtavaggo catuttho.

The Fourth Chapter: The Divine Messengers.

Tassuddānaṃ –

The Summary:

Brahma ānanda sāriputto, nidānaṃ hatthakena ca;Dūtā duve ca rājāno, sukhumālādhipateyyena cāti.

Brahma, Ananda, Sariputta; the Nidana and Hatthaka; two messengers, two kings, and the Sukhumāla and Ādhipateyya suttas.
