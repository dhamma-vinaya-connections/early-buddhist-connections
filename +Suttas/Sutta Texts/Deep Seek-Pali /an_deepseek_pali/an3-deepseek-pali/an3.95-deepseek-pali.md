# AN 3.95 Parisāsuttaṃ

## AN 3.95 The Assembly Discourse

96. ‘‘Tisso imā, bhikkhave, parisā. Katamā tisso? Aggavatī parisā, vaggā parisā, samaggā parisā.

96.Monks, there are these three kinds of assemblies. Which three? The foremost assembly, the factious assembly, and the harmonious assembly.

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, aggavatī parisā? Idha, bhikkhave, yassaṃ parisāyaṃ therā bhikkhū na bāhulikā honti na sāthalikā, okkamane nikkhittadhurā paviveke pubbaṅgamā, vīriyaṃ ārabhanti appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāya, tesaṃ pacchimā janatā diṭṭhānugatiṃ āpajjati. Sāpi hoti na bāhulikā na sāthalikā okkamane nikkhittadhurā paviveke pubbaṅgamā, vīriyaṃ ārabhati appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāya. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, aggavatī parisā.

And what, monks, is a foremost assembly? Here, monks, in an assembly the elder monks are not fond of company nor fond of talk, they have laid down the burden of backsliding and take the lead in seclusion, they arouse energy for attaining the unattained, achieving the unachieved, realizing the unrealized. The subsequent generation follows their example. They too are not fond of company nor fond of talk, they have laid down the burden of backsliding and take the lead in seclusion, they arouse energy for attaining the unattained, achieving the unachieved, realizing the unrealized. This, monks, is called a foremost assembly.

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, vaggā parisā? Idha, bhikkhave, yassaṃ parisāyaṃ bhikkhū bhaṇḍanajātā kalahajātā vivādāpannā aññamaññaṃ mukhasattīhi vitudantā viharanti, ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, vaggā parisā.

And what, monks, is a factious assembly? Here, monks, in an assembly the monks are quarrelsome, disputatious, and contentious, wounding each other with verbal weapons. This, monks, is called a factious assembly.

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, samaggā parisā? Idha, bhikkhave, yassaṃ parisāyaṃ bhikkhū samaggā sammodamānā avivadamānā khīrodakībhūtā aññamaññaṃ piyacakkhūhi sampassantā viharanti, ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, samaggā parisā.

And what, monks, is a harmonious assembly? Here, monks, in whatever assembly the monks dwell in harmony, rejoicing together, without dispute, blending like milk and water, regarding each other with eyes of affection. This, monks, is called a harmonious assembly.

‘‘Yasmiṃ, bhikkhave, samaye bhikkhū samaggā sammodamānā avivadamānā khīrodakībhūtā aññamaññaṃ piyacakkhūhi sampassantā viharanti, bahuṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhū tasmiṃ samaye puññaṃ pasavanti. Brahmaṃ, bhikkhave, vihāraṃ tasmiṃ samaye bhikkhū viharanti, yadidaṃ muditāya cetovimuttiyā. Pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vediyati, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati.

When, monks, the monks dwell in harmony, rejoicing together, without dispute, blending like milk and water, viewing each other with eyes of affection, at that time, monks, the monks generate much merit. At that time, monks, the monks dwell in the divine abiding, that is, the liberation of mind through sympathetic joy. For one who is gladdened, rapture arises; for one with a mind imbued with rapture, the body becomes tranquil; one whose body is tranquil feels happiness; the mind of one who is happy becomes collected.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, uparipabbate thullaphusitake deve vassante taṃ udakaṃ yathāninnaṃ pavattamānaṃ pabbatakandarapadarasākhā paripūreti, pabbatakandarapadarasākhā paripūrā kusobbhe [kussumbhe (sī. pī.), kusumbhe (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] paripūrenti, kusobbhā paripūrā mahāsobbhe paripūrenti, mahāsobbhā paripūrā kunnadiyo paripūrenti, kunnadiyo paripūrā mahānadiyo paripūrenti, mahānadiyo paripūrā samuddaṃ [samuddasāgare (ka.)] paripūrenti.

Just as, monks, when heavy rain falls on a mountain top, the water flowing down according to the slope fills the mountain clefts, gullies, and creeks; these being full fill the pools; the pools being full fill the lakes; the lakes being full fill the streams; the streams being full fill the rivers; and the rivers being full fill the great ocean.

‘‘Evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, yasmiṃ samaye bhikkhū samaggā sammodamānā avivadamānā khīrodakībhūtā aññamaññaṃ piyacakkhūhi sampassantā viharanti, bahuṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhū tasmiṃ samaye puññaṃ pasavanti. Brahmaṃ, bhikkhave, vihāraṃ tasmiṃ samaye bhikkhū viharanti, yadidaṃ muditāya cetovimuttiyā. Pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vediyati, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Imā kho, bhikkhave, tisso parisā’’ti. Catutthaṃ.

In the same way, monks, when the monks dwell in harmony, rejoicing together, without dispute, blending like milk and water, viewing each other with eyes of affection, at that time, monks, the monks generate much merit. At that time, monks, the monks dwell in the divine abiding, that is, the liberation of mind through sympathetic joy. For one who is gladdened, rapture arises; for one with a mind imbued with rapture, the body becomes tranquil; one whose body is tranquil feels happiness; the mind of one who is happy becomes collected. These, monks, are the three kinds of assemblies.
