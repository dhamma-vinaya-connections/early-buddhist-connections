102.Bhikkhus, there are coarse defilements of gold: dust, sand, gravel, and grit. A goldsmith or his apprentice first pours it into a trough and washes, rinses, and cleanses it. When these are removed and eliminated, there remain medium defilements in the gold: fine gravel and coarse sand. The goldsmith or his apprentice washes, rinses, and cleanses it again. When these are removed and eliminated, there remain subtle defilements in the gold: fine sand and black impurities. The goldsmith or his apprentice washes, rinses, and cleanses it once more. When these are removed and eliminated, then only gold dust remains. The goldsmith or his apprentice then places the gold in a crucible, blows on it, melts it, and refines it. The gold becomes blown on, melted, and refined, but not yet free from dross; it is neither soft nor workable, nor radiant, and is brittle, and does not properly lend itself to crafting. There comes a time, bhikkhus, when the goldsmith or his apprentice blows on, melts, and refines that gold again. The gold then becomes blown on, melted, and refined, free from dross, and is soft and workable and radiant, and not brittle, and properly lends itself to crafting.
