# AN 9.36 Jhānasuttaṃ

## AN 9.36 Jhānas

36. ‘‘Paṭhamampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; dutiyampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; tatiyampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; catutthampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; ākāsānañcāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; viññāṇañcāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; ākiñcaññāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; nevasaññānāsaññāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi; saññāvedayitanirodhampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi.

36.Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the first jhāna. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the second jhāna. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the third jhāna. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the fourth jhāna. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the base of infinite space. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the base of infinite consciousness. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the base of nothingness. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the cessation of perception and feeling.

‘‘‘Paṭhamampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi…pe… paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti rūpagataṃ vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti [patiṭṭhāpeti (syā.), paṭipādeti (ka.) ma. ni. 2.133 passitabbaṃ]. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā [patiṭṭhāpetvā (syā.), paṭipādetvā (ka.)] amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā.

‘Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the first jhāna.’ This was said. In reference to what was it said? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures…pe… enters and dwells in the first jhāna. He regards whatever phenomena are found there connected with form, feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as misery, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it to the deathless element: ‘This is the peaceful, this is the sublime, that is, the stilling of all formations, the relinquishing of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established in that, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, through that passion for the Dhamma, that delight in the Dhamma, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, without returning from that world.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, issāso vā issāsantevāsī vā tiṇapurisarūpake vā mattikāpuñje vā yoggaṃ karitvā, so aparena samayena dūrepātī ca hoti akkhaṇavedhī ca mahato ca kāyassa padāletā [padālitā (ka.) a. ni. 3.134; 4.181]; evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi…pe… paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti rūpagataṃ vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā. ‘Paṭhamampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

Suppose, bhikkhus, an archer or an archer’s apprentice has practiced on a straw man or a clay mound. After a time, he becomes a long-range shooter, a rapid-fire shooter, and a piercer of great masses. So too, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures…pe… enters and dwells in the first jhāna. He regards whatever phenomena are found there connected with form, feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as misery, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it to the deathless element: ‘This is the peaceful, this is the sublime, that is, the stilling of all formations, the relinquishing of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established in that, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, through that passion for the Dhamma, that delight in the Dhamma, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, without returning from that world. ‘Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the first jhāna’: it is in this sense that this was said.

‘‘Dutiyampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya…pe… tatiyampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya… ‘catutthampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti rūpagataṃ vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā.

‘Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the second jhāna…pe… by relying on the third jhāna…pe… by relying on the fourth jhāna.’ This was said. In reference to what was it said? Here, bhikkhus, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the previous passing away of joy and displeasure, a bhikkhu enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which is neither painful nor pleasant and includes the purification of mindfulness by equanimity. He regards whatever phenomena are found there connected with form, feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as misery, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it to the deathless element: ‘This is the peaceful, this is the sublime, that is, the stilling of all formations, the relinquishing of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established in that, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, through that passion for the Dhamma, that delight in the Dhamma, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, without returning from that world.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, issāso vā issāsantevāsī vā tiṇapurisarūpake vā mattikāpuñje vā yoggaṃ karitvā, so aparena samayena dūrepātī ca hoti akkhaṇavedhī ca mahato ca kāyassa padāletā; evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sukhassa ca pahānā, dukkhassa ca pahānā, pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti rūpagataṃ vedanāgataṃ…pe… anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā. ‘Catutthampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

Suppose, bhikkhus, an archer or an archer’s apprentice has practiced on a straw man or a clay mound. After a time, he becomes a long-range shooter, a rapid-fire shooter, and a piercer of great masses. So too, bhikkhus, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain…pe… a bhikkhu enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna. He regards whatever phenomena are found there…pe… without returning from that world. ‘Bhikkhus, I say that there is a destruction of the taints by relying on the fourth jhāna’: it is in this sense that this was said.

‘‘‘Ākāsānañcāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, jhānaṃ nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā.

‘Bhikkhus, I say that the destruction of the taints is to be achieved by relying on the jhāna of the base of the infinity of space.’ This was said. In reference to what was it said? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, by completely surmounting perceptions of form, with the passing away of perceptions of resistance, with non-attention to perceptions of diversity, aware that ‘space is infinite,’ enters and dwells in the base of the infinity of space. Whatever feeling, perception, volitional formations, or consciousness is comprised therein, he regards those phenomena as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as a calamity, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it toward the deathless element: ‘This is peaceful, this is sublime, that is, the stilling of all volitional formations, the relinquishment of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established therein, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then through that very passion for the Dhamma and that delight in the Dhamma, with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, not liable to return from that world.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, issāso vā issāsantevāsī vā tiṇapurisarūpake vā mattikāpuñje vā yoggaṃ karitvā, so aparena samayena dūrepātī ca hoti akkhaṇavedhī ca mahato ca kāyassa padāletā; evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ…pe… anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā. ‘Ākāsānañcāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

Suppose, bhikkhus, an archer or an archer’s apprentice, having practiced on a straw man or a clay mound, would in time become one who shoots from a distance, a rapid shooter, and a piercer of large objects. So too, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, by completely surmounting perceptions of form, with the passing away of perceptions of resistance, with non-attention to perceptions of diversity, aware that ‘space is infinite,’ enters and dwells in the base of the infinity of space. Whatever feeling, perception, volitional formations, or consciousness is comprised therein ... he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, not liable to return from that world. ‘Bhikkhus, I say that the destruction of the taints is to be achieved by relying on the base of the infinity of space.’ It was in reference to this that this was said.

‘‘‘Viññāṇañcāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya…pe… ākiñcaññāyatanampāhaṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā.

‘Bhikkhus, I say that the destruction of the taints is to be achieved by relying on the base of the infinity of consciousness ... and by relying on the base of nothingness.’ Now this was said, and in reference to what was it said? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, by completely surmounting the base of the infinity of consciousness, aware that ‘There is nothing,’ enters and dwells in the base of nothingness. Whatever feeling, perception, volitional formations, or consciousness is comprised therein, he regards those phenomena as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as a calamity, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it toward the deathless element: ‘This is peaceful, this is sublime—that is, the stilling of all volitional formations, the relinquishment of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established therein, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then through that very passion for the Dhamma and that delight in the Dhamma, with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, not liable to return from that world.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, issāso vā issāsantevāsī vā tiṇapurisarūpake vā mattikāpuñje vā yoggaṃ karitvā, so aparena samayena dūrepātī ca hoti akkhaṇavedhī ca mahato ca kāyassa padāletā; evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. So yadeva tattha hoti vedanāgataṃ saññāgataṃ saṅkhāragataṃ viññāṇagataṃ, te dhamme aniccato dukkhato rogato gaṇḍato sallato aghato ābādhato parato palokato suññato anattato samanupassati. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpeti. So tehi dhammehi cittaṃ paṭivāpetvā amatāya dhātuyā cittaṃ upasaṃharati – ‘etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ sabbasaṅkhārasamatho sabbūpadhipaṭinissaggo taṇhākkhayo virāgo nirodho nibbāna’nti. So tattha ṭhito āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti. No ce āsavānaṃ khayaṃ pāpuṇāti, teneva dhammarāgena tāya dhammanandiyā pañcannaṃ orambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ parikkhayā opapātiko hoti tattha parinibbāyī anāvattidhammo tasmā lokā. ‘Ākiñcaññāyatanampāhaṃ, nissāya āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmī’ti, iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

Suppose, bhikkhus, an archer or an archer’s apprentice, having practiced on a straw man or a clay mound, would in time become one who shoots from a distance, a rapid shooter, and a piercer of large objects. So too, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, by completely surmounting the base of the infinity of consciousness, aware that ‘There is nothing,’ enters and dwells in the base of nothingness. Whatever feeling, perception, volitional formations, or consciousness is comprised therein, he regards those phenomena as impermanent, as suffering, as a disease, as a boil, as a dart, as a calamity, as an affliction, as alien, as disintegrating, as empty, as nonself. He turns his mind away from those phenomena. Having turned his mind away from them, he directs it toward the deathless element: ‘This is peaceful, this is sublime—that is, the stilling of all volitional formations, the relinquishment of all acquisitions, the destruction of craving, dispassion, cessation, Nibbāna.’ Established therein, he attains the destruction of the taints. If he does not attain the destruction of the taints, then through that very passion for the Dhamma and that delight in the Dhamma, with the utter destruction of the five lower fetters, he becomes one of spontaneous birth, due to attain final Nibbāna there, not liable to return from that world. ‘Bhikkhus, I say that the destruction of the taints is to be achieved by relying on the base of nothingness.’ It was in reference to this that this was said.

‘‘Iti kho, bhikkhave, yāvatā saññāsamāpatti tāvatā aññāpaṭivedho. Yāni ca kho imāni, bhikkhave, nissāya dve āyatanāni – nevasaññānāsaññāyatanasamāpatti ca saññāvedayitanirodho ca, jhāyīhete, bhikkhave, samāpattikusalehi samāpattivuṭṭhānakusalehi samāpajjitvā vuṭṭhahitvā sammā akkhātabbānīti vadāmī’’ti. Pañcamaṃ.

Thus, bhikkhus, as far as the attainments involving perception extend, so far extends the penetration of final knowledge. As for these two bases—the attainment of the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception and the cessation of perception and feeling—I say they are to be properly explained by meditators skilled in the attainment and in emerging from it, having entered and emerged from them. The fifth.
