# AN 5.232 Piyasuttaṃ

## AN 5.232 The Discourse on the Beloved

232. ‘‘Pañcahi, bhikkhave, dhammehi samannāgato āvāsiko bhikkhu sabrahmacārīnaṃ piyo ca hoti manāpo ca garu ca bhāvanīyo ca.

232.“Monks, a resident bhikkhu endowed with five qualities is dear and agreeable to his fellow bhikkhus, respected and estimable.

‘‘Katamehi pañcahi? Sīlavā hoti, pātimokkhasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati ācāragocarasampanno aṇumattesu vajjesu bhayadassāvī, samādāya sikkhati sikkhāpadesu; bahussuto hoti sutadharo sutasannicayo, ye te dhammā ādikalyāṇā majjhekalyāṇā pariyosānakalyāṇā sātthaṃ sabyañjanaṃ kevalaparipuṇṇaṃ parisuddhaṃ brahmacariyaṃ abhivadanti, tathārūpāssa dhammā bahussutā honti dhātā vacasā paricitā manasānupekkhitā diṭṭhiyā suppaṭividdhā; kalyāṇavāco hoti kalyāṇavākkaraṇo poriyā vācāya samannāgato vissaṭṭhāya anelagalāya atthassa viññāpaniyā; catunnaṃ jhānānaṃ ābhicetasikānaṃ diṭṭhadhammasukhavihārānaṃ nikāmalābhī hoti akicchalābhī akasiralābhī; āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati. Imehi kho, bhikkhave, pañcahi dhammehi samannāgato āvāsiko bhikkhu sabrahmacārīnaṃ piyo ca hoti manāpo ca garu ca bhāvanīyo cā’’ti. Dutiyaṃ.

With which five? Here, he is virtuous, he dwells restrained by the Pātimokkha, accomplished in conduct and resort, seeing danger in the slightest faults, undertaking the training and training in the training rules; he is learned, one who remembers what he has heard, one who accumulates what he has heard. Those teachings that are good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, with their meaning and their phrasing, which proclaim the perfectly complete and purified holy life—such teachings he has heard much, retained, recited verbally, mentally examined, and penetrated well by understanding; he is one of good speech, one of good utterance, endowed with refined, clear, and articulate speech that conveys the meaning; he is one who obtains at will, without difficulty, without trouble, the four jhānas that are of the higher mind, pleasant abidings in this very life; and with the destruction of the taints, he enters and dwells in the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, having realized it for himself with direct knowledge in this very life. Monks, a resident bhikkhu endowed with these five qualities is dear and agreeable to his fellow bhikkhus, respected and estimable.” This is the second discourse.
