# AN 10.165 Cundasuttaṃ

## AN 10.165 The Cunda Sutta

176. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā pāvāyaṃ [campāyaṃ (ka. sī.) dī. ni. 2.189 passitabbaṃ] viharati cundassa kammāraputtassa ambavane. Atha kho cundo kammāraputto yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinnaṃ kho cundaṃ kammāraputtaṃ bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘kassa no tvaṃ, cunda, soceyyāni rocesī’’ti? ‘‘Brāhmaṇā, bhante, pacchābhūmakā kamaṇḍalukā sevālamālikā [sevālamālakā (sī. syā. pī.)] aggiparicārikā udakorohakā soceyyāni paññapenti; tesāhaṃ soceyyāni rocemī’’ti.

176.Thus have I heard – At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Pāvā in the mango grove of Cunda the metalworker. Then Cunda the metalworker approached the Blessed One; having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the Blessed One said to Cunda the metalworker: “Whose purifications, Cunda, do you approve of?” “Venerable sir, the brahmins of later generations, carrying water pots, wearing garlands of water weeds, attending the fire, and immersing themselves in water, prescribe purifications; I approve of their purifications.”

‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana, cunda, brāhmaṇā pacchābhūmakā kamaṇḍalukā sevālamālikā aggiparicārikā udakorohakā soceyyāni paññapentī’’ti? ‘‘Idha, bhante, brāhmaṇā pacchābhūmakā kamaṇḍalukā sevālamālikā aggiparicārikā udakorohakā. Te sāvakaṃ [sāvake (syā. ka.)] evaṃ samādapenti – ‘ehi tvaṃ, ambho purisa, kālasseva [sakālasseva (syā.)] uṭṭhahantova [uṭṭhahanto (syā.), vuṭṭhahantova (pī. ka.)] sayanamhā pathaviṃ āmaseyyāsi; no ce pathaviṃ āmaseyyāsi, allāni gomayāni āmaseyyāsi; no ce allāni gomayāni āmaseyyāsi, haritāni tiṇāni āmaseyyāsi; no ce haritāni tiṇāni āmaseyyāsi, aggiṃ paricareyyāsi; no ce aggiṃ paricareyyāsi, pañjaliko ādiccaṃ namasseyyāsi; no ce pañjaliko ādiccaṃ namasseyyāsi, sāyatatiyakaṃ udakaṃ oroheyyāsī’ti. Evaṃ kho, bhante, brāhmaṇā pacchābhūmakā kamaṇḍalukā sevālamālikā aggiparicārikā udakorohakā soceyyāni paññapenti; tesāhaṃ soceyyāni rocemī’’ti.

“And how, Cunda, do the brahmins of later generations, carrying water pots, wearing garlands of water weeds, attending the fire, and immersing themselves in water, prescribe purifications?” “Here, venerable sir, are brahmins of later generations, carrying water pots, wearing garlands of water weeds, attending the fire, and immersing themselves in water. They instruct their disciples thus: ‘Come now, good man, rising early from bed, you should touch the earth; if you do not touch the earth, you should touch fresh cow dung; if you do not touch fresh cow dung, you should touch green grass; if you do not touch green grass, you should attend the fire; if you do not attend the fire, you should salute the sun with joined palms; if you do not salute the sun with joined palms, you should immerse yourself in water three times in the evening.’ Thus, venerable sir, the brahmins of later generations, carrying water pots, wearing garlands of water weeds, attending the fire, and immersing themselves in water, prescribe purifications; I approve of their purifications.”

‘‘Aññathā kho, cunda, brāhmaṇā pacchābhūmakā kamaṇḍalukā sevālamālikā aggiparicārikā udakorohakā soceyyāni paññapenti, aññathā ca pana ariyassa vinaye soceyyaṃ hotī’’ti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana, bhante, ariyassa vinaye soceyyaṃ hoti? Sādhu me, bhante, bhagavā tathā dhammaṃ desetu yathā ariyassa vinaye soceyyaṃ hotī’’ti.

“Otherwise, Cunda, brahmins of later generations, carrying water pots, wearing garlands of water weeds, tending the sacred fire, and immersing themselves in water, prescribe purifications in one way, but in the Noble One’s discipline, purification is quite different.” “But how, venerable sir, is purification in the Noble One’s discipline? It would be good, venerable sir, if the Blessed One would teach the Dhamma as to how purification is in the Noble One’s discipline.”

‘‘Tena hi, cunda, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho cundo kammāraputto bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

“Very well, Cunda, listen and pay close attention; I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” replied Cunda the metalworker to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

‘‘Tividhaṃ kho, cunda, kāyena asoceyyaṃ hoti; catubbidhaṃ vācāya asoceyyaṃ hoti; tividhaṃ manasā asoceyyaṃ hoti.

“Cunda, there is threefold bodily impurity, fourfold verbal impurity, and threefold mental impurity.”

‘‘Kathañca, cunda, tividhaṃ kāyena asoceyyaṃ hoti? ‘‘Idha, cunda, ekacco pāṇātipātī hoti luddo lohitapāṇi hatapahate niviṭṭho adayāpanno sabbapāṇabhūtesu [pāṇabhūtesu (ka.)].

“And how, Cunda, is there threefold bodily impurity? Here, Cunda, someone is a killer of living beings, cruel, bloody-handed, devoted to killing and violence, merciless to all living beings.

‘‘Adinnādāyī hoti. Yaṃ taṃ parassa paravittūpakaraṇaṃ gāmagataṃ vā araññagataṃ vā taṃ adinnaṃ theyyasaṅkhātaṃ ādātā hoti.

He takes what is not given. He takes, as a thief, what belongs to others—their possessions—whether in a village or in the wilderness, considering it as not given.”

‘‘Kāmesumicchācārī hoti. Yā tā māturakkhitā piturakkhitā mātāpiturakkhitā [natthi sī. syā. pī. potthakesu] bhāturakkhitā bhaginirakkhitā ñātirakkhitā gottarakkhitā [natthi sī. syā. pī. potthakesu] dhammarakkhitā sasāmikā saparidaṇḍā antamaso mālāguḷaparikkhittāpi, tathārūpāsu cārittaṃ āpajjitā hoti. Evaṃ kho, cunda, tividhaṃ kāyena asoceyyaṃ hoti.

He engages in sexual misconduct. He has sexual relations with women who are protected by their mother, by their father, by both mother and father, by their brother, by their sister, by their relatives, by their clan, or by the Dhamma; women with a husband; women liable to punishment; or even those betrothed with a garland. Thus, Cunda, there is threefold bodily impurity.”

‘‘Kathañca, cunda, catubbidhaṃ vācāya asoceyyaṃ hoti? Idha, cunda, ekacco musāvādī hoti. Sabhaggato vā parisaggato vā ñātimajjhagato vā pūgamajjhagato vā rājakulamajjhagato vā abhinīto sakkhipuṭṭho – ‘ehambho purisa, yaṃ jānāsi taṃ vadehī’ti [so ajānaṃ vā ahaṃ jānāmīti, jānaṃ vā ahaṃ na jānāmīti, apassaṃ vā ahaṃ passāmīti, passaṃ vā ahaṃ na passāmīti (pī. ka.) evamuparipi], so ajānaṃ vā āha ‘jānāmī’ti, jānaṃ vā āha ‘na jānāmī’ti; apassaṃ vā āha ‘passāmī’ti, passaṃ vā āha ‘na passāmī’ti [so ajānaṃ vā ahaṃ jānāmīti, jānaṃ vā ahaṃ na jānāmīti, apassaṃ vā ahaṃ passāmīti, passaṃ vā ahaṃ na passāmīti (pī. ka.) evamuparipi]. Iti attahetu vā parahetu vā āmisakiñcikkhahetu vā sampajānamusā bhāsitā hoti.

“And how, Cunda, is there fourfold verbal impurity? Here, Cunda, someone is a liar. When summoned to a council, an assembly, a gathering of relatives, a gathering of guilds, or to the royal court, and questioned as a witness: ‘Come, good man, say what you know,’ not knowing, he says, ‘I know’; or knowing, he says, ‘I do not know’; not seeing, he says, ‘I see’; or seeing, he says, ‘I do not see.’ Thus, for his own sake, for another’s sake, or for some trifling material gain, he knowingly speaks a lie.

‘‘Pisuṇavāco hoti. Ito sutvā amutra akkhātā imesaṃ bhedāya, amutra vā sutvā imesaṃ akkhātā amūsaṃ bhedāya. Iti samaggānaṃ vā bhettā [bhedātā (ka.)], bhinnānaṃ vā anuppadātā, vaggārāmo vaggarato vagganandī vaggakaraṇiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti.

He speaks divisively. Having heard something here, he reports it elsewhere to create division among these people, or having heard something elsewhere, he reports it to these people to create division among those. Thus he is a splitter of the united, or one who encourages division among the divided; he delights in discord, enjoys discord, is pleased with discord, and speaks words that create discord.

‘‘Pharusavāco hoti. Yā sā vācā aṇḍakā kakkasā parakaṭukā parābhisajjanī kodhasāmantā asamādhisaṃvattanikā, tathārūpiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti.

He speaks harshly. He utters speech that is rough, harsh, offensive to others, abusive, connected with anger, and unconducive to samādhi.

‘‘Samphappalāpī hoti akālavādī abhūtavādī anatthavādī adhammavādī avinayavādī; anidhānavatiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti akālena anapadesaṃ apariyantavatiṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ. Evaṃ kho, cunda, catubbidhaṃ vācāya asoceyyaṃ hoti.

He engages in idle chatter. He speaks out of season, speaks what is untrue, speaks what is unbeneficial, speaks what is not the Dhamma, speaks what is not the Vinaya. He speaks speech that is without a store, without basis, without limit, not connected with benefit. Thus, Cunda, there is fourfold verbal impurity.”

‘‘Kathañca, cunda, tividhaṃ manasā asoceyyaṃ hoti? Idha, cunda, ekacco abhijjhālu hoti. Yaṃ taṃ parassa paravittūpakaraṇaṃ taṃ abhijjhātā [abhijjhitā (ka.) ma. ni. 1.440 passitabbaṃ] hoti – ‘aho vata yaṃ parassa taṃ mamassā’ti.

“And how, Cunda, is there threefold mental impurity? Here, Cunda, someone is covetous. He covets the wealth and possessions of others, thinking, ‘Oh, that what belongs to others would be mine!’”

‘‘Byāpannacitto hoti paduṭṭhamanasaṅkappo – ‘ime sattā haññantu vā bajjhantu vā ucchijjantu vā vinassantu vā mā vā ahesu’nti [mā vā ahesuṃ iti vā ti (sī. pī. ka.)].

He has a mind affected by ill will, with thoughts of malevolence: ‘May these beings be killed, be bound, be destroyed, be annihilated, or may they not exist at all!’”

‘‘Micchādiṭṭhiko hoti viparītadassano – ‘natthi dinnaṃ, natthi yiṭṭhaṃ, natthi hutaṃ, natthi sukaṭadukkaṭānaṃ [natthettha pāṭhabhedo] kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko, natthi ayaṃ loko, natthi paro loko, natthi mātā, natthi pitā, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi loke samaṇabrāhmaṇā sammaggatā sammāpaṭipannā ye imañca lokaṃ parañca lokaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā pavedentī’ti. Evaṃ kho, cunda, manasā tividhaṃ asoceyyaṃ hoti.

He has wrong view, distorted perspective: ‘There is no gift, no sacrifice, no offering; there is no fruit or result of good and bad actions; there is no this world, no next world; there is no mother, no father; there are no spontaneously arisen beings; there are in the world no ascetics and brahmins who are rightly gone, rightly practicing, who, having realized for themselves through direct knowledge, declare this world and the next.’ Thus, Cunda, there is threefold mental impurity.”

‘‘Ime kho, cunda, dasa akusalakammapathā [akusalā kammapathā (?)]. Imehi kho, cunda, dasahi akusalehi kammapathehi samannāgato kālasseva uṭṭhahantova sayanamhā pathaviṃ cepi āmasati, asuciyeva hoti; no cepi pathaviṃ āmasati, asuciyeva hoti.

“These, Cunda, are the ten unwholesome courses of action. Endowed with these ten unwholesome courses of action, Cunda, even if one rises early and touches the earth straight from bed, one is impure; and even if one does not touch the earth, one is impure.”

‘‘Allāni cepi gomayāni āmasati, asuciyeva hoti; no cepi allāni gomayāni āmasati, asuciyeva hoti.

“Even if one touches fresh cow dung, one is impure; even if one does not touch fresh cow dung, one is impure.”

‘‘Haritāni cepi tiṇāni āmasati, asuciyeva hoti; no cepi haritāni tiṇāni āmasati, asuciyeva hoti.

“Even if one touches green grass, one is impure; even if one does not touch green grass, one is impure.”

‘‘Aggiṃ cepi paricarati, asuciyeva hoti, no cepi aggiṃ paricarati, asuciyeva hoti.

“Even if one tends a fire, one is impure; even if one does not tend a fire, one is impure.”

‘‘Pañjaliko cepi ādiccaṃ namassati, asuciyeva hoti; no cepi pañjaliko ādiccaṃ namassati, asuciyeva hoti.

“Even if one worships the sun with joined palms, one is impure; even if one does not worship the sun with joined palms, one is impure.”

‘‘Sāyatatiyakaṃ cepi udakaṃ orohati, asuciyeva hoti; no cepi sāyatatiyakaṃ udakaṃ orohati, asuciyeva hoti. Taṃ kissa hetu? Ime, cunda, dasa akusalakammapathā asucīyeva [asucicceva (syā.)] honti asucikaraṇā ca.

“Even if one immerses oneself in water three times in the evening, one is impure; and even if one does not immerse oneself in water three times in the evening, one is impure. Why is that? Because, Cunda, these ten unwholesome courses of action are impure and make one impure.”

‘‘Imesaṃ pana, cunda, dasannaṃ akusalānaṃ kammapathānaṃ samannāgamanahetu nirayo paññāyati, tiracchānayoni paññāyati, pettivisayo paññāyati, yā vā [yā ca (ka.)] panaññāpi kāci duggatiyo [duggati hoti (syā. ka.)].

“Due to engaging in these ten unwholesome courses of action, Cunda, hell is known, the animal realm is known, the realm of ghosts is known, or whatever other states of woe there may be.”

‘‘Tividhaṃ kho, cunda, kāyena soceyyaṃ hoti; catubbidhaṃ vācāya soceyyaṃ hoti; tividhaṃ manasā soceyyaṃ hoti.

“Cunda, there is threefold bodily purity, fourfold verbal purity, and threefold mental purity.”

‘‘Kathaṃ, cunda, tividhaṃ kāyena soceyyaṃ hoti? Idha, cunda, ekacco pāṇātipātaṃ pahāya pāṇātipātā paṭivirato hoti nihitadaṇḍo nihitasattho, lajjī dayāpanno, sabbapāṇabhūtahitānukampī viharati.

“How, Cunda, is there threefold bodily purity? Here, Cunda, someone, having abandoned the destruction of life, abstains from the destruction of life; with rod and weapon laid aside, conscientious, kindly, dwells compassionate to all living beings.

‘‘Adinnādānaṃ pahāya, adinnādānā paṭivirato hoti. Yaṃ taṃ parassa paravittūpakaraṇaṃ gāmagataṃ vā araññagataṃ vā, na taṃ adinnaṃ [taṃ nādinnaṃ (ka. sī., ma. ni. 1.441)] theyyasaṅkhātaṃ ādātā hoti.

Having abandoned taking what is not given, he abstains from taking what is not given. Regarding the wealth or belongings of others, whether in the village or the wilderness, he does not take such things that are not given, with the intention to steal.”

‘‘Kāmesumicchācāraṃ pahāya, kāmesumicchācārā paṭivirato hoti yā tā māturakkhitā piturakkhitā mātāpiturakkhitā bhāturakkhitā bhaginirakkhitā ñātirakkhitā gottarakkhitā dhammarakkhitā sasāmikā saparidaṇḍā antamaso mālāguḷaparikkhittāpi, tathārūpāsu na cārittaṃ āpajjitā hoti. Evaṃ kho, cunda, tividhaṃ kāyena soceyyaṃ hoti.

Having abandoned sexual misconduct, he abstains from sexual misconduct. He does not engage with women protected by their mother, by their father, by both mother and father, by their brother, by their sister, by their relatives, by their clan, or by the Dhamma; women with a husband; women liable to punishment; or even those betrothed with a garland. Thus, Cunda, there is threefold bodily purity.”

‘‘Kathañca, cunda, catubbidhaṃ vācāya soceyyaṃ hoti? Idha, cunda, ekacco musāvādaṃ pahāya musāvādā paṭivirato hoti. Sabhaggato vā parisaggato vā ñātimajjhagato vā pūgamajjhagato vā rājakulamajjhagato vā abhinīto sakkhipuṭṭho – ‘ehambho purisa, yaṃ jānāsi taṃ vadehī’ti, so ajānaṃ vā āha ‘na jānāmī’ti, jānaṃ vā āha ‘jānāmī’ti, apassaṃ vā āha ‘na passāmī’ti, passaṃ vā āha ‘passāmī’ti. Iti attahetu vā parahetu vā āmisakiñcikkhahetu vā na sampajānamusā bhāsitā hoti.

And how, Cunda, is there fourfold purity of speech? Here, Cunda, one, having abandoned false speech, abstains from false speech. When summoned to a council, or to an assembly, or to a gathering of relatives, or to a guild, or to the royal court, and questioned as a witness: 'Come, good sir, say what you know,' if he does not know, he says, 'I do not know'; if he knows, he says, 'I know'; if he has not seen, he says, 'I have not seen'; if he has seen, he says, 'I have seen.' Thus he does not speak a deliberate lie for his own sake, or for the sake of another, or for the sake of some material gain.

‘‘Pisuṇaṃ vācaṃ pahāya, pisuṇāya vācāya paṭivirato hoti – na ito sutvā amutra akkhātā imesaṃ bhedāya, na amutra vā sutvā imesaṃ akkhātā amūsaṃ bhedāya. Iti bhinnānaṃ vā sandhātā sahitānaṃ vā anuppadātā samaggārāmo samaggarato samagganandī samaggakaraṇiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti.

Having abandoned malicious speech, one abstains from malicious speech. He does not repeat elsewhere what he has heard here in order to divide these people from those, nor does he repeat to these people what he has heard elsewhere in order to divide those people from these. Thus he is a reconciler of those who are divided and an encourager of those who are united. He delights in concord, rejoices in concord, loves concord; he speaks words that promote concord.

‘‘Pharusaṃ vācaṃ pahāya, pharusāya vācāya paṭivirato hoti. Yā sā vācā nelā kaṇṇasukhā pemanīyā hadayaṅgamā porī bahujanakantā bahujanamanāpā, tathārūpiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti.

Having abandoned harsh speech, one abstains from harsh speech. He speaks such words as are gentle, pleasing to the ear, agreeable, going to the heart, urbane, pleasing and likable to the multitude.

‘‘Samphappalāpaṃ pahāya, samphappalāpā paṭivirato hoti kālavādī bhūtavādī atthavādī dhammavādī vinayavādī; nidhānavatiṃ vācaṃ bhāsitā hoti kālena sāpadesaṃ pariyantavatiṃ atthasaṃhitaṃ. Evaṃ kho, cunda, catubbidhaṃ vācāya soceyyaṃ hoti.

Having abandoned idle chatter, one abstains from idle chatter. He speaks at the proper time, speaks what is true, speaks of the good, speaks of the Dhamma, speaks of the Discipline. He speaks words that are a treasure, full of good reasons, well-defined, and connected with the goal. Thus, Cunda, is there fourfold purity of speech.

‘‘Kathañca, cunda, tividhaṃ manasā soceyyaṃ hoti? Idha, cunda, ekacco anabhijjhālu hoti. Yaṃ taṃ parassa paravittūpakaraṇaṃ taṃ anabhijjhitā hoti – ‘aho vata yaṃ parassa taṃ mamassā’ti.

How, Cunda, is there threefold purity of mind? Here, Cunda, one is non-covetous. He does not covet the wealth and belongings of others, thinking: 'Oh, if only what belongs to others were mine!'

‘‘Abyāpannacitto hoti appaduṭṭhamanasaṅkappo – ‘ime sattā averā hontu [idaṃ padaṃ sī. syā. pī. potthakesu natthi, tathā ma. ni. 1.441] abyāpajjā, anīghā sukhī attānaṃ pariharantū’ti.

He is without ill will, with a mind unsullied by malice. He thinks: 'May these beings be free from enmity, free from affliction, free from trouble, and may they live happily!'

‘‘Sammādiṭṭhiko hoti aviparītadassano – ‘atthi dinnaṃ, atthi yiṭṭhaṃ, atthi hutaṃ, atthi sukaṭadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko, atthi ayaṃ loko, atthi paro loko, atthi mātā, atthi pitā, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi loke samaṇabrāhmaṇā sammaggatā sammāpaṭipannā ye imañca lokaṃ parañca lokaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā pavedentī’ti. Evaṃ kho, cunda, tividhaṃ manasā soceyyaṃ hoti.

He possesses right view, having an undistorted vision—believing: ‘There is what is given, there is what is offered in sacrifice, there is what is offered in oblations; there is fruit and result of good and bad actions; there is this world and the next world; there is mother and father; there are spontaneously arisen beings; there are in the world ascetics and brahmins who have gone the good way and practiced rightly, and who, having realized this world and the next for themselves by direct knowledge, make them known.’ Thus, Cunda, is there threefold purity of mind.

‘‘Ime kho, cunda, dasa kusalakammapathā. Imehi kho, cunda, dasahi kusalehi kammapathehi samannāgato kālasseva uṭṭhahantova sayanamhā pathaviṃ cepi āmasati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi pathaviṃ āmasati, suciyeva hoti.

These, Cunda, are the ten wholesome courses of action. Endowed with these ten wholesome courses of action, Cunda, even if one rises early from bed and touches the earth, one is pure; and if one does not touch the earth, one is pure.

‘‘Allāni cepi gomayāni āmasati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi allāni gomayāni āmasati, suciyeva hoti.

Even if one touches wet cow dung, one is pure; and if one does not touch wet cow dung, one is pure.

‘‘Haritāni cepi tiṇāni āmasati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi haritāni tiṇāni āmasati, suciyeva hoti.

Even if one touches green grass, one is pure; and if one does not touch green grass, one is pure.

‘‘Aggiṃ cepi paricarati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi aggiṃ paricarati, suciyeva hoti.

Even if one attends to a fire, one is pure; and if one does not attend to a fire, one is pure.

‘‘Pañjaliko cepi ādiccaṃ namassati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi pañjaliko ādiccaṃ namassati, suciyeva hoti.

Even if one worships the sun with hands clasped in reverence, one is pure; and if one does not worship the sun with hands clasped in reverence, one is pure.

‘‘Sāyatatiyakaṃ cepi udakaṃ orohati, suciyeva hoti; no cepi sāyatatiyakaṃ udakaṃ orohati, suciyeva hoti. Taṃ kissa hetu? Ime, cunda, dasa kusalakammapathā sucīyeva honti sucikaraṇā ca.

Even if one plunges into water for a third dip in the evening, one is pure; and if one does not plunge into water for a third dip in the evening, one is pure. Why is that? Because these ten wholesome courses of action, Cunda, are themselves pure and are purifiers.

‘‘Imesaṃ pana, cunda, dasannaṃ kusalānaṃ kammapathānaṃ samannāgamanahetu devā paññāyanti, manussā paññāyanti, yā vā panaññāpi kāci sugatiyo’’ti [sugati hotīti (syā.), sugati hoti (ka.)].

By being endowed with these ten wholesome courses of action, Cunda, devas are discerned, humans are discerned, and whatever other happy destinations there may be.

Evaṃ vutte cundo kammāraputto bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘abhikkantaṃ, bhante…pe… upāsakaṃ maṃ, bhante, bhagavā dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gata’’nti. Dasamaṃ.

When this was said, Cunda the metalworker said to the Blessed One: "Excellent, venerable sir! ... Let the Blessed One remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life, from today until the end of my life." The tenth.
