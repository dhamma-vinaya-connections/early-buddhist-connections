# AN 10.23 Kāyasuttaṃ

## AN 10.23 The Discourse on the Body

23. ‘‘Atthi, bhikkhave, dhammā kāyena pahātabbā, no vācāya. Atthi, bhikkhave, dhammā vācāya pahātabbā, no kāyena. Atthi, bhikkhave, dhammā neva kāyena pahātabbā no vācāya, paññāya disvā [disvā disvā (sī. syā.)] pahātabbā.

23.“Monks, there are phenomena that are to be abandoned by the body, not by speech. Monks, there are phenomena that are to be abandoned by speech, not by the body. Monks, there are phenomena that are to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā kāyena pahātabbā, no vācāya? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu akusalaṃ āpanno hoti kiñci desaṃ [kañci deva desaṃ (sī. syā.)] kāyena. Tamenaṃ anuvicca viññū sabrahmacārī evamāhaṃsu – ‘āyasmā kho akusalaṃ āpanno kiñci desaṃ kāyena. Sādhu vatāyasmā kāyaduccaritaṃ pahāya kāyasucaritaṃ bhāvetū’ti. So anuvicca viññūhi sabrahmacārīhi vuccamāno kāyaduccaritaṃ pahāya kāyasucaritaṃ bhāveti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, dhammā kāyena pahātabbā, no vācāya.

“And what, monks, are the phenomena that are to be abandoned by the body, not by speech? Here, monks, a bhikkhu has fallen into some unwholesome state by way of the body. His wise fellow practitioners, having investigated, say to him: ‘The venerable one has fallen into some unwholesome state by way of the body. It would be good if the venerable one were to abandon bodily misconduct and develop bodily good conduct.’ Being spoken to thus by his wise fellow practitioners, he abandons bodily misconduct and develops bodily good conduct. These, monks, are called the phenomena that are to be abandoned by the body, not by speech.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā vācāya pahātabbā, no kāyena? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu akusalaṃ āpanno hoti kiñci desaṃ vācāya. Tamenaṃ anuvicca viññū sabrahmacārī evamāhaṃsu – ‘āyasmā kho akusalaṃ āpanno kiñci desaṃ vācāya. Sādhu vatāyasmā vacīduccaritaṃ pahāya vacīsucaritaṃ bhāvetū’ti. So anuvicca viññūhi sabrahmacārīhi vuccamāno vacīduccaritaṃ pahāya vacīsucaritaṃ bhāveti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, dhammā vācāya pahātabbā, no kāyena.

And what, monks, are the phenomena that are to be abandoned by speech, not by the body? Here, monks, a bhikkhu has fallen into some unwholesome state by way of speech. His wise fellow practitioners, having investigated, say to him: ‘The venerable one has fallen into some unwholesome state by way of speech. It would be good if the venerable one were to abandon verbal misconduct and develop verbal good conduct.’ Being spoken to thus by his wise fellow practitioners, he abandons verbal misconduct and develops verbal good conduct. These, monks, are called the phenomena that are to be abandoned by speech, not by the body.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā neva kāyena pahātabbā no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbā? Lobho, bhikkhave, neva kāyena pahātabbo no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbo. Doso, bhikkhave…pe… moho… kodho… upanāho… makkho… paḷāso … macchariyaṃ, bhikkhave, neva kāyena pahātabbaṃ no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbaṃ.

And what, monks, are the phenomena that are to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom? Greed, monks, is to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom. Hatred… delusion… anger… resentment… denigration… insolence… stinginess, monks, are to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom.

‘‘Pāpikā, bhikkhave, issā neva kāyena pahātabbā no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbā. Katamā ca, bhikkhave, pāpikā issā? Idha, bhikkhave, ijjhati gahapatissa vā gahapatiputtassa vā dhanena vā dhaññena vā rajatena vā jātarūpena vā. Tatrāññatarassa dāsassa vā upavāsassa vā evaṃ hoti – ‘aho vatimassa gahapatissa vā gahapatiputtassa vā na ijjheyya dhanena vā dhaññena vā rajatena vā jātarūpena vā’ti. Samaṇo vā pana brāhmaṇo vā lābhī hoti cīvarapiṇḍapātasenāsanagilānapaccayabhesajjaparikkhārānaṃ. Tatrāññatarassa samaṇassa vā brāhmaṇassa vā evaṃ hoti – ‘aho vata ayamāyasmā na lābhī assa cīvarapiṇḍapātasenāsanagilānapaccayabhesajjaparikkhārāna’nti. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, pāpikā issā.

Evil envy, monks, is to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom. And what, monks, is evil envy? Here, monks, a householder or a householder’s son prospers with wealth, grain, silver, or gold. And a certain slave or hired worker thinks: ‘Oh, may this householder or householder’s son not prosper with wealth, grain, silver, or gold!’ Or a recluse or brahmin obtains robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicinal requisites for the sick. And a certain recluse or brahmin thinks: ‘Oh, may this venerable one not obtain robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicinal requisites for the sick!’ This, monks, is called evil envy.

‘‘Pāpikā, bhikkhave, icchā neva kāyena pahātabbā no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbā. Katamā ca, bhikkhave, pāpikā icchā? [vibha. 851] Idha, bhikkhave, ekacco assaddho samāno ‘saddhoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; dussīlo samāno ‘sīlavāti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; appassuto samāno ‘bahussutoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; saṅgaṇikārāmo samāno ‘pavivittoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; kusīto samāno ‘āraddhavīriyoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; muṭṭhassati samāno ‘upaṭṭhitassatīti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; asamāhito samāno ‘samāhitoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; duppañño samāno ‘paññavāti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati; akhīṇāsavo samāno ‘khīṇāsavoti maṃ jāneyyu’nti icchati. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, pāpikā icchā. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, dhammā neva kāyena pahātabbā no vācāya, paññāya disvā pahātabbā.

Evil desire, monks, is to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom. And what, monks, is evil desire? Here, monks, someone without faith wishes: ‘May they know me as one with faith!’ Someone immoral wishes: ‘May they know me as virtuous!’ Someone of little learning wishes: ‘May they know me as very learned!’ Someone delighting in company wishes: ‘May they know me as secluded!’ Someone lazy wishes: ‘May they know me as energetic!’ Someone with confused mindfulness wishes: ‘May they know me as one whose mindfulness is established!’ Someone uncollected wishes: ‘May they know me as collected!’ Someone unwise wishes: ‘May they know me as wise!’ Someone whose taints are not destroyed wishes: ‘May they know me as one whose taints are destroyed!’ This, monks, is called evil desire. These, monks, are the phenomena that are to be abandoned neither by the body nor by speech, but by seeing with wisdom.

‘‘Tañce, bhikkhave, bhikkhuṃ lobho abhibhuyya iriyati, doso… moho… kodho… upanāho… makkho… paḷāso… macchariyaṃ… pāpikā issā… pāpikā icchā abhibhuyya iriyati. So evamassa veditabbo – ‘nāyamāyasmā tathā pajānāti yathā pajānato lobho na hoti, tathāhimaṃ āyasmantaṃ lobho abhibhuyya iriyati; nāyamāyasmā tathā pajānāti yathā pajānato doso na hoti… moho… kodho… upanāho… makkho… paḷāso… macchariyaṃ… pāpikā issā… pāpikā icchā na hoti, tathāhimaṃ āyasmantaṃ pāpikā icchā abhibhuyya iriyatī’ti.

If, monks, a bhikkhu is overcome by greed, hatred, delusion, anger, resentment, denigration, insolence, stinginess, evil envy, or evil desire, and behaves accordingly, he should be understood thus: ‘This venerable one does not understand in such a way that for one who understands, greed does not arise; thus, this venerable one is overcome by greed and behaves accordingly. This venerable one does not understand in such a way that for one who understands, hatred… delusion… anger… resentment… denigration… insolence… stinginess… evil envy… or evil desire does not arise; thus, this venerable one is overcome by evil desire and behaves accordingly.’

‘‘Tañce, bhikkhave, bhikkhuṃ lobho nābhibhuyya iriyati, doso… moho… kodho… upanāho… makkho… paḷāso… macchariyaṃ… pāpikā issā… pāpikā icchā nābhibhuyya iriyati, so evamassa veditabbo – ‘tathā ayamāyasmā pajānāti yathā pajānato lobho na hoti, tathāhimaṃ āyasmantaṃ lobho nābhibhuyya iriyati; tathā ayamāyasmā pajānāti yathā pajānato doso na hoti… moho… kodho … upanāho… makkho… paḷāso… macchariyaṃ… pāpikā issā… pāpikā icchā na hoti, tathāhimaṃ āyasmantaṃ pāpikā icchā nābhibhuyya iriyatī’’’ti. Tatiyaṃ.

If, monks, a bhikkhu is not overcome by greed, hatred, delusion, anger, resentment, denigration, insolence, stinginess, evil envy, or evil desire, and does not behave accordingly, he should be understood thus: ‘This venerable one understands in such a way that for one who understands, greed does not arise; thus, this venerable one is not overcome by greed and does not behave accordingly. This venerable one understands in such a way that for one who understands, hatred… delusion… anger… resentment… denigration… insolence… stinginess… evil envy… or evil desire does not arise; thus, this venerable one is not overcome by evil desire and does not behave accordingly.’ This is the third.
