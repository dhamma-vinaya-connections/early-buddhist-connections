# AN 10.116 Ajitasuttaṃ

## AN 10.116 Ajitasutta: The Discourse to Ajita

116. Atha kho ajito paribbājako yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavatā saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho ajito paribbājako bhagavantaṃ etadavoca –

116.Then the wanderer Ajita approached the Blessed One; having approached, he exchanged polite greetings with the Blessed One. After an exchange of friendly, courteous conversation, he sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the wanderer Ajita said this to the Blessed One:

‘‘Amhākaṃ, bho gotama, paṇḍito nāma sabrahmacārī. Tena pañcamattāni cittaṭṭhānasatāni cintitāni, yehi aññatitthiyā upāraddhāva jānanti [upāraddhā pajānanti (sī.)] upāraddhasmā’’ti [upāraddhamhāti (sī. pī.)].

“Master Gotama, we have a wise co-religious brother. He has devised five hundred mental positions by which members of other sects know when they are being attacked and when they are not.”

Atha kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘dhāretha no tumhe, bhikkhave, paṇḍitavatthūnī’’ti? ‘‘Etassa, bhagavā, kālo etassa, sugata, kālo yaṃ bhagavā bhāseyya, bhagavato sutvā bhikkhū dhāressantī’’ti.

Then the Blessed One addressed the monks: “Monks, do you bear in mind the qualities that make one a wise person?” “Now is the time, Blessed One. Now is the time, Sugata, for the Blessed One to speak. Having heard from the Blessed One, the monks will remember it.”

‘‘Tena hi, bhikkhave, suṇātha, sādhukaṃ manasi karotha; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca –

“Then listen, monks, apply your mind well, I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” those monks replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

‘‘Idha, bhikkhave, ekacco adhammikena vādena adhammikaṃ vādaṃ abhiniggaṇhāti abhinippīḷeti, tena ca adhammikaṃ parisaṃ rañjeti. Tena sā adhammikā parisā uccāsaddamahāsaddā hoti – ‘paṇḍito vata, bho, paṇḍito vata, bho’ti.

“Here, monks, someone with an unrighteous argument attacks an unrighteous argument and crushes it, and thereby pleases an unrighteous assembly. That unrighteous assembly becomes loud and uproarious, saying, ‘How wise he is, sir! How wise he is, sir!’

‘‘Idha pana, bhikkhave, ekacco adhammikena vādena dhammikaṃ vādaṃ abhiniggaṇhāti abhinippīḷeti, tena ca adhammikaṃ parisaṃ rañjeti. Tena sā adhammikā parisā uccāsaddamahāsaddā hoti – ‘paṇḍito vata, bho, paṇḍito vata, bho’ti.

“Here, monks, someone with an unrighteous argument attacks a righteous argument and crushes it with an unrighteous argument, and thereby pleases an unrighteous assembly. That unrighteous assembly becomes loud and uproarious, saying, ‘How wise he is, good sir! How wise he is, good sir!’

‘‘Idha pana, bhikkhave, ekacco adhammikena vādena dhammikañca vādaṃ adhammikañca vādaṃ abhiniggaṇhāti abhinippīḷeti, tena ca adhammikaṃ parisaṃ rañjeti. Tena sā adhammikā parisā uccāsaddamahāsaddā hoti – ‘paṇḍito vata, bho, paṇḍito vata, bho’ti.

“Here, monks, someone with an unrighteous argument refutes and oppresses both a righteous and an unrighteous argument, and thereby pleases an unrighteous assembly. That unrighteous assembly becomes loud and uproarious, saying, ‘How wise he is, sir! How wise he is, sir!’

‘‘Adhammo ca, bhikkhave, veditabbo dhammo ca; anattho ca veditabbo attho ca. Adhammañca viditvā dhammañca, anatthañca viditvā atthañca yathā dhammo yathā attho tathā paṭipajjitabbaṃ.

“Monks, one should understand what is unrighteous and what is righteous, what is harmful and what is beneficial. Having understood what is unrighteous and what is righteous, what is harmful and what is beneficial, one should practice in accordance with the Dhamma and the benefit.”

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, adhammo, katamo ca dhammo, katamo ca anattho, katamo ca attho? Micchādiṭṭhi, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammādiṭṭhi dhammo; ye ca micchādiṭṭhipaccayā aneke pāpakā akusalā dhammā sambhavanti, ayaṃ anattho; sammādiṭṭhipaccayā ca aneke kusalā dhammā bhāvanāpāripūriṃ gacchanti, ayaṃ attho.

“And what, monks, is unrighteousness, what is righteousness, what is disadvantage, and what is advantage? Wrong view, monks, is unrighteousness; right view is righteousness. And the many evil, unwholesome qualities that arise conditioned by wrong view: this is disadvantage. And the many wholesome qualities that go to the perfection of development conditioned by right view: this is advantage.”

‘‘Micchāsaṅkappo, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāsaṅkappo dhammo… micchāvācā, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāvācā dhammo… micchākammanto, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammākammanto dhammo… micchāājīvo, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāājīvo dhammo … micchāvāyāmo, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāvāyāmo dhammo… micchāsati, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāsati dhammo… micchāsamādhi, bhikkhave adhammo; sammāsamādhi dhammo… micchāñāṇaṃ, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāñāṇaṃ dhammo.

“Wrong thought, monks, is unrighteousness; right thought is righteousness… Wrong speech, monks, is unrighteousness; right speech is righteousness… Wrong action, monks, is unrighteousness; right action is righteousness… Wrong livelihood, monks, is unrighteousness; right livelihood is righteousness… Wrong effort, monks, is unrighteousness; right effort is righteousness… Wrong mindfulness, monks, is unrighteousness; right mindfulness is righteousness… Wrong samādhi, monks, is unrighteousness; right samādhi is righteousness… Wrong knowledge, monks, is unrighteousness; right knowledge is righteousness.”

‘‘Micchāvimutti, bhikkhave, adhammo; sammāvimutti dhammo; ye ca micchāvimuttipaccayā aneke pāpakā akusalā dhammā sambhavanti, ayaṃ anattho; sammāvimuttipaccayā ca aneke kusalā dhammā bhāvanāpāripūriṃ gacchanti, ayaṃ attho.

“Wrong liberation, monks, is unrighteousness; right liberation is righteousness. And whatever numerous evil, unwholesome states arise conditioned by wrong liberation, this is harm. But conditioned by right liberation, numerous wholesome states go to the perfection of development; this is benefit.”

‘‘‘Adhammo ca, bhikkhave, veditabbo dhammo ca; anattho ca veditabbo attho ca. Adhammañca viditvā dhammañca, anatthañca viditvā atthañca yathā dhammo yathā attho tathā paṭipajjitabba’nti, iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vutta’’nti. Catutthaṃ.

“‘Monks, the unrighteous should be understood, and the righteous should be understood; the disadvantage should be understood, and the advantage should be understood. Having understood the unrighteous and the righteous, having understood the disadvantage and the advantage, one should practice according to the righteous, according to the advantage.’ That is what was said, and this is why it was said.” The fourth.
