# Snp 1.12 Munisuttaṃ

## Snp 1.12 The Discourse on the Sage

209.

Santhavāto [sandhavato (ka.)] bhayaṃ jātaṃ, niketā jāyate rajo;Aniketamasanthavaṃ, etaṃ ve munidassanaṃ.

From intimacy, fear is born; from a dwelling, dust arises. The state of homelessness, free from intimacy—this, indeed, is the vision of the sage.

210.

Yo jātamucchijja na ropayeyya, jāyantamassa nānuppavecche;Tamāhu ekaṃ muninaṃ carantaṃ, addakkhi so santipadaṃ mahesi.

He who, having cut off what is born, would not plant it again, nor would he let it grow again—him they call a solitary sage, wandering alone. The great seer has seen the peaceful state.

211.

Saṅkhāya vatthūni pamāya [pahāya (ka. sī. ka.), samāya (ka.) pa + mī + tvā = pamāya, yathā nissāyātipadaṃ] bījaṃ, sinehamassa nānuppavecche;Sa ve munī jātikhayantadassī, takkaṃ pahāya na upeti saṅkhaṃ.

Having reckoned the objects, having measured the seed, he would not foster affection. That sage, indeed, seeing the destruction of birth, having abandoned reasoning, does not resort to reckoning.

212.

Aññāya sabbāni nivesanāni, anikāmayaṃ aññatarampi tesaṃ;Sa ve munī vītagedho agiddho, nāyūhatī pāragato hi hoti.

Having known all abodes, not desiring any one of them, that sage, indeed, is without longing, unattached. He does not accumulate, for he has gone beyond.

213.

Sabbābhibhuṃ sabbaviduṃ sumedhaṃ, sabbesu dhammesu anūpalittaṃ;Sabbañjahaṃ taṇhakkhaye vimuttaṃ, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni [muniṃ (sī. pī.)] vedayanti.

The all-conquering, all-knowing, wise, untainted by all things, the all-renouncing, freed through the destruction of craving—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

214.

Paññābalaṃ sīlavatūpapannaṃ, samāhitaṃ jhānarataṃ satīmaṃ;Saṅgā pamuttaṃ akhilaṃ anāsavaṃ, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

Him who is endowed with the strength of wisdom, virtuous, composed, delighting in jhāna, mindful; freed from attachment, free from defect, without influxes—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

215.

Ekaṃ carantaṃ munimappamattaṃ, nindāpasaṃsāsu avedhamānaṃ;Sīhaṃva saddesu asantasantaṃ, vātaṃva jālamhi asajjamānaṃ;Padmaṃva [padumaṃva (sī. syā. pī.)] toyena alippamānaṃ [alimpamānaṃ (syā. ka.)], netāramaññesamanaññaneyyaṃ;Taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

Him who wanders alone, the sage, ever heedful, unshaken by blame and praise; like a lion unmoved by sounds, like the wind not caught in a net, like a lotus unstained by water; a leader of others, not to be led by others—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

216.

Yo ogahaṇe thambhorivābhijāyati, yasmiṃ pare vācāpariyantaṃ [vācaṃ pariyantaṃ (ka.)] vadanti;Taṃ vītarāgaṃ susamāhitindriyaṃ, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

He who, like a pillar at a ford, stands unshaken, against whom others speak words to their limit; him, free from passion, with senses well-composed—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

217.

Yo ve ṭhitatto tasaraṃva ujju, jigucchati kammehi pāpakehi;Vīmaṃsamāno visamaṃ samañca, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

He who stands firm, indeed, like an arrow-shaft, straight, who loathes evil deeds; discerning the uneven and the even—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

218.

Yo saññatatto na karoti pāpaṃ, daharo majjhimo ca muni [daharo ca majjho ca munī (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] yatatto;Arosaneyyo na so roseti kañci [na roseti (syā.)], taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

He who is self-controlled does no evil, whether young or middle-aged, a sage controlled in mind; he is not deserving of anger and angers no one—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

219.

Yadaggato majjhato sesato vā, piṇḍaṃ labhetha paradattūpajīvī;Nālaṃ thutuṃ nopi nipaccavādī, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

Whether he obtains alms from the first, middle, or last portion, living on what others give, he is not prone to praise, nor does he speak blame—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

220.

Muniṃ carantaṃ virataṃ methunasmā, yo yobbane nopanibajjhate kvaci;Madappamādā virataṃ vippamuttaṃ, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

The sage who wanders, restrained from sexual intercourse, who in youth is not engrossed anywhere; restrained from intoxication and heedlessness, completely liberated—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

221.

Aññāya lokaṃ paramatthadassiṃ, oghaṃ samuddaṃ atitariya tādiṃ;Taṃ chinnaganthaṃ asitaṃ anāsavaṃ, taṃ vāpi dhīrā muni vedayanti.

Having understood the world, seeing the ultimate reality, having crossed the flood, the ocean, the steadfast one; him, with fetters cut, unattached, without taints—him, too, the wise declare a sage.

222.

Asamā ubho dūravihāravuttino, gihī [gihi (ka.)] dāraposī amamo ca subbato;Parapāṇarodhāya gihī asaññato, niccaṃ munī rakkhati pāṇine [pāṇino (sī.)] yato.

The householder who supports wife and children, and the well-behaved monk, both have far-separated modes of living and are unequal. The unrestrained householder obstructs the life of other beings, whereas the sage always protects living beings.

223.

Sikhī yathā nīlagīvo [nīlagivo (syā.)] vihaṅgamo, haṃsassa nopeti javaṃ kudācanaṃ;Evaṃ gihī nānukaroti bhikkhuno, munino vivittassa vanamhi jhāyatoti.

Just as the blue-necked peacock never matches the speed of the swan, so the householder does not emulate the bhikkhu, the sage secluded and meditating in the forest.

Munisuttaṃ dvādasamaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ.

The Discourse on the Sage, the twelfth, is concluded.

Uragavaggo paṭhamo niṭṭhito.

The first Chapter on the Serpent is concluded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

The Summary:

Urago dhaniyo ceva, visāṇañca tathā kasi;Cundo parābhavo ceva, vasalo mettabhāvanā.

Uraga, Dhaniya, and also Visāṇa, Kasi; Cunda, Parābhava, and Vasala, Mettābhāvanā.

Sātāgiro āḷavako, vijayo ca tathā muni;Dvādasetāni suttāni, uragavaggoti vuccatīti.

Sātāgira, Āḷavaka, Vijaya, and the Sage too; these twelve suttas are called the Uraga Chapter.
