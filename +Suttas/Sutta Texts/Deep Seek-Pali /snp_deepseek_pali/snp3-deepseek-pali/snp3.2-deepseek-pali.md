# Snp 3.2 Padhānasuttaṃ

## Snp 3.2 The Discourse on Striving

427.

‘‘Taṃ maṃ padhānapahitattaṃ, nadiṃ nerañjaraṃ pati;Viparakkamma jhāyantaṃ, yogakkhemassa pattiyā.

As I was striving resolutely near the Nerañjarā river, meditating with utmost exertion for the attainment of security from bondage,

428.

‘‘Namucī karuṇaṃ vācaṃ, bhāsamāno upāgami;‘Kiso tvamasi dubbaṇṇo, santike maraṇaṃ tava.

Namucī approached me, speaking words of pity: ‘You are emaciated and ill-favored; you are near to death.

429.

‘‘‘Sahassabhāgo maraṇassa, ekaṃso tava jīvitaṃ;Jīva bho jīvitaṃ seyyo, jīvaṃ puññāni kāhasi.

“‘A thousand parts of you belong to death, only one part to life. Live, good sir, life is better! By living you can perform meritorious deeds.

430.

‘‘‘Carato ca te brahmacariyaṃ, aggihuttañca jūhato;Pahūtaṃ cīyate puññaṃ, kiṃ padhānena kāhasi.

“‘By practicing the holy life and tending the sacrificial fire, much merit is accumulated. What can you accomplish by striving?

431.

‘‘‘Duggo maggo padhānāya, dukkaro durabhisambhavo’’’;Imā gāthā bhaṇaṃ māro, aṭṭhā buddhassa santike.

“‘The path of striving is hard to follow, difficult, hard to accomplish.’ Speaking these verses, Māra stood near the Buddha.

432.

Taṃ tathāvādinaṃ māraṃ, bhagavā etadabravi;‘‘Pamattabandhu pāpima, yenatthena [senatthena (?), attano atthena (aṭṭha. saṃvaṇṇanā)] idhāgato.

To Māra who had spoken thus, the Blessed One replied: “O Evil One, kinsman of the negligent, you have come here for your own ends.

433.

‘‘Aṇumattopi [aṇumattenapi (sī. syā.)] puññena, attho mayhaṃ na vijjati;Yesañca attho puññena, te māro vattumarahati.

“I have not the slightest need for merit. It is those who have need of merit that Māra is fit to address.

434.

‘‘Atthi saddhā tathā [tato (sī. pī.), tapo (syā. ka.)] vīriyaṃ, paññā ca mama vijjati;Evaṃ maṃ pahitattampi, kiṃ jīvamanupucchasi.

“I have faith and also energy, and wisdom is found in me. Since I am so resolved, why do you ask me to live?

435.

‘‘Nadīnamapi sotāni, ayaṃ vāto visosaye;Kiñca me pahitattassa, lohitaṃ nupasussaye.

“This wind could dry up even the currents of rivers; why, then, when I am so resolved, should it not dry up my blood?

436.

‘‘Lohite sussamānamhi, pittaṃ semhañca sussati;Maṃsesu khīyamānesu, bhiyyo cittaṃ pasīdati;Bhiyyo sati ca paññā ca, samādhi mama tiṭṭhati.

“As my blood dries up, bile and phlegm dry up; as my flesh wastes away, my mind becomes all the more serene, and my mindfulness, wisdom, and samādhi stand more firmly.

437.

‘‘Tassa mevaṃ viharato, pattassuttamavedanaṃ;Kāmesu [kāme (sī. syā.)] nāpekkhate cittaṃ, passa sattassa suddhataṃ.

“For me dwelling thus, having attained the supreme feeling, my mind does not long for sensual pleasures. Behold the purity of a being!

438.

‘‘Kāmā te paṭhamā senā, dutiyā arati vuccati;Tatiyā khuppipāsā te, catutthī taṇhā pavuccati.

“Sensual pleasures are your first army, the second is called discontent, the third is hunger and thirst, the fourth is called craving.

439.

‘‘Pañcamaṃ [pañcamī (sī. pī.)] thinamiddhaṃ te, chaṭṭhā bhīrū pavuccati;Sattamī vicikicchā te, makkho thambho te aṭṭhamo.

“Your fifth is sloth and torpor, the sixth is called cowardice, your seventh is doubt, your eighth is hypocrisy and stubbornness.

440.

‘‘Lābho siloko sakkāro, micchāladdho ca yo yaso;Yo cattānaṃ samukkaṃse, pare ca avajānati.

“Gain, renown, and honor, and fame wrongly obtained; and whoever extols himself and disparages others—

441.

‘‘Esā namuci te senā, kaṇhassābhippahārinī;Na naṃ asūro jināti, jetvā ca labhate sukhaṃ.

“This, Namucī, is your army, the striking force of the Black One. A coward cannot conquer it, but by conquering it one finds happiness.

442.

‘‘Esa muñjaṃ parihare, dhiratthu mama [ida (ka.)] jīvitaṃ;Saṅgāme me mataṃ seyyo, yaṃ ce jīve parājito.

“I wear this muñja grass! Shame on life in this world! Better for me is death in battle than that I should live on defeated.

443.

‘‘Pagāḷhettha na dissanti, eke samaṇabrāhmaṇā;Tañca maggaṃ na jānanti, yena gacchanti subbatā.

“Some ascetics and brahmins, having plunged into this battle, are lost to sight. They do not know the path by which those of good vows travel.

444.

‘‘Samantā dhajiniṃ disvā, yuttaṃ māraṃ savāhanaṃ;Yuddhāya paccuggacchāmi, mā maṃ ṭhānā acāvayi.

“Seeing his army arrayed on all sides, and Māra on his mount, I go forth to battle. May he not dislodge me from my place!

445.

‘‘Yaṃ te taṃ nappasahati, senaṃ loko sadevako;Taṃ te paññāya bhecchāmi [gacchāmi (sī.), vecchāmi (syā.), vajjhāmi (ka.)], āmaṃ pattaṃva asmanā [pakkaṃva amunā (ka.)].

“That army of yours, which the world with its gods cannot overcome, I will shatter with wisdom, as one shatters an unbaked pot with a stone.

446.

‘‘Vasīkaritvā [vasiṃ karitvā (bahūsu)] saṅkappaṃ, satiñca sūpatiṭṭhitaṃ;Raṭṭhā raṭṭhaṃ vicarissaṃ, sāvake vinayaṃ puthū.

“Having mastered my thoughts and with mindfulness well established, I will wander from country to country, training many disciples.

447.

‘‘Te appamattā pahitattā, mama sāsanakārakā;Akāmassa [akāmā (ka.)] te gamissanti, yattha gantvā na socare’’.

“They, diligent and resolute, followers of my teaching, will go, against your will, to that state where, having gone, they do not grieve.”

448.

‘‘Satta vassāni bhagavantaṃ, anubandhiṃ padāpadaṃ;Otāraṃ nādhigacchissaṃ, sambuddhassa satīmato.

For seven years I followed the Blessed One step by step, but I found no opening in the mindful, fully enlightened one.

449.

‘‘Medavaṇṇaṃva pāsāṇaṃ, vāyaso anupariyagā;Apettha muduṃ [mudu (sī.)] vindema, api assādanā siyā.

A crow circled a rock that looked like a lump of fat, thinking: ‘Perhaps I can find something soft here, something tasty.’

450.

‘‘Aladdhā tattha assādaṃ, vāyasetto apakkami;Kākova selamāsajja, nibbijjāpema gotamaṃ’’.

“Finding no taste there, the crow departed. Just as the crow attacked the rock, so I, in disgust, will depart from Gotama.”

451.

Tassa sokaparetassa, vīṇā kacchā abhassatha;Tato so dummano yakkho, tatthevantaradhāyathāti.

From him, overcome by sorrow, the lute slipped from under his arm. Then that dejected yakkha vanished right on that very spot.

Padhānasuttaṃ dutiyaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ.

The Discourse on Striving, the second, is finished.
