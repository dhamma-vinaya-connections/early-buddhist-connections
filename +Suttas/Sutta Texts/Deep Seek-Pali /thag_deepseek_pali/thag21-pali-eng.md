# Thag 21. Mahānipāto
## 21. The Great Chapter

1218.
‘‘Nikkhantaṃ vata maṃ santaṃ, agārasmānagāriyaṃ;Vitakkā upadhāvanti, pagabbhā kaṇhato ime.

Though I have gone forth in peace from the home to the homeless life, still these insolent thoughts from the Dark One assail me.

1219.
‘‘Uggaputtā mahissāsā, sikkhitā daḷhadhammino [daḷhadhanvino (sī. aṭṭha.)];Samantā parikireyyuṃ, sahassaṃ apalāyinaṃ.

Like fierce warriors, great archers, well-trained and strong-bowed, a thousand who would never flee, might surround one on all sides.

1220.
‘‘Sacepi ettakā [etato (saṃ. ni. 1.209)] bhiyyo, āgamissanti itthiyo;Neva maṃ byādhayissanti [byāthayissanti (?)], dhamme samhi [dhammesvamhi (syā. ka.)] patiṭṭhito.

Even if many more women than that should come, they would not trouble me, for I am firmly established in the Dhamma.

1221.
‘‘Sakkhī hi me sutaṃ etaṃ, buddhassādiccabandhuno;Nibbānagamanaṃ maggaṃ, tattha me nirato mano. I have heard this directly from the Buddha, kinsman of the sun: the path that goes to Nibbāna—in that my mind delights.


1222.
‘‘Evaṃ ce maṃ viharantaṃ, pāpima upagacchasi;Tathā maccu karissāmi, na me maggampi dakkhasi.

If you approach me while I am dwelling thus, O Evil One, I will act in such a way, O Death, that you will not even see my path.

1223.
‘‘Aratiñca [aratiṃ (bahūsu)] ratiñca pahāya, sabbaso gehasitañca vitakkaṃ;Vanathaṃ na kareyya kuhiñci, nibbanatho avanatho sa [nibbanatho arato sa hi (saṃ. ni. 1.210)] bhikkhu.

Having abandoned discontent and delight and all thoughts based on the household life, he would not create a jungle of craving anywhere. That bhikkhu is free from craving’s jungle, free from craving.

1224.
‘‘Yamidha pathaviñca vehāsaṃ, rūpagataṃ jagatogadhaṃ kiñci;Parijīyati sabbamaniccaṃ, evaṃ samecca caranti mutattā.

Whatever has form here, on earth or in the sky, whatever is included in the world—all that is impermanent and perishes. Having understood this, the liberated fare on.

1225.
‘‘Upadhīsu janā gadhitāse, diṭṭhasute [diṭṭhe sute (sī.)] paṭighe ca mute ca;Ettha vinodaya chandamanejo, yo hettha na limpati muni tamāhu [taṃ munimāhu (saṃ. ni. 1.210)].

People are greedy for the substrates of existence, for what is seen and heard, sensed and cognized. Dispel desire for these things, O imperturbable one. One who is not smeared by these things they call a sage.

1226.
‘‘Atha saṭṭhisitā savitakkā, puthujjanatāya [puthū janatāya (saṃ. ni. 1.210)] adhammā niviṭṭhā;Na ca vaggagatassa kuhiñci, no pana duṭṭhullagāhī [duṭṭhullabhāṇī (saṃ. ni. 1.210)] sa bhikkhu.

Those attached to the sixty speculative views, full of thoughts, are settled in the ways of worldlings, in what is not the Dhamma. But that bhikkhu does not belong to any faction, nor does he grasp at anything gross.

1227.
‘‘Dabbo cirarattasamāhito, akuhako nipako apihālu;Santaṃ padaṃ ajjhagamā muni, paṭicca parinibbuto kaṅkhati kālaṃ. Dabba, long collected, is not a fraud; he is prudent and unenvious. The sage has attained the peaceful state; having attained final Nibbāna, he awaits his time.


1228.
‘‘Mānaṃ pajahassu gotama, mānapathañca jahassu asesaṃ;Mānapathamhi sa mucchito, vippaṭisārīhuvā cirarattaṃ.

Abandon conceit, Gotama! Abandon the path of conceit completely. Infatuated with the path of conceit, one is remorseful for a long time.

1229.
‘‘Makkhena makkhitā pajā, mānahatā nirayaṃ papatanti;Socanti janā cirarattaṃ, mānahatā nirayaṃ upapannā.

People smeared with contempt, slain by conceit, fall into hell. Slain by conceit, reborn in hell, people grieve for a long time.

1230.
‘‘Na hi socati bhikkhu kadāci, maggajino sammā paṭipanno;Kittiñca sukhañcānubhoti, dhammadasoti tamāhu tathattaṃ.

A bhikkhu never grieves, a conqueror of the path who has practiced rightly. He experiences fame and happiness; they call him in truth ‘one who has seen the Dhamma.’

1231.
‘‘Tasmā akhilo idha [akhilo (sī.), akhilodha (saṃ. ni. 1.211)] padhānavā, nīvaraṇāni pahāya visuddho;Mānañca pahāya asesaṃ, vijjāyantakaro samitāvī.

Therefore, being faultless and striving here, having abandoned the hindrances, purified; having abandoned conceit completely, one makes an end with knowledge, being at peace.

1232.
‘‘Kāmarāgena ḍayhāmi, cittaṃ me pariḍayhati;Sādhu nibbāpanaṃ brūhi, anukampāya gotama.

I am burning with the fire of lust; my mind is ablaze. Out of compassion, O Gotama, please tell me the way to quench it.

1233.
‘‘Saññāya vipariyesā, cittaṃ te pariḍayhati;Nimittaṃ parivajjehi, subhaṃ rāgūpasaṃhitaṃ ( ) [(saṅkhāre parato passa, dukkhato mā ca attato; nibbāpehi mahārāgaṃ, mā dayhittho punappunaṃ;) (sī. saṃ. ni. 1.212) uddānagāthāyaṃ ekasattatītisaṅkhyā ca, theragāthāṭṭhakathā ca passitabbā].

Your mind is ablaze because of a perversion of perception. You should avoid the sign of the beautiful connected with lust.

1234.
‘‘Asubhāya cittaṃ bhāvehi, ekaggaṃ susamāhitaṃ;Sati kāyagatā tyatthu, nibbidābahulo bhava.

Develop your mind on the foul, one-pointed and well collected. Let your mindfulness be directed to the body; be full of disenchantment.

1235.
‘‘Animittañca bhāvehi, mānānusayamujjaha;Tato mānābhisamayā, upasanto carissasi.

And develop the signless; uproot the underlying tendency to conceit. Then, through the full understanding of conceit, you will fare on at peace.

1236.
‘‘Tameva vācaṃ bhāseyya, yāyattānaṃ na tāpaye;Pare ca na vihiṃseyya, sā ve vācā subhāsitā.

One should speak only that word by which one would not torment oneself nor harm others. That is, indeed, a well-spoken word.

1237.
‘‘Piyavācameva bhāseyya, yā vācā paṭinanditā;Yaṃ anādāya pāpāni, paresaṃ bhāsate piyaṃ.

One should speak only what is pleasant, the word that is welcomed. When, taking up nothing evil, one speaks what is pleasing to others.

1238.
‘‘Saccaṃ ve amatā vācā, esa dhammo sanantano;Sacce atthe ca dhamme ca, āhu santo patiṭṭhitā.

Truth is indeed the immortal word: this is the eternal Dhamma. The good, they say, are established in truth, in the goal, and in the Dhamma.

1239.
‘‘Yaṃ buddho bhāsati vācaṃ, khemaṃ nibbānapattiyā;Dukkhassantakiriyāya, sā ve vācānamuttamā. The word the Buddha speaks, secure, for the attainment of Nibbāna, for making an end of suffering—that is truly the best of words.


1240.
‘‘Gambhīrapañño medhāvī, maggāmaggassa kovido;Sāriputto mahāpañño, dhammaṃ deseti bhikkhunaṃ. Sāriputta, of profound wisdom, intelligent, an expert in the path and the non-path, one of great wisdom, teaches the Dhamma to the bhikkhus.


1241.
‘‘Saṅkhittenapi deseti, vitthārenapi bhāsati;Sālikāyiva nigghoso, paṭibhānaṃ udiyyati [udīrayi (sī.), udīyyati (syā.), udayyati (?) uṭṭhahatīti taṃsaṃvaṇṇanā].

He teaches both in brief and speaks in detail. Like the sound of a myna bird, his inspiration shines forth.

1242.
‘‘Tassa taṃ desayantassa, suṇanti madhuraṃ giraṃ;Sarena rajanīyena, savanīyena vaggunā;Udaggacittā muditā, sotaṃ odhenti bhikkhavo.

While he is teaching, they listen to his sweet speech. With a voice that is lovely, pleasant to hear, and melodious, the bhikkhus, with elated and joyful minds, incline their ear.

1243.
‘‘Ajja pannarase visuddhiyā, bhikkhū pañcasatā samāgatā;Saṃyojanabandhanacchidā, anīghā khīṇapunabbhavā isī.

Today is the fifteenth, the day of purity; five hundred bhikkhus have assembled. They are seers who have cut the fetters and bonds, untroubled, their renewed existence destroyed.

1244.
‘‘Cakkavattī yathā rājā, amaccaparivārito;Samantā anupariyeti, sāgarantaṃ mahiṃ imaṃ.

Just as a wheel-turning king, surrounded by his ministers, travels all around this earth bounded by the ocean.

1245.
‘‘Evaṃ vijitasaṅgāmaṃ, satthavāhaṃ anuttaraṃ;Sāvakā payirupāsanti, tevijjā maccuhāyino.

Even so do the disciples attend upon the victor in battle, the unsurpassed caravan leader—they who possess the triple knowledge and have abandoned death.

1246.
‘‘Sabbe bhagavato puttā, palāpettha na vijjati;Taṇhāsallassa hantāraṃ, vande ādiccabandhunaṃ.

All are sons of the Blessed One; there is no chaff to be found here. I pay homage to the Kinsman of the Sun, the remover of the dart of craving.

1247.
‘‘Parosahassaṃ bhikkhūnaṃ, sugataṃ payirupāsati;Desentaṃ virajaṃ dhammaṃ, nibbānaṃ akutobhayaṃ. More than a thousand bhikkhus attend upon the Well-Farer as he teaches the stainless Dhamma, Nibbāna, which is free from fear from any quarter.


1248.
‘‘Suṇanti dhammaṃ vimalaṃ, sammāsambuddhadesitaṃ;Sobhati vata sambuddho, bhikkhusaṅghapurakkhato. They listen to the immaculate Dhamma taught by the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One. The Enlightened One truly shines, honored by the Saṅgha of bhikkhus.


1249.
‘‘‘Nāganāmo’si bhagavā, isīnaṃ isisattamo;Mahāmeghova hutvāna, sāvake abhivassasi. You are the Nāga by name, O Blessed One, the seventh of the seers. Having become like a great rain cloud, you rain down upon your disciples.


1250.
‘‘Divā vihārā nikkhamma, satthudassanakamyatā;Sāvako te mahāvīra, pāde vandati vaṅgiso. Coming forth from his day’s abiding, desiring to see the Teacher, your disciple Vaṅgīsa, O great hero, venerates your feet.


1251.
‘‘Ummaggapathaṃ mārassa, abhibhuyya carati pabhijja khīlāni;Taṃ passatha bandhapamuñcakaraṃ, asitaṃva bhāgaso pavibhajja. Having overcome Māra’s devious path, he fares on, having broken the stakes. See him, the one who brings release from bonds, who analyzes things piece by piece, unattached.


1252.
‘‘Oghassa hi nitaraṇatthaṃ, anekavihitaṃ maggaṃ akkhāsi;Tasmiñca amate akkhāte, dhammadasā ṭhitā asaṃhīrā.

For the sake of crossing the flood he has proclaimed the path in its many aspects. And when that deathless state has been proclaimed, those who see the Dhamma stand firm, unshakable.

1253.
‘‘Pajjotakaro ativijjha [ativijjha dhammaṃ (sī.)], sabbaṭhitīnaṃ atikkamamaddasa [atikkamamadda (sī. ka.)];Ñatvā ca sacchikatvā ca, aggaṃ so desayi dasaddhānaṃ.

The light-maker, having penetrated, saw the overcoming of all standpoints. Having known it and realized it for himself, he taught the supreme state to all.

1254.
‘‘Evaṃ sudesite dhamme, ko pamādo vijānataṃ dhammaṃ;Tasmā hi tassa bhagavato sāsane, appamatto sadā namassamanusikkhe.

When the Dhamma has been so well expounded, what occasion is there for negligence for those who understand it? Therefore, in the Blessed One’s dispensation, one should always train heedfully, paying homage.

1255.
‘‘Buddhānubuddho yo thero, koṇḍañño tibbanikkamo;Lābhī sukhavihārānaṃ, vivekānaṃ abhiṇhaso. The elder who was awakened after the Buddha, Koṇḍañña of keen exertion, is a constant gainer of pleasant abidings, of states of seclusion.


1256.
‘‘Yaṃ sāvakena pattabbaṃ, satthu sāsanakārinā;Sabbassa taṃ anuppattaṃ, appamattassa sikkhato.

Whatever is to be attained by a disciple who carries out the Teacher’s instruction, all that has been attained by him, training heedfully.

1257.
‘‘Mahānubhāvo tevijjo, cetopariyakovido;Koṇḍañño buddhadāyādo, pāde vandati satthuno. Of great power, possessing the triple knowledge, an expert in encompassing the minds of others, Koṇḍañña, the Buddha’s heir, venerates the Teacher’s feet.


1258.
‘‘Nagassa passe āsīnaṃ, muniṃ dukkhassa pāraguṃ;Sāvakā payirupāsanti, tevijjā maccuhāyino.

Seated on the mountain slope, the sage who has gone beyond suffering, disciples attend upon him, possessors of the threefold knowledge, overcomers of death.

1259.
‘‘Cetasā [te cetasā (saṃ. ni. 1.218)] anupariyeti, moggallāno mahiddhiko;Cittaṃ nesaṃ samanvesaṃ [samannesaṃ (saṃ. ni. 1.218)], vippamuttaṃ nirūpadhiṃ. Moggallāna of great psychic power encompasses them with his mind, investigating their minds, liberated and without substrate.


1260.
‘‘Evaṃ sabbaṅgasampannaṃ, muniṃ dukkhassa pāraguṃ;Anekākārasampannaṃ, payirupāsanti gotamaṃ.

Thus they attend upon Gotama, the sage who has gone beyond suffering, accomplished in all respects, endowed with numerous qualities.

1261.
‘‘Cando yathā vigatavalāhake nabhe, virocati vītamalova bhāṇumā;Evampi aṅgīrasa tvaṃ mahāmuni, atirocasi yasasā sabbalokaṃ. Just as the moon shines in a cloudless sky, and the sun, free from stain, is radiant, so too, O Aṅgīrasa, great sage, you outshine the entire world with your glory.


1262.
‘‘Kāveyyamattā vicarimha pubbe, gāmā gāmaṃ purā puraṃ;Athaddasāma sambuddhaṃ, sabbadhammāna pāraguṃ.

Intoxicated with poetry, we once wandered from village to village, from city to city; then we saw the Perfectly Enlightened One, who has gone beyond all phenomena.

1263.
‘‘So me dhammamadesesi, muni dukkhassa pāragū;Dhammaṃ sutvā pasīdimha, saddhā [addhā (sī. aṭṭha.)] no udapajjatha.

He, the sage who has gone beyond suffering, taught me the Dhamma. Having heard the Dhamma, we gained confidence, and faith arose in us.

1264.
‘‘Tassāhaṃ vacanaṃ sutvā, khandhe āyatanāni ca;Dhātuyo ca viditvāna, pabbajiṃ anagāriyaṃ.

Having heard his words, and having understood the aggregates, the sense bases, and the elements, I went forth into homelessness.

1265.
‘‘Bahūnaṃ vata atthāya, uppajjanti tathāgatā;Itthīnaṃ purisānañca, ye te sāsanakārakā. Indeed, Tathāgatas arise for the welfare of many, for women and men who are followers of the teaching.


1266.
‘‘Tesaṃ kho vata atthāya, bodhimajjhagamā muni;Bhikkhūnaṃ bhikkhunīnañca, ye nirāmagataddasā. Indeed, it was for their benefit that the sage attained enlightenment—for the bhikkhus and bhikkhunīs who have seen the passionless state.


1267.
‘‘Sudesitā cakkhumatā, buddhenādiccabandhunā;Cattāri ariyasaccāni, anukampāya pāṇinaṃ.

The Four Noble Truths have been well taught by the One with Vision, the Buddha, the Kinsman of the Sun, out of compassion for living beings:

1268.
‘‘Dukkhaṃ dukkhasamuppādaṃ, dukkhassa ca atikkamaṃ;Ariyaṃ caṭṭhaṅgikaṃ maggaṃ, dukkhūpasamagāminaṃ.

suffering, the origin of suffering, the overcoming of suffering, and the Noble Eightfold Path leading to the stilling of suffering.

1269.
‘‘Evamete tathā vuttā, diṭṭhā me te yathā tathā;Sadattho me anuppatto, kataṃ buddhassa sāsanaṃ.

As they were declared, so have they been seen by me. My true goal has been attained; the Buddha’s teaching has been done.

1270.
‘‘Svāgataṃ vata me āsi, mama buddhassa santike;Suvibhattesu [savibhattesu (sī. ka.)] dhammesu, yaṃ seṭṭhaṃ tadupāgamiṃ.

Welcome indeed was my coming into the Buddha’s presence; among the well-expounded Dhammas, I have attained that which is best.

1271.
‘‘Abhiññāpāramippatto, sotadhātu visodhitā;Tevijjo iddhipattomhi, cetopariyakovido.

I have reached the perfection of the direct knowledges; my faculty of hearing is purified. I possess the three true knowledges, I have attained psychic powers, I am skilled in comprehending the minds of others.

1272.
‘‘Pucchāmi satthāramanomapaññaṃ, diṭṭheva dhamme yo vicikicchānaṃ chettā;Aggāḷave kālamakāsi bhikkhu, ñāto yasassī abhinibbutatto. I ask the Teacher of noble wisdom, who cuts off doubt in this very life: At Aggāḷava a bhikkhu passed away, renowned and famous, with self fully quenched.


1273.
‘‘Nigrodhakappo iti tassa nāmaṃ, tayā kataṃ bhagavā brāhmaṇassa;So taṃ namassaṃ acari mutyapekho, āraddhavīriyo daḷhadhammadassī.

Nigrodhakappa was the name you gave him, Blessed One, to that brahmin. Seeking release, he lived revering you, energetic in his striving, a steadfast seer of the Dhamma.

1274.
‘‘Taṃ sāvakaṃ sakka mayampi sabbe, aññātumicchāma samantacakkhu;Samavaṭṭhitā no savanāya sotā [hetuṃ (sī. syā.) suttanipātaṭṭhakathā passitabbā], tuvaṃ no satthā tvamanuttarosi’’.

We all wish to know about that disciple, O Able One, O All-Seeing One. Our ears are ready to hear; you are our Teacher, you are the unsurpassed.

1275.
Chinda no vicikicchaṃ brūhi metaṃ, parinibbutaṃ vedaya bhūripañña;Majjheva no bhāsa samantacakkhu, sakkova devāna sahassanetto.

Cut off our doubt, tell us about him, make known the fully extinguished one, O you of extensive wisdom. Speak in our midst, O All-Seeing One, like Sakka, the thousand-eyed chief of the devas.

1276.
‘‘Ye keci ganthā idha mohamaggā, aññāṇapakkhā vicikicchaṭhānā;Tathāgataṃ patvā na te bhavanti, cakkhuñhi etaṃ paramaṃ narānaṃ. Whatever fetters there are here—paths of delusion, factions of ignorance, grounds for doubt—on reaching the Tathāgata they no longer exist, for he is the supreme eye for humankind.


1277.
‘‘No ce hi jātu puriso kilese, vāto yathā abbhaghanaṃ vihāne;Tamovassa nivuto sabbaloko, jotimantopi na pabhāseyyuṃ [na jotimantopi narā tapeyyuṃ (su. ni. 350)].

If a man would not dispel his defilements, as the wind disperses a mass of clouds, the whole world would be shrouded in darkness; even the luminous would not shine.

1278.
‘‘Dhīrā ca pajjotakarā bhavanti, taṃ taṃ ahaṃ vīra tatheva maññe;Vipassinaṃ jānamupāgamimha, parisāsu no āvikarohi kappaṃ.

But the wise are illuminators, and I consider you to be such a one, O Hero. We have come to you, the one with insight, the knower; reveal Kappa to us in this assembly.

1279.
‘‘Khippaṃ giraṃ eraya vaggu vagguṃ, haṃsova paggayha saṇikaṃ nikūja;Bindussarena suvikappitena, sabbeva te ujjugatā suṇoma.

Quickly send forth your lovely, charming voice; like a swan stretching its neck, coo gently. With your melodious and well-composed voice, we all listen to you attentively.

1280.
‘‘Pahīnajātimaraṇaṃ asesaṃ, niggayha dhonaṃ vadessāmi [paṭivediyāmi (sī. ka.)] dhammaṃ;Na kāmakāro hi [hoti (sī. ka.)] puthujjanānaṃ, saṅkheyyakāro ca [va (bahūsu)] tathāgatānaṃ. I will speak the Dhamma that subdues and cleanses, that has completely abandoned birth and death. For worldlings act on impulse, but Tathāgatas act with deliberation.


1281.
‘‘Sampannaveyyākaraṇaṃ tavedaṃ, samujjupaññassa samuggahītaṃ;Ayamañjali pacchimo suppaṇāmito, mā mohayī jānamanomapañña.

This perfect explanation of yours has been grasped by one of upright wisdom. This is the last reverential salutation I offer; do not delude me, O Knower of noble wisdom.

1282.
‘‘Paroparaṃ ariyadhammaṃ viditvā, mā mohayī jānamanomavīriya;Vāriṃ yathā ghammani ghammatatto, vācābhikaṅkhāmi sutaṃ pavassa.

Having known the noble Dhamma in its entirety, do not delude me, O Knower of noble energy. As one scorched by heat in the hot season longs for water, I long for your speech; rain down the teaching!

1283.
‘‘Yadatthikaṃ brahmacariyaṃ acarī, kappāyano kaccissataṃ amoghaṃ;Nibbāyi so ādu saupādiseso [anupādisesā (sī.), anupādiseso (ka.)], yathā vimutto ahu taṃ suṇoma. The holy life that Kappāyana lived for a purpose—was it fruitful for him? Was it not in vain? Did he attain Nibbāna with no residue remaining, or with a residue? Let us hear how he was liberated.


1284.
‘‘‘Acchecchi taṇhaṃ idha nāmarūpe,(Iti bhagavā) kaṇhassa sotaṃ dīgharattānusayitaṃ;Atāri jātiṃ maraṇaṃ asesaṃ’, iccabravi bhagavā pañcaseṭṭho.

“He has cut off craving here in name-and-form,” said the Blessed One, “the dark stream that has long lain latent. He has crossed over birth and death completely.” Thus spoke the Blessed One, the best of the five.

1285.
‘‘Esa sutvā pasīdāmi, vaco te isisattama;Amoghaṃ kira me puṭṭhaṃ, na maṃ vañcesi brāhmaṇo.

Having heard this word of yours, O best of seers, I am filled with confidence. Indeed, my question was not in vain; the Brahmin has not deceived me.

1286.
‘‘Yathā vādī tathā kārī, ahu buddhassa sāvako;Acchecchi maccuno jālaṃ, tataṃ māyāvino daḷhaṃ.

As he spoke, so he acted, a disciple of the Buddha. He cut the net of Death, spread out strong by the Deceiver.

1287.
‘‘Addasa bhagavā ādiṃ, upādānassa kappiyo;Accagā vata kappāno, maccudheyyaṃ suduttaraṃ. Kappiya saw the origin of clinging, O Blessed One. Indeed, Kappāna has passed beyond the realm of Death, so very hard to cross.


1288.
‘‘Taṃ devadevaṃ vandāmi, puttaṃ te dvipaduttama;Anujātaṃ mahāvīraṃ, nāgaṃ nāgassa orasa’’nti. I pay homage to that deva of devas, your son, the best of bipeds; the great hero born in your likeness, a nāga, the true-born son of the Nāga. Itthaṃ sudaṃ āyasmā vaṅgīso thero gāthāyo Thus indeed the venerable elder Vangīsa spoke these verses. Abhāsitthāti.

Thus he spoke. Mahānipāto niṭṭhito. The Great Chapter is concluded. Tatruddānaṃ – The summary: Sattatimhi nipātamhi, vaṅgīso paṭibhāṇavā;Ekova thero natthañño, gāthāyo ekasattatīti. In the Chapter of Seventy, Vangīsa the eloquent; The only elder, there is no other; The verses are seventy-one. Niṭṭhitā theragāthāyo. The Verses of the Elders are concluded. Tatruddānaṃ – The summary: Sahassaṃ honti tā gāthā, tīṇi saṭṭhisatāni ca;Therā ca dve satā saṭṭhi, cattāro ca pakāsitā. There are a thousand verses, and three hundred and sixty; and two hundred and sixty-four elders have been revealed. Sīhanādaṃ naditvāna, buddhaputtā anāsavā;Khemantaṃ pāpuṇitvāna, aggikhandhāva nibbutāti. Having roared the lion’s roar, the Buddha’s sons, free from taints, having reached the secure state, are extinguished like a mass of fire. Theragāthāpāḷi niṭṭhitā. The Pāli text of the Verses of the Elder Monks is concluded.
