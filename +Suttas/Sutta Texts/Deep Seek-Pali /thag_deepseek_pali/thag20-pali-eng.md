# Thag 20. Saṭṭhinipāto
## 20. The Chapter of Twenties

1149.
‘‘Āraññikā piṇḍapātikā, uñchāpattāgate ratā;Dālemu maccuno senaṃ, ajjhattaṃ susamāhitā.

As forest-dwellers and alms-food-eaters, delighting in what is obtained by gleaning, let us, inwardly well-collected, shatter the army of Death.

1150.
‘‘Āraññikā piṇḍapātikā, uñchāpattāgate ratā;Dhunāma maccuno senaṃ, naḷāgāraṃva kuñjaro.

As forest-dwellers and alms-food-eaters, delighting in what is obtained by gleaning, let us shake off the army of Death as an elephant shakes off a reed hut.

1151.
‘‘Rukkhamūlikā sātatikā, uñchāpattāgate ratā;Dālemu maccuno senaṃ, ajjhattaṃ susamāhitā.

As persevering tree-root-dwellers, delighting in what is obtained by gleaning, let us, inwardly well-collected, shatter the army of Death.

1152.
‘‘Rukkhamūlikā sātatikā, uñchāpattāgate ratā;Dhunāma maccuno senaṃ, naḷāgāraṃva kuñjaro.

As persevering tree-root-dwellers, delighting in what is obtained by gleaning, let us shake off the army of Death as an elephant shakes off a reed hut.

1153.
‘‘Aṭṭhikaṅkalakuṭike, maṃsanhārupasibbite;Dhiratthu pure duggandhe, paragatte mamāyase.

You cherish another’s body—a little hut of bones, stitched together with flesh and sinews. Fie on this foul-smelling citadel!

1154.
‘‘Gūthabhaste taconaddhe, uragaṇḍipisācini;Nava sotāni te kāye, yāni sandanti sabbadā.

O bag of filth bound up with skin, O she-demon with a boil on your breast! There are nine streams in your body which are always flowing.

1155.
‘‘Tava sarīraṃ navasotaṃ, duggandhakaraṃ paribandhaṃ;Bhikkhu parivajjayate taṃ, mīḷhaṃ ca yathā sucikāmo.

Your body is nine-streamed, a foul-smelling bundle. A bhikkhu avoids it, just as one who loves purity avoids excrement.

1156.
‘‘Evañce taṃ jano jaññā, yathā jānāmi taṃ ahaṃ;Ārakā parivajjeyya, gūthaṭṭhānaṃva pāvuse’’.

If other people knew you as I know you, they would avoid you from afar, like a cesspool in the rainy season.

1157.
‘‘Evametaṃ mahāvīra, yathā samaṇa bhāsasi;Ettha ceke visīdanti, paṅkamhiva jaraggavo.

It is just so, great hero; it is as you say, ascetic. And yet some sink down in this, like an old ox in the mud.

1158.
‘‘Ākāsamhi haliddiyā, yo maññetha rajetave;Aññena vāpi raṅgena, vighātudayameva taṃ.

One who would think to dye the sky with turmeric, or with any other color, would only reap vexation.

1159.
‘‘Tadākāsasamaṃ cittaṃ, ajjhattaṃ susamāhitaṃ;Mā pāpacitte āsādi, aggikhandhaṃva pakkhimā.

My mind is like the sky, inwardly well-collected. Do not assail it, O evil-minded one, as a moth assails a mass of fire.

1160.
‘‘Passa cittakataṃ bimbaṃ, arukāyaṃ samussitaṃ;Āturaṃ bahusaṅkappaṃ, yassa natthi dhuvaṃ ṭhiti.

Behold this decorated image, a body of wounds, built up, diseased, much-fancied, for which there is no lasting stability.

1161.
‘‘Passa cittakataṃ rūpaṃ, maṇinā kuṇḍalena ca;Aṭṭhiṃ tacena onaddhaṃ, saha vatthehi sobhati.

Behold the decorated form, with a jewel and an earring. A bone bound with skin, it shines together with its clothes.

1162.
‘‘Alattakakatā pādā, mukhaṃ cuṇṇakamakkhitaṃ;Alaṃ bālassa mohāya, no ca pāragavesino.

The feet are dyed with lac, the face is smeared with powder. Enough to delude a fool, but not one who seeks the far shore.

1163.
‘‘Aṭṭhapadakatā kesā, nettā añjanamakkhitā;Alaṃ bālassa mohāya, no ca pāragavesino.

The hair is styled in plaits, the eyes are smeared with collyrium. Enough to delude a fool, but not one who seeks the far shore.

1164.
‘‘Añjanīva navā cittā, pūtikāyo alaṅkato;Alaṃ bālassa mohāya, no ca pāragavesino.

Like a new, painted cosmetic box, this foul body is adorned. Enough to delude a fool, but not one who seeks the far shore.

1165.
‘‘Odahi migavo pāsaṃ, nāsadā vāguraṃ migo;Bhutvā nivāpaṃ gacchāma, kaddante migabandhake.

The hunter set a snare, but the deer did not approach the trap. Having eaten the bait, we depart, while the deer-trapper laments.

1166.
‘‘Chinno pāso migavassa, nāsadā vāguraṃ migo;Bhutvā nivāpaṃ gacchāma, socante migaluddake.

The hunter’s snare is cut; the deer did not fall into the trap. Having eaten the bait, we depart, while the deer-hunter grieves.

1167.
‘‘Tadāsi yaṃ bhiṃsanakaṃ, tadāsi lomahaṃsanaṃ;Anekākārasampanne, sāriputtamhi nibbute. Then there was terror, then there was horror, when Sāriputta, endowed with numerous qualities, attained final Nibbāna.


1168.
[dī. ni. 2.221, 272; saṃ. ni. 1.186; 2.143; apa. thera 1.2.115; jā. 1.1.95] ‘‘Aniccā vata saṅkhārā uppādavaya dhammino.Upajjitvā nirujjhanti, tesaṃ vūpasamo sukho.

Impermanent, alas, are all formations, their nature is to arise and pass away. Having arisen, they cease; their calming is true bliss.

1169.
‘‘Sukhumaṃ te paṭivijjhanti, vālaggaṃ usunā yathā;Ye pañcakkhandhe passanti, parato no ca attato.

They penetrate what is subtle, as one splits a hair-tip with an arrow: those who see the five aggregates as other, and not as self.

1170.
‘‘Ye ca passanti saṅkhāre, parato no ca attato;Paccabyādhiṃsu nipuṇaṃ, vālaggaṃ usunā yathā.

And those who see formations as other, and not as self, have pierced the subtle point, as one splits a hair-tip with an arrow.

1171.
[saṃ. ni. 1.21, 97] ‘‘Sattiyā viya omaṭṭho, ḍayhamānova matthake;Kāmarāgappahānāya, sato bhikkhu paribbaje.

As if struck by a spear, as if with one’s head on fire, a bhikkhu should wander mindfully for the abandoning of sensual lust.

1172.
[saṃ. ni. 1.21, 97]‘‘Sattiyā viya omaṭṭho, ḍayhamānova matthake;Bhavarāgappahānāya, sato bhikkhu paribbaje’’.

As if struck by a spear, as if with one’s head on fire, a bhikkhu should wander mindfully for the abandoning of lust for existence.

1173.
‘‘Codito bhāvitattena, sarīrantimadhārinā;Migāramātupāsādaṃ, pādaṅguṭṭhena kampayiṃ. Urged on by him of developed self, the bearer of the final body, I shook the palace of Migāra’s mother with my big toe.


1174.
‘‘Nayidaṃ sithilamārabbha, nayidaṃ appena thāmasā;Nibbānamadhigantabbaṃ, sabbagantha-pamocanaṃ. Not by taking it up slackly, not with little strength, is Nibbāna to be attained, the release from all bonds.


1175.
‘‘Ayañca daharo bhikkhu, ayamuttamaporiso;Dhāreti antimaṃ dehaṃ, jetvā māraṃ savāhiniṃ [savāhanaṃ (ka.)]. This young bhikkhu here is a supreme person; having conquered Māra with his army, he wears his final body.


1176.
‘‘Vivaramanupabhanti vijjutā, vebhārassa ca paṇḍavassa ca;Nagavivaragato jhāyati, putto appaṭimassa tādino. Lightning flashes through the cleft between the Vebhāra and Paṇḍava mountains. Gone into that mountain cleft, he meditates—the son of the peerless Such-a-one.


1177.
‘‘Upasanto uparato, pantasenāsano muni;Dāyādo buddhaseṭṭhassa, brahmunā abhivandito. Calmed and restrained, the sage with a secluded lodging, heir of the Buddha, the best of beings, is venerated by Brahmā.


1178.
‘‘Upasantaṃ uparataṃ, pantasenāsanaṃ muniṃ;Dāyādaṃ buddhaseṭṭhassa, vanda brāhmaṇa kassapaṃ.

O brahmin, venerate Kassapa: the calmed and restrained sage with a secluded lodging, the heir of the Buddha, the best of beings.

1179.
‘‘Yo ca jātisataṃ gacche, sabbā brāhmaṇajātiyo;Sottiyo vedasampanno, manussesu punappunaṃ.

One who for a hundred births might be reborn again and again among humans in any brahmin family, a scholar accomplished in the Vedas—

1180.
‘‘Ajjhāyakopi ce assa, tiṇṇaṃ vedāna pāragū;Etassa vandanāyetaṃ, kalaṃ nāgghati soḷasiṃ.

and even if he were a reciter, a master of the three Vedas, he is not worth a sixteenth part of the veneration paid to this one.

1181.
‘‘Yo so aṭṭha vimokkhāni, purebhattaṃ aphassayi [apassayi (sī. ka.), aphussayi (syā.)];Anulomaṃ paṭilomaṃ, tato piṇḍāya gacchati.

He who, before his meal, has attained the eight liberations in both forward and reverse order, and then goes for alms-food—

1182.
‘‘Tādisaṃ bhikkhuṃ māsādi [mā hani (sī.)], māttānaṃ khaṇi brāhmaṇa;Abhippasādehi manaṃ, arahantamhi tādine;Khippaṃ pañjaliko vanda, mā te vijaṭi matthakaṃ.

do not assail such a bhikkhu! O brahmin, do not harm yourself. Inspire your mind with confidence in this Arahant, this Such-a-one. Quickly, with cupped hands, venerate him, lest your head should split apart.

1183.
‘‘Neso passati saddhammaṃ, saṃsārena purakkhato;Adhogamaṃ jimhapathaṃ, kummaggamanudhāvati. He does not see the good Dhamma, being driven on by saṃsāra. He runs along the wrong track, the crooked, downward-leading path.


1184.
‘‘Kimīva mīḷhasallitto, saṅkhāre adhimucchito;Pagāḷho lābhasakkāre, tuccho gacchati poṭṭhilo.

Like a worm smeared with filth, infatuated with formations, sunk in gain and honor, the hollow man goes on, empty.

1185.
‘‘Imañca passa āyantaṃ, sāriputtaṃ sudassanaṃ;Vimuttaṃ ubhatobhāge, ajjhattaṃ susamāhitaṃ. And see this Sāriputta coming, so fair to behold, liberated in both ways, inwardly well-collected.


1186.
‘‘Visallaṃ khīṇasaṃyogaṃ, tevijjaṃ maccuhāyinaṃ;Dakkhiṇeyyaṃ manussānaṃ, puññakkhettaṃ anuttaraṃ.

The one without a dart, whose fetters are destroyed, endowed with the three true knowledges, who has left Death behind, worthy of the offerings of humans, an unsurpassed field of merit.

1187.
‘‘Ete sambahulā devā, iddhimanto yasassino;Dasa devasahassāni, sabbe brahmapurohitā;Moggallānaṃ namassantā, tiṭṭhanti pañjalīkatā. These many devas here, powerful and glorious—ten thousand devas, all from Brahmā’s retinue—stand with cupped hands, venerating Moggallāna.


1188.
‘‘‘Namo te purisājañña, namo te purisuttama;Yassa te āsavā khīṇā, dakkhiṇeyyosi mārisa’.

‘Homage to you, O thoroughbred of men! Homage to you, O supreme man! You whose taints are destroyed; you are worthy of offerings, venerable sir.’

1189.
‘‘Pūjito naradevena, uppanno maraṇābhibhū;Puṇḍarīkaṃva toyena, saṅkhārenupalippati.

Honored by humans and devas, a conqueror of death, he is unsullied by formations, like a white lotus by water.

1190.
‘‘Yassa muhuttena sahassadhā loko, saṃvidito sabrahmakappo vasi;Iddhiguṇe cutupapāte kāle, passati devatā sa bhikkhu.

That bhikkhu, by the quality of his psychic power, sees those devas who are masters of the thousandfold world-system together with the Brahma-world, and knows them at the time of their passing away and rebirth.

1191.
‘‘Sāriputtova paññāya, sīlena upasamena ca;Yopi pāraṅgato bhikkhu, etāvaparamo siyā. In wisdom, virtue, and peace, Sāriputta is the standard. Any bhikkhu who has gone to the far shore can at most be his equal.


1192.
‘‘Koṭisatasahassassa, attabhāvaṃ khaṇena nimmine;Ahaṃ vikubbanāsu kusalo, vasībhūtomhi iddhiyā. I can create a hundred thousand koṭis of bodies in an instant. I am skilled in transformations; I have attained mastery in psychic power.


1193.
‘‘Samādhivijjāvasipāramīgato, moggallānagotto asitassa sāsane;Dhīro samucchindi samāhitindriyo, nāgo yathā pūtilataṃva bandhanaṃ. Having reached the peak of mastery in samādhi and knowledge, he of the Moggallāna clan, in the teaching of the Seer, the steadfast one with faculties composed, has cut through his bonds like an elephant tearing a rotten creeper.


1194.
‘‘Pariciṇṇo mayā satthā, kataṃ buddhassa sāsanaṃ;Ohito garuko bhāro, bhavanetti samūhatā.

I have served the Teacher, I have done the Buddha’s teaching. The heavy burden is laid down, the conduit to existence is rooted out.

1195.
‘‘Yassa catthāya pabbajito, agārasmānagāriyaṃ;So me attho anuppatto, sabbasaṃyojanakkhayo.

The goal for which I went forth from the home life into homelessness—that goal I have now reached: the destruction of all fetters.

1196.
[ma. ni. 1.513] ‘‘Kīdiso nirayo āsi, yattha dussī apaccatha;Vidhuraṃ sāvakamāsajja, kakusandhañca brāhmaṇaṃ. What kind of hell was it where Dūsī was tormented for having assaulted the disciple Vidhura and the brahmin Kakusandha?


1197.
‘‘Sataṃ āsi ayosaṅkū, sabbe paccattavedanā;Īdiso nirayo āsi, yattha dussī apaccatha;Vidhuraṃ sāvakamāsajja, kakusandhañca brāhmaṇaṃ. There were a hundred iron stakes, each one individually experienced. Such was the hell where Dūsī was tormented for having assaulted the disciple Vidhura and the brahmin Kakusandha.


1198.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti, bhikkhu buddhassa sāvako;Tādisaṃ bhikkhumāsajja, kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1199.
‘‘Majjhesarasmiṃ [sarassa (sī.), sāgarasmiṃ (ka.)] tiṭṭhanti, vimānā kappaṭhāyino;Veḷuriyavaṇṇā rucirā, accimanto pabhassarā;Accharā tattha naccanti, puthu nānattavaṇṇiyo.

In the middle of the lake stand celestial mansions, lasting for an aeon; the color of beryl, lovely, blazing, and luminous. There nymphs dance, of many various hues.

1200.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1201.
‘‘Yo ve buddhena codito, bhikkhusaṅghassa pekkhato;Migāramātupāsādaṃ, pādaṅguṭṭhena kampayi. He who, when urged on by the Buddha, while the Saṅgha of bhikkhus looked on, made the palace of Migāra’s Mother tremble with his big toe.


1202.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1203.
‘‘Yo vejayantapāsādaṃ, pādaṅguṭṭhena kampayi;Iddhibalenupatthaddho, saṃvejesi ca devatā.

He who made the Vejayanta Palace tremble with his big toe; supported by the power of iddhi, he stirred up the devas.

1204.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1204.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1205.
‘‘Yo vejayantapāsāde, sakkaṃ so paripucchati;Api āvuso jānāsi, taṇhakkhayavimuttiyo;Tassa sakko viyākāsi, pañhaṃ puṭṭho yathātathaṃ.

He who in the Vejayanta Palace questioned Sakka: ‘Friend, do you know the liberation that is the destruction of craving?’ When asked the question, Sakka answered him in accordance with fact.

1206.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1207.
‘‘Yo brahmānaṃ paripucchati, sudhammāyaṃ ṭhito [sudhammāyā’bhito (syā.)] sabhaṃ;Ajjāpi tyāvuso sā diṭṭhi, yā te diṭṭhi pure ahu;Passasi vītivattantaṃ, brahmaloke pabhassaraṃ. He who, standing in the Sudhammā Assembly Hall, questioned Brahmā: ‘Friend, do you still hold that view which you held formerly? Do you see the radiance in the Brahmā world being surpassed?’


1208.
‘‘Tassa brahmā viyākāsi, pañhaṃ puṭṭho yathātathaṃ;Na me mārisa sā diṭṭhi, yā me diṭṭhi pure ahu. When asked the question, Brahmā answered him in accordance with fact: ‘Good sir, I no longer hold that view which I held formerly.’


1209.
‘‘Passāmi vītivattantaṃ, brahmaloke pabhassaraṃ;Sohaṃ ajja kathaṃ vajjaṃ, ahaṃ niccomhi sassato. ‘I see the radiance in the Brahmā world being surpassed. How, then, could I say today that I am permanent, eternal?’


1210.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti…pe… kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1211.
‘‘Yo mahāneruno kūṭaṃ, vimokkhena aphassayi [apassayi (sī. ka.)];Vanaṃ pubbavidehānaṃ, ye ca bhūmisayā narā.

He who, by means of liberation, has touched the peak of Great Neru, the people of Pubbavideha, and those humans who sleep on the ground.

1212.
‘‘Yo etamabhijānāti, bhikkhu buddhassa sāvako;Tādisaṃ bhikkhumāsajja, kaṇha dukkhaṃ nigacchasi.

The bhikkhu who understands this, a disciple of the Buddha—by assailing such a bhikkhu, O Dark One, you incur suffering.

1213.
‘‘Na ve aggi cetayati, ahaṃ bālaṃ ḍahāmīti;Bālova jalitaṃ aggiṃ, āsajja naṃ paḍayhati.

A fire does not think, ‘I will burn the fool’; but the fool, on touching the blazing fire, is thereby burned.

1214.
‘‘Evameva tuvaṃ māra, āsajja naṃ tathāgataṃ;Sayaṃ ḍahissasi attānaṃ, bālo aggiṃva samphusaṃ. In the very same way, Māra, by assailing the Tathāgata, you will burn yourself, like a fool touching a fire.


1215.
‘‘Apuññaṃ pasavī māro, āsajja naṃ tathāgataṃ;Kiṃ nu maññasi pāpima, na me pāpaṃ vipaccati. By assailing the Tathāgata, Māra has generated demerit. What do you imagine, Evil One? Do you think, ‘My evil deed does not ripen’?


1216.
‘‘Karato te cīyate [miyyate (sabbattha) ma. ni. 1.513 passitabbaṃ] pāpaṃ, cirarattāya antaka;Māra nibbinda buddhamhā, āsaṃ mākāsi bhikkhusu. Your evil deed, as you do it, is stored up for a long time, O End-maker. Māra, turn away from the Buddha! Place no hope in the bhikkhus.


1217.
‘‘Iti māraṃ atajjesi, bhikkhu bhesakaḷāvane;Tato so dummano yakkho, tatthevantaradhāyathā’’ti. Thus the bhikkhu rebuked Māra in the Bhesakaḷā Wood. Then that disheartened yakkha vanished right there. Itthaṃ sudaṃ āyasmā mahāmoggallāno [mahāmoggalāno (ka.)] thero gāthāyo abhāsitthāti. Thus, it is said, the Venerable Elder Mahāmoggallāna spoke these verses. Saṭṭhinipāto niṭṭhito.

The Book of the Sixties is finished. Tatruddānaṃ – The summary: Saṭṭhikamhi nipātamhi, moggallāno mahiddhiko;Ekova theragāthāyo, aṭṭhasaṭṭhi bhavanti tāti. In the Book of the Sixties, Moggallāna of great psychic power is the one elder alone; his verses are sixty-eight in number.
