# Thag 10. Dasakanipāto
## 10. The Chapter of Tens

527.
‘‘Aṅgārino dāni dumā bhadante, phalesino chadanaṃ vippahāya;Te accimantova pabhāsayanti, samayo mahāvīra bhāgī rasānaṃ.

Venerable sir, the trees are now like embers; having shed their foliage, they are aspiring to fruit. They blaze as if with flames. It is time, Great Hero, to partake of the essences.

528.
‘‘Dumāni phullāni manoramāni, samantato sabbadisā pavanti;Pattaṃ pahāya phalamāsasānā [phalamāsamāno (ka.)], kālo ito pakkamanāya vīra.

The trees are in full bloom, delightful; their fragrance spreads in all directions. Having shed their leaves and hoping for fruit, O Hero, it is time to depart from here.

529.
‘‘Nevātisītaṃ na panātiuṇhaṃ, sukhā utu addhaniyā bhadante;Passantu taṃ sākiyā koḷiyā ca, pacchāmukhaṃ rohiniyaṃ tarantaṃ. Venerable sir, it is neither too cold nor too hot; the season is pleasant for travel. May the Sakyans and the Koliyans see you crossing the Rohinī, facing westwards.


530.
‘‘Āsāya kasate khettaṃ, bījaṃ āsāya vappati;Āsāya vāṇijā yanti, samuddaṃ dhanahārakā;Yāya āsāya tiṭṭhāmi, sā me āsā samijjhatu.

With hope the farmer plows the field; with hope the seed is sown. With hope merchants, seekers of wealth, travel the sea. The hope on which I stand—may that hope of mine be fulfilled.

531.
[saṃ. ni. 1.198] ‘‘Punappunaṃ ceva vapanti bījaṃ, punappunaṃ vassati devarājā;Punappunaṃ khettaṃ kasanti kassakā, punappunaṃ dhaññamupeti raṭṭhaṃ.

Again and again they sow the seed, again and again the king of the devas sends rain; again and again farmers plow the field, again and again grain comes to the kingdom.

532.
[saṃ. ni. 1.198] ‘‘Punappunaṃ yācanakā caranti, punappunaṃ dānapatī dadanti;Punappunaṃ dānapatī daditvā, punappunaṃ saggamupenti ṭhānaṃ.

Again and again beggars wander about; again and again patrons give. Having given again and again, patrons again and again go to a heavenly place.

533.
‘‘Vīro have sattayugaṃ puneti, yasmiṃ kule jāyati bhūripañño;Maññāmahaṃ sakkati devadevo, tayā hi jāto [tayābhijāto (sī.)] muni saccanāmo.

A hero of vast wisdom indeed purifies seven generations in the clan in which he is born. I think you are honored, O god among gods, for from you was born the sage whose name is Truth.

534.
‘‘Suddhodano nāma pitā mahesino, buddhassa mātā pana māyanāmā;Yā bodhisattaṃ parihariya kucchinā, kāyassa bhedā tidivamhi modati. Suddhodana by name was the father of the Great Seer, and the Buddha’s mother was named Māyā. She who carried the Bodhisatta in her womb, at the breaking up of the body, rejoices in heaven.


535.
‘‘Sā gotamī kālakatā ito cutā, dibbehi kāmehi samaṅgibhūtā;Sā modati kāmaguṇehi pañcahi, parivāritā devagaṇehi tehi. That Gotamī, having passed away from here, is endowed with divine sensual pleasures; she rejoices in the five strands of sensual pleasure, surrounded by those hosts of devas.


536.
‘‘Buddhassa puttomhi asayhasāhino, aṅgīrasassappaṭimassa tādino;Pitupitā mayhaṃ tuvaṃsi sakka, dhammena me gotama ayyakosī’’ti. I am the son of the Buddha, the unassailable conqueror, of the Angīrasa, the matchless, the steadfast one. You, O Sakyan, are my grandfather; by the Dhamma, O Gotama, you are my grandfather. … Kāḷudāyī thero…. ... The Elder Kāḷudāyī ... 2. Ekavihāriyattheragāthā 2.The Verses of the Elder Ekavihāriya


537.
‘‘Purato pacchato vāpi, aparo ce na vijjati;Atīva phāsu bhavati, ekassa vasato vane.

If before or behind no other is to be found, it is exceedingly pleasant for one dwelling alone in the forest.

538.
‘‘Handa eko gamissāmi, araññaṃ buddhavaṇṇitaṃ;Phāsu [phāsuṃ (syā. pī.)] ekavihārissa, pahitattassa bhikkhuno.

Come, I will go alone to the forest praised by the Buddha; it is pleasant for a solitary bhikkhu who is resolute and striving.

539.
‘‘Yogī-pītikaraṃ rammaṃ, mattakuñjarasevitaṃ;Eko attavasī khippaṃ, pavisissāmi kānanaṃ.

Joyful and delightful, frequented by intoxicated elephants; alone and master of myself, I will quickly enter the forest.

540.
‘‘Supupphite sītavane, sītale girikandare;Gattāni parisiñcitvā, caṅkamissāmi ekako.

In the cool, well-flowered wood, in a cool mountain cleft, having bathed my limbs, I will walk in meditation all alone.

541.
‘‘Ekākiyo adutiyo, ramaṇīye mahāvane;Kadāhaṃ viharissāmi, katakicco anāsavo.

Alone, without a companion, in the delightful great forest, when shall I dwell, my task done, free from taints?

542.
‘‘Evaṃ me kattukāmassa, adhippāyo samijjhatu;Sādhiyissāmahaṃyeva, nāñño aññassa kārako.

May this intention of mine, who wishes to act thus, succeed. I myself shall accomplish it; no one can act for another.

543.
‘‘Esa bandhāmi sannāhaṃ, pavisissāmi kānanaṃ;Na tato nikkhamissāmi, appatto āsavakkhayaṃ.

Here I don my armor and will enter the forest. I will not leave it until I have attained the destruction of the taints.

544.
‘‘Mālute upavāyante, sīte surabhigandhike [gandhake (syā. pī. ka.)];Avijjaṃ dālayissāmi, nisinno nagamuddhani.

While the cool and fragrant breezes blow, I will shatter ignorance, seated on a mountain peak.

545.
‘‘Vane kusumasañchanne, pabbhāre nūna sītale;Vimuttisukhena sukhito, ramissāmi giribbaje.

In the forest covered with flowers, on a cool mountain slope, gladdened by the bliss of liberation, I will delight in the mountain fastness.

546.
‘‘Sohaṃ paripuṇṇasaṅkappo, cando pannaraso yathā;Sabbāsavaparikkhīṇo, natthi dāni punabbhavo’’ti.

With my intention fulfilled, like the full moon on the fifteenth day, with all taints destroyed, for me there is now no renewed existence. … Ekavihāriyo thero…. ... The Elder Ekavihāriya ... 3. Mahākappinattheragāthā 3.The Verses of the Elder Mahākappina

547.
‘‘Anāgataṃ yo paṭikacca [paṭigacca (sī.)] passati, hitañca atthaṃ ahitañca taṃ dvayaṃ;Viddesino tassa hitesino vā, randhaṃ na passanti samekkhamānā.

One who foresees what is to come—that pair, the beneficial and the harmful—his enemies or his well-wishers, though scrutinizing him, do not find a weak spot.

548.
[paṭi. ma. 1.160 paṭisambhidāmagge] ‘‘Ānāpānasatī yassa, paripuṇṇā subhāvitā;Anupubbaṃ paricitā, yathā buddhena desitā;Somaṃ lokaṃ pabhāseti, abbhā muttova candimā.

One whose mindfulness of breathing is complete and well-developed, cultivated in sequence as taught by the Buddha, illuminates this world like the moon freed from a cloud.

549.
‘‘Odātaṃ vata me cittaṃ, appamāṇaṃ subhāvitaṃ;Nibbiddhaṃ paggahītañca, sabbā obhāsate disā.

Oh, my mind is pure, immeasurable, and well-developed; penetrated by wisdom and uplifted, it illuminates all directions.

550.
‘‘Jīvate vāpi sappañño, api vittaparikkhayo;Paññāya ca alābhena, vittavāpi na jīvati.

A wise person lives on even when his wealth is destroyed; but without the gain of wisdom, even a wealthy person does not truly live.

551.
‘‘Paññā sutavinicchinī, paññā kittisilokavaddhanī;Paññāsahito naro idha, api dukkhesu sukhāni vindati.

Wisdom is the arbiter of what is heard; wisdom increases fame and renown. A person endowed with wisdom here finds happiness even amid suffering.

552.
‘‘Nāyaṃ ajjatano dhammo, nacchero napi abbhuto;Yattha jāyetha mīyetha, tattha kiṃ viya abbhutaṃ.

This is no modern phenomenon, nothing amazing or wonderful. That one dies where one was born—what is so wonderful in that?

553.
‘‘Anantaraṃ hi jātassa, jīvitā maraṇaṃ dhuvaṃ;Jātā jātā marantīdha, evaṃdhammā hi pāṇino.

For one who has been born, death is the certain outcome of life. Again and again beings are born and die here, for such is the nature of living beings.

554.
‘‘Na hetadatthāya matassa hoti, yaṃ jīvitatthaṃ paraporisānaṃ;Matamhi ruṇṇaṃ na yaso na lokyaṃ, na vaṇṇitaṃ samaṇabrāhmaṇehi.

This weeping is not for the benefit of the dead. Weeping for the dead brings no fame or worldly success, nor is it praised by ascetics and brahmins.

555.
‘‘Cakkhuṃ sarīraṃ upahanti tena [upahanti ruṇṇaṃ (sī.), upahanti roṇṇaṃ (syā. pī.)], nihīyati vaṇṇabalaṃ matī ca;Ānandino tassa disā bhavanti, hitesino nāssa sukhī bhavanti.

By it one harms one's eyes and body; one's complexion, strength, and intelligence decline. His enemies rejoice over it, while his well-wishers are not made happy.

556.
‘‘Tasmā hi iccheyya kule vasante, medhāvino ceva bahussute ca;Yesaṃ hi paññāvibhavena kiccaṃ, taranti nāvāya nadiṃva puṇṇa’’nti.

Therefore, one should wish for wise and learned members in one's family. For by the power of their wisdom, one can accomplish what needs to be done, like crossing a full river with a boat. … Mahākappino thero…. ... The Elder Mahākappina ... 4. Cūḷapanthakattheragāthā 4.The Verses of the Elder Cūḷapanthaka

557.
‘‘Dandhā mayhaṃ gatī āsi, paribhūto pure ahaṃ;Bhātā ca maṃ paṇāmesi, ‘gaccha dāni tuvaṃ gharaṃ’.

“My progress was slow; formerly I was scorned. And my brother dismissed me: ‘Now you go home.’

558.
‘‘Sohaṃ paṇāmito santo [bhātā (aṭṭha.)], saṅghārāmassa koṭṭhake;Dummano tattha aṭṭhāsiṃ, sāsanasmiṃ apekkhavā.

“Having been dismissed, I stood disheartened at the monastery’s gatehouse, still longing for the Dispensation.

559.
‘‘Bhagavā tattha āgacchi [āgañchi (sī. pī.)], sīsaṃ mayhaṃ parāmasi;Bāhāya maṃ gahetvāna, saṅghārāmaṃ pavesayi.

“The Blessed One came there and stroked my head; taking me by the arm, he led me into the monastery.

560.
‘‘Anukampāya me satthā, pādāsi pādapuñchaniṃ;‘Etaṃ suddhaṃ adhiṭṭhehi, ekamantaṃ svadhiṭṭhitaṃ’.

“Out of compassion for me, the Teacher gave a foot-wiper, saying: ‘Resolve upon this clean object, well-established in a secluded place.’

561.
‘‘Tassāhaṃ vacanaṃ sutvā, vihāsiṃ sāsane rato;Samādhiṃ paṭipādesiṃ, uttamatthassa pattiyā.

“Having heard his word, I dwelled delighting in the Dispensation; I developed samādhi for the attainment of the ultimate goal.

562.
‘‘Pubbenivāsaṃ jānāmi, dibbacakkhu visodhitaṃ;Tisso vijjā anuppattā, kataṃ buddhassa sāsanaṃ.

“I know my former abodes, the divine eye is purified; the three true knowledges are attained, the Buddha’s teaching has been done.

563.
‘‘Sahassakkhattumattānaṃ, nimminitvāna panthako;Nisīdambavane ramme, yāva kālappavedanā.

“Panthaka created himself a thousand times and sat in the delightful mango grove until the time was announced.

564.
‘‘Tato me satthā pāhesi, dūtaṃ kālappavedakaṃ;Paveditamhi kālamhi, vehāsādupasaṅkamiṃ [vehāsānupasaṅkamiṃ (syā. ka.)].

“Then the Teacher sent me a messenger to announce the time. When the time was announced, I approached through the air.

565.
‘‘Vanditvā satthuno pāde, ekamantaṃ nisīdahaṃ;Nisinnaṃ maṃ viditvāna, atha satthā paṭiggahi.

“Having paid homage at the Teacher’s feet, I sat down to one side. Seeing that I was seated, the Teacher then accepted the offering.

566.
‘‘Āyāgo sabbalokassa, āhutīnaṃ paṭiggaho;Puññakkhettaṃ manussānaṃ, paṭigaṇhittha dakkhiṇa’’nti.

“‘Worthy of oblations from the whole world, a receiver of sacrificial gifts, a field of merit for humankind, let him accept this dedicated gift.’” … Cūḷapanthako thero…. … The Elder Cūḷapanthaka … 5. Kappattheragāthā 5.Verses of the Elder Kappa

567.
‘‘Nānākulamalasampuṇṇo, mahāukkārasambhavo;Candanikaṃva paripakkaṃ, mahāgaṇḍo mahāvaṇo.

“Filled with various kinds of filth, sprung from a great rubbish heap; like a ripe cesspit, a great boil, a great wound.

568.
‘‘Pubbaruhirasampuṇṇo, gūthakūpena gāḷhito [gūthakūpe nigāḷhito (syā. pī. ka.)];Āpopaggharaṇo kāyo, sadā sandati pūtikaṃ.

“Full of pus and blood, steeped in a pit of excrement; the body is a source of oozing, always trickling what is foul.

569.
‘‘Saṭṭhikaṇḍarasambandho, maṃsalepanalepito;Cammakañcukasannaddho, pūtikāyo niratthako.

“Bound by sixty sinews, plastered with a coating of flesh, clad in a jacket of skin—a foul and worthless body.

570.
‘‘Aṭṭhisaṅghātaghaṭito, nhārusuttanibandhano;Nekesaṃ saṃgatībhāvā, kappeti iriyāpathaṃ.

“Constructed from a collection of bones, bound with threads of sinews; from the coming together of many parts, it produces its postures.

571.
‘‘Dhuvappayāto maraṇāya, maccurājassa santike;Idheva chaḍḍayitvāna, yenakāmaṅgamo naro.

“Headed for certain death, into the presence of the King of Death; a person must abandon it right here and go wherever his kamma takes him.

572.
‘‘Avijjāya nivuto kāyo, catuganthena ganthito;Oghasaṃsīdano kāyo, anusayajālamotthato.

“A body veiled by ignorance, tied up with the four knots; a body sinking in the flood, overspread with the net of underlying tendencies.

573.
‘‘Pañcanīvaraṇe yutto, vitakkena samappito;Taṇhāmūlenānugato, mohacchādanachādito.

“Yoked to the five hindrances, endowed with thought, pursued by the root of craving, covered by the cloak of delusion.

574.
‘‘Evāyaṃ vattate kāyo, kammayantena yantito;Sampatti ca vipatyantā, nānābhāvo vipajjati.

“Thus this body proceeds, impelled by the machine of kamma. Success has failure as its end; its diverse nature perishes.

575.
‘‘Yemaṃ kāyaṃ mamāyanti, andhabālā puthujjanā;Vaḍḍhenti kaṭasiṃ ghoraṃ, ādiyanti punabbhavaṃ.

“Those blind, foolish worldlings who consider this body ‘mine’ enlarge the horrid charnel ground and take on renewed existence.

576.
‘‘Yemaṃ kāyaṃ vivajjenti, gūthalittaṃva pannagaṃ;Bhavamūlaṃ vamitvāna, parinibbissantināsavā’’ti [parinibbantunāsavā (sī.)]. “But those who shun this body as one would a snake smeared with filth, having vomited up the root of being, will attain final Nibbāna without taints.”

… Kappo thero…. … The Elder Kappa … 6. Vaṅgantaputtaupasenattheragāthā 6.The Verses of the Elder Upasena, Son of Vaṅganta

577.
‘‘Vivittaṃ appanigghosaṃ, vāḷamiganisevitaṃ;Seve senāsanaṃ bhikkhu, paṭisallānakāraṇā.

“A bhikkhu should resort to a lodging that is secluded, quiet, frequented by beasts of prey, for the sake of solitude.

578.
‘‘Saṅkārapuñjā āhatvā [āhitvā (ka.)], susānā rathiyāhi ca;Tato saṅghāṭikaṃ katvā, lūkhaṃ dhāreyya cīvaraṃ.

“Having collected rags from rubbish heaps, from charnel grounds and streets, and having made a patchwork robe from them, one should wear that coarse robe.

579.
‘‘Nīcaṃ manaṃ karitvāna, sapadānaṃ kulā kulaṃ;Piṇḍikāya care bhikkhu, guttadvāro susaṃvuto.

“Having humbled his mind, a bhikkhu should walk for alms from family to family in sequence, with doors guarded, well-restrained.

580.
‘‘Lūkhenapi vā [lūkhenapi ca (bahūsu)] santusse, nāññaṃ patthe rasaṃ bahuṃ;Rasesu anugiddhassa, jhāne na ramatī mano. “He should be content even with coarse food and not desire many other flavors. For one greedy for flavors, the mind does not delight in jhāna.


581.
‘‘Appiccho ceva santuṭṭho, pavivitto vase muni;Asaṃsaṭṭho gahaṭṭhehi, anāgārehi cūbhayaṃ.

“The sage should dwell with few wishes, contented, and secluded, unassociated with both householders and the homeless.

582.
‘‘Yathā jaḷo va mūgo va, attānaṃ dassaye tathā;Nātivelaṃ sambhāseyya, saṅghamajjhamhi paṇḍito.

“A wise one should show himself as if dull and mute; he should not speak at length in the midst of the Sangha.

583.
‘‘Na so upavade kañci, upaghātaṃ vivajjaye;Saṃvuto pātimokkhasmiṃ, mattaññū cassa bhojane. “He should not disparage anyone; he should avoid doing harm. He should be restrained in the Pātimokkha and moderate in eating.


584.
‘‘Suggahītanimittassa, cittassuppādakovido;Samaṃ anuyuñjeyya, kālena ca vipassanaṃ.

“Skilled in the arising of the mind, having well grasped the meditation sign, he should cultivate serenity and, at the proper time, insight.

585.
‘‘Vīriyasātaccasampanno, yuttayogo sadā siyā;Na ca appatvā dukkhantaṃ, vissāsaṃ eyya paṇḍito.

“Endowed with energy and perseverance, he should always be devoted to the practice. A wise one should not become complacent before reaching the end of suffering.

586.
‘‘Evaṃ viharamānassa, suddhikāmassa bhikkhuno;Khīyanti āsavā sabbe, nibbutiñcādhigacchatī’’ti. “For a bhikkhu dwelling thus, one who desires purity, all the taints are destroyed, and he attains Nibbāna.” … Upaseno vaṅgantaputto thero….

… The Elder Upasena Vangantaputta … 7. (Apara)-gotamattheragāthā 7.Verses of the Elder Gotama

587.
‘‘Vijāneyya sakaṃ atthaṃ, avalokeyyātha pāvacanaṃ;Yañcettha assa patirūpaṃ, sāmaññaṃ ajjhupagatassa.

“One should discern one’s own good and examine the scriptures, and what is suitable therein for one who has undertaken the ascetic’s life.

588.
‘‘Mittaṃ idha ca kalyāṇaṃ, sikkhā vipulaṃ samādānaṃ;Sussūsā ca garūnaṃ, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Good friends here, undertaking the extensive training, and eagerness to learn from one's teachers—this is befitting for a recluse.

589.
‘‘Buddhesu sagāravatā, dhamme apaciti yathābhūtaṃ;Saṅghe ca cittikāro, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Reverence for the Buddhas, true respect for the Dhamma, and high esteem for the Sangha—this is befitting for a recluse.

590.
‘‘Ācāragocare yutto, ājīvo sodhito agārayho;Cittassa ca saṇṭhapanaṃ, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Devoted to good conduct and resort, a purified livelihood that is blameless, and the stabilizing of the mind—this is befitting for a recluse.

591.
‘‘Cārittaṃ atha vārittaṃ, iriyāpathiyaṃ pasādaniyaṃ;Adhicitte ca āyogo, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Observances and avoidance, inspiring deportment, and application to the higher mind—this is befitting for a recluse.

592.
‘‘Āraññakāni senāsanāni, pantāni appasaddāni;Bhajitabbāni muninā, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Wilderness lodgings, remote and quiet, should be frequented by the sage—this is befitting for a recluse.

593.
‘‘Sīlañca bāhusaccañca, dhammānaṃ pavicayo yathābhūtaṃ;Saccānaṃ abhisamayo, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“Virtue and great learning, true investigation of phenomena, the penetration of the truths—this is befitting for a recluse.

594.
‘‘Bhāveyya ca aniccanti, anattasaññaṃ asubhasaññañca;Lokamhi ca anabhiratiṃ, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“One should cultivate the perception of impermanence, the perception of not-self, and the perception of foulness, and non-delight in the world—this is befitting for a recluse.

595.
‘‘Bhāveyya ca bojjhaṅge, iddhipādāni indriyāni balāni;Aṭṭhaṅgamaggamariyaṃ, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpaṃ.

“One should develop the factors of enlightenment, the bases for spiritual power, the faculties, the powers, and the Noble Eightfold Path—this is befitting for a recluse.

596.
‘‘Taṇhaṃ pajaheyya muni, samūlake āsave padāleyya;Vihareyya vippamutto, etaṃ samaṇassa patirūpa’’nti.

“The sage should abandon craving, he should shatter the taints with their root, and dwell fully released—this is befitting for a recluse.” … Gotamo thero…. … The Elder Gotama … Dasakanipāto niṭṭhito. The Book of the Tens is finished. Tatruddānaṃ – The summary: Kāḷudāyī ca so thero, ekavihārī ca kappino;Cūḷapanthako kappo ca, upaseno ca gotamo;Sattime dasake therā, gāthāyo cettha sattatīti. The Elder Kāḷudāyī, Ekavihārī, and Kappina, Cūḷapanthaka and Kappa, Upasena and Gotama: these seven elders are in the Book of the Tens, and herein are seventy verses.
