# Thag 13. Terasanipāto
## 13. The Chapter of Thirteens

632.
‘‘Yāhu raṭṭhe samukkaṭṭho, rañño aṅgassa paddhagū [patthagū (syā.), paṭṭhagū (ka.)];Svājja dhammesu ukkaṭṭho, soṇo dukkhassa pāragū. He who was once foremost in the realm, the charioteer for the king of Aṅga, is today foremost in the Dhamma. Soṇa has gone to the far shore of suffering.


633.
‘‘Pañca chinde pañca jahe, pañca cuttari bhāvaye;Pañcasaṅgātigo bhikkhu, oghatiṇṇoti vuccati.

One should cut off five, abandon five, and cultivate five further. A bhikkhu who has overcome the five attachments is called one who has crossed over the flood.

634.
‘‘Unnaḷassa pamattassa, bāhirāsassa [bāhirāsayassa (ka.)] bhikkhuno;Sīlaṃ samādhi paññā ca, pāripūriṃ na gacchati.

For an arrogant and heedless bhikkhu, one whose aspirations are directed externally, virtue, samādhi, and wisdom do not come to fulfillment.

635.
‘‘Yañhi kiccaṃ apaviddhaṃ [tadapaviddhaṃ (sī. syā.)], akiccaṃ pana karīyati;Unnaḷānaṃ pamattānaṃ, tesaṃ vaḍḍhanti āsavā.

When what should be done is neglected and what should not be done is done, the taints of the arrogant and heedless increase.

636.
‘‘Yesañca susamāraddhā, niccaṃ kāyagatā sati;Akiccaṃ te na sevanti, kicce sātaccakārino;Satānaṃ sampajānānaṃ, atthaṃ gacchanti āsavā.

But for those whose mindfulness directed to the body is ever well-aroused, they do not resort to what should not be done; they are persistent in doing what should be done. For such ones, mindful and clearly comprehending, the taints go to destruction.

637.
‘‘Ujumaggamhi akkhāte, gacchatha mā nivattatha;Attanā codayattānaṃ, nibbānamabhihāraye. The straight path has been proclaimed: travel on it, do not turn back! Urge yourself on by yourself; one should lead oneself to Nibbāna.


638.
‘‘Accāraddhamhi vīriyamhi, satthā loke anuttaro;Vīṇopamaṃ karitvā me, dhammaṃ desesi cakkhumā;Tassāhaṃ vacanaṃ sutvā, vihāsiṃ sāsane rato.

When my energy was over-exerted, the Teacher, unsurpassed in the world, the One with Vision, taught me the Dhamma using the simile of the lute. Having heard his word, I dwelt delighting in his Dispensation.

639.
‘‘Samathaṃ paṭipādesiṃ, uttamatthassa pattiyā;Tisso vijjā anuppattā, kataṃ buddhassa sāsanaṃ.

I undertook serenity for the attainment of the supreme goal. The three true knowledges have been attained; the Buddha’s teaching has been done.

640.
‘‘Nekkhamme [nikkhame (ka.), nekkhammaṃ (mahāva. 244; a. ni. 6.55)] adhimuttassa, pavivekañca cetaso;Abyāpajjhādhimuttassa [abyāpajjhādhimhattassa (ka.)], upādānakkhayassa ca.

Of one intent on renunciation and on seclusion of mind, of one intent on non-ill will and on the destruction of clinging,

641.
‘‘Taṇhakkhayādhimuttassa, asammohañca cetaso;Disvā āyatanuppādaṃ, sammā cittaṃ vimuccati.

Of one intent on the destruction of craving and on non-delusion of mind: having seen the arising of the sense bases, the mind is rightly liberated.

642.
‘‘Tassa sammā vimuttassa, santacittassa bhikkhuno;Katassa paṭicayo natthi, karaṇīyaṃ na vijjati.

For that bhikkhu, rightly liberated, with a peaceful mind, there is no piling up of what has been done; there is nothing further to be done.

643.
‘‘Selo yathā ekaghano [ekaghano (ka.)], vātena na samīrati;Evaṃ rūpā rasā saddā, gandhā phassā ca kevalā.

Just as a solid, one-mass rock is not stirred by the wind, so too forms, tastes, sounds, odors, and all kinds of tactile objects,

644.
‘‘Iṭṭhā dhammā aniṭṭhā ca, nappavedhenti tādino;Ṭhitaṃ cittaṃ visaññuttaṃ, vayañcassānupassatī’’ti.

—both desirable and undesirable phenomena—do not make the Such-a-one tremble. His mind is steady and detached, as he sees their passing away. … Soṇo koḷiviso thero…. … The Elder Soṇa Koḷivisa … Terasanipāto niṭṭhito. The Chapter of Thirteens is finished. Tatruddānaṃ – The summary: Soṇo koḷiviso thero, ekoyeva mahiddhiko;Terasamhi nipātamhi, gāthāyo cettha terasāti. The Elder Soṇa Koḷivisa, the one of great spiritual power; in the Chapter of Thirteens, there are thirteen verses here.
