# Thag 19. Paññāsanipāto
## 19. The Chapter of Nineteens

1094.
‘‘Kadā nuhaṃ pabbatakandarāsu, ekākiyo addutiyo vihassaṃ;Aniccato sabbabhavaṃ vipassaṃ, taṃ me idaṃ taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When shall I dwell in mountain caves, alone and without a companion, seeing all existence as impermanent? When, oh when, will that be for me?

1095.
‘‘Kadā nuhaṃ bhinnapaṭandharo muni, kāsāvavattho amamo nirāso;Rāgañca dosañca tatheva mohaṃ, hantvā sukhī pavanagato vihassaṃ.

When shall I, a sage wearing patched robes, clad in the saffron garment, possessionless and without longing, having slain lust, hatred, and delusion, dwell happily, having gone to the forest?

1096.
‘‘Kadā aniccaṃ vadharoganīḷaṃ, kāyaṃ imaṃ maccujarāyupaddutaṃ;Vipassamāno vītabhayo vihassaṃ, eko vane taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When, seeing this body as impermanent, a nest of slaughter and disease, afflicted by death and old age, shall I dwell free from fear, alone in the forest? Oh, when will that be?

1097.
‘‘Kadā nuhaṃ bhayajananiṃ dukhāvahaṃ, taṇhālataṃ bahuvidhānuvattaniṃ;Paññāmayaṃ tikhiṇamasiṃ gahetvā, chetvā vase tampi kadā bhavissati.

When shall I, taking the sharp sword of wisdom, cut down the creeper of craving—which generates fear, brings suffering, and follows in many ways—and dwell at peace? When will that too come to be?

1098.
‘‘Kadā nu paññāmayamuggatejaṃ, satthaṃ isīnaṃ sahasādiyitvā;Māraṃ sasenaṃ sahasā bhañjissaṃ, sīhāsane taṃ nu kadā bhavissati. When shall I, swiftly seizing the seers' sword of lofty power, forged from wisdom, swiftly shatter Māra with his army? When, oh when, will that lion's seat be mine?


1099.
‘‘Kadā nuhaṃ sabbhi samāgamesu, diṭṭho bhave dhammagarūhi tādibhi;Yāthāvadassīhi jitindriyehi, padhāniyo taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When, striving diligently, shall I be seen in gatherings of the good, by Such Ones who revere the Dhamma, who see things as they are and have conquered their faculties? Oh, when will that be?

1100.
‘‘Kadā nu maṃ tandi khudā pipāsā, vātātapā kīṭasarīsapā vā;Na bādhayissanti na taṃ giribbaje, atthatthiyaṃ taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When will lethargy, hunger, and thirst, wind and sun, insects and reptiles no longer afflict me in the mountain fastness as I strive for the goal? Oh, when will that be?

1101.
‘‘Kadā nu kho yaṃ viditaṃ mahesinā, cattāri saccāni sududdasāni;Samāhitatto satimā agacchaṃ, paññāya taṃ taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When shall I, with collected mind and mindfulness, attain with wisdom those Four Noble Truths, so very hard to see, which were understood by the Great Seer? Oh, when will that be?

1102.
‘‘Kadā nu rūpe amite ca sadde, gandhe rase phusitabbe ca dhamme;Ādittatohaṃ samathehi yutto, paññāya dacchaṃ tadidaṃ kadā me.

When shall I, endowed with serenity, see with wisdom the unlimited forms and sounds, odors, tastes, tangibles, and mental phenomena as all ablaze? When will this be for me?

1103.
‘‘Kadā nuhaṃ dubbacanena vutto, tatonimittaṃ vimano na hessaṃ;Atho pasatthopi tatonimittaṃ, tuṭṭho na hessaṃ tadidaṃ kadā me.

When, being addressed with harsh words, will I not be dejected on that account? And when praised, will I not be pleased on that account? When will this be for me?

1104.
‘‘Kadā nu kaṭṭhe ca tiṇe latā ca, khandhe imehaṃ amite ca dhamme;Ajjhattikāneva ca bāhirāni ca, samaṃ tuleyyaṃ tadidaṃ kadā me.

When shall I weigh equally wood, grass, and creepers, along with these aggregates and the immeasurable phenomena, both internal and external alike? When will this be for me?

1105.
‘‘Kadā nu maṃ pāvusakālamegho, navena toyena sacīvaraṃ vane;Isippayātamhi pathe vajantaṃ, ovassate taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When will the monsoon cloud drench me and my robe with fresh water, as I am walking in the forest on the path trodden by the seers? Oh, when will that be?

1106.
‘‘Kadā mayūrassa sikhaṇḍino vane, dijassa sutvā girigabbhare rutaṃ;Paccuṭṭhahitvā amatassa pattiyā, saṃcintaye taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When, hearing in a mountain cave the cry of the crested peacock, shall I rise up and reflect, intent on attaining the Deathless? Oh, when will that be?

1107.
‘‘Kadā nu gaṅgaṃ yamunaṃ sarassatiṃ, pātālakhittaṃ vaḷavāmukhañca [balavāmukhañca (ka.)];Asajjamāno patareyyamiddhiyā, vibhiṃsanaṃ taṃ nu kadā bhavissati. When shall I, unattached, cross by psychic power the Gaṅgā, the Yamunā, the Sarassatī, the Pātāla chasm, and the Mare's Mouth? When will that terrifying crossing be?


1108.
‘‘Kadā nu nāgova asaṅgacārī, padālaye kāmaguṇesu chandaṃ;Nibbajjayaṃ sabbasubhaṃ nimittaṃ, jhāne yuto taṃ nu kadā bhavissati. When shall I, wandering unattached like a bull elephant, shatter desire for sensual pleasures, rejecting every sign of the beautiful and devoted to jhāna? Oh, when will that be?


1109.
‘‘Kadā iṇaṭṭova daliddako [daḷiddako (sī.)] nidhiṃ, ārādhayitvā dhanikehi pīḷito;Tuṭṭho bhavissaṃ adhigamma sāsanaṃ, mahesino taṃ nu kadā bhavissati.

When shall I, like a poor man in debt oppressed by his creditors who rejoices on finding a treasure, rejoice upon attaining the teaching of the Great Seer? Oh, when will that be?

1110.
‘‘Bahūni vassāni tayāmhi yācito, ‘agāravāsena alaṃ nu te idaṃ’;Taṃ dāni maṃ pabbajitaṃ samānaṃ, kiṃkāraṇā citta tuvaṃ na yuñjasi.

For many years I was begged by you, O mind: ‘Enough of this household life for you!’ Now that I have gone forth, for what reason do you not apply yourself?

1111.
‘‘Nanu ahaṃ citta tayāmhi yācito, ‘giribbaje citrachadā vihaṅgamā’;Mahindaghosatthanitābhigajjino, te taṃ ramessanti vanamhi jhāyinaṃ.

Was I not begged by you, O mind: ‘In the mountain fastness, birds with colorful plumage, thundering with a voice like Indra’s, will delight you as you meditate in the forest’?

1112.
‘‘Kulamhi mitte ca piye ca ñātake, khiḍḍāratiṃ kāmaguṇañca loke;Sabbaṃ pahāya imamajjhupāgato, athopi tvaṃ citta na mayha tussasi.

Having abandoned family, friends, dear ones, and relatives, play and amusement, and sensual pleasures in the world—having abandoned all this, I have come to this. And yet, O mind, you are not pleased with me.

1113.
‘‘Mameva etaṃ na hi tvaṃ paresaṃ, sannāhakāle paridevitena kiṃ;Sabbaṃ idaṃ calamiti pekkhamāno, abhinikkhamiṃ amatapadaṃ jigīsaṃ.

You are mine indeed, O mind, not another’s. What use is lamentation at the time for arming? Seeing that all this is transient, I went forth, desiring to conquer the deathless state.

1114.
‘‘Suyuttavādī dvipadānamuttamo, mahābhisakko naradammasārathi [sārathī (sī.)];‘Cittaṃ calaṃ makkaṭasannibhaṃ iti, avītarāgena sudunnivārayaṃ’.

The speaker of what is apt, the best of bipeds, the great physician, the tamer of persons to be tamed has said: ‘The mind is fickle, like a monkey; it is very hard to restrain for one who is not free from lust.’

1115.
‘‘Kāmā hi citrā madhurā manoramā, aviddasū yattha sitā puthujjanā;Te dukkhamicchanti punabbhavesino, cittena nītā niraye nirākatā.

Sensual pleasures are indeed varied, sweet, and delightful, wherein the unwise, ordinary people, are attached. Seeking renewed existence, they desire suffering; led by the mind, they are cast down into hell.

1116.
‘‘‘Mayūrakoñcābhirutamhi kānane, dīpīhi byagghehi purakkhato vasaṃ;Kāye apekkhaṃ jaha mā virādhaya’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Dwell in the forest resounding with the calls of peacocks and herons, attended by leopards and tigers. Abandon concern for the body; do not fail!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1117.
‘‘‘Bhāvehi jhānāni ca indriyāni ca, balāni bojjhaṅgasamādhibhāvanā;Tisso ca vijjā phusa buddhasāsane’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi. ‘Develop the jhānas and the faculties, the powers, the factors of enlightenment, and the development of samādhi. Realize the three true knowledges in the Buddha’s Dispensation!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.


1118.
‘‘‘Bhāvehi maggaṃ amatassa pattiyā, niyyānikaṃ sabbadukhakkhayogadhaṃ;Aṭṭhaṅgikaṃ sabbakilesasodhanaṃ’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Develop the eightfold path for attaining the Deathless—the path that leads out, that yokes one to the destruction of all suffering, that purifies all defilements!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1119.
‘‘‘Dukkhanti khandhe paṭipassa yoniso, yato ca dukkhaṃ samudeti taṃ jaha;Idheva dukkhassa karohi antaṃ’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Wisely see the aggregates as suffering, and abandon that from which suffering arises; make an end of suffering right here and now!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1120.
‘‘‘Aniccaṃ dukkhanti vipassa yoniso, suññaṃ anattāti aghaṃ vadhanti ca;Manovicāre uparundha cetaso’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Wisely see things as impermanent and suffering, as empty and not-self, as a calamity and a slaughterhouse. Restrain the discursive thoughts of your mind!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1121.
‘‘‘Muṇḍo virūpo abhisāpamāgato, kapālahatthova kulesu bhikkhasu;Yuñjassu satthuvacane mahesino’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Shaven-headed, of altered appearance, as if under a curse, go begging from family to family with bowl in hand. Apply yourself to the word of the Teacher, the Great Seer!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1122.
‘‘‘Susaṃvutatto visikhantare caraṃ, kulesu kāmesu asaṅgamānaso;Cando yathā dosinapuṇṇamāsiyā’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘With self well-restrained, wander in the street, with mind unattached to sensual pleasures among the families, like the moon on the night of the full moon!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1123.
‘‘‘Āraññiko hohi ca piṇḍapātiko, sosāniko hohi ca paṃsukūliko;Nesajjiko hohi sadā dhute rato’, itissu maṃ citta pure niyuñjasi.

‘Be a forest-dweller and an alms-food-eater; be a charnel-ground-dweller and a rag-robe-wearer. Be one who practices the sitter’s observance, ever delighting in the ascetic practices!’—thus, O mind, you formerly urged me.

1124.
‘‘Ropetva rukkhāni yathā phalesī, mūle taruṃ chettu tameva icchasi;Tathūpamaṃ cittamidaṃ karosi, yaṃ maṃ aniccamhi cale niyuñjasi.

Just as one who seeks fruit, after planting a tree, might then wish to cut it down at the root, you do a similar thing, O mind, when you urge me back to the impermanent and fickle.

1125.
‘‘Arūpa dūraṅgama ekacāri, na te karissaṃ vacanaṃ idānihaṃ;Dukkhā hi kāmā kaṭukā mahabbhayā, nibbānamevābhimano carissaṃ. O formless one, far-going, solitary-faring! I will not do your bidding now. For sensual pleasures are painful, bitter, fraught with great danger. I will fare on with my mind set only on Nibbāna.


1126.
‘‘Nāhaṃ alakkhyā ahirikkatāya vā, na cittahetū na ca dūrakantanā;Ājīvahetū ca ahaṃ na nikkhamiṃ, kato ca te citta paṭissavo mayā.

I did not go forth because of misfortune or shamelessness, nor because of madness, O mind, nor out of loneliness. I did not go forth for the sake of a livelihood. But a promise was made by me to you, O mind.

1127.
‘‘‘Appicchatā sappurisehi vaṇṇitā, makkhappahānaṃ vūpasamo dukhassa’;Itissu maṃ citta tadā niyuñjasi, idāni tvaṃ gacchasi pubbaciṇṇaṃ.

‘Fewness of wishes is praised by the good; so too the abandoning of hypocrisy and the appeasement of suffering.’ Thus, O mind, you urged me then. Now you revert to your old habits.

1128.
‘‘Taṇhā avijjā ca piyāpiyañca, subhāni rūpāni sukhā ca vedanā;Manāpiyā kāmaguṇā ca vantā, vante ahaṃ āvamituṃ na ussahe.

Craving and ignorance, the dear and the not-dear, beautiful forms and pleasant feelings, and agreeable sensual pleasures have all been vomited out. I am unable to swallow again what I have vomited.

1129.
‘‘Sabbattha te citta vaco kataṃ mayā, bahūsu jātīsu na mesi kopito;Ajjhattasambhavo kataññutāya te, dukkhe ciraṃ saṃsaritaṃ tayā kate.

In all things, O mind, your word was done by me; in many births I did not anger you. You who arise from within, because of my obedience to you, I have wandered long in suffering through your deeds.

1130.
‘‘Tvaññeva no citta karosi brāhmaṇo [brāhmaṇe (sī.), brāhmaṇaṃ (?) bhāvalopa-tappadhānatā gahetabbā], tvaṃ khattiyo rājadasī [rājadisī (syā. ka.)] karosi;Vessā ca suddā ca bhavāma ekadā, devattanaṃ vāpi taveva vāhasā.

You alone, O mind, make one a brahmin; you make one a khattiya or a royal sage. At times we become vessas and suddas; and it is by your power that one attains the state of a deva.

1131.
‘‘Taveva hetū asurā bhavāmase, tvaṃmūlakaṃ nerayikā bhavāmase;Atho tiracchānagatāpi ekadā, petattanaṃ vāpi taveva vāhasā.

For your sake we become asuras; rooted in you, we become beings in hell. And sometimes we go to the animal realm, or attain the state of a hungry ghost—all by your power.

1132.
‘‘Nanu dubbhissasi maṃ punappunaṃ, muhuṃ muhuṃ cāraṇikaṃva dassayaṃ;Ummattakeneva mayā palobhasi, kiñcāpi te citta virādhitaṃ mayā.

Surely you will not betray me again and again, repeatedly displaying yourself like a wandering actor? You entice me as if I were mad. What wrong have I done to you, O mind?

1133.
‘‘Idaṃ pure cittamacāri cārikaṃ, yenicchakaṃ yatthakāmaṃ yathāsukhaṃ;Tadajjahaṃ niggahessāmi yoniso, hatthippabhinnaṃ viya aṅkusaggaho.

Formerly this mind wandered where it wished, where it liked, as it pleased. Today I will restrain it wisely, as a mahout with a hook restrains a rutting elephant.

1134.
‘‘Satthā ca me lokamimaṃ adhiṭṭhahi, aniccato addhuvato asārato;Pakkhanda maṃ citta jinassa sāsane, tārehi oghā mahatā suduttarā.

The Teacher has explained this world to me as impermanent, unstable, and essenceless. Leap, O mind, into the Conqueror’s Dispensation! Then carry me across the great flood, so very hard to cross.

1135.
‘‘Na te idaṃ citta yathā purāṇakaṃ, nāhaṃ alaṃ tuyha vase nivattituṃ [vasena vattituṃ (?)];Mahesino pabbajitomhi sāsane, na mādisā honti vināsadhārino.

Things are not for you now, O mind, as they were before. I am no longer fit to live under your control. I have gone forth in the Great Seer’s Dispensation; those like me are not followers of a ruinous path.

1136.
‘‘Nagā samuddā saritā vasundharā, disā catasso vidisā adho divā;Sabbe aniccā tibhavā upaddutā, kuhiṃ gato citta sukhaṃ ramissasi.

Mountains, oceans, rivers, and the earth; the four directions, the intermediate directions, what is below and what is in the sky—all are impermanent; the three realms of existence are afflicted. Where can you go, O mind, to delight in happiness?

1137.
‘‘Dhitipparaṃ kiṃ mama citta kāhisi, na te alaṃ citta vasānuvattako;Na jātu bhastaṃ ubhatomukhaṃ chupe, dhiratthu pūraṃ nava sotasandaniṃ.

What can you do to me, O mind, a man of firm resolve? I am no longer fit to be your follower, O mind. One should never touch a two-mouthed bellows. Shame on this body, full and flowing from its nine streams!

1138.
‘‘Varāhaeṇeyyavigāḷhasevite, pabbhārakuṭṭe pakateva sundare;Navambunā pāvusasitthakānane, tahiṃ guhāgehagato ramissasi.

You will delight on a lovely mountain slope, beautiful as if specially made, frequented by boars and deer, in a forest sprinkled by the fresh rains of the monsoon, having gone to your home in a cave.

1139.
‘‘Sunīlagīvā susikhā supekhunā, sucittapattacchadanā vihaṅgamā;Sumañjughosatthanitābhigajjino, te taṃ ramessanti vanamhi jhāyinaṃ.

Birds with lovely blue necks, fine crests, and beautiful tail-feathers, with wings of variegated colors, thundering with a sweet and lovely sound—they will delight you in the forest as you meditate.

1140.
‘‘Vuṭṭhamhi deve caturaṅgule tiṇe, saṃpupphite meghanibhamhi kānane;Nagantare viṭapisamo sayissaṃ, taṃ me mudū hehiti tūlasannibhaṃ.

When the sky-god has rained and the grass is four fingers high, when the cloud-like forest is in full bloom, I will lie down among the trees like a tree; that will be soft for me, like cotton.

1141.
‘‘Tathā tu kassāmi yathāpi issaro, yaṃ labbhati tenapi hotu me alaṃ;Na tāhaṃ kassāmi yathā atandito, biḷārabhastaṃva yathā sumadditaṃ.

I will exert myself like a master, and be content with whatever I obtain. I will not exert myself like a lazy person, like a well-softened cat-skin bag.

1142.
‘‘Tathā tu kassāmi yathāpi issaro, yaṃ labbhati tenapi hotu me alaṃ;Vīriyena taṃ mayha vasānayissaṃ, gajaṃva mattaṃ kusalaṅkusaggaho.

I will exert myself like a master, and be content with whatever I obtain. With energy I will bring it under my control, like a skilled goad-holder a rutting elephant.

1143.
‘‘Tayā sudantena avaṭṭhitena hi, hayena yoggācariyova ujjunā;Pahomi maggaṃ paṭipajjituṃ sivaṃ, cittānurakkhīhi sadā nisevitaṃ.

With you, my mind, well-tamed and steadfast, I am like a trainer with an upright horse. I am able to enter upon the auspicious path, always frequented by those who guard their minds.

1144.
‘‘Ārammaṇe taṃ balasā nibandhisaṃ, nāgaṃva thambhamhi daḷhāya rajjuyā;Taṃ me suguttaṃ satiyā subhāvitaṃ, anissitaṃ sabbabhavesu hehisi.

I will bind you by force to the meditation object, like an elephant to a post with a strong rope. Well-guarded by my mindfulness and well-developed, you will be independent of all kinds of existence.

1145.
‘‘Paññāya chetvā vipathānusārinaṃ, yogena niggayha pathe nivesiya;Disvā samudayaṃ vibhavañca sambhavaṃ, dāyādako hehisi aggavādino.

Having cut off with wisdom your tendency to follow wrong paths, having restrained you by spiritual application and set you on the right path, having seen the origin and passing away of being, you will be an heir of the Foremost Proponent.

1146.
‘‘Catubbipallāsavasaṃ adhiṭṭhitaṃ, gāmaṇḍalaṃva parinesi citta maṃ;Nanu [nūna (sī.)] saṃyojanabandhanacchidaṃ, saṃsevase kāruṇikaṃ mahāmuniṃ.

O mind, you lead me about like a village headman, you who are under the sway of the four distortions. Do you not attend upon the compassionate Great Sage, the cutter of the bonds of fetters?

1147.
‘‘Migo yathā seri sucittakānane, rammaṃ giriṃ pāvusaabbhamāliniṃ [māliṃ (?)];Anākule tattha nage ramissaṃ [ramissasi (syā. ka.)], asaṃsayaṃ citta parā bhavissasi.

Just as a wild beast roams freely in a lovely forest, on a delightful mountain garlanded with rain clouds, so will I delight on that untroubled mountain. Without a doubt, O mind, you will be vanquished.

1148.
‘‘Ye tuyha chandena vasena vattino, narā ca nārī ca anubhonti yaṃ sukhaṃ;Aviddasū māravasānuvattino, bhavābhinandī tava citta sāvakā’’ti. Those men and women who live under the sway of your will, experiencing whatever pleasure there is—they are ignorant, followers of Māra’s domain, delighting in existence. O mind, they are your disciples. … Tālapuṭo thero…. … The Elder Tālapuṭa … Paññāsanipāto niṭṭhito. The Nipāta of Fifty Verses is finished. Tatruddānaṃ –

The summary: Paññāsamhi nipātamhi, eko tālapuṭo suci;Gāthāyo tattha paññāsa, puna pañca ca uttarīti. In the Nipāta of Fifty, there is the one, the pure Tālapuṭa. The verses there are fifty and five more besides.
