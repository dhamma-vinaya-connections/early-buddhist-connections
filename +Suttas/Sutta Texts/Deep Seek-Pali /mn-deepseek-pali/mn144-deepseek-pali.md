# 144. Nandakovādasuttaṃ

## 144. The Discourse on Nandaka’s Exhortation

398. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Atha kho mahāpajāpatigotamī pañcamattehi bhikkhunisatehi saddhiṃ yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ aṭṭhāsi. Ekamantaṃ ṭhitā kho mahāpajāpatigotamī bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘ovadatu, bhante, bhagavā bhikkhuniyo; anusāsatu, bhante, bhagavā bhikkhuniyo; karotu, bhante, bhagavā bhikkhunīnaṃ dhammiṃ katha’’nti [dhammikathanti (syā. kaṃ. ka.)].

398.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Park. Then Mahāpajāpatī Gotamī, together with approximately five hundred bhikkhunis, approached the Blessed One; having approached the Blessed One, she paid homage to him and stood to one side. While standing to one side, Mahāpajāpatī Gotamī said this to the Blessed One: “Bhante, let the Blessed One advise the bhikkhunis; let the Blessed One instruct the bhikkhunis; let the Blessed One give the bhikkhunis a Dhamma talk.”

Tena kho pana samayena therā bhikkhū bhikkhuniyo ovadanti pariyāyena. Āyasmā nandako na icchati bhikkhuniyo ovadituṃ pariyāyena. Atha kho bhagavā āyasmantaṃ ānandaṃ āmantesi – ‘‘kassa nu kho, ānanda, ajja pariyāyo bhikkhuniyo ovadituṃ pariyāyenā’’ti? ‘‘Sabbeheva, bhante, kato [nandakassa bhante (sī. pī.)] pariyāyo bhikkhuniyo ovadituṃ pariyāyena. Ayaṃ, bhante, āyasmā nandako na icchati bhikkhuniyo ovadituṃ pariyāyenā’’ti.

At that time, the elder bhikkhus were instructing the bhikkhunis in turn. The Venerable Nandaka did not wish to instruct the bhikkhunis in turn. Then the Blessed One addressed the Venerable Ānanda: “Ānanda, whose turn is it today to instruct the bhikkhunis?” “Bhante, everyone has already taken their turn to instruct the bhikkhunis. This Venerable Nandaka, Bhante, does not wish to instruct the bhikkhunis in turn.”

Atha kho bhagavā āyasmantaṃ nandakaṃ āmantesi – ‘‘ovada, nandaka, bhikkhuniyo; anusāsa, nandaka, bhikkhuniyo; karohi tvaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhunīnaṃ dhammiṃ katha’’nti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho āyasmā nandako bhagavato paṭissutvā pubbaṇhasamayaṃ nivāsetvā pattacīvaramādāya sāvatthiṃ piṇḍāya pāvisi. Sāvatthiyaṃ piṇḍāya caritvā pacchābhattaṃ piṇḍapātapaṭikkanto attadutiyo yena rājakārāmo tenupasaṅkami. Addasaṃsu kho tā bhikkhuniyo āyasmantaṃ nandakaṃ dūratova āgacchantaṃ. Disvāna āsanaṃ paññāpesuṃ, udakañca pādānaṃ upaṭṭhapesuṃ. Nisīdi kho āyasmā nandako paññatte āsane. Nisajja pāde pakkhālesi. Tāpi kho bhikkhuniyo āyasmantaṃ nandakaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Ekamantaṃ nisinnā kho tā bhikkhuniyo āyasmā nandako etadavoca – ‘‘paṭipucchakathā kho, bhaginiyo, bhavissati. Tattha ājānantīhi – ‘ājānāmā’ tissa vacanīyaṃ, na ājānantīhi – ‘na ājānāmā’ tissa vacanīyaṃ. Yassā vā panassa kaṅkhā vā vimati vā ahameva tattha paṭipucchitabbo – ‘idaṃ, bhante, kathaṃ; imassa kvattho’’’ti? ‘‘Ettakenapi mayaṃ, bhante, ayyassa nandakassa attamanā abhiraddhā [abhinandāma (syā. kaṃ.)] yaṃ no ayyo nandako pavāretī’’ti.

Then the Blessed One addressed the Venerable Nandaka: “Nandaka, advise the bhikkhunis; Nandaka, instruct the bhikkhunis; brahmin, give the bhikkhunis a Dhamma talk.” “Yes, Bhante,” replied the Venerable Nandaka to the Blessed One. In the morning, having dressed and taken his bowl and robe, he entered Sāvatthī for alms. After wandering for alms in Sāvatthī and returning from his almsround, accompanied by a companion, he went to the royal park. Those bhikkhunis saw the Venerable Nandaka approaching from afar. Having seen him, they prepared a seat and set out water for his feet. The Venerable Nandaka sat down on the prepared seat and washed his feet. Those bhikkhunis then paid homage to the Venerable Nandaka and sat down to one side. While seated to one side, the Venerable Nandaka said to them: “Sisters, there will be a discussion by way of questions and answers. Those who understand should say, ‘We understand,’ and those who do not understand should say, ‘We do not understand.’ Or if anyone has any doubt or uncertainty, she should question me about it: ‘Bhante, how is this? What is the meaning of this?’” “Even this much, Bhante, makes us joyful and delighted with the Venerable Nandaka, that he gives us this opportunity.”

399. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, cakkhu niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, sotaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante…pe… ghānaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’… ‘‘jivhā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’… ‘‘kāyo nicco vā anicco vā’’ti? ‘‘Anicco, bhante’’… ‘‘mano nicco vā anicco vā’’ti? ‘‘Anicco, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Pubbeva no etaṃ, bhante, yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhaṃ – ‘itipime cha ajjhattikā āyatanā aniccā’’’ti. ‘‘Sādhu, sādhu, bhaginiyo! Evañhetaṃ, bhaginiyo, hoti ariyasāvakassa yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato’’.

399.“What do you think, sisters, is the eye permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “What do you think, sisters, is the ear permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is the nose permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is the tongue permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is the body permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is the mind permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “For what reason?” “Because, Bhante, we have already seen this as it really is with proper wisdom: ‘These six internal sense bases are impermanent.’” “Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees as it really is with proper wisdom.”

400. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, rūpā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, saddā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante…pe… gandhā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’… ‘‘rasā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’… ‘‘phoṭṭhabbā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’… ‘‘dhammā niccā vā aniccā vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccā, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Pubbeva no etaṃ, bhante, yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhaṃ – ‘itipime cha bāhirā āyatanā aniccā’’’ti. ‘‘Sādhu, sādhu, bhaginiyo! Evañhetaṃ, bhaginiyo, hoti ariyasāvakassa yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato’’.

400.“What do you think, sisters, are forms permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “What do you think, sisters, are sounds permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Are odors permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Are tastes permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Are tactile objects permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Are mental phenomena permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “For what reason?” “Because, Bhante, we have already seen this as it really is with proper wisdom: ‘These six external sense bases are impermanent.’” “Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees as it really is with proper wisdom.”

401. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, cakkhuviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhaginiyo, sotaviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante…pe… ghānaviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’… ‘‘jivhāviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’… ‘‘kāyaviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’… ‘‘manoviññāṇaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vāti’’? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Pubbeva no etaṃ, bhante, yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhaṃ – ‘itipime cha viññāṇakāyā aniccā’’’ti. ‘‘Sādhu, sādhu, bhaginiyo! Evañhetaṃ, bhaginiyo, hoti ariyasāvakassa yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato’’.

401.“What do you think, sisters, is eye-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “What do you think, sisters, is ear-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is nose-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is tongue-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is body-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante…” “Is mind-consciousness permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, Bhante.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it proper to regard that as: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, Bhante, it is not.” “For what reason?” “Because, Bhante, we have already seen this as it really is with proper wisdom: ‘These six groups of consciousness are impermanent.’” “Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees as it really is with proper wisdom.”

402. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhaginiyo, telappadīpassa jhāyato telampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, vaṭṭipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā, accipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā, ābhāpi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā. Yo nu kho, bhaginiyo, evaṃ vadeyya – ‘amussa telappadīpassa jhāyato telampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, vaṭṭipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā, accipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā; yā ca khvāssa ābhā sā niccā dhuvā sassatā avipariṇāmadhammā’ti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Amussa hi, bhante, telappadīpassa jhāyato telampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, vaṭṭipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā, accipi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā; pagevassa ābhā aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, bhaginiyo, yo nu kho evaṃ vadeyya – ‘cha khome ajjhattikā āyatanā aniccā [aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā (?)]; yañca kho cha ajjhattike āyatane paṭicca paṭisaṃvedeti sukhaṃ vā dukkhaṃ vā adukkhamasukhaṃ vā taṃ niccaṃ dhuvaṃ sassataṃ avipariṇāmadhamma’nti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Tajjaṃ tajjaṃ, bhante, paccayaṃ paṭicca tajjā tajjā vedanā uppajjanti. Tajjassa tajjassa paccayassa nirodhā tajjā tajjā vedanā nirujjhantī’’ti. ‘‘Sādhu, sādhu, bhaginiyo! Evañhetaṃ, bhaginiyo, hoti ariyasāvakassa yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato’’.

402.“Suppose, sisters, an oil lamp is burning. The oil is impermanent, subject to change; the wick is impermanent, subject to change; the flame is impermanent, subject to change; the light is impermanent, subject to change. Now, if someone were to say: ‘The oil of this burning lamp is impermanent, subject to change; the wick is impermanent, subject to change; the flame is impermanent, subject to change; but its light is permanent, lasting, eternal, and not subject to change,’ would they be speaking rightly, sisters?” “No, venerable sir.” “Why is that?” “Because, venerable sir, when that oil lamp is burning, the oil is impermanent, subject to change; the wick is impermanent, subject to change; the flame is impermanent, subject to change—how much more so its light, which is impermanent, subject to change.” “Just so, sisters, if someone were to say: ‘These six internal sense bases are impermanent; but whatever one feels—pleasant, painful, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant—dependent on these six internal sense bases, that is permanent, lasting, eternal, and not subject to change,’ would they be speaking rightly, sisters?” “No, venerable sir.” “Why is that?” “Because, venerable sir, dependent on each specific condition, each specific feeling arises. With the cessation of that specific condition, that specific feeling ceases.” “Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees with right wisdom as it really is.”

403. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhaginiyo, mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato mūlampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, khandhopi anicco vipariṇāmadhammo, sākhāpalāsampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, chāyāpi aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā. Yo nu kho, bhaginiyo, evaṃ vadeyya – ‘amussa mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato mūlampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, khandhopi anicco vipariṇāmadhammo, sākhāpalāsampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, yā ca khvāssa chāyā sā niccā dhuvā sassatā avipariṇāmadhammā’ti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Amussa hi, bhante, mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato mūlampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ, khandhopi anicco vipariṇāmadhammo, sākhāpalāsampi aniccaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ; pagevassa chāyā aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, bhaginiyo, yo nu kho evaṃ vadeyya – ‘cha khome bāhirā āyatanā aniccā [aniccā vipariṇāmadhammā (sī. pī.)]. Yañca kho cha bāhire āyatane paṭicca paṭisaṃvedeti sukhaṃ vā dukkhaṃ vā adukkhamasukhaṃ vā taṃ niccaṃ dhuvaṃ sassataṃ avipariṇāmadhamma’nti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Tajjaṃ tajjaṃ, bhante, paccayaṃ paṭicca tajjā tajjā vedanā uppajjanti. Tajjassa tajjassa paccayassa nirodhā tajjā tajjā vedanā nirujjhantī’’ti. ‘‘Sādhu, sādhu, bhaginiyo! Evañhetaṃ, bhaginiyo, hoti ariyasāvakassa yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato’’.

403.“Suppose, sisters, there were a great tree standing with heartwood. Its roots are impermanent, subject to change; its trunk is impermanent, subject to change; its branches and leaves are impermanent, subject to change; and its shadow is impermanent, subject to change. If someone were to say, ‘Though this great tree standing with heartwood has roots that are impermanent, subject to change; a trunk that is impermanent, subject to change; branches and leaves that are impermanent, subject to change; yet its shadow is permanent, lasting, eternal, not subject to change’—would they be speaking rightly, sisters?” “No, venerable sir.” “Why is that?” “Because, venerable sir, that great tree standing with heartwood has roots that are impermanent, subject to change; a trunk that is impermanent, subject to change; branches and leaves that are impermanent, subject to change—how much more so its shadow, which is impermanent, subject to change.” “Just so, sisters, if someone were to say: ‘These six external sense bases are impermanent; but whatever one feels—pleasant, painful, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant—dependent on these six external sense bases, that is permanent, lasting, eternal, and not subject to change,’ would they be speaking rightly, sisters?” “No, venerable sir.” “Why is that?” “Because, venerable sir, dependent on each specific condition, each specific feeling arises. With the cessation of that specific condition, that specific feeling ceases.” “Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees with right wisdom as it really is.”

404. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhaginiyo, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā gāviṃ vadhitvā tiṇhena govikantanena gāviṃ saṅkanteyya anupahacca antaraṃ maṃsakāyaṃ anupahacca bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ. Yaṃ yadeva tattha antarā vilimaṃsaṃ [vilimaṃ (sī. pī. ka.)] antarā nhāru antarā bandhanaṃ taṃ tadeva tiṇhena govikantanena sañchindeyya saṅkanteyya sampakanteyya samparikanteyya. Sañchinditvā saṅkantitvā sampakantitvā samparikantitvā vidhunitvā bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ teneva cammena taṃ gāviṃ paṭicchādetvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘tathevāyaṃ gāvī saṃyuttā imināva cammenā’ti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Amu hi, bhante, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā gāviṃ vadhitvā tiṇhena govikantanena gāviṃ saṅkanteyya anupahacca antaraṃ maṃsakāyaṃ anupahacca bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ. Yaṃ yadeva tattha antarā vilimaṃsaṃ antarā nhāru antarā bandhanaṃ taṃ tadeva tiṇhena govikantanena sañchindeyya saṅkanteyya sampakanteyya samparikanteyya. Sañchinditvā saṅkantitvā sampakantitvā samparikantitvā vidhunitvā bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ teneva cammena taṃ gāviṃ paṭicchādetvā kiñcāpi so evaṃ vadeyya – ‘tathevāyaṃ gāvī saṃyuttā imināva cammenā’ti; atha kho sā gāvī visaṃyuttā teneva cammenā’’ti.

404.“Suppose, sisters, a skilled butcher or his apprentice were to kill a cow and then carve it up with a sharp butcher’s knife without damaging the inner flesh or the outer hide. Whatever loose flesh, sinews, and ligaments are in between, he would cut, carve, slice through, and sever them all with that sharp butcher’s knife. Having cut, carved, sliced through, and severed them, he would then strip off the outer hide and cover that cow again with that same hide and say: ‘This cow is still bound by this very hide.’ Would he be speaking rightly, sisters?” “No, venerable sir.” “Why is that?” “Because, venerable sir, though that skilled butcher or his apprentice killed the cow, carved it up with a sharp butcher’s knife without damaging the inner flesh or the outer hide, and cut through, carved, sliced through, and severed all the loose flesh, sinews, and ligaments in between—having cut, carved, sliced through, and severed them—if he then stripped off the outer hide and covered that cow again with that same hide and said, ‘This cow is still bound by this very hide,’ still, that cow would actually be freed from that hide.”

‘‘Upamā kho me ayaṃ, bhaginiyo, katā atthassa viññāpanāya. Ayamevettha attho; ‘antarā maṃsakāyo’ti kho, bhaginiyo, channetaṃ ajjhattikānaṃ āyatanānaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘bāhiro cammakāyo’ti kho bhaginiyo, channetaṃ bāhirānaṃ āyatanānaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘antarā vilimaṃsaṃ, antarā nhāru, antarā bandhana’nti kho, bhaginiyo, nandīrāgassetaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘tiṇhaṃ govikantana’nti kho, bhaginiyo, ariyāyetaṃ paññāya adhivacanaṃ; yāyaṃ ariyā paññā antarā kilesaṃ antarā saṃyojanaṃ antarā bandhanaṃ sañchindati saṅkantati sampakantati samparikantati.

“Sisters, I have made this simile to explain the meaning. This is its meaning: ‘The inner flesh’ is a designation for the six internal sense bases. ‘The outer hide’ is a designation for the six external sense bases. ‘The loose flesh, the sinews, the ligaments in between’ is a designation for delight and lust. ‘The sharp butcher’s knife’ is a designation for noble wisdom—that noble wisdom which cuts through, carves through, completely cuts through, and utterly cuts through the defilements, fetters, and bonds in between.”

405. ‘‘Satta kho panime, bhaginiyo, bojjhaṅgā, yesaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati. Katame satta? Idha, bhaginiyo, bhikkhu satisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ, dhammavicayasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti…pe… vīriyasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… pītisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… passaddhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… samādhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… upekkhāsambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ. Ime kho, bhaginiyo, satta bojjhaṅgā, yesaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharatī’’ti.

405.“Sisters, there are these seven factors of awakening, which when developed and cultivated, lead a bhikkhu to the destruction of the taints, to the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, realizing it for himself in this very life, having directly known it. What are these seven? Here, sisters, a bhikkhu develops the factor of awakening of mindfulness, which is based upon seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, culminating in relinquishment. He develops the factor of awakening of investigation of phenomena... the factor of awakening of energy... the factor of awakening of joy... the factor of awakening of tranquility... the factor of awakening of samādhi... the factor of awakening of equanimity, which is based upon seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, culminating in relinquishment. These, sisters, are the seven factors of awakening, which when developed and cultivated, lead a bhikkhu to the destruction of the taints, to the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, realizing it for himself in this very life, having directly known it.”

What is the meaning of this?'" "Bhante, we are delighted and satisfied with Venerable Nandaka even by this much, that he has given us the opportunity to question him."

411."Just as, sisters, when an oil lamp is burning, the oil is inconstant and subject to change, the wick is inconstant and subject to change, the flame is inconstant and subject to change, and the light is inconstant and subject to change. "No, Bhante." "For what reason?" "Because, Bhante, in an oil lamp that is burning, the oil is inconstant and subject to change, the wick is inconstant and subject to change, the flame is inconstant and subject to change—how much more so the light, which is inconstant and subject to change.""So too, sisters, if someone were to say: 'These six internal sense bases are inconstant. But whatever feeling one experiences—pleasant, painful, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant—dependent on these six internal sense bases, that is constant, stable, eternal, and not subject to change'—would he be speaking rightly, sisters?" "No, Bhante." "For what reason?" "Because, Bhante, dependent on specific conditions, specific feelings arise. With the cessation of those specific conditions, those specific feelings cease." "Good, good, sisters! So it is, sisters, for a noble disciple who sees with right wisdom as it truly is."

412."Sisters, suppose there were a great, sound tree standing, with its roots impermanent, subject to change; its trunk impermanent, subject to change; its branches and leaves impermanent, subject to change; and its shadow also impermanent, subject to change. 413. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhaginiyo, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā gāviṃ vadhitvā tiṇhena govikantanena gāviṃ saṅkanteyya anupahacca antaraṃ maṃsakāyaṃ anupahacca bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ. Yaṃ yadeva tattha antarā vilimaṃsaṃ antarā nhāru antarā bandhanaṃ taṃ tadeva tiṇhena govikantanena sañchindeyya saṅkanteyya sampakanteyya samparikanteyya. Sañchinditvā saṅkantitvā sampakantitvā samparikantitvā vidhunitvā bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ teneva cammena taṃ gāviṃ paṭicchādetvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘tathevāyaṃ gāvī saṃyuttā imināva cammenā’ti; sammā nu kho so, bhaginiyo, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Amu hi, bhante, dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā gāviṃ vadhitvā tiṇhena govikantanena gāviṃ saṅkanteyya anupahacca antaraṃ maṃsakāyaṃ anupahacca bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ. Yaṃ yadeva tattha antarā vilimaṃsaṃ antarā nhāru antarā bandhanaṃ taṃ tadeva tiṇhena govikantanena sañchindeyya saṅkanteyya sampakanteyya samparikanteyya. Sañchinditvā saṅkantitvā sampakantitvā samparikantitvā vidhunitvā bāhiraṃ cammakāyaṃ teneva cammena taṃ gāviṃ paṭicchādetvā kiñcāpi so evaṃ vadeyya – ‘tathevāyaṃ gāvī saṃyuttā imināva cammenā’ti; atha kho sā gāvī visaṃyuttā teneva cammenā’’ti.

413."Sisters, suppose a skilled butcher or his apprentice were to kill a cow and cut it up with a sharp butcher’s knife, without damaging the inner flesh or the outer hide. Whatever loose flesh, sinews, and ligaments there are within, he would cut, sever, slice, and completely sever them all with the sharp butcher’s knife. Having cut, severed, sliced, and completely severed them, he would shake off the outer hide and cover the cow again with that same hide and say, ‘This cow is still joined together by this very hide.’ Would he be speaking rightly?" "No, venerable sir." "Why is that?" "Because, venerable sir, that skilled butcher or his apprentice killed the cow, cut it up with a sharp butcher’s knife, without damaging the inner flesh or the outer hide. Whatever loose flesh, sinews, and ligaments there are within, he cut, severed, sliced, and completely severed them all with the sharp butcher’s knife. Having cut, severed, sliced, and completely severed them, even if he shakes off the outer hide and then covers the cow again with that same hide and says, ‘This cow is still joined together by this very hide,’ still, that cow is actually disjoined from that hide."

‘‘Upamā kho me ayaṃ, bhaginiyo, katā atthassa viññāpanāya ayamevettha attho. ‘Antarā maṃsakāyo’ti kho, bhaginiyo, channetaṃ ajjhattikānaṃ āyatanānaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘bāhiro cammakāyo’ti kho, bhaginiyo, channetaṃ bāhirānaṃ āyatanānaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘antarā vilimaṃsaṃ antarā nhāru antarā bandhana’nti kho, bhaginiyo, nandīrāgassetaṃ adhivacanaṃ; ‘tiṇhaṃ govikantana’nti kho, bhaginiyo, ariyāyetaṃ paññāya adhivacanaṃ; yāyaṃ ariyā paññā antarā kilesaṃ antarā saṃyojanaṃ antarā bandhanaṃ sañchindati saṅkantati sampakantati samparikantati.

"This is a simile I have made, sisters, to explain the meaning, for that is the meaning here. ‘The inner flesh’ is a designation for the six internal sense bases. ‘The outer hide’ is a designation for the six external sense bases. ‘The flesh between, the sinews between, the ligaments between’ is a designation for delight and lust. ‘The sharp butcher’s knife’ is a designation for noble wisdom—the noble wisdom that cuts, severs, slices, and completely severs the defilements, fetters, and bonds between."

414. ‘‘Satta kho panime, bhaginiyo, bojjhaṅgā, yesaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati. Katame satta? Idha, bhaginiyo, bhikkhu satisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ. Dhammavicayasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti…pe… vīriyasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… pītisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… passaddhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… samādhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… upekkhāsambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ. Ime kho, bhaginiyo, satta bojjhaṅgā yesaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharatī’’ti.

414."Sisters, there are these seven factors of enlightenment. When developed and cultivated, a monk, through the destruction of the taints, realizes for himself with direct knowledge, in this very life, the taintless liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom, and having attained it, abides in it. What are the seven? Here, sisters, a monk develops the mindfulness enlightenment factor, which is based on seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, and ripens to relinquishment. He develops the investigation of phenomena enlightenment factor... He develops the energy enlightenment factor... He develops the rapture enlightenment factor... He develops the tranquility enlightenment factor... He develops the samādhi enlightenment factor... He develops the equanimity enlightenment factor, which is based on seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, and ripens to relinquishment. These, sisters, are the seven factors of enlightenment. When developed and cultivated, a monk, through the destruction of the taints, realizes for himself with direct knowledge, in this very life, the taintless liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom, and having attained it, abides in it."

415. Atha kho āyasmā nandako tā bhikkhuniyo iminā ovādena ovaditvā uyyojesi – ‘‘gacchatha, bhaginiyo; kālo’’ti. Atha kho tā bhikkhuniyo āyasmato nandakassa bhāsitaṃ abhinanditvā anumoditvā uṭṭhāyāsanā āyasmantaṃ nandakaṃ abhivādetvā padakkhiṇaṃ katvā yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkamiṃsu; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ aṭṭhaṃsu. Ekamantaṃ ṭhitā kho tā bhikkhuniyo bhagavā etadavoca –‘‘gacchatha, bhikkhuniyo; kālo’’ti. Atha kho tā bhikkhuniyo bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā padakkhiṇaṃ katvā pakkamiṃsu. Atha kho bhagavā acirapakkantīsu tāsu bhikkhunīsu bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, tadahuposathe pannarase na hoti bahuno janassa kaṅkhā vā vimati vā – ‘ūno nu kho cando, puṇṇo nu kho cando’ti, atha kho puṇṇo candotveva hoti; evameva kho, bhikkhave, tā bhikkhuniyo nandakassa dhammadesanāya attamanā ceva paripuṇṇasaṅkappā ca. Tāsaṃ, bhikkhave, pañcannaṃ bhikkhunisatānaṃ yā pacchimitā bhikkhunī sā [yā pacchimā bhikkhunī, sā (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), yā pacchimikā, tā bhikkhuniyo (ka.)] sotāpannā avinipātadhammā niyatā sambodhiparāyanā’’ti.

415.Then Venerable Nandaka, having advised those nuns with this exhortation, dismissed them: “Go, sisters; it is time.” Then those nuns, having delighted in and rejoiced at Venerable Nandaka’s words, rose from their seats, paid homage to him, circumambulated him, and approached the Blessed One. Having approached, they paid homage to the Blessed One and stood to one side. Standing to one side, the Blessed One said to those nuns: “Go, nuns; it is time.” Then those nuns paid homage to the Blessed One, circumambulated him, and departed. Soon after those nuns had left, the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus: “bhikkhus, just as on the fifteenth day of the uposatha, there is no doubt or uncertainty among many people—‘Is the moon waning or full?’—but the moon is indeed full; in the same way, bhikkhus, those nuns were delighted and fully satisfied with Nandaka’s teaching. Among those five hundred nuns, bhikkhus, even the last nun is a stream-enterer, no longer subject to falling into the lower realms, certain, headed for enlightenment.”

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

The Blessed One said this. The bhikkhus, delighted, rejoiced at the Blessed One's words.

Nandakovādasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ catutthaṃ.

The Nandakovāda Sutta, the Fourth, is finished.
