# 135. Saḷāyatanavibhaṅgasuttaṃ

## 135. The Discourse on the Analysis of the Six Sense Bases

304. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tatra kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘bhikkhavo’’ti. ‘‘Bhadante’’ti te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘saḷāyatanavibhaṅgaṃ vo, bhikkhave, desessāmi. Taṃ suṇātha, sādhukaṃ manasi karotha; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca –

304.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's Monastery. There the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus: "bhikkhus!" "Venerable sir," those bhikkhus replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this: "bhikkhus, I will teach you the analysis of the six sense bases. Listen to it, attend carefully, I shall speak." "Yes, venerable sir," those bhikkhus replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

‘‘‘Cha ajjhattikāni āyatanāni veditabbāni, cha bāhirāni āyatanāni veditabbāni, cha viññāṇakāyā veditabbā, cha phassakāyā veditabbā, aṭṭhārasa manopavicārā veditabbā, chattiṃsa sattapadā veditabbā, tatra idaṃ nissāya idaṃ pajahatha, tayo satipaṭṭhānā yadariyo sevati yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahati, so vuccati yoggācariyānaṃ [yogācariyānaṃ (ka.)] anuttaro purisadammasārathī’ti – ayamuddeso saḷāyatanavibhaṅgassa.

"'Six internal sense bases are to be known, six external sense bases are to be known, six classes of consciousness are to be known, six classes of contact are to be known, eighteen mental excursions are to be known, thirty-six states of sentient beings are to be known. Depending on this, abandon that. The three foundations of mindfulness that the noble one cultivates, and cultivating which, the noble one is fit to instruct the group as a teacher, he is called the unsurpassed trainer of persons to be tamed, the charioteer.' This is the summary of the analysis of the six sense bases."

305. ‘‘‘Cha ajjhattikāni āyatanāni veditabbānī’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? ‘Cakkhāyatanaṃ sotāyatanaṃ ghānāyatanaṃ jivhāyatanaṃ kāyāyatanaṃ manāyatanaṃ – cha ajjhattikāni āyatanāni veditabbānī’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

305."'The six internal sense bases are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? 'The eye base, the ear base, the nose base, the tongue base, the body base, the mind base—the six internal sense bases are to be known'—it was with reference to this that this was said."

‘‘‘Cha bāhirāni āyatanāni veditabbānī’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? ‘Rūpāyatanaṃ saddāyatanaṃ gandhāyatanaṃ rasāyatanaṃ phoṭṭhabbāyatanaṃ dhammāyatanaṃ – cha bāhirāni āyatanāni veditabbānī’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"'The six external sense bases are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? 'The visible form base, the sound base, the odor base, the flavor base, the tactile object base, the mind-object base—the six external sense bases are to be known'—it was with reference to this that this was said."

‘‘‘Cha viññāṇakāyā veditabbā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? ‘Cakkhuviññāṇaṃ sotaviññāṇaṃ ghānaviññāṇaṃ jivhāviññāṇaṃ kāyaviññāṇaṃ manoviññāṇaṃ – cha viññāṇakāyā veditabbā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"'The six classes of consciousness are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? 'Eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, mind-consciousness—the six classes of consciousness are to be known'—it was with reference to this that this was said."

‘‘‘Cha phassakāyā veditabbā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? ‘Cakkhusamphasso sotasamphasso ghānasamphasso jivhāsamphasso kāyasamphasso manosamphasso – cha phassakāyā veditabbā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"'The six classes of contact are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? 'Eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, mind-contact—the six classes of contact are to be known'—it was with reference to this that this was said."

‘‘‘Aṭṭhārasa manopavicārā veditabbā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? ‘Cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā somanassaṭṭhānīyaṃ rūpaṃ upavicarati, domanassaṭṭhānīyaṃ rūpaṃ upavicarati, upekkhāṭṭhānīyaṃ rūpaṃ upavicarati. Sotena saddaṃ sutvā…pe… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya somanassaṭṭhānīyaṃ dhammaṃ upavicarati, domanassaṭṭhānīyaṃ dhammaṃ upavicarati, upekkhāṭṭhānīyaṃ dhammaṃ upavicarati. Iti cha somanassūpavicārā, cha domanassūpavicārā, cha upekkhūpavicārā, aṭṭhārasa manopavicārā veditabbā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"'The eighteen mental excursions are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? 'Having seen a form with the eye, one makes an excursion about a form that is a basis for joy, makes an excursion about a form that is a basis for sorrow, makes an excursion about a form that is a basis for equanimity. Having heard a sound with the ear… having smelled an odor with the nose… having tasted a flavor with the tongue… having touched a tactile object with the body… having cognized a mental phenomenon with the mind, one makes an excursion about a phenomenon that is a basis for joy, makes an excursion about a phenomenon that is a basis for sorrow, makes an excursion about a phenomenon that is a basis for equanimity. Thus there are six excursions of joy, six excursions of sorrow, and six excursions of equanimity. These are the eighteen mental excursions that are to be known.' It was with reference to this that this was said."

306. ‘‘‘Chattiṃsa sattapadā veditabbā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Cha gehasitāni [gehassitāni (?)] somanassāni, cha nekkhammasitāni [nekkhammassitāni (ṭīkā)] somanassāni, cha gehasitāni domanassāni, cha nekkhammasitāni domanassāni, cha gehasitā upekkhā, cha nekkhammasitā upekkhā. Tattha katamāni cha gehasitāni somanassāni? Cakkhuviññeyyānaṃ rūpānaṃ iṭṭhānaṃ kantānaṃ manāpānaṃ manoramānaṃ lokāmisapaṭisaṃyuttānaṃ paṭilābhaṃ vā paṭilābhato samanupassato pubbe vā paṭiladdhapubbaṃ atītaṃ niruddhaṃ vipariṇataṃ samanussarato uppajjati somanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ somanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati gehasitaṃ somanassaṃ. Sotaviññeyyānaṃ saddānaṃ… ghānaviññeyyānaṃ gandhānaṃ… jivhāviññeyyānaṃ rasānaṃ… kāyaviññeyyānaṃ phoṭṭhabbānaṃ… manoviññeyyānaṃ dhammānaṃ iṭṭhānaṃ kantānaṃ manāpānaṃ…pe… somanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ somanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati gehasitaṃ somanassaṃ. Imāni cha gehasitāni somanassāni.

306."'The thirty-six states of sentient beings are to be known'—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? There are six household-based joys, six renunciation-based joys, six household-based sorrows, six renunciation-based sorrows, six household-based equanimities, and six renunciation-based equanimities. Herein, what are the six household-based joys? Joy arises when one sees the acquisition of forms cognizable by the eye—desirable, agreeable, pleasing, charming, connected with worldly things—or when one recollects something previously acquired that is past, ceased, and changed. Such joy is called household-based joy. Joy arises when one sees the acquisition of sounds cognizable by the ear… smells cognizable by the nose… tastes cognizable by the tongue… tactile objects cognizable by the body… mental phenomena cognizable by the mind—desirable, agreeable, pleasing, charming, connected with worldly things—or when one recollects something previously acquired that is past, ceased, and changed. Such joy is called household-based joy. These are the six household-based joys."

‘‘Tattha katamāni cha nekkhammasitāni somanassāni? Rūpānaṃtveva aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ [vipariṇāmaṃ virāgaṃ nirodhaṃ (ka.)], ‘pubbe ceva rūpā etarahi ca sabbe te rūpā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato uppajjati somanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ somanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati nekkhammasitaṃ somanassaṃ. Saddānaṃtveva… gandhānaṃtveva… rasānaṃtveva… phoṭṭhabbānaṃtveva… dhammānaṃtve aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ, ‘pubbe ceva dhammā etarahi ca sabbe te dhammā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato uppajjati somanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ somanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati nekkhammasitaṃ somanassaṃ. Imāni cha nekkhammasitāni somanassāni.

Herein, what are the six renunciation-based joys? Knowing the impermanence of forms—their change, fading away, and cessation—when one sees with right wisdom as it truly is that ‘both past and present, all these forms are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ joy arises. Such joy is called renunciation-based joy. Knowing the impermanence of sounds… Knowing the impermanence of smells… Knowing the impermanence of tastes… Knowing the impermanence of tactile objects… Knowing the impermanence of mental phenomena—their change, fading away, and cessation—when one sees with right wisdom as it truly is that ‘both past and present, all these mental phenomena are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ joy arises. Such joy is called renunciation-based joy. These are the six renunciation-based joys."

307. ‘‘Tattha katamāni cha gehasitāni domanassāni? Cakkhuviññeyyānaṃ rūpānaṃ…pe… sotaviññeyyānaṃ saddānaṃ… ghānaviññeyyānaṃ gandhānaṃ… jivhāviññeyyānaṃ rasānaṃ… kāyaviññeyyānaṃ phoṭṭhabbānaṃ… manoviññeyyānaṃ dhammānaṃ iṭṭhānaṃ kantānaṃ manāpānaṃ manoramānaṃ lokāmisapaṭisaṃyuttānaṃ appaṭilābhaṃ vā appaṭilābhato samanupassato pubbe vā appaṭiladdhapubbaṃ atītaṃ niruddhaṃ vipariṇataṃ samanussarato uppajjati domanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ domanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati gehasitaṃ domanassaṃ. Imāni cha gehasitāni domanassāni.

307.Herein, what are the six household-based sorrows? Sorrow arises when one sees the non-acquisition of forms cognizable by the eye—desirable, agreeable, pleasing, charming, connected with worldly things—or when one recollects something previously not acquired that is past, ceased, and changed. Such sorrow is called household-based sorrow. Sorrow arises when one sees the non-acquisition of sounds cognizable by the ear… smells cognizable by the nose… tastes cognizable by the tongue… tactile objects cognizable by the body… mental phenomena cognizable by the mind—desirable, agreeable, pleasing, charming, connected with worldly things—or when one recollects something previously not acquired that is past, ceased, and changed. Such sorrow is called household-based sorrow. These are the six household-based sorrows."

‘‘Tattha katamāni cha nekkhammasitāni domanassāni? Rūpānaṃtveva aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ, ‘pubbe ceva rūpā etarahi ca sabbe te rūpā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā anuttaresu vimokkhesu pihaṃ upaṭṭhāpeti – ‘kudāssu [kadāssu (syā. kaṃ. pī.)] nāmāhaṃ tadāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharissāmi yadariyā etarahi āyatanaṃ upasampajja viharantī’ti iti anuttaresu vimokkhesu pihaṃ upaṭṭhāpayato uppajjati pihapaccayā domanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ domanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati nekkhammasitaṃ domanassaṃ. Saddānaṃtveva…pe… gandhānaṃtveva… rasānaṃtveva… phoṭṭhabbānaṃtveva… dhammānaṃtveva aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ, ‘pubbe ceva dhammā etarahi ca sabbe te dhammā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā anuttaresu vimokkhesu pihaṃ upaṭṭhāpeti – ‘kudāssu nāmāhaṃ tadāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharissāmi yadariyā etarahi āyatanaṃ upasampajja viharantī’ti iti anuttaresu vimokkhesu pihaṃ upaṭṭhāpayato uppajjati pihapaccayā domanassaṃ. Yaṃ evarūpaṃ domanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati nekkhammasitaṃ domanassaṃ. Imāni cha nekkhammasitāni domanassāni.

Herein, what are the six renunciation-based sorrows? Having understood the impermanence of forms—their change, fading away, and cessation—seeing with right wisdom as it truly is: ‘Both in the past and now, all those forms are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ one arouses longing for the unsurpassed liberations: ‘When shall I enter and dwell in that state which the noble ones now enter and dwell in?’ Thus, when one arouses longing for the unsurpassed liberations, sorrow arises conditioned by longing. Such sorrow is called renunciation-based sorrow. Having understood the impermanence of sounds… smells… tastes… tactile objects… mental phenomena—their change, fading away, and cessation—seeing with right wisdom as it truly is: ‘Both in the past and now, all those mental phenomena are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ one arouses longing for the unsurpassed liberations: ‘When shall I enter and dwell in that state which the noble ones now enter and dwell in?’ Thus, when one arouses longing for the unsurpassed liberations, sorrow arises conditioned by longing. Such sorrow is called renunciation-based sorrow. These are the six renunciation-based sorrows."

308. ‘‘Tattha katamā cha gehasitā upekkhā? Cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā uppajjati upekkhā bālassa mūḷhassa ( ) [(mandassa) (ka.)] puthujjanassa anodhijinassa avipākajinassa anādīnavadassāvino assutavato puthujjanassa. Yā evarūpā upekkhā, rūpaṃ sā nātivattati. Tasmā sā [sāyaṃ (ka.)] upekkhā ‘gehasitā’ti vuccati. Sotena saddaṃ sutvā… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya uppajjati upekkhā bālassa mūḷhassa puthujjanassa anodhijinassa avipākajinassa anādīnavadassāvino assutavato puthujjanassa. Yā evarūpā upekkhā, dhammaṃ sā nātivattati. Tasmā sā upekkhā ‘gehasitā’ti vuccati. Imā cha gehasitā upekkhā.

308."And what, bhikkhus, are the six kinds of equanimity rooted in the household life? Having seen a form with the eye, equanimity arises in a foolish, deluded, uninstructed ordinary person, one who has not overcome defilements, not overcome future results, and does not see the danger. Such equanimity does not transcend form. Therefore, that equanimity is called ‘rooted in the household life.’ Having heard a sound with the ear… Having smelled an odour with the nose… Having tasted a flavour with the tongue… Having touched a tangible with the body… Having cognized a mental phenomenon with the mind, equanimity arises in a foolish, deluded, uninstructed ordinary person, one who has not overcome defilements, not overcome future results, and does not see the danger. Such equanimity does not transcend mental phenomena. Therefore, that equanimity is called ‘rooted in the household life.’ These are the six kinds of equanimity rooted in the household life."

‘‘Tattha katamā cha nekkhammasitā upekkhā? Rūpānaṃtveva aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ, ‘pubbe ceva rūpā etarahi ca sabbe te rūpā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato uppajjati upekkhā. Yā evarūpā upekkhā, rūpaṃ sā ativattati. Tasmā sā upekkhā ‘nekkhammasitā’ti vuccati. Saddānaṃtveva… gandhānaṃtveva… rasānaṃtveva… phoṭṭhabbānaṃtveva… dhammānaṃtveva aniccataṃ viditvā vipariṇāmavirāganirodhaṃ, ‘pubbe ceva dhammā etarahi ca sabbe te dhammā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya passato uppajjati upekkhā. Yā evarūpā upekkhā, dhammaṃ sā ativattati. Tasmā sā upekkhā ‘nekkhammasitā’ti vuccati. Imā cha nekkhammasitā upekkhā. ‘Chattiṃsa sattapadā veditabbā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"And what, bhikkhus, are the six kinds of equanimity rooted in renunciation? Having understood the impermanence of forms, their change, dispassion, and cessation, and seeing with proper wisdom as it really is that ‘Both formerly and now, all those forms are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ equanimity arises. Such equanimity transcends form. Therefore, that equanimity is called ‘rooted in renunciation.’ Having understood the impermanence of sounds… Having understood the impermanence of odours… Having understood the impermanence of tastes… Having understood the impermanence of tactile objects… Having understood the impermanence of mental phenomena, their change, dispassion, and cessation, and seeing with proper wisdom as it really is that ‘Both formerly and now, all those phenomena are impermanent, suffering, and subject to change,’ equanimity arises. Such equanimity transcends mental phenomena. Therefore, that equanimity is called ‘rooted in renunciation.’ These are the six kinds of equanimity rooted in renunciation. ‘Thirty-six bases of beings should be understood’—that was said in reference to this."

309. ‘‘Tatra idaṃ nissāya idaṃ pajahathā’’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ; kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Tatra, bhikkhave, yāni cha nekkhammasitāni somanassāni tāni nissāya tāni āgamma yāni cha gehasitāni somanassāni tāni pajahatha, tāni samatikkamatha. Evametesaṃ pahānaṃ hoti, evametesaṃ samatikkamo hoti.

309."‘Relying on this, abandon that’—so it was said. In reference to what was this said? Here, bhikkhus, relying on those six kinds of joy rooted in renunciation, and taking them as a basis, abandon and transcend those six kinds of joy rooted in the household life. Thus is their abandoning, thus is their transcending."

‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yāni cha nekkhammasitāni domanassāni tāni nissāya tāni āgamma yāni cha gehasitāni domanassāni tāni pajahatha, tāni samatikkamatha. Evametesaṃ pahānaṃ hoti, evametesaṃ samatikkamo hoti.

"Bhikkhus, relying on those six kinds of sorrow rooted in renunciation, and taking them as a basis, abandon and transcend those six kinds of sorrow rooted in the household life. Thus is their abandoning, thus is their transcending."

‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yā cha nekkhammasitā upekkhā tā nissāya tā āgamma yā cha gehasitā upekkhā tā pajahatha, tā samatikkamatha. Evametāsaṃ pahānaṃ hoti, evametāsaṃ samatikkamo hoti.

"Bhikkhus, relying on those six kinds of equanimity rooted in renunciation, and taking them as a basis, abandon and transcend those six kinds of equanimity rooted in the household life. Thus is their abandoning, thus is their transcending."

‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yāni cha nekkhammasitāni somanassāni tāni nissāya tāni āgamma yāni cha nekkhammasitāni domanassāni tāni pajahatha, tāni samatikkamatha. Evametesaṃ pahānaṃ hoti, evametesaṃ samatikkamo hoti.

"Bhikkhus, relying on those six kinds of joy rooted in renunciation, and taking them as a basis, abandon and transcend those six kinds of sorrow rooted in renunciation. Thus is their abandoning, thus is their transcending."

‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yā cha nekkhammasitā upekkhā tā nissāya tā āgamma yāni cha nekkhammasitāni somanassāni tāni pajahatha, tāni samatikkamatha. Evametesaṃ pahānaṃ hoti, evametesaṃ samatikkamo hoti.

"Bhikkhus, relying on those six kinds of equanimity rooted in renunciation, and taking them as a basis, abandon and transcend those six kinds of joy rooted in renunciation. Thus is their abandoning, thus is their transcending."

310. ‘‘Atthi, bhikkhave, upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā, atthi upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā. Katamā ca, bhikkhave, upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā? Atthi, bhikkhave, upekkhā rūpesu, atthi saddesu, atthi gandhesu, atthi rasesu, atthi phoṭṭhabbesu – ayaṃ, bhikkhave, upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā. Katamā ca, bhikkhave, upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā? Atthi, bhikkhave, upekkhā ākāsānañcāyatananissitā, atthi viññāṇañcāyatananissitā, atthi ākiñcaññāyatananissitā, atthi nevasaññānāsaññāyatananissitā – ayaṃ, bhikkhave, upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā.

310."Bhikkhus, there is equanimity that is diverse and rooted in diversity, and there is equanimity that is unified and rooted in unity. And what, bhikkhus, is equanimity that is diverse and rooted in diversity? There is equanimity regarding forms, there is equanimity regarding sounds, there is equanimity regarding odours, there is equanimity regarding tastes, there is equanimity regarding tactile objects—this, bhikkhus, is equanimity that is diverse and rooted in diversity. And what, bhikkhus, is equanimity that is unified and rooted in unity? There is equanimity dependent on the base of infinite space, there is equanimity dependent on the base of infinite consciousness, there is equanimity dependent on the base of nothingness, there is equanimity dependent on the base of neither perception nor non-perception—this, bhikkhus, is equanimity that is unified and rooted in unity."

‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yāyaṃ upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā taṃ nissāya taṃ āgamma yāyaṃ upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā taṃ pajahatha, taṃ samatikkamatha. Evametissā pahānaṃ hoti, evametissā samatikkamo hoti.

"Bhikkhus, relying on that equanimity which is unified and rooted in unity, and taking it as a basis, abandon and transcend that equanimity which is diverse and rooted in diversity. Thus is its abandoning, thus is its transcending."

‘‘Atammayataṃ, bhikkhave, nissāya atammayataṃ āgamma yāyaṃ upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā taṃ pajahatha, taṃ samatikkamatha. Evametissā pahānaṃ hoti, evametissā samatikkamo hoti. ‘Tatra idaṃ nissāya idaṃ pajahathā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"Bhikkhus, relying on non-identification and taking it as a basis, abandon and transcend that equanimity which is unified and rooted in unity. Thus is its abandoning, thus is its transcending. ‘Relying on this, abandon that’—that was said in reference to this."

311. ‘‘‘Tayo satipaṭṭhānā yadariyo sevati, yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahatī’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ; kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, satthā sāvakānaṃ dhammaṃ deseti anukampako hitesī anukampaṃ upādāya – ‘idaṃ vo hitāya, idaṃ vo sukhāyā’ti. Tassa sāvakā na sussūsanti, na sotaṃ odahanti, na aññā cittaṃ upaṭṭhapenti, vokkamma ca satthusāsanā vattanti. Tatra, bhikkhave, tathāgato na ceva anattamano hoti, na ca anattamanataṃ paṭisaṃvedeti, anavassuto ca viharati sato sampajāno. Idaṃ, bhikkhave, paṭhamaṃ satipaṭṭhānaṃ yadariyo sevati, yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahati.

311."‘The three establishments of mindfulness that the Noble One frequents, frequenting which the Teacher is fit to instruct the assembly’—so it was said. In reference to what was this said? Here, bhikkhus, the Teacher teaches the Dhamma to the disciples out of compassion, desiring their welfare: ‘This is for your welfare, this is for your happiness.’ But his disciples do not give ear, do not lend an ear, do not apply their minds to understanding, and they turn aside from the Teacher’s instruction. In this case, bhikkhus, the Tathāgata neither becomes discontented nor experiences discontent; and he dwells unaffected, mindful and clearly comprehending. This, bhikkhus, is the first establishment of mindfulness that the Noble One frequents, frequenting which the Teacher is fit to instruct the assembly."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, bhikkhave, satthā sāvakānaṃ dhammaṃ deseti anukampako hitesī anukampaṃ upādāya – ‘idaṃ vo hitāya, idaṃ vo sukhāyā’ti. Tassa ekacce sāvakā na sussūsanti, na sotaṃ odahanti, na aññā cittaṃ upaṭṭhapenti, vokkamma ca satthusāsanā vattanti; ekacce sāvakā sussūsanti, sotaṃ odahanti, aññā cittaṃ upaṭṭhapenti, na ca vokkamma satthusāsanā vattanti. Tatra, bhikkhave, tathāgato na ceva anattamano hoti, na ca anattamanataṃ paṭisaṃvedeti; na ca attamano hoti, na ca attamanataṃ paṭisaṃvedeti. Anattamanatā ca attamanatā ca – tadubhayaṃ abhinivajjetvā upekkhako viharati sato sampajāno. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, dutiyaṃ satipaṭṭhānaṃ yadariyo sevati, yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahati.

"Furthermore, bhikkhus, the Teacher teaches the Dhamma to the disciples out of compassion, desiring their welfare: ‘This is for your welfare, this is for your happiness.’ Some of his disciples do not give ear, do not lend an ear, do not apply their minds to understanding, and they turn aside from the Teacher’s instruction. But some disciples give ear, lend an ear, apply their minds to understanding, and do not turn aside from the Teacher’s instruction. In that case, bhikkhus, the Tathāgata is neither discontented nor does he experience discontent; nor is he contented nor does he experience content. Having abandoned both discontent and content, he dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending. This, bhikkhus, is called the second establishment of mindfulness which the Noble One frequents, frequenting which the Teacher is fit to instruct the assembly."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, bhikkhave, satthā sāvakānaṃ dhammaṃ deseti anukampako hitesī anukampaṃ upādāya – ‘idaṃ vo hitāya, idaṃ vo sukhāyā’ti. Tassa sāvakā sussūsanti, sotaṃ odahanti, aññācittaṃ upaṭṭhapenti, na ca vokkamma satthusāsanā vattanti. Tatra, bhikkhave, tathāgato attamano ceva hoti, attamanatañca paṭisaṃvedeti, anavassuto ca viharati sato sampajāno. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, tatiyaṃ satipaṭṭhānaṃ yadariyo sevati, yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahati. ‘Tayo satipaṭṭhānā yadariyo sevati, yadariyo sevamāno satthā gaṇamanusāsitumarahatī’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

"Furthermore, bhikkhus, the Teacher teaches the Dhamma to the disciples out of compassion, desiring their welfare: ‘This is for your welfare, this is for your happiness.’ His disciples give ear, lend an ear, apply their minds to understanding, and do not turn aside from the Teacher’s instruction. In this case, bhikkhus, the Tathāgata is both contented and experiences content, yet he dwells unaffected, mindful and clearly comprehending. This, bhikkhus, is called the third establishment of mindfulness that the Noble One frequents, frequenting which the Teacher is fit to instruct the assembly. ‘Three establishments of mindfulness that the Noble One frequents, frequenting which the Noble One is fit to instruct the assembly’—that was said in reference to this."

312. ‘‘‘So vuccati yoggācariyānaṃ anuttaro purisadammasārathī’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Hatthidamakena, bhikkhave, hatthidammo sārito ekaṃyeva disaṃ dhāvati – puratthimaṃ vā pacchimaṃ vā uttaraṃ vā dakkhiṇaṃ vā. Assadamakena, bhikkhave, assadammo sārito ekaññeva disaṃ dhāvati – puratthimaṃ vā pacchimaṃ vā uttaraṃ vā dakkhiṇaṃ vā. Godamakena, bhikkhave, godammo sārito ekaṃyeva disaṃ dhāvati – puratthimaṃ vā pacchimaṃ vā uttaraṃ vā dakkhiṇaṃ vā. Tathāgatena hi, bhikkhave, arahatā sammāsambuddhena purisadammo sārito aṭṭha disā vidhāvati. Rūpī rūpāni passati – ayaṃ ekā disā; ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī bahiddhā rūpāni passati – ayaṃ dutiyā disā; subhantveva adhimutto hoti – ayaṃ tatiyā disā; sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati – ayaṃ catutthī disā; sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati – ayaṃ pañcamī disā; sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati – ayaṃ chaṭṭhī disā; sabbaso ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati – ayaṃ sattamī disā; sabbaso nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ upasampajja viharati – ayaṃ aṭṭhamī disā. Tathāgatena, bhikkhave, arahatā sammāsambuddhena purisadammo sārito imā aṭṭha disā vidhāvati. ‘So vuccati yoggācariyānaṃ anuttaro purisadammasārathī’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vutta’’nti.

312.“‘He is called the unsurpassed trainer of persons, the charioteer of persons to be tamed’—so it was said. And with reference to what was this said? bhikkhus, when an elephant-tamer has tamed an elephant, it runs in only one direction—east, west, north, or south. When a horse-tamer has tamed a horse, it runs in only one direction—east, west, north, or south. When a cattle-tamer has tamed an ox, it runs in only one direction—east, west, north, or south. But when the Tathāgata, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One, has tamed a person, that person runs in eight directions. Having form, one sees forms—this is the first direction. Not perceiving forms internally, one sees external forms—this is the second direction. One is resolved upon the beautiful—this is the third direction. With the complete overcoming of perceptions of form, with the disappearance of perceptions of resistance, not attending to perceptions of diversity, one enters and dwells in the base of infinite space, thinking: ‘Infinite is space’—this is the fourth direction. With the complete overcoming of the base of infinite space, one enters and dwells in the base of infinite consciousness, thinking: ‘Infinite is consciousness’—this is the fifth direction. With the complete overcoming of the base of infinite consciousness, one enters and dwells in the base of nothingness, thinking: ‘There is nothing’—this is the sixth direction. With the complete overcoming of the base of nothingness, one enters and dwells in the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception—this is the seventh direction. With the complete overcoming of the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, one enters and dwells in the cessation of perception and feeling—this is the eighth direction. bhikkhus, when the Tathāgata, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One, has tamed a person, that person runs in these eight directions. ‘He is called the unsurpassed trainer of persons, the charioteer of persons to be tamed’—it was in reference to this that it was said.”

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

The Blessed One said this. The bhikkhus were delighted and rejoiced in the Blessed One’s words.

Saḷāyatanavibhaṅgasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ sattamaṃ.

The seventh discourse, the Saḷāyatanavibhaṅga Sutta, is concluded.
