# 137. Araṇavibhaṅgasuttaṃ

## 137. The Analysis of Non-contention

323. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tatra kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi ‘‘bhikkhavo’’ti. ‘‘Bhadante’’ti te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘araṇavibhaṅgaṃ vo, bhikkhave, desessāmi. Taṃ suṇātha, sādhukaṃ manasi karotha; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca –

323.Thus have I heard: At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. There the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus: “Bhikkhus!” “Venerable sir!” those bhikkhus replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this: “Bhikkhus, I will teach you the analysis of non-contention. Listen carefully, attend closely; I shall speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” those bhikkhus replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said:

‘‘Na kāmasukhamanuyuñjeyya hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, na ca attakilamathānuyogamanuyuñjeyya dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ. Ete kho, bhikkhave [ete kho (sī.), ete te (syā. kaṃ. pī.)], ubho ante anupagamma majjhimā paṭipadā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā, cakkhukaraṇī ñāṇakaraṇī upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattati. Ussādanañca jaññā, apasādanañca jaññā; ussādanañca ñatvā apasādanañca ñatvā nevussādeyya, na apasādeyya [nāpasādeyya (sī.)], dhammameva deseyya. Sukhavinicchayaṃ jaññā; sukhavinicchayaṃ ñatvā ajjhattaṃ sukhamanuyuñjeyya. Rahovādaṃ na bhāseyya, sammukhā na khīṇaṃ [nātikhīṇaṃ (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] bhaṇe. Ataramānova bhāseyya, no taramāno. Janapadaniruttiṃ nābhiniveseyya, samaññaṃ nātidhāveyyāti – ayamuddeso araṇavibhaṅgassa.

One should not pursue sensual happiness, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial; nor should one pursue self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial. Avoiding both these extremes, bhikkhus, the Middle Way has been awakened to by the Tathāgata; it produces vision, produces knowledge, and leads to peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, and Nibbāna. One should know what elation is, and one should know what dejection is. Knowing elation and knowing dejection, one should neither elate nor deject, but should teach the Dhamma alone. One should know the determination of happiness. Knowing the determination of happiness, one should pursue happiness internally. One should not speak behind one’s back, nor speak defiled speech in public. One should speak unhurriedly, not hurriedly. One should not insist on local language, nor overstep conventional usage.” This is the summary of the Analysis of Non-contention.

324. ‘‘‘Na kāmasukhamanuyuñjeyya hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, na ca attakilamathānuyogamanuyuñjeyya dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhita’nti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ; kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Yo kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogo hīno gammo pothujjaniko anariyo anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Yo kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ ananuyogo hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Yo attakilamathānuyogo dukkho anariyo anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Yo attakilamathānuyogaṃ ananuyogo dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. ‘Na kāmasukhamanuyuñjeyya hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, na ca attakilamathānuyogaṃ anuyuñjeyya dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhita’nti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

324.‘One should not pursue sensual happiness, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial; nor should one pursue self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial.’ This has been said. And with reference to what was it said? The pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial, is a state of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; it is wrong practice. The non-pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial, is a state free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; it is right practice. The pursuit of self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial, is a state of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; it is wrong practice. The non-pursuit of self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial, is a state free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; it is right practice. ‘One should not pursue sensual happiness, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial; nor should one pursue self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial.’ That is what was said, and it was said with reference to this.”

325. ‘‘‘Ete kho ubho ante anupagamma majjhimā paṭipadā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā, cakkhukaraṇī ñāṇakaraṇī upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattatī’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Ayameva ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo, seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi, sammāsaṅkappo, sammāvācā, sammākammanto, sammāājīvo, sammāvāyāmo, sammāsati, sammāsamādhi. ‘Ete kho ubho ante anupagamma majjhimā paṭipadā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā, cakkhukaraṇī ñāṇakaraṇī upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattatī’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

325.‘Avoiding these two extremes, the Tathāgata has awakened to the Middle Way, which produces vision, produces knowledge, and leads to peace, to direct knowledge, to enlightenment, to Nibbāna.’ Thus it was said. And with reference to what was it said? Precisely this ![[Noble Path]]. ‘Avoiding these two extremes, the Tathāgata has awakened to the Middle Way, which produces vision, which produces knowledge, and leads to peace, to direct knowledge, to enlightenment, to Nibbāna.’ That is what was said, and it was said with reference to this.”

326. ‘‘‘Ussādanañca jaññā, apasādanañca jaññā; ussādanañca ñatvā apasādanañca ñatvā nevussādeyya, na apasādeyya, dhammameva deseyyā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Kathañca, bhikkhave, ussādanā ca hoti apasādanā ca, no ca dhammadesanā? ‘Ye kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ anuyuttā hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ [iti paraṃ (ka.)] ittheke apasādeti.

326.‘One should know what elation is, and one should know what dejection is. Knowing elation and knowing dejection, one should neither elate nor deject, but should teach the Dhamma alone.’ This has been said. And with reference to what was it said? And how, bhikkhus, is there both elation and dejection, but no teaching of the Dhamma? Saying, ‘All those who are devoted to the pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures—low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial—are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly,’ one disparages some.

‘‘‘Ye kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ ananuyuttā hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ ittheke ussādeti.

Saying, ‘All those who are not devoted to the pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial, are states free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; they are practicing rightly,’ one elates some.

‘‘‘Ye attakilamathānuyogaṃ anuyuttā dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ ittheke apasādeti.

Saying, ‘All those who are devoted to self-mortification—painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial—are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly,’ one disparages some.

‘‘‘Ye attakilamathānuyogaṃ ananuyuttā dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ ittheke ussādeti.

Saying, ‘All those who are not devoted to self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial, are states free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; they are practicing rightly,’ one elates some.

‘‘‘Yesaṃ kesañci bhavasaṃyojanaṃ appahīnaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ ittheke apasādeti.

Saying, ‘Those in whom any fetter of becoming is not abandoned are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly,’ one disparages some.

‘‘‘Yesaṃ kesañci bhavasaṃyojanaṃ pahīnaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – iti vadaṃ ittheke ussādeti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, ussādanā ca hoti apasādanā ca, no ca dhammadesanā.

Saying, ‘Those in whom any fetter of becoming is abandoned are states free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; they are practicing rightly,’ one elates some. Thus, bhikkhus, there is both elation and dejection, but no teaching of the Dhamma.

327. ‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, nevussādanā hoti na apasādanā, dhammadesanā ca [dhammadesanāva (syā. kaṃ.)]? ‘Ye kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ anuyuttā hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Anuyogo ca kho, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti.

327.And how, bhikkhus, is there neither elation nor dejection, but the teaching of the Dhamma? One does not say: ‘All those who are devoted to the pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures—low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial—are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly.’ Instead, one says: ‘This devotion itself is a state of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; it is wrong practice.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma alone.

‘‘‘Ye kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ ananuyuttā hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Ananuyogo ca kho, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti.

One does not say: ‘All those who are not devoted to the pursuit of mental pleasure by one who delights in sensual pleasures, which is low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial, are states free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; they are practicing rightly.’ Instead, one says: ‘This non-devotion itself is a state free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; it is right practice.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma alone.

‘‘‘Ye attakilamathānuyogaṃ anuyuttā dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Anuyogo ca kho, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti.

One does not say: ‘All those who are devoted to self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial, are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly.’ Instead, one says: ‘This devotion itself is a state of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; it is wrong practice.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma alone.

‘‘‘Ye attakilamathānuyogaṃ ananuyuttā dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Ananuyogo ca kho, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti.

One does not say: ‘All those who are not devoted to self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial, are states free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; they are practicing rightly.’ Instead, one says: ‘This non-devotion itself is a state free from suffering, free from affliction, free from distress, free from fever; it is right practice.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma alone.

‘‘‘Yesaṃ kesañci bhavasaṃyojanaṃ appahīnaṃ, sabbe te sadukkhā saupaghātā saupāyāsā sapariḷāhā micchāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Bhavasaṃyojane ca kho appahīne bhavopi appahīno hotī’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti.

One does not say: ‘Those in whom any fetter of becoming is not abandoned are states of suffering, with affliction, with distress, with fever; they are practicing wrongly.’ Instead, one says: ‘When the fetter of becoming is not abandoned, becoming too is not abandoned.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma alone.

‘‘‘Yesaṃ kesañci bhavasaṃyojanaṃ pahīnaṃ, sabbe te adukkhā anupaghātā anupāyāsā apariḷāhā sammāpaṭipannā’ti – na evamāha. ‘Bhavasaṃyojane ca kho pahīne bhavopi pahīno hotī’ti – iti vadaṃ dhammameva deseti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, nevussādanā hoti na apasādanā, dhammadesanā ca. ‘Ussādanañca jaññā, apasādanañca jaññā; ussādanañca ñatvā apasādanañca ñatvā nevussādeyya, na apasādeyya, dhammameva deseyyā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

‘Those in whom the fetter of existence is abandoned are all free from suffering, without affliction, without distress, without fever; they are rightly practiced’—one does not say this. Rather, one says: ‘When the fetter of existence is abandoned, existence too is abandoned.’ Speaking thus, one teaches the Dhamma. Thus, bhikkhus, there is neither exalting nor disparaging, but a teaching of the Dhamma. ‘One should know what is exalting and what is disparaging; having known what is exalting and what is disparaging, one should neither exalt nor disparage, but teach the Dhamma.’ That is what was said with reference to this.

328. ‘‘‘Sukhavinicchayaṃ jaññā; sukhavinicchayaṃ ñatvā ajjhattaṃ sukhamanuyuñjeyyā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Pañcime, bhikkhave, kāmaguṇā. Katame pañca? Cakkhuviññeyyā rūpā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā, sotaviññeyyā saddā… ghānaviññeyyā gandhā… jivhāviññeyyā rasā… kāyaviññeyyā phoṭṭhabbā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā – ime kho, bhikkhave, pañca kāmaguṇā. Yaṃ kho, bhikkhave, ime pañca kāmaguṇe paṭicca uppajjati sukhaṃ somanassaṃ idaṃ vuccati kāmasukhaṃ mīḷhasukhaṃ puthujjanasukhaṃ anariyasukhaṃ. ‘Na āsevitabbaṃ, na bhāvetabbaṃ, na bahulīkātabbaṃ, bhāyitabbaṃ etassa sukhassā’ti – vadāmi. Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā ajjhattaṃ sampasādanaṃ cetaso ekodibhāvaṃ avitakkaṃ avicāraṃ samādhijaṃ pītisukhaṃ dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Pītiyā ca virāgā upekkhako ca viharati…pe… tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ… catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Idaṃ vuccati nekkhammasukhaṃ pavivekasukhaṃ upasamasukhaṃ sambodhisukhaṃ. ‘Āsevitabbaṃ, bhāvetabbaṃ, bahulīkātabbaṃ, na bhāyitabbaṃ etassa sukhassā’ti – vadāmi. ‘Sukhavinicchayaṃ jaññā; sukhavinicchayaṃ ñatvā ajjhattaṃ sukhamanuyuñjeyyā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

328.‘One should know the discernment of pleasure; having known the discernment of pleasure, one should devote oneself to inner happiness’—so it has been said. With reference to what was this said? There are, bhikkhus, these five strands of sensual pleasure. What are the five? Forms cognizable by the eye that are desirable, agreeable, charming, pleasing, associated with sensual desire, and enticing; sounds cognizable by the ear… smells cognizable by the nose… tastes cognizable by the tongue… touches cognizable by the body that are desirable, agreeable, charming, pleasing, associated with sensual desire, and enticing. These, bhikkhus, are the five strands of sensual pleasure. The pleasure and joy that arise, bhikkhus, dependent on these five strands of sensual pleasure—this is called sensual pleasure, the pleasure of filth, the pleasure of ordinary people, ignoble pleasure. ‘This pleasure is not to be pursued, not to be developed, not to be cultivated; one should be afraid of this pleasure’—so I say. Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome states, enters and dwells in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by thought and examination, born of seclusion, with rapture and happiness. With the subsiding of thought and examination, he enters and dwells in the second jhāna, which has internal confidence and unification of mind, is without thought and examination, born of samādhi, with rapture and happiness. With the fading away of rapture, he dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, and experiences happiness with the body; he enters and dwells in the third jhāna… he enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna. This is called the pleasure of renunciation, the pleasure of seclusion, the pleasure of peace, the pleasure of awakening. ‘This pleasure is to be pursued, is to be developed, is to be cultivated; one should not be afraid of this pleasure’—so I say. ‘One should know the discernment of pleasure; having known the discernment of pleasure, one should devote oneself to inner happiness’—that is what was said with reference to this.

329. ‘‘‘Rahovādaṃ na bhāseyya, sammukhā na khīṇaṃ bhaṇe’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Tatra, bhikkhave, yaṃ jaññā rahovādaṃ abhūtaṃ atacchaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ sasakkaṃ [sampattaṃ (ka.)] taṃ rahovādaṃ na bhāseyya. Yampi jaññā rahovādaṃ bhūtaṃ tacchaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ tassapi sikkheyya avacanāya. Yañca kho jaññā rahovādaṃ bhūtaṃ tacchaṃ atthasaṃhitaṃ tatra kālaññū assa tassa rahovādassa vacanāya. Tatra, bhikkhave, yaṃ jaññā sammukhā khīṇavādaṃ abhūtaṃ atacchaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ sasakkaṃ taṃ sammukhā khīṇavādaṃ na bhāseyya. Yampi jaññā sammukhā khīṇavādaṃ bhūtaṃ tacchaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ tassapi sikkheyya avacanāya. Yañca kho jaññā sammukhā khīṇavādaṃ bhūtaṃ tacchaṃ atthasaṃhitaṃ tatra kālaññū assa tassa sammukhā khīṇavādassa vacanāya. ‘Rahovādaṃ na bhāseyya, sammukhā na khīṇaṃ bhaṇe’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

329.‘One should not speak in private, nor speak disparagingly face to face’—so it has been said. With reference to what was this said? Here, bhikkhus, if one knows that private speech is untrue, incorrect, unbeneficial, and accompanied by force, one should not engage in such private speech. Even if one knows that private speech is true and factual but unbeneficial, one should train oneself not to speak it. But if one knows that private speech is true, factual, and beneficial, then one should be discerning of the right time to engage in that private speech. Similarly, bhikkhus, if one knows that disparaging speech face to face is untrue, incorrect, unbeneficial, and accompanied by force, one should not engage in such disparaging speech face to face. Even if one knows that disparaging speech face to face is true and factual but unbeneficial, one should train oneself not to speak it. But if one knows that disparaging speech face to face is true, factual, and beneficial, then one should be discerning of the right time to engage in that disparaging speech face to face. ‘One should not speak in private, nor speak disparagingly face to face’—that is what was said with reference to this.

330. ‘‘‘Ataramānova bhāseyya no taramāno’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Tatra, bhikkhave, taramānassa bhāsato kāyopi kilamati, cittampi upahaññati [ūhaññati (sī.)], saropi upahaññati [ūhaññati (sī.)], kaṇṭhopi āturīyati, avisaṭṭhampi hoti aviññeyyaṃ taramānassa bhāsitaṃ. Tatra, bhikkhave, ataramānassa bhāsato kāyopi na kilamati, cittampi na upahaññati, saropi na upahaññati, kaṇṭhopi na āturīyati, visaṭṭhampi hoti viññeyyaṃ ataramānassa bhāsitaṃ. ‘Ataramānova bhāseyya, no taramāno’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

330.‘One should speak unhurriedly, not hurriedly’—this has been said. With reference to what was this said? Here, bhikkhus, when one speaks hurriedly, the body becomes weary, the mind is impaired, the voice is impaired, the throat becomes afflicted, and the speech of one speaking hurriedly is indistinct and unintelligible. But when one speaks unhurriedly, the body does not become weary, the mind is not impaired, the voice is not impaired, the throat is not afflicted, and the speech of one speaking unhurriedly is distinct and intelligible. ‘One should speak unhurriedly, not hurriedly’—that is what was said with reference to this.

331. ‘‘‘Janapadaniruttiṃ nābhiniveseyya, samaññaṃ nātidhāveyyā’ti – iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ. Kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Kathañca, bhikkhave, janapadaniruttiyā ca abhiniveso hoti samaññāya ca atisāro? Idha, bhikkhave, tadevekaccesu janapadesu ‘pātī’ti sañjānanti, ‘patta’nti sañjānanti, ‘vitta’nti [viṭṭhanti (syā. kaṃ.)] sañjānanti, ‘sarāva’nti sañjānanti ‘dhāropa’nti [harosanti (syā. kaṃ.)] sañjānanti, ‘poṇa’nti sañjānanti, ‘pisīlava’nti [pisīlanti (sī. pī.), pipilanti (syā. kaṃ.)] sañjānanti. Iti yathā yathā naṃ tesu tesu janapadesu sañjānanti tathā tathā thāmasā parāmāsā [parāmassa (sī.)] abhinivissa voharati – ‘idameva saccaṃ, moghamañña’nti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, janapadaniruttiyā ca abhiniveso hoti samaññāya ca atisāro.

331.‘One should not adhere to local terminology, nor should one deviate from common usage’—this has been said. With reference to what was this said? And how, bhikkhus, does adherence to local terminology and deviation from common usage occur? Here, bhikkhus, in different regions, the same thing is designated as ‘pāti’, as ‘patta’, as ‘vitta’, as ‘sarāva’, as ‘dhāropa’, as ‘poṇa’, or as ‘pisīlava’. As it is designated in those various regions, one adheres to it firmly, grasping and insisting, ‘This alone is true; anything else is wrong.’ Thus, bhikkhus, there is adherence to local terminology and deviation from common usage.

332. ‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, janapadaniruttiyā ca anabhiniveso hoti samaññāya ca anatisāro? Idha, bhikkhave, tadevekaccesu janapadesu ‘pātī’ti sañjānanti, ‘patta’nti sañjānanti, ‘vitta’nti sañjānanti, ‘sarāva’nti sañjānanti, ‘dhāropa’nti sañjānanti, ‘poṇa’nti sañjānanti, ‘pisīlava’nti sañjānanti. Iti yathā yathā naṃ tesu tesu janapadesu sañjānanti ‘idaṃ kira me [idaṃ kira te ca (ka.)] āyasmanto sandhāya voharantī’ti tathā tathā voharati aparāmasaṃ. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, janapadaniruttiyā ca anabhiniveso hoti, samaññāya ca anatisāro. ‘Janapadaniruttiṃ nābhiniveseyya samaññaṃ nātidhāveyyā’ti – iti yaṃ taṃ vuttaṃ, idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

332.And how, bhikkhus, is there non-adherence to local terminology and non-deviation from common usage? Here, bhikkhus, in some regions they designate the same thing as ‘pāti’, as ‘patta’, as ‘vitta’, as ‘sarāva’, as ‘dhāropa’, as ‘poṇa’, or as ‘pisīlava’. In whatever way they designate it in those various regions, one uses it without adhering to it, thinking, ‘The venerable ones are speaking with this in mind.’ Thus, bhikkhus, there is non-adherence to local terminology and non-deviation from common usage. ‘One should not adhere to local terminology, nor should one deviate from common usage’—that is what was said, and it was with reference to this that it was said.

333. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yo kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogo hīno gammo pothujjaniko anariyo anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yo kāmapaṭisandhisukhino somanassānuyogaṃ ananuyogo hīnaṃ gammaṃ pothujjanikaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

333.Here, bhikkhus, the pursuit of pleasure and joy connected with sensual rebirth—low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial—is a state of suffering, affliction, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, this state is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, the non-pursuit of pleasure and joy connected with sensual rebirth—low, vulgar, worldly, ignoble, and unbeneficial—is a state free from suffering, affliction, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, this state is non-contentious.

334. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yo attakilamathānuyogo dukkho anariyo anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yo attakilamathānuyogaṃ ananuyogo dukkhaṃ anariyaṃ anatthasaṃhitaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

334.Here, bhikkhus, the pursuit of self-mortification is painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial. This state is one of suffering, affliction, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, this state is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, the non-pursuit of self-mortification is not painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial; this state is free from suffering, affliction, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, this state is non-contentious.

335. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yāyaṃ majjhimā paṭipadā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā, cakkhukaraṇī ñāṇakaraṇī upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattati, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

335.Here, bhikkhus, that Middle Way understood by the Tathāgata—which makes for vision, makes for knowledge, leads to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, to Nibbāna—is without suffering, without affliction, without distress, without fever. It is right practice. Therefore, this state is non-contentious.

336. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yāyaṃ ussādanā ca apasādanā ca no ca dhammadesanā, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yāyaṃ nevussādanā ca na apasādanā ca dhammadesanā ca, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

336.Here, bhikkhus, that which is both exalting and disparaging, without teaching the Dhamma, is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, that which is neither exalting nor disparaging, but teaching the Dhamma, is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

337. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yamidaṃ kāmasukhaṃ mīḷhasukhaṃ pothujjanasukhaṃ anariyasukhaṃ, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yamidaṃ nekkhammasukhaṃ pavivekasukhaṃ upasamasukhaṃ sambodhisukhaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

337.Here, bhikkhus, sensual pleasure—the pleasure of the flesh, vulgar, worldly, ignoble—is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, the pleasure of renunciation, seclusion, peace, and enlightenment is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

338. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ rahovādo abhūto ataccho anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ rahovādo bhūto taccho anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ rahovādo bhūto taccho atthasaṃhito, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

338.Here, bhikkhus, secret speech that is unreal, incorrect, and unbeneficial is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, secret speech that is real, correct, but unbeneficial is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, secret speech that is real, correct, and beneficial is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

339. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ sammukhā khīṇavādo abhūto ataccho anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ sammukhā khīṇavādo bhūto taccho anatthasaṃhito, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ sammukhā khīṇavādo bhūto taccho atthasaṃhito, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

339.Here, bhikkhus, face-to-face speech that is unreal, incorrect, and unbeneficial is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, face-to-face speech that is real, correct, but unbeneficial is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, face-to-face speech that is real, correct, and beneficial is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

340. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yamidaṃ taramānassa bhāsitaṃ, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra, bhikkhave, yamidaṃ ataramānassa bhāsitaṃ, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

340.Here, bhikkhus, speech uttered in haste is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, speech uttered without haste is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

341. ‘‘Tatra, bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ janapadaniruttiyā ca abhiniveso samaññāya ca atisāro, sadukkho eso dhammo saupaghāto saupāyāso sapariḷāho; micchāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo saraṇo. Tatra bhikkhave, yvāyaṃ janapadaniruttiyā ca anabhiniveso samaññāya ca anatisāro, adukkho eso dhammo anupaghāto anupāyāso apariḷāho; sammāpaṭipadā. Tasmā eso dhammo araṇo.

341.Here, bhikkhus, insistence on a regional language and overstepping conventional usage is fraught with suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is wrong practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is contentious. Here, bhikkhus, non-insistence on a regional language and not overstepping conventional usage is free from suffering, harm, distress, and fever; it is right practice. Therefore, that Dhamma is non-contentious.

‘‘Tasmātiha, bhikkhave, ‘saraṇañca dhammaṃ jānissāma, araṇañca dhammaṃ jānissāma; saraṇañca dhammaṃ ñatvā araṇañca dhammaṃ ñatvā araṇapaṭipadaṃ paṭipajjissāmā’ti evañhi vo, bhikkhave, sikkhitabbaṃ. Subhūti ca pana, bhikkhave, kulaputto araṇapaṭipadaṃ paṭipanno’’ti.

Therefore, bhikkhus, you should train yourselves thus: ‘We will know the contentious Dhamma and the non-contentious Dhamma. Having known the contentious Dhamma and the non-contentious Dhamma, we will practice the path of non-contention.’ For indeed, bhikkhus, the clansman Subhūti has entered upon the path of non-contention.

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

The Blessed One spoke this. The bhikkhus were delighted and rejoiced in the Blessed One’s words.

Araṇavibhaṅgasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ navamaṃ.

The Discourse on the Exposition of Non-Conflict, the ninth, is finished.
