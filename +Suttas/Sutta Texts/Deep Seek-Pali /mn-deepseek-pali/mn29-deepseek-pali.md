# 29. Mahāsāropamasuttaṃ

## 29. The Greater Discourse on the Simile of the Heartwood

307. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā rājagahe viharati gijjhakūṭe pabbate acirapakkante devadatte. Tatra kho bhagavā devadattaṃ ārabbha bhikkhū āmantesi –

307.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Rājagaha, on the Vulture Peak Mountain, not long after Devadatta had departed. There, concerning Devadatta, the Blessed One addressed the monks:

‘‘Idha, bhikkhave, ekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attānukkaṃseti paraṃ vambheti – ‘ahamasmi lābhasakkārasilokavā [lābhī silokavā (sī. pī.), lābhī sakkāra silokavā (syā.)], ime panaññe bhikkhū appaññātā appesakkhā’ti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena majjati pamajjati pamādaṃ āpajjati, pamatto samāno dukkhaṃ viharati.

"Here, monks, a certain clansman goes forth from the household life into homelessness out of faith, thinking: ‘I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair—overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps an end to this entire mass of suffering can be discerned.’ Having gone forth thus, he generates gain, honor, and fame. He is pleased with that gain, honor, and fame, and his intention is fulfilled. On account of that gain, honor, and fame, he exalts himself and disparages others: ‘I am one who possesses gain, honor, and fame, but these other monks are unknown and of little influence.’ Intoxicated and heedless due to that gain, honor, and fame, he falls into negligence. Being negligent, he dwells in suffering."

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ atikkamma papaṭikaṃ, sākhāpalāsaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkameyya ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Tamenaṃ cakkhumā puriso disvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘na vatāyaṃ bhavaṃ puriso aññāsi sāraṃ, na aññāsi phegguṃ, na aññāsi tacaṃ, na aññāsi papaṭikaṃ, na aññāsi sākhāpalāsaṃ. Tathā hayaṃ [tathāpāyaṃ (ka.)] bhavaṃ puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ atikkamma papaṭikaṃ, sākhāpalāsaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissatī’ti. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, idhekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attānukkaṃseti, paraṃ vambheti ‘ahamasmi lābhasakkārasilokavā, ime panaññe bhikkhū appaññātā appesakkhā’ti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena majjati pamajjati pamādaṃ āpajjati, pamatto samāno dukkhaṃ viharati. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sākhāpalāsaṃ aggahesi brahmacariyassa; tena ca vosānaṃ āpādi.

"Suppose, monks, a man in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes upon a great standing tree possessed of heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, passing over the outer bark, he cuts off the twigs and foliage and takes them away, thinking they are heartwood. A man with good eyesight, seeing him, would say: ‘This good man does not know heartwood, sapwood, inner bark, outer bark, or twigs and foliage. Thus, this good man, in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes upon a great standing tree possessed of heartwood. Yet, passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, passing over the outer bark, he cuts off the twigs and foliage and takes them away, thinking they are heartwood. Whatever he needs to accomplish with heartwood, that purpose he will not achieve.’ In the same way, monks, here a certain clansman goes forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness, thinking: ‘I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, and death; by sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair. I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps an end to this whole mass of suffering can be discerned.’ Having gone forth thus, he generates gain, honor, and renown. He is gratified by that gain, honor, and renown, and his intention is fulfilled. On account of that gain, honor, and renown, he exalts himself and disparages others: ‘I am one who has gain, honor, and renown, but these other monks are unknown and of little influence.’ Thus, intoxicated and heedless because of that gain, honor, and renown, he falls into heedlessness. Dwelling heedless, he lives in suffering. This, monks, is called a monk who has grasped the twigs and foliage of the holy life and reached its limit with that."

308. ‘‘Idha pana, bhikkhave, ekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati. Appamatto samāno sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya attānukkaṃseti, paraṃ vambheti – ‘ahamasmi sīlavā kalyāṇadhammo, ime panaññe bhikkhū dussīlā pāpadhammā’ti. So tāya sīlasampadāya majjati pamajjati pamādaṃ āpajjati, pamatto samāno dukkhaṃ viharati.

So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati. Appamatto samāno sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya majjati pamajjati pamādaṃ āpajjati, pamatto samāno dukkhaṃ viharati. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bhikkhu papaṭikaṃ aggahesi brahmacariyassa; tena ca vosānaṃ āpādi.

I am overwhelmed by suffering, beset by suffering. Perhaps an end to this entire mass of suffering can be discerned.’ Having gone forth thus, he generates gain, honor, and renown. Being heedless, he lives in suffering. This, monks, is called a monk who has grasped the outer bark of the holy life and has come to an end with that."

309. ‘‘Idha pana, bhikkhave, ekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. “Suppose, bhikkhus, a man in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, would come upon a great standing tree possessed of heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, he would cut off the outer bark and take it away, thinking, ‘This is heartwood.’ A man with good eyesight would see him and say: ‘This good man does not know heartwood, does not know sapwood, does not know inner bark, does not know outer bark, does not know twigs and leaves. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissatī’ti. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, idhekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno samādhisampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya samādhisampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno ñāṇadassanaṃ ārādheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena ñāṇadassanena attānukkaṃseti, paraṃ vambheti – ‘ahamasmi jānaṃ passaṃ viharāmi, ime panaññe bhikkhū ajānaṃ apassaṃ viharantī’ti. So tena ñāṇadassanena majjati pamajjati pamādaṃ āpajjati, pamatto samāno dukkhaṃ viharati. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bhikkhu phegguṃ aggahesi brahmacariyassa; tena ca vosānaṃ āpādi.

“Suppose, bhikkhus, a man in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes to a great standing tree possessed of heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, he cuts away the sapwood and takes it away, thinking it is heartwood. A discerning person seeing him would say: ‘This good man does not know heartwood, does not know sapwood, does not know inner bark, does not know outer bark, does not know twigs and leaves. Thus, this good man, in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes to a great standing tree possessed of heartwood, yet passes over the heartwood, cuts away the sapwood, and takes it away, thinking it is heartwood. Whatever purpose he had for the heartwood, he will not achieve it.’ Just so, bhikkhus, here a certain clansman goes forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness, thinking: ‘I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair; I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps an end to this whole mass of suffering can be discerned.’ Having gone forth thus, he obtains gain, honor, and renown. He is not pleased with that gain, honor, and renown, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that gain, honor, and renown. He does not become intoxicated, negligent, or heedless because of that gain, honor, and renown. Being heedful, he achieves perfection in virtue. He is pleased with that perfection in virtue, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection in virtue. He does not become intoxicated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection in virtue. Being heedful, he achieves perfection in samādhi. He is pleased with that perfection in samādhi, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection in samādhi. He does not become intoxicated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection in samādhi. Being heedful, he achieves knowledge and vision. He is pleased with that knowledge and vision, and his aspiration is fulfilled. Because of that knowledge and vision, he exalts himself and disparages others: ‘I dwell knowing and seeing, but these other bhikkhus dwell unknowing and unseeing.’ Because of that knowledge and vision, he becomes intoxicated, negligent, and heedless. Being heedless, he dwells in suffering. This, bhikkhus, is called a bhikkhu who has grasped the sapwood of the spiritual life, and with that he comes to an end.

311. ‘‘Idha pana, bhikkhave, ekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti, na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno samādhisampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya samādhisampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno ñāṇadassanaṃ ārādheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena ñāṇadassanena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno asamayavimokkhaṃ ārādheti. Aṭṭhānametaṃ [aṭṭhānaṃ kho panetaṃ (ka.)], bhikkhave, anavakāso yaṃ so bhikkhu tāya asamayavimuttiyā parihāyetha.

311.Here, bhikkhus, some clansman goes forth from the household life into homelessness out of faith, thinking: ‘I am afflicted by birth, aging, and death, by sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair; I am afflicted by suffering, overcome by suffering. Perhaps an ending of this whole mass of suffering might be discerned.’ Having thus gone forth, he acquires gain, honor, and renown. He is not content with that gain, honor, and renown, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that gain, honor, and renown. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that gain, honor, and renown. Being heedful, he achieves the perfection of virtue. He is content with that perfection of virtue, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection of virtue. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection of virtue. Being heedful, he achieves the perfection of samādhi. He is content with that perfection of samādhi, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection of samādhi. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection of samādhi. Being heedful, he achieves knowledge and vision. He is content with that knowledge and vision, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that knowledge and vision. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that knowledge and vision. Being heedful, he achieves timeless liberation. It is impossible, bhikkhus, there is no possibility that this bhikkhu would fall away from that timeless liberation.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato sāraññeva chetvā ādāya pakkameyya ‘sāra’nti jānamāno. Tamenaṃ cakkhumā puriso disvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘aññāsi vatāyaṃ bhavaṃ puriso sāraṃ, aññāsi phegguṃ, aññāsi tacaṃ, aññāsi papaṭikaṃ, aññāsi sākhāpalāsaṃ. Tathā hayaṃ bhavaṃ puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato sāraññeva chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti jānamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ anubhavissatī’ti.

Just as, bhikkhus, a man in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, would approach a large tree standing, possessed of heartwood. He would cut just the heartwood and go away with it, knowing it was heartwood. A man with good eyesight, seeing him, would say: ‘This good man indeed knows heartwood, knows sapwood, knows bark, knows outer bark, knows twigs and leaves. So too, this good man, in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, approached a large tree standing, possessed of heartwood, cut just the heartwood, and went away with it, knowing it was heartwood. And whatever purpose he has for heartwood, that purpose will be fulfilled for him.’

‘‘Evameva kho, bhikkhave, idhekacco kulaputto saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti, na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno samādhisampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya samādhisampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno ñāṇadassanaṃ ārādheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena ñāṇadassanena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena na majjati nappamajjati na pamādaṃ āpajjati, appamatto samāno asamayavimokkhaṃ ārādheti. Aṭṭhānametaṃ, bhikkhave, anavakāso yaṃ so bhikkhu tāya asamayavimuttiyā parihāyetha.

Even so, bhikkhus, some clansman goes forth from the household life into homelessness out of faith, thinking: ‘I am afflicted by birth, aging, and death, by sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair; I am afflicted by suffering, overcome by suffering. Perhaps an ending of this whole mass of suffering might be discerned.’ Having thus gone forth, he acquires gain, honor, and renown. He is not content with that gain, honor, and renown, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that gain, honor, and renown. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that gain, honor, and renown. Being heedful, he achieves the perfection of virtue. He is content with that perfection of virtue, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection of virtue. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection of virtue. Being heedful, he achieves the perfection of samādhi. He is content with that perfection of samādhi, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that perfection of samādhi. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that perfection of samādhi. Being heedful, he achieves knowledge and vision. He is content with that knowledge and vision, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others because of that knowledge and vision. He does not become infatuated, negligent, or heedless because of that knowledge and vision. Being heedful, he achieves timeless liberation. It is impossible, bhikkhus, there is no possibility that this bhikkhu would fall away from that timeless liberation.

‘‘Iti kho, bhikkhave, nayidaṃ brahmacariyaṃ lābhasakkārasilokānisaṃsaṃ, na sīlasampadānisaṃsaṃ, na samādhisampadānisaṃsaṃ, na ñāṇadassanānisaṃsaṃ. Yā ca kho ayaṃ, bhikkhave, akuppā cetovimutti – etadatthamidaṃ, bhikkhave, brahmacariyaṃ, etaṃ sāraṃ etaṃ pariyosāna’’nti.

Thus, bhikkhus, this holy life is not for the sake of benefits of gain, honor, and renown, nor for the sake of benefits of the perfection of virtue, nor for the sake of benefits of the perfection of samādhi, nor for the sake of benefits of knowledge and vision. But that unshakeable liberation of mind—for this, bhikkhus, is this holy life; this is its essence; this is its culmination.

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

This the Blessed One said. The bhikkhus were pleased and delighted with the Blessed One’s words.

Mahāsāropamasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ navamaṃ.

The Greater Discourse on the Simile of the Heartwood, the ninth, is ended.
