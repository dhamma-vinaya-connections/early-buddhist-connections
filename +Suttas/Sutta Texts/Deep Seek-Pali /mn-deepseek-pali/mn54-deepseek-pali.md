# 54. Potaliyasuttaṃ

## 54. The Potaliya Sutta

31. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā aṅguttarāpesu viharati āpaṇaṃ nāma aṅguttarāpānaṃ nigamo. Atha kho bhagavā pubbaṇhasamayaṃ nivāsetvā pattacīvaramādāya āpaṇaṃ piṇḍāya pāvisi. Āpaṇe piṇḍāya caritvā pacchābhattaṃ piṇḍapātapaṭikkanto yenaññataro vanasaṇḍo tenupasaṅkami divāvihārāya. Taṃ vanasaṇḍaṃ ajjhogāhetvā [ajjhogahetvā (sī. syā. kaṃ.), ajjhogāhitvā (pī. ka.)] aññatarasmiṃ rukkhamūle divāvihāraṃ nisīdi. Potaliyopi kho gahapati sampannanivāsanapāvuraṇo [pāpuraṇo (sī. syā. kaṃ.)] chattupāhanāhi [chattupāhano (ka.)] jaṅghāvihāraṃ anucaṅkamamāno anuvicaramāno yena so vanasaṇḍo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā taṃ vanasaṇḍaṃ ajjhogāhetvā yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavatā saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ aṭṭhāsi. Ekamantaṃ ṭhitaṃ kho potaliyaṃ gahapatiṃ bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘saṃvijjanti kho, gahapati, āsanāni; sace ākaṅkhasi nisīdā’’ti. Evaṃ vutte, potaliyo gahapati ‘‘gahapativādena maṃ samaṇo gotamo samudācaratī’’ti kupito anattamano tuṇhī ahosi. Dutiyampi kho bhagavā…pe… tatiyampi kho bhagavā potaliyaṃ gahapatiṃ etadavoca – ‘‘saṃvijjanti kho, gahapati, āsanāni; sace ākaṅkhasi nisīdā’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ vutte, potaliyo gahapati gahapativādena maṃ samaṇo gotamo samudācaratī’’ti kupito anattamano bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘tayidaṃ, bho gotama, nacchannaṃ, tayidaṃ nappatirūpaṃ, yaṃ maṃ tvaṃ gahapativādena samudācarasī’’ti. ‘‘Te hi te, gahapati, ākārā, te liṅgā, te nimittā yathā taṃ gahapatissā’’ti. ‘‘Tathā hi pana me, bho gotama, sabbe kammantā paṭikkhittā, sabbe vohārā samucchinnā’’ti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana te, gahapati, sabbe kammantā paṭikkhittā, sabbe vohārā samucchinnā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho gotama, yaṃ ahosi dhanaṃ vā dhaññaṃ vā rajataṃ vā jātarūpaṃ vā sabbaṃ taṃ puttānaṃ dāyajjaṃ niyyātaṃ, tatthāhaṃ anovādī anupavādī ghāsacchādanaparamo viharāmi. Evaṃ kho me [evañca me (syā.), evaṃ me (ka.)], bho gotama, sabbe kammantā paṭikkhittā, sabbe vohārā samucchinnā’’ti. ‘‘Aññathā kho tvaṃ, gahapati, vohārasamucchedaṃ vadasi, aññathā ca pana ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedo hotī’’ti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana, bhante, ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedo hoti? Sādhu me, bhante, bhagavā tathā dhammaṃ desetu yathā ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedo hotī’’ti. ‘‘Tena hi, gahapati, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho potaliyo gahapati bhagavato paccassosi.

31.Thus have I heard: At one time the Blessed One was dwelling among the Anguttarāpas, in their town named Āpaṇa. Then the Blessed One, having dressed in the morning, took his bowl and outer robe and entered Āpaṇa for alms. Having walked for alms in Āpaṇa, after the meal, on returning from the alms round, he approached a certain grove for the day's abiding. Having entered that grove, he sat down at the foot of a tree for the day's abiding. Potaliya the householder, fully clad in lower and upper garments, carrying a parasol and wearing sandals, was walking and wandering when he approached that grove. Having entered the grove, he approached the Blessed One. Having approached, he exchanged courteous greetings and amiable talk with the Blessed One, and then stood to one side. Standing to one side, the Blessed One said to Potaliya the householder: “There are seats, householder; if you wish, sit down.” When this was said, Potaliya the householder, angry and displeased, remained silent, thinking: “The ascetic Gotama addresses me by the term ‘householder.’” A second time the Blessed One… A third time the Blessed One said to Potaliya the householder: “There are seats, householder; if you wish, sit down.” When this was said, Potaliya the householder, angry and displeased, said to the Blessed One: “It is unsuitable, Master Gotama, it is unfitting that you address me by the term ‘householder.’” “But, householder, your ways, your signs, your indications are those of a householder.” “But Master Gotama, all my undertakings are abandoned, all my affairs are cut off.” “But how, householder, are all your undertakings abandoned, all your affairs cut off?” “Here, Master Gotama, whatever wealth or grain, silver or gold I had, all that I have transferred to my sons as their inheritance. I dwell not advising, not reproaching, with food and clothing as my ultimate concern. Thus, Master Gotama, all my undertakings are abandoned, all my affairs are cut off.” “Householder, you speak of the cutting off of affairs in one way, but in the Noble Discipline, the cutting off of affairs is different.” “But how, venerable sir, is the cutting off of affairs in the Noble Discipline? It would be good, venerable sir, if the Blessed One would teach me the Dhamma in such a way that the cutting off of affairs in the Noble Discipline comes about.” “Then listen, householder, and attend carefully; I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” Potaliya the householder assented to the Blessed One.

32. Bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘aṭṭha kho ime, gahapati, dhammā ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedāya saṃvattanti. Katame aṭṭha? Apāṇātipātaṃ nissāya pāṇātipāto pahātabbo; dinnādānaṃ nissāya adinnādānaṃ pahātabbaṃ; saccavācaṃ [saccaṃ vācaṃ (syā.)] nissāya musāvādo pahātabbo; apisuṇaṃ vācaṃ nissāya pisuṇā vācā pahātabbā; agiddhilobhaṃ nissāya giddhilobho pahātabbo; anindārosaṃ nissāya nindāroso pahātabbo; akkodhūpāyāsaṃ nissāya kodhūpāyāso pahātabbo; anatimānaṃ nissāya atimāno pahātabbo. Ime kho, gahapati, aṭṭha dhammā saṃkhittena vuttā, vitthārena avibhattā, ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedāya saṃvattantī’’ti. ‘‘Ye me [ye me pana (syā. ka.)], bhante, bhagavatā aṭṭha dhammā saṃkhittena vuttā, vitthārena avibhattā, ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedāya saṃvattanti, sādhu me, bhante, bhagavā ime aṭṭha dhamme vitthārena [vitthāretvā (ka.)] vibhajatu anukampaṃ upādāyā’’ti. ‘‘Tena hi, gahapati, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho potaliyo gahapati bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

32.The Blessed One said: “Householder, these eight things in the Noble Discipline lead to the cutting off of affairs. Which eight? By relying on non-killing, killing is to be abandoned; by relying on taking what is given, taking what is not given is to be abandoned; by relying on truthful speech, false speech is to be abandoned; by relying on non-divisive speech, divisive speech is to be abandoned; by relying on non-greed, greed is to be abandoned; by relying on non-censure and non-malice, censure and malice are to be abandoned; by relying on non-anger and non-ill-will, anger and ill-will are to be abandoned; by relying on non-conceit, conceit is to be abandoned. These, householder, are the eight things stated in brief, not analyzed in detail, that lead to the cutting off of affairs in the Noble Discipline.” “Venerable sir, the Blessed One has stated these eight things in brief, not analyzed in detail, which lead to the cutting off of affairs in the Noble Discipline. It would be good, venerable sir, if the Blessed One would analyze these eight things for me in detail out of compassion.” “Then, householder, listen and attend carefully; I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” Potaliya the householder assented to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said:

33. ‘‘‘Apāṇātipātaṃ nissāya pāṇātipāto pahātabbo’ti iti kho panetaṃ vuttaṃ kiñcetaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ? Idha, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘yesaṃ kho ahaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ hetu pāṇātipātī assaṃ, tesāhaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ pahānāya samucchedāya paṭipanno. Ahañceva [ahañce (?)] kho pana pāṇātipātī assaṃ, attāpi maṃ upavadeyya pāṇātipātapaccayā, anuviccāpi maṃ viññū [anuvicca viññū (sī. syā. pī.)] garaheyyuṃ pāṇātipātapaccayā, kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā duggati pāṭikaṅkhā pāṇātipātapaccayā. Etadeva kho pana saṃyojanaṃ etaṃ nīvaraṇaṃ yadidaṃ pāṇātipāto. Ye ca pāṇātipātapaccayā uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, pāṇātipātā paṭiviratassa evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti’. ‘Apāṇātipātaṃ nissāya pāṇātipāto pahātabbo’ti – iti yantaṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

33.“‘By relying on non-killing, killing is to be abandoned’—this was said. With reference to what was this said? Here, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: ‘For the sake of which fetters would I kill living beings? For the abandoning and utter destruction of those fetters I have entered upon the practice. If I were to kill living beings, I myself would reproach me because of killing living beings; and the wise, having examined, would censure me because of killing living beings; and with the breakup of the body, after death, a state of woe would be expected because of killing living beings. Indeed, this killing of living beings is itself a fetter, this is a hindrance. And the defilements, anguish, and torment that would arise because of killing living beings do not exist for one who abstains from killing living beings.’ ‘By relying on non-killing, killing is to be abandoned’—that was said with reference to this.”

39.'Anger and affliction are to be abandoned by relying on non-anger and non-affliction.' This has been said. With reference to what was this said? Here, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'On account of which fetters I might be angry and afflicted, I am practicing for the abandoning and eradication of those fetters. If I were to be angry and afflicted, my own self would reproach me on account of anger and affliction; having examined, the wise would censure me on account of anger and affliction; and at the breaking up of the body, after death, an unhappy destination is to be expected on account of anger and affliction. Indeed, this very anger and affliction is a fetter, a hindrance. And those taints, distress, and fever that might arise on account of anger and affliction—for one who is not angry and afflicted, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise.' 'Anger and affliction are to be abandoned by relying on non-anger and non-affliction.' This was said with reference to this.

Etadeva kho pana saṃyojanaṃ etaṃ nīvaraṇaṃ yadidaṃ atimāno. Ye ca atimānapaccayā uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, anatimānissa evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti’. ‘Anatimānaṃ nissāya atimāno pahātabbo’ti – iti yantaṃ vuttaṃ idametaṃ paṭicca vuttaṃ.

40.'Conceit is to be abandoned by relying on non-conceit.' This has been said. With reference to what was this said? Here, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'On account of which fetters I might be conceited, I am practicing for the abandoning and eradication of those fetters. If I were to be conceited, my own self would reproach me on account of conceit; having examined, the wise would censure me on account of conceit; and at the breaking up of the body, after death, an unhappy destination is to be expected on account of conceit. Indeed, this very conceit is a fetter, a hindrance. And those taints, distress, and fever that might arise on account of conceit—for one who is not conceited, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise.' 'Conceit is to be abandoned by relying on non-conceit.' This was said with reference to this.

41. ‘‘Ime kho, gahapati, aṭṭha dhammā saṃkhittena vuttā, vitthārena vibhattā [avibhattā (syā. ka.)], ye ariyassa vinaye vohārasamucchedāya saṃvattanti; na tveva tāva ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo hotī’’ti.

41."These, householder, are the eight qualities stated in brief and expounded in detail, which conduce to the cessation of transactions in the Noble One’s Discipline. Yet, in the Noble One’s Discipline, the cessation of transactions is not yet complete, total, and in every way."

‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana, bhante, ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo hoti? Sādhu me, bhante, bhagavā tathā dhammaṃ desetu yathā ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo hotī’’ti. ‘‘Tena hi, gahapati, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho potaliyo gahapati bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

"But how, venerable sir, in the Noble One’s Discipline, is there the complete, total, utter cessation of all transactions? It would be good, venerable sir, if the Blessed One would teach me the Dhamma in such a way that in the Noble One’s Discipline there is the complete, total, utter cessation of all transactions." "Well then, householder, listen and apply your mind well, I will speak." "Yes, venerable sir," replied the householder Potaliya to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

## Kāmādīnavakathā

### The Dangers of Sensuality

42. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, kukkuro jighacchādubbalyapareto goghātakasūnaṃ paccupaṭṭhito assa. Tamenaṃ dakkho goghātako vā goghātakantevāsī vā aṭṭhikaṅkalaṃ sunikkantaṃ nikkantaṃ nimmaṃsaṃ lohitamakkhitaṃ upasumbheyya [upacchubheyya (sī. pī.), upacchūbheyya (syā. kaṃ.), upaccumbheyya (ka.)]. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, api nu kho so kukkuro amuṃ aṭṭhikaṅkalaṃ sunikkantaṃ nikkantaṃ nimmaṃsaṃ lohitamakkhitaṃ palehanto jighacchādubbalyaṃ paṭivineyyā’’ti?

42."Suppose, householder, a dog overcome by hunger and debility were to approach a slaughterhouse. Then a skilled butcher or a butcher's apprentice were to toss to it a skeleton-bone scraped clean, fleshless, and smeared with blood. What do you think, householder? Would that dog, by licking that skeleton-bone scraped clean, fleshless, and smeared with blood, allay its hunger and debility?"

‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’.

"No, venerable sir."

‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’?

"Why is that?"

‘‘Aduñhi, bhante, aṭṭhikaṅkalaṃ sunikkantaṃ nikkantaṃ nimmaṃsaṃ lohitamakkhitaṃ. Yāvadeva pana so kukkuro kilamathassa vighātassa bhāgī assāti. Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘aṭṭhikaṅkalūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā [bahūpāyāsā (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)], ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā yāyaṃ upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā taṃ abhinivajjetvā, yāyaṃ upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā yattha sabbaso lokāmisūpādānā aparisesā nirujjhanti tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

"Venerable sir, this skeleton-bone is scraped clean, fleshless, and smeared with blood. That dog will only get weariness and vexation from it." "Even so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'Sensual pleasures have been compared by the Blessed One to a skeleton-bone—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

43. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, gijjho vā kaṅko vā kulalo vā maṃsapesiṃ ādāya uḍḍīyeyya [uḍḍayeyya (syā. pī.)]. Tamenaṃ gijjhāpi kaṅkāpi kulalāpi anupatitvā anupatitvā vitaccheyyuṃ vissajjeyyuṃ [virājeyyuṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)]. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, sace so gijjho vā kaṅko vā kulalo vā taṃ maṃsapesiṃ na khippameva paṭinissajjeyya, so tatonidānaṃ maraṇaṃ vā nigaccheyya maraṇamattaṃ vā dukkha’’nti?

43."Suppose, householder, a vulture, a heron, or a kite were to carry off a piece of meat and fly off. Then other vultures, herons, and kites would follow it, pursuing, tearing, and snatching it away. What do you think, householder? If that vulture, heron, or kite does not quickly relinquish that piece of meat, would it not meet with death or suffering like unto death because of that?"

‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’.

"Yes, venerable sir."

‘‘Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘maṃsapesūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā yāyaṃ upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā taṃ abhinivajjetvā yāyaṃ upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā yattha sabbaso lokāmisūpādānā aparisesā nirujjhanti tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

"Just so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'The Blessed One has said sensual pleasures are comparable to a piece of meat—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder."

44. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, puriso ādittaṃ tiṇukkaṃ ādāya paṭivātaṃ gaccheyya. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, sace so puriso taṃ ādittaṃ tiṇukkaṃ na khippameva paṭinissajjeyya tassa sā ādittā tiṇukkā hatthaṃ vā daheyya bāhuṃ vā daheyya aññataraṃ vā aññataraṃ vā aṅgapaccaṅgaṃ [daheyya. aññataraṃ vā aṅgapaccaṅga (sī. pī.)] daheyya, so tatonidānaṃ maraṇaṃ vā nigaccheyya maraṇamattaṃ vā dukkha’’nti?

44."Suppose, householder, a man were to take a blazing torch of grass and go against the wind. What do you think, householder? If that man does not quickly relinquish that blazing torch of grass, would that blazing torch of grass burn his hand or his arm or some other limb or minor limb? Would he thereby meet death or suffering like unto death?"

‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’.

"Yes, venerable sir."

‘‘Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘tiṇukkūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā…pe… tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

Just so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'The Blessed One has declared sensual pleasures to be like a torch of grass—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

45. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, aṅgārakāsu sādhikaporisā, pūrā aṅgārānaṃ vītaccikānaṃ vītadhūmānaṃ. Atha puriso āgaccheyya jīvitukāmo amaritukāmo sukhakāmo dukkhappaṭikkūlo. Tamenaṃ dve balavanto purisā nānābāhāsu gahetvā aṅgārakāsuṃ upakaḍḍheyyuṃ. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, api nu so puriso iticiticeva kāyaṃ sannāmeyyā’’ti?

45."Suppose, householder, there were a pit of coals, more than a man's height in depth, full of coals that were flameless and smokeless. Then a man would come along—desiring to live, not desiring to die, desiring happiness, averse to pain. Then two strong men would seize him by both arms and drag him to the pit of coals. What do you think, householder? Wouldn't that man bend his body this way and that?"

‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’.

"Yes, venerable sir."

‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’?

"Why is that?"

‘‘Viditañhi, bhante, tassa purisassa imañcāhaṃ aṅgārakāsuṃ papatissāmi, tatonidānaṃ maraṇaṃ vā nigacchissāmi maraṇamattaṃ vā dukkha’’nti. ‘‘Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘aṅgārakāsūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā…pe… tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

"For, venerable sir, that man knows, 'If I fall into this pit of coals, then because of that I will meet death or suffering like unto death.'" "Just so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'Sensual pleasures have been compared by the Blessed One to a pit of coals—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

46. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, puriso supinakaṃ passeyya ārāmarāmaṇeyyakaṃ vanarāmaṇeyyakaṃ bhūmirāmaṇeyyakaṃ pokkharaṇirāmaṇeyyakaṃ. So paṭibuddho na kiñci paṭipasseyya [passeyya (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)]. Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘supinakūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti…pe… tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

46."Just as, householder, a man might see in a dream a delightful park, a delightful forest, a delightful piece of ground, or a delightful lotus pond; but when he awoke, he would see nothing at all. In the same way, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'The Blessed One has said that sensual pleasures are like a dream—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

47. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, puriso yācitakaṃ bhogaṃ yācitvā yānaṃ vā [yānaṃ (syā. kaṃ. pī.)] poriseyyaṃ [poroseyyaṃ (sī. pī. ka.), oropeyya (syā. kaṃ.)] pavaramaṇikuṇḍalaṃ. So tehi yācitakehi bhogehi purakkhato parivuto antarāpaṇaṃ paṭipajjeyya. Tamenaṃ jano disvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘bhogī vata, bho, puriso, evaṃ kira bhogino bhogāni bhuñjantī’ti. Tamenaṃ sāmikā yattha yattheva passeyyuṃ tattha tattheva sāni hareyyuṃ. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, alaṃ nu kho tassa purisassa aññathattāyā’’ti?

47."Suppose, householder, a man, having borrowed wealth—a vehicle, personal attendants, or the finest jewels and earrings—and being attended and surrounded by these borrowed possessions, were to proceed to the marketplace. Seeing him, people might say, ‘Indeed, this man is rich! It seems the rich enjoy their riches in this way.’ But wherever the owners saw him, there they would take back what is theirs. What do you think, householder? Wouldn't that man then be altered?"

‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’.

"Yes, venerable sir."

‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’?

"Why is that?"

‘‘Sāmino hi, bhante, sāni harantī’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘yācitakūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti…pe… tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

"Sir, it is the owners who take back what is theirs." "Just so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: 'Sensual pleasures have been compared by the Blessed One to borrowed goods—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.' Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

48. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, gahapati, gāmassa vā nigamassa vā avidūre tibbo vanasaṇḍo. Tatrassa rukkho sampannaphalo ca upapannaphalo [uppannaphalo (syā.)] ca, na cassu kānici phalāni bhūmiyaṃ patitāni. Atha puriso āgaccheyya phalatthiko phalagavesī phalapariyesanaṃ caramāno. So taṃ vanasaṇḍaṃ ajjhogāhetvā taṃ rukkhaṃ passeyya sampannaphalañca upapannaphalañca. Tassa evamassa – ‘ayaṃ kho rukkho sampannaphalo ca upapannaphalo ca, natthi ca kānici phalāni bhūmiyaṃ patitāni. Jānāmi kho panāhaṃ rukkhaṃ ārohituṃ [āruhituṃ (sī.)]. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ imaṃ rukkhaṃ ārohitvā yāvadatthañca khādeyyaṃ ucchaṅgañca pūreyya’nti. So taṃ rukkhaṃ ārohitvā yāvadatthañca khādeyya ucchaṅgañca pūreyya. Atha dutiyo puriso āgaccheyya phalatthiko phalagavesī phalapariyesanaṃ caramāno tiṇhaṃ kuṭhāriṃ [kudhāriṃ (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] ādāya. So taṃ vanasaṇḍaṃ ajjhogāhetvā taṃ rukkhaṃ passeyya sampannaphalañca upapannaphalañca. Tassa evamassa – ‘ayaṃ kho rukkho sampannaphalo ca upapannaphalo ca, natthi ca kānici phalāni bhūmiyaṃ patitāni. Na kho panāhaṃ jānāmi rukkhaṃ ārohituṃ. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ imaṃ rukkhaṃ mūlato chetvā yāvadatthañca khādeyyaṃ ucchaṅgañca pūreyya’nti. So taṃ rukkhaṃ mūlatova chindeyya. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, amuko [asu (sī. pī.)] yo so puriso paṭhamaṃ rukkhaṃ ārūḷho sace so na khippameva oroheyya tassa so rukkho papatanto hatthaṃ vā bhañjeyya pādaṃ vā bhañjeyya aññataraṃ vā aññataraṃ vā aṅgapaccaṅgaṃ bhañjeyya, so tatonidānaṃ maraṇaṃ vā nigaccheyya maraṇamattaṃ vā dukkha’’nti?

48.Suppose, householder, near a village or town there was a dense thicket. In it stood a tree laden with sweet and abundant fruit, and no fruits had fallen to the ground. Then a man might come along, seeking fruit, searching for fruit, going about in search of fruit. Having entered the thicket, he would see that tree laden with sweet and abundant fruit and think: 'This tree is laden with sweet and abundant fruit, and no fruits have fallen to the ground. I know how to climb a tree. Why don’t I climb this tree, eat as much as I like and fill my lap?' So he would climb the tree, eat as much as he liked and fill his lap. Then a second man might come along, also seeking fruit, searching for fruit, carrying a sharp axe. Having entered the thicket, he would see that tree laden with sweet and abundant fruit and think: 'This tree is laden with sweet and abundant fruit, and no fruits have fallen to the ground. I do not know how to climb a tree. Why don’t I cut down this tree at its root, eat as much as I like and fill my lap?' So he would cut down the tree at its root. What do you think, householder? If that man who first climbed the tree did not quickly come down, wouldn’t the falling tree break his hand or his foot or some other limb or minor limb, so that from that cause he might meet with death or suffering like unto death?

‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’.

Yes, venerable sir.

‘‘Evameva kho, gahapati, ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘rukkhaphalūpamā kāmā vuttā bhagavatā bahudukkhā bahupāyāsā, ādīnavo ettha bhiyyo’ti. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā yāyaṃ upekkhā nānattā nānattasitā taṃ abhinivajjetvā yāyaṃ upekkhā ekattā ekattasitā yattha sabbaso lokāmisūpādānā aparisesā nirujjhanti tamevūpekkhaṃ bhāveti.

Just so, householder, a noble disciple reflects thus: ‘The Blessed One has said that sensual pleasures are like tree-fruits—of much suffering, of much tribulation, and there is greater danger in them.’ Seeing this as it truly is with right wisdom, he utterly rejects the equanimity that is diverse, based on diversity, and develops the equanimity that is unified, based on unity, where all grasping at worldly allurements ceases without remainder.

49. ‘‘Sa kho so, gahapati, ariyasāvako imaṃyeva anuttaraṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ āgamma anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarati, seyyathidaṃ – ekampi jātiṃ dvepi jātiyo…pe… iti sākāraṃ sauddesaṃ anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarati.

49.That noble disciple, householder, having come to this unsurpassed purity of equanimity and mindfulness, recollects his manifold past lives, that is, one birth, two births... Thus he recollects his manifold past lives with their aspects and details.

‘‘Sa kho so, gahapati, ariyasāvako imaṃyeva anuttaraṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ āgamma dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena satte passati cavamāne upapajjamāne hīne paṇīte suvaṇṇe dubbaṇṇe sugate duggate…pe… yathākammūpage satte pajānāti.

Now, householder, that noble disciple, having come to this unsurpassed purity of equanimity and mindfulness, with the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, sees beings passing away and reappearing—inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate—and understands how beings fare according to their deeds.

‘‘Sa kho so, gahapati, ariyasāvako imaṃyeva anuttaraṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ āgamma āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati. Ettāvatā kho, gahapati, ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo hoti.

Indeed, householder, that noble disciple, having come to this unsurpassed purity of equanimity and mindfulness, by the destruction of the taints, enters and dwells in the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, having realized it for himself with direct knowledge in this very life. Thus, householder, in the Noble One’s discipline, there is the complete and utter cutting off of all conventional usage.

50. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, gahapati, yathā ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo hoti, api nu tvaṃ evarūpaṃ vohārasamucchedaṃ attani samanupassasī’’ti? ‘‘Ko cāhaṃ, bhante, ko ca ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedo! Ārakā ahaṃ, bhante, ariyassa vinaye sabbena sabbaṃ sabbathā sabbaṃ vohārasamucchedā. Mayañhi, bhante, pubbe aññatitthiye paribbājake anājānīyeva samāne ājānīyāti amaññimha, anājānīyeva samāne ājānīyabhojanaṃ bhojimha, anājānīyeva samāne ājānīyaṭhāne ṭhapimha; bhikkhū pana mayaṃ, bhante, ājānīyeva samāne anājānīyāti amaññimha, ājānīyeva samāne anājānīyabhojanaṃ bhojimha, ājānīyeva samāne anājānīyaṭhāne ṭhapimha; idāni pana mayaṃ, bhante, aññatitthiye paribbājake anājānīyeva samāne anājānīyāti jānissāma, anājānīyeva samāne anājānīyabhojanaṃ bhojessāma, anājānīyeva samāne anājānīyaṭhāne ṭhapessāma. Bhikkhū pana mayaṃ, bhante, ājānīyeva samāne ājānīyāti jānissāma ājānīyeva samāne ājānīyabhojanaṃ bhojessāma, ājānīyeva samāne ājānīyaṭhāne ṭhapessāma. Ajanesi vata me, bhante, bhagavā samaṇesu samaṇappemaṃ, samaṇesu samaṇappasādaṃ, samaṇesu samaṇagāravaṃ. Abhikkantaṃ, bhante, abhikkantaṃ, bhante! Seyyathāpi, bhante, nikkujjitaṃ vā ukkujjeyya, paṭicchannaṃ vā vivareyya, mūḷhassa vā maggaṃ ācikkheyya, andhakāre vā telapajjotaṃ dhāreyya, cakkhumanto rūpāni dakkhantīti; evamevaṃ kho, bhante, bhagavatā anekapariyāyena dhammo pakāsito. Esāhaṃ, bhante, bhagavantaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi dhammañca bhikkhusaṅghañca. Upāsakaṃ maṃ bhagavā dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gata’’nti.

50."What do you think, householder? When conventional usage is completely and utterly cut off in the Noble One’s Discipline, do you see such a cutting off of conventional usage in yourself?" "Who am I, venerable sir, and what is the complete and utter cutting off of all conventional usage in the Noble One’s Discipline! I am far, venerable sir, from the complete and utter cutting off of all conventional usage in the Noble One’s Discipline. For previously, venerable sir, we regarded other sectarian wanderers, who were not thoroughbreds, as thoroughbreds; we gave food for thoroughbreds to those who were not thoroughbreds; we placed those who were not thoroughbreds in the position of thoroughbreds. But we regarded the bhikkhus, who were thoroughbreds, as not thoroughbreds; we gave food for non-thoroughbreds to those who were thoroughbreds; we placed those who were thoroughbreds in the position of non-thoroughbreds. Now, venerable sir, we will recognize other sectarian wanderers, who are not thoroughbreds, as not thoroughbreds; we will give food for non-thoroughbreds to those who are not thoroughbreds; we will place those who are not thoroughbreds in the position of non-thoroughbreds. And we will recognize the bhikkhus, who are thoroughbreds, as thoroughbreds; we will give food for thoroughbreds to those who are thoroughbreds; we will place those who are thoroughbreds in the position of thoroughbreds. Truly, venerable sir, the Blessed One has generated in me love for recluses, confidence in recluses, and reverence for recluses. Magnificent, venerable sir! Magnificent, venerable sir! Just as one might set upright what was overturned, reveal what was hidden, show the way to one who was lost, or hold up a lamp in the darkness so that those with eyes could see forms—so has the Blessed One made the Dhamma clear in various ways. I go for refuge to the Blessed One, to the Dhamma, and to the Sangha of bhikkhus. Let the Blessed One remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge from today onwards for as long as life lasts."

Potaliyasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ catutthaṃ.

The Potaliya Sutta, the fourth, is concluded.
