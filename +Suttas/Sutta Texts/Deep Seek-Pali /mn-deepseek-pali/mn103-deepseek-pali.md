# 103. Sāmagāmasuttaṃ

## 103. The Sāmagāma Sutta.

41. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sakkesu viharati sāmagāme. Tena kho pana samayena nigaṇṭho nāṭaputto [nāthaputto (sī. pī.)] pāvāyaṃ adhunākālaṅkato [kālakato (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] hoti. Tassa kālaṅkiriyāya bhinnā nigaṇṭhā dvedhikajātā [dveḷhakajātā (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] bhaṇḍanajātā kalahajātā vivādāpannā aññamaññaṃ mukhasattīhi vitudantā viharanti – ‘‘na tvaṃ imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ ājānāsi, ahaṃ imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ ājānāmi. Kiṃ tvaṃ imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ ājānissasi! Micchāpaṭipanno tvamasi, ahamasmi sammāpaṭipanno. Sahitaṃ me, asahitaṃ te. Purevacanīyaṃ pacchā avaca, pacchāvacanīyaṃ pure avaca. Adhiciṇṇaṃ [aviciṇṇaṃ (sī. pī.)] te viparāvattaṃ. Āropito te vādo. Niggahitosi, cara vādappamokkhāya; nibbeṭhehi vā sace pahosī’’ti. Vadhoyeva kho [vadhoyeveko (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] maññe nigaṇṭhesu nāṭaputtiyesu vattati. Yepi nigaṇṭhassa nāṭaputtassa sāvakā gihī odātavasanā tepi nigaṇṭhesu nāṭaputtiyesu nibbinnarūpā [nibbindarūpā (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] virattarūpā paṭivānarūpā yathā taṃ durakkhāte dhammavinaye duppavedite aniyyānike anupasamasaṃvattanike asammāsambuddhappavedite bhinnathūpe appaṭisaraṇe.

41.Thus have I heard—at one time the Blessed One was dwelling among the Sakyans at Sāmagāma. Now at that time, the Nigaṇṭha Nāṭaputta had recently passed away at Pāvā. Upon his death, the Nigaṇṭhas became divided into two factions, quarrelsome, contentious, and disputatious, stabbing each other with verbal daggers: "You don’t understand this Dhamma and Discipline—I understand it! How could you understand this Dhamma and Discipline? You’re practicing wrongly—I’m practicing rightly! What I say is consistent, what you say is not. You said what should have been said first last, and what should have been said last first. What you have long practiced has been overturned. Your argument has been refuted. You are defeated! Go, try to free yourself from this debate, or disentangle yourself if you can!" It seemed as though slaughter was taking place among the Nigaṇṭha disciples of Nāṭaputta. Even the lay followers of Nāṭaputta, dressed in white, were disgusted, disaffected, and disillusioned with the Nigaṇṭhas, because their Dhamma and Discipline was poorly proclaimed, not well expounded, not leading to liberation, not conducive to peace, not taught by a Perfectly Self-Enlightened One—broken-down, without refuge.

42. Atha kho cundo samaṇuddeso pāvāyaṃ vassaṃvuṭṭho [vassaṃvuttho (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] yena sāmagāmo yenāyasmā ānando tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmantaṃ ānandaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho cundo samaṇuddeso āyasmantaṃ ānandaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘nigaṇṭho, bhante, nāṭaputto pāvāyaṃ adhunākālaṅkato. Tassa kālaṅkiriyāya bhinnā nigaṇṭhā dvedhikajātā…pe… bhinnathūpe appaṭisaraṇe’’ti. Evaṃ vutte, āyasmā ānando cundaṃ samaṇuddesaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘atthi kho idaṃ, āvuso cunda, kathāpābhataṃ bhagavantaṃ dassanāya. Āyāma, āvuso cunda, yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkamissāma; upasaṅkamitvā etamatthaṃ bhagavato ārocessāmā’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho cundo samaṇuddeso āyasmato ānandassa paccassosi.

42.Then the novice Cunda, having completed the rains retreat at Pāvā, went to Sāmagāma, to where Venerable Ānanda was. Approaching him, he paid homage to Venerable Ānanda and sat down to one side. Seated to one side, the novice Cunda said to Venerable Ānanda: "Venerable sir, the Nigaṇṭha Nāṭaputta has recently passed away at Pāvā. Upon his death, the Nigaṇṭhas have split into two factions... broken-down, without refuge." When this was said, Venerable Ānanda said to Cunda: "Friend Cunda, this is a matter to be brought to the Blessed One's attention. Come, friend Cunda, let us go to the Blessed One; having approached him, we shall report this matter to him." "Yes, venerable sir," the novice Cunda replied to Venerable Ānanda.

Atha kho āyasmā ca ānando cundo ca samaṇuddeso yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkamiṃsu; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho āyasmā ānando bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘ayaṃ, bhante, cundo samaṇuddeso evamāha – ‘nigaṇṭho, bhante, nāṭaputto pāvāyaṃ adhunākālaṅkato. Tassa kālaṅkiriyāya bhinnā nigaṇṭhā dvedhikajātā…pe… bhinnathūpe appaṭisaraṇe’ti. Tassa mayhaṃ, bhante, evaṃ hoti – ‘māheva bhagavato accayena saṅghe vivādo uppajji; svāssa [so (sī. pī.), svāyaṃ (ka.)] vivādo bahujanāhitāya bahujanāsukhāya bahuno janassa anatthāya ahitāya dukkhāya devamanussāna’’’nti.

Then Venerable Ānanda and the novice Cunda approached the Blessed One. Having approached, they paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Seated to one side, Venerable Ānanda said this to the Blessed One: "Venerable sir, this novice Cunda says: ‘Venerable sir, the Nigaṇṭha Nāṭaputta has recently passed away at Pāvā. Upon his death, the Nigaṇṭhas have split into two factions... broken-down, without refuge.’ It occurred to me, venerable sir: ‘May a dispute not arise in the Saṅgha after the Blessed One’s passing; for such a dispute would be for the harm of many people, for the suffering of many people, for the disadvantage, harm, and pain of many people, of gods and humans.’"

43. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, ānanda, ye vo mayā dhammā abhiññā desitā, seyyathidaṃ – cattāro satipaṭṭhānā cattāro sammappadhānā cattāro iddhipādā pañcindriyāni pañca balāni satta bojjhaṅgā ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo, passasi no tvaṃ, ānanda, imesu dhammesu dvepi bhikkhū nānāvāde’’ti? ‘‘Ye me, bhante, dhammā bhagavatā abhiññā desitā, seyyathidaṃ – cattāro satipaṭṭhānā cattāro sammappadhānā cattāro iddhipādā pañcindriyāni pañca balāni satta bojjhaṅgā ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo, nāhaṃ passāmi imesu dhammesu dvepi bhikkhū nānāvāde. Ye ca kho [santi ca kho (syā. kaṃ.), santi ca (ka.)], bhante, puggalā bhagavantaṃ patissayamānarūpā viharanti tepi bhagavato accayena saṅghe vivādaṃ janeyyuṃ ajjhājīve vā adhipātimokkhe vā. Svāssa [sossa (sī. pī.), svāyaṃ (ka.)] vivādo bahujanāhitāya bahujanāsukhāya bahuno janassa anatthāya ahitāya dukkhāya devamanussāna’’nti. Appamattako so, ānanda, vivādo yadidaṃ – ajjhājīve vā adhipātimokkhe vā. Magge vā hi, ānanda, paṭipadāya vā saṅghe vivādo uppajjamāno uppajjeyya; svāssa vivādo bahujanāhitāya bahujanāsukhāya bahuno janassa anatthāya ahitāya dukkhāya devamanussānaṃ.

43."What do you think, Ānanda? Regarding the Dhamma taught by me through direct knowledge—namely, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of enlightenment, and the Noble Eightfold Path—do you see, Ānanda, even two monks holding different views on these teachings?" "Venerable sir, regarding the Dhamma taught by the Blessed One through direct knowledge—namely, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of enlightenment, and the Noble Eightfold Path—I do not see even two monks holding different views on these teachings. But, venerable sir, those individuals who live dependent on the Blessed One might, after the Blessed One’s passing, cause disputes in the Saṅgha regarding their means of livelihood or the higher Pātimokkha. That dispute, Ānanda, is trivial—that is, one concerning means of livelihood or the higher Pātimokkha. But if a dispute were to arise in the Saṅgha concerning the path or the practice, that dispute would be for the harm of many people, for the suffering of many people, for the disadvantage, harm, and pain of many people, of gods and humans."

44. ‘‘Chayimāni, ānanda, vivādamūlāni. Katamāni cha? Idhānanda, bhikkhu kodhano hoti upanāhī. Yo so, ānanda, bhikkhu kodhano hoti upanāhī so sattharipi agāravo viharati appatisso, dhammepi agāravo viharati appatisso, saṅghepi agāravo viharati appatisso, sikkhāyapi na paripūrakārī hoti. Yo so, ānanda, bhikkhu satthari agāravo viharati appatisso, dhamme… saṅghe agāravo viharati appatisso, sikkhāya na paripūrakārī hoti, so saṅghe vivādaṃ janeti; yo hoti vivādo bahujanāhitāya bahujanāsukhāya, bahuno janassa anatthāya ahitāya dukkhāya devamanussānaṃ. Evarūpañce tumhe, ānanda, vivādamūlaṃ ajjhattaṃ vā bahiddhā vā samanupasseyyātha, tatra tumhe, ānanda, tasseva pāpakassa vivādamūlassa pahānāya vāyameyyātha. Evarūpañce tumhe, ānanda, vivādamūlaṃ ajjhattaṃ vā bahiddhā vā na samanupasseyyātha. Tatra tumhe, ānanda, tasseva pāpakassa vivādamūlassa āyatiṃ anavassavāya paṭipajjeyyātha. Evametassa pāpakassa vivādamūlassa pahānaṃ hoti, evametassa pāpakassa vivādamūlassa āyatiṃ anavassavo hoti.

44."Ānanda, there are these six roots of contention. What six? Here, Ānanda, a monk is angry and resentful. That monk, Ānanda, who is angry and resentful dwells without reverence and respect toward the Teacher, without reverence and respect toward the Dhamma, without reverence and respect toward the Saṅgha, and is not a complete fulfiller of the training. That monk, Ānanda, who dwells without reverence and respect toward the Teacher, toward the Dhamma, toward the Saṅgha, and is not a complete fulfiller of the training, gives rise to disputes in the Saṅgha; and that dispute is for the harm of many people, for the unhappiness of many people, for the disadvantage, harm, and pain of many people, of gods and humans. If, Ānanda, you perceive such a root of contention, whether internal or external, you should strive for the abandoning of that evil root of contention. If, Ānanda, you do not perceive such a root of contention, whether internal or external, you should practice so that that evil root of contention does not flow into the future. Thus, that evil root of contention is abandoned; thus, there is no future flowing in of that evil root of contention.

45. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhu makkhī hoti paḷāsī…pe… issukī hoti maccharī…pe… saṭho hoti māyāvī…pe… pāpiccho hoti micchādiṭṭhi [micchādiṭṭhī (syā. kaṃ. pī. ka.)] …pe… sandiṭṭhiparāmāsī hoti ādhānaggāhī duppaṭinissaggī. Yo so, ānanda, bhikkhu sandiṭṭhiparāmāsī hoti ādhānaggāhī duppaṭinissaggī so sattharipi agāravo viharati appatisso, dhammepi agāravo viharati appatisso, saṅghepi agāravo viharati appatisso, sikkhāyapi na paripūrakārī hoti. Yo so, ānanda, bhikkhu satthari agāravo viharati appatisso, dhamme… saṅghe… sikkhāya na paripūrakārī hoti so saṅghe vivādaṃ janeti; yo hoti vivādo bahujanāhitāya bahujanāsukhāya, bahuno janassa anatthāya ahitāya dukkhāya devamanussānaṃ. Evarūpañce tumhe, ānanda, vivādamūlaṃ ajjhattaṃ vā bahiddhā vā samanupasseyyātha. Tatra tumhe, ānanda, tasseva pāpakassa vivādamūlassa pahānāya vāyameyyātha. Evarūpañce tumhe, ānanda, vivādamūlaṃ ajjhattaṃ vā bahiddhā vā na samanupasseyyātha, tatra tumhe, ānanda, tasseva pāpakassa vivādamūlassa āyatiṃ anavassavāya paṭipajjeyyātha. Evametassa pāpakassa vivādamūlassa pahānaṃ hoti, evametassa pāpakassa vivādamūlassa āyatiṃ anavassavo hoti. Imāni kho, ānanda, cha vivādamūlāni.

45."Furthermore, Ānanda, a monk is a denier and disparager... is envious and stingy... is deceitful and fraudulent... has evil desires and wrong view... is obstinately attached to his own views, tenacious, and difficult to relinquish them. That monk, Ānanda, who is obstinately attached to his own views, tenacious, and difficult to relinquish them, dwells without reverence and respect toward the Teacher, without reverence and respect toward the Dhamma, without reverence and respect toward the Saṅgha, and is not a complete fulfiller of the training. That monk, Ānanda, who dwells without reverence and respect toward the Teacher, toward the Dhamma... toward the Saṅgha... and is not a complete fulfiller of the training, generates disputes in the Saṅgha; and that dispute is for the harm of many people, for the unhappiness of many people, for the disadvantage, harm, and pain of many people, of gods and humans. If, Ānanda, you perceive such a root of contention, whether internal or external, you should strive for the abandoning of that evil root of contention. If, Ānanda, you do not perceive such a root of contention, whether internal or external, you should practice so that that evil root of contention does not flow into the future. Thus, that evil root of contention is abandoned; thus, there is no future flowing in of that evil root of contention. These, Ānanda, are the six roots of contention.

46. ‘‘Cattārimāni, ānanda, adhikaraṇāni. Katamāni cattāri? Vivādādhikaraṇaṃ, anuvādādhikaraṇaṃ, āpattādhikaraṇaṃ, kiccādhikaraṇaṃ – imāni kho, ānanda, cattāri adhikaraṇāni. Satta kho panime, ānanda, adhikaraṇasamathā – uppannuppannānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ samathāya vūpasamāya sammukhāvinayo dātabbo, sativinayo dātabbo, amūḷhavinayo dātabbo, paṭiññāya kāretabbaṃ, yebhuyyasikā, tassapāpiyasikā, tiṇavatthārako.

46."Ānanda, there are these four kinds of legal issues. What are the four? Legal issues concerning disputes, legal issues concerning accusations, legal issues concerning offenses, and legal issues concerning duties. These, Ānanda, are the four kinds of legal issues. And there are, Ānanda, these seven ways of settling legal issues—for the resolution and pacification of legal issues as they arise: settlement in the presence, settlement by mindfulness, settlement for one formerly insane, settlement by acknowledgment, settlement by majority, settlement for obstinacy, and covering over with grass.

47. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, sammukhāvinayo hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhū vivadanti dhammoti vā adhammoti vā vinayoti vā avinayoti vā. Tehānanda, bhikkhūhi sabbeheva samaggehi sannipatitabbaṃ. Sannipatitvā dhammanetti samanumajjitabbā. Dhammanettiṃ samanumajjitvā yathā tattha sameti tathā taṃ adhikaraṇaṃ vūpasametabbaṃ. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, sammukhāvinayo hoti; evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – sammukhāvinayena.

47."And how, Ānanda, does settlement in the presence come about? Here, Ānanda, monks dispute about whether something is Dhamma or not Dhamma, Vinaya or not Vinaya. Ānanda, all those monks should assemble in concord. Having assembled, the guiding principle of the Dhamma should be examined. After examining the guiding principle of the Dhamma, that legal issue should be settled in accordance with how it accords therewith. Thus, Ānanda, does settlement in the presence come about; and this is how some legal issues are settled here—namely, by settlement in the presence.

48. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, yebhuyyasikā hoti? Te ce, ānanda, bhikkhū na sakkonti taṃ adhikaraṇaṃ tasmiṃ āvāse vūpasametuṃ. Tehānanda, bhikkhūhi yasmiṃ āvāse bahutarā bhikkhū so āvāso gantabbo. Tattha sabbeheva samaggehi sannipatitabbaṃ. Sannipatitvā dhammanetti samanumajjitabbā. Dhammanettiṃ samanumajjitvā yathā tattha sameti tathā taṃ adhikaraṇaṃ vūpasametabbaṃ. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, yebhuyyasikā hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – yebhuyyasikāya.

48."And how, Ānanda, does settlement by majority come about? If, Ānanda, those monks are unable to settle that legal issue in that monastery, then, Ānanda, those monks should go to a monastery where there are more monks. There, all should assemble in concord. Having assembled, the guiding principle of the Dhamma should be examined. After examining the guiding principle of the Dhamma, that legal issue should be settled in accordance with how it accords therewith. Thus, Ānanda, does settlement by majority come about; and this is how some legal issues are settled here—namely, by settlement by majority.

49. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, sativinayo hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhū bhikkhuṃ evarūpāya garukāya āpattiyā codenti pārājikena vā pārājikasāmantena vā – ‘saratāyasmā evarūpiṃ [evarūpaṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.) evarūpāya-iti vuccamānavacanena sameti. vinayenapi saṃsandetabbaṃ] garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti? So evamāha – ‘na kho ahaṃ, āvuso, sarāmi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. Tassa kho [tassa kho evaṃ (sabbattha)], ānanda, bhikkhuno sativinayo dātabbo. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, sativinayo hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – sativinayena.

49."And how, Ānanda, does settlement by mindfulness come about? Here, Ānanda, monks accuse a monk of a serious offense of this kind, either a defeat or bordering on a defeat, saying: ‘Venerable, do you remember committing such a serious offense, a defeat or bordering on a defeat?’ He replies: ‘Friends, I do not remember committing such a serious offense, a defeat or bordering on a defeat.’ Then, Ānanda, settlement by mindfulness should be given to that monk. Thus, Ānanda, does settlement by mindfulness come about; and this is how some legal issues are settled here—namely, by settlement by mindfulness.

50. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, amūḷhavinayo hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhū bhikkhuṃ evarūpāya garukāya āpattiyā codenti pārājikena vā pārājikasāmantena vā – ‘saratāyasmā evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti? (So evamāha – ‘na kho ahaṃ, āvuso, sarāmi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. Tamenaṃ so nibbeṭhentaṃ ativeṭheti – ‘iṅghāyasmā sādhukameva jānāhi yadi sarasi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti.) [( ) etthantare pāṭho cūḷava. 237 natthi tassapāpiyasikāvāreevetena bhavitabbaṃ] So evamāha – ‘ahaṃ kho, āvuso, ummādaṃ pāpuṇiṃ cetaso vipariyāsaṃ. Tena me ummattakena bahuṃ assāmaṇakaṃ ajjhāciṇṇaṃ bhāsitaparikkantaṃ [bhāsitaparikantaṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)]. Nāhaṃ taṃ sarāmi. Mūḷhena me etaṃ kata’nti. Tassa kho [tassa kho evaṃ (syā. kaṃ. ka.)], ānanda, bhikkhuno amūḷhavinayo dātabbo. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, amūḷhavinayo hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – amūḷhavinayena.

50.And how, Ānanda, does the verdict of innocence due to insanity come about? Here, Ānanda, monks accuse a monk of a serious offense, either a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense, saying: ‘Venerable, do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense?’ (He replies: ‘Friends, I do not recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense.’ They press him further as he tries to evade the issue: ‘Come, venerable, reflect carefully—do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense.’). He says: ‘Friends, I became insane, my mind was deranged. While insane, I did many things unbecoming a recluse, and there was transgression in speech. I do not recall it. This was done due to my insanity.’ Then, Ānanda, the verdict of innocence due to insanity should be granted to that monk. Thus, Ānanda, the verdict of innocence due to insanity comes about, and in this way certain disputes are settled—namely, by the verdict of innocence due to insanity.

51. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, paṭiññātakaraṇaṃ hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhu codito vā acodito vā āpattiṃ sarati, vivarati uttānīkaroti [uttāniṃ karoti (ka.)]. Tena, ānanda, bhikkhunā vuḍḍhataraṃ bhikkhuṃ [vuḍḍhataro bhikkhu (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] upasaṅkamitvā ekaṃsaṃ cīvaraṃ katvā pāde vanditvā ukkuṭikaṃ nisīditvā añjaliṃ paggahetvā evamassa vacanīyo – ‘ahaṃ, bhante, itthannāmaṃ āpattiṃ āpanno, taṃ paṭidesemī’ti. So evamāha – ‘passasī’ti? ‘Āma passāmī’ti. ‘Āyatiṃ saṃvareyyāsī’ti. (‘Saṃvarissāmī’ti.) [( ) vinaye natthi] Evaṃ kho, ānanda, paṭiññātakaraṇaṃ hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – paṭiññātakaraṇena.

51.And how, Ānanda, does confession take place? Here, Ānanda, a monk, whether accused or not, remembers an offense, reveals it, and makes it plain. Then, Ānanda, that monk should approach a more senior monk, arrange his robe over one shoulder, bow at his feet, sit in a squatting position with hands raised in respect, and say: ‘Venerable, I have committed such-and-such an offense. I confess it.’ He asks: ‘Do you see it?’ ‘Yes, I see it.’ ‘In the future, restrain yourself.’ (‘I will restrain myself.’) Thus, Ānanda, confession takes place, and in this way some disputes are settled—namely, by confession.

52. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, tassapāpiyasikā hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhu bhikkhuṃ evarūpāya garukāya āpattiyā codeti pārājikena vā pārājikasāmantena vā – ‘saratāyasmā evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti? So evamāha – ‘na kho ahaṃ, āvuso, sarāmi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. Tamenaṃ so nibbeṭhentaṃ ativeṭheti – ‘iṅghāyasmā sādhukameva jānāhi yadi sarasi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. So evamāha – ‘na kho ahaṃ, āvuso, sarāmi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā; sarāmi ca kho ahaṃ, āvuso, evarūpiṃ appamattikaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā’ti. Tamenaṃ so nibbeṭhentaṃ ativeṭheti – ‘iṅghāyasmā sādhukameva jānāhi yadi sarasi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti? So evamāha – ‘imañhi nāmāhaṃ, āvuso, appamattikaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitvā apuṭṭho paṭijānissāmi. Kiṃ panāhaṃ evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitvā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā puṭṭho napaṭijānissāmī’ti? So evamāha – ‘imañhi nāma tvaṃ, āvuso, appamattikaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitvā apuṭṭho napaṭijānissasi, kiṃ pana tvaṃ evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitvā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā puṭṭho [apuṭṭho (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] paṭijānissasi? Iṅghāyasmā sādhukameva jānāhi yadi sarasi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. So evamāha – ‘sarāmi kho ahaṃ, āvuso, evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā. Davā me etaṃ vuttaṃ, ravā me etaṃ vuttaṃ – nāhaṃ taṃ sarāmi evarūpiṃ garukaṃ āpattiṃ āpajjitā pārājikaṃ vā pārājikasāmantaṃ vā’ti. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, tassapāpiyasikā hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – tassapāpiyasikāya.

52.And how, Ānanda, does a tassapāpiyasikā occur? Here, Ānanda, a monk accuses another monk of a serious offense, either a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense, saying: 'Venerable, do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense?' He replies: 'No, friend, I do not recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense.' Then the accuser, while cross-examining him, presses him further: 'Come, venerable, think carefully—do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense?' He replies: 'No, friend, I do not recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense; but I do recall committing such a minor offense.' Then the accuser, while cross-examining him, presses him further: 'Come, venerable, think carefully—do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense?' He replies: 'Indeed, friend, having committed such a minor offense, I would confess it even if unasked. How then could I, having committed such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense, not confess it when asked?' The accuser then says: 'Indeed, friend, you would not confess such a minor offense, even if unasked; how then would you, having committed such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense, confess it when asked? Come, venerable, think carefully—do you recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense?' He then says: 'Friend, I do recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense. But I spoke in haste, I spoke carelessly—I said I did not recall committing such a serious offense, a defeat offense or one bordering on a defeat offense.' Thus, Ānanda, a tassapāpiyasikā occurs. And in this way, some disputes are settled—namely, by the tassapāpiyasikā.

53. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, tiṇavatthārako hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhūnaṃ bhaṇḍanajātānaṃ kalahajātānaṃ vivādāpannānaṃ viharataṃ bahuṃ assāmaṇakaṃ ajjhāciṇṇaṃ hoti bhāsitaparikkantaṃ. Tehānanda, bhikkhūhi sabbeheva samaggehi sannipatitabbaṃ. Sannipatitvā ekatopakkhikānaṃ bhikkhūnaṃ byattena [byattatarena (sī. pī. ka.)] bhikkhunā uṭṭhāyāsanā ekaṃsaṃ cīvaraṃ katvā añjaliṃ paṇāmetvā saṅgho ñāpetabbo –

53.And how, Ānanda, does a grass-covering take place? Here, Ānanda, when monks are living in quarreling, contention, and dispute, much unascetic, unbefitting conduct has become habitual, and there has been transgression in speech. Then, Ānanda, all those monks should gather together harmoniously. Having gathered, a competent monk from one side should rise from his seat, arrange his robe over one shoulder, salute with joined palms, and inform the Saṅgha:

‘Suṇātu me, bhante, saṅgho. Idaṃ amhākaṃ bhaṇḍanajātānaṃ kalahajātānaṃ vivādāpannānaṃ viharataṃ bahuṃ assāmaṇakaṃ ajjhāciṇṇaṃ bhāsitaparikkantaṃ. Yadi saṅghassa pattakallaṃ, ahaṃ yā ceva imesaṃ āyasmantānaṃ āpatti yā ca attano āpatti, imesañceva āyasmantānaṃ atthāya attano ca atthāya, saṅghamajjhe tiṇavatthārakena deseyyaṃ, ṭhapetvā thullavajjaṃ ṭhapetvā gihipaṭisaṃyutta’’’nti.

'Venerable Saṅgha, hear me. While we were dwelling in quarreling, contention, and dispute, much unascetic conduct has been habitually practiced, and there has been transgression in speech. If it is fitting for the Saṅgha, then for the sake of these venerable ones and for my own sake, I will make a grass-covering declaration in the midst of the Saṅgha, setting aside major offenses and those connected with laypeople.'

‘‘Athāparesaṃ ekatopakkhikānaṃ bhikkhūnaṃ byattena bhikkhunā uṭṭhāyāsanā ekaṃsaṃ cīvaraṃ katvā añjaliṃ paṇāmetvā saṅgho ñāpetabbo –

Then, a competent monk from the other side should rise from his seat, arrange his robe over one shoulder, salute with joined palms, and inform the Saṅgha:

‘Suṇātu me, bhante, saṅgho. Idaṃ amhākaṃ bhaṇḍanajātānaṃ kalahajātānaṃ vivādāpannānaṃ viharataṃ bahuṃ assāmaṇakaṃ ajjhāciṇṇaṃ bhāsitaparikkantaṃ. Yadi saṅghassa pattakallaṃ, ahaṃ yā ceva imesaṃ āyasmantānaṃ āpatti yā ca attano āpatti, imesañceva āyasmantānaṃ atthāya attano ca atthāya, saṅghamajjhe tiṇavatthārakena deseyyaṃ, ṭhapetvā thullavajjaṃ ṭhapetvā gihipaṭisaṃyutta’’’nti.

Venerable sir, may the Saṅgha hear me. While we were dwelling in quarrels, disputes, and contentions, much unascetic, unbefitting conduct has been habitually practiced, and there has been transgression in speech. If it is fitting for the Saṅgha, I would declare both the offenses of these venerable ones and my own offenses in the midst of the Saṅgha by means of a grass-covering declaration—both for the sake of these venerable ones and for my own sake—setting aside major offenses and those connected with laypeople.

‘‘Evaṃ kho, ānanda, tiṇavatthārako hoti, evañca panidhekaccānaṃ adhikaraṇānaṃ vūpasamo hoti yadidaṃ – tiṇavatthārakena.

Thus, Ānanda, there is the grass-covering. And in this way, for some here, disputes are settled, that is, by means of the grass-covering.

54. ‘‘Chayime, ānanda, dhammā sāraṇīyā piyakaraṇā garukaraṇā saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattanti. Katame cha? Idhānanda, bhikkhuno mettaṃ kāyakammaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti sabrahmacārīsu āvi ceva raho ca. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

54.Ānanda, these six principles lead to cordiality, engender affection, promote respect, lead to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity. What six? Here, Ānanda, a monk maintains bodily acts of loving-kindness toward his fellow practitioners of the holy life, both in public and in private. This principle leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno mettaṃ vacīkammaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti sabrahmacārīsu āvi ceva raho ca. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu maintains loving speech in his verbal action towards his fellow practitioners of the holy life, both in public and in private. This principle, too, leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno mettaṃ manokammaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti sabrahmacārīsu āvi ceva raho ca. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu maintains a benevolent mental attitude toward his fellow practitioners of the holy life, both in public and in private. This principle, too, leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhu – ye te lābhā dhammikā dhammaladdhā antamaso pattapariyāpannamattampi tathārūpehi lābhehi – apaṭivibhattabhogī hoti, sīlavantehi sabrahmacārīhi sādhāraṇabhogī. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu—whatever legitimate acquisitions he obtains, gained in accordance with the Dhamma, even so little as the food received in his alms bowl—is one who partakes of them in common with his virtuous fellow practitioners of the holy life. This principle, too, leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhu – yāni tāni sīlāni akhaṇḍāni acchiddāni asabalāni akammāsāni bhujissāni viññuppasatthāni aparāmaṭṭhāni samādhisaṃvattanikāni tathārūpesu sīlesu – sīlasāmaññagato viharati sabrahmacārīhi āvi ceva raho ca. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

Furthermore, Ānanda, a monk, possessing virtue, dwells in the company of his fellow practitioners of the holy life, both openly and privately, with regard to those virtues that are unbroken, without gaps, unspotted, unblemished, freeing, praised by the wise, ungrasped, and conducive to samādhi. This principle, too, leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhu – yāyaṃ diṭṭhi ariyā niyyānikā niyyāti takkarassa sammā dukkhakkhayā tathārūpāya diṭṭhiyā – diṭṭhisāmaññagato viharati sabrahmacārīhi āvi ceva raho ca. Ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati. Ime kho, ānanda, cha sāraṇīyā dhammā piyakaraṇā garukaraṇā saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattanti.

Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu—through that view which is noble, emancipating, and leads one who acts rightly to the complete destruction of suffering—lives in communion with his fellow practitioners of the holy life, both in public and in private. This principle, too, leads to cordiality, engenders affection, promotes respect, leads to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity. These, Ānanda, are the six principles conducive to cordiality, engendering affection, promoting respect, leading to cohesion, to non-dispute, to concord, and to unity.

‘‘Ime ce tumhe, ānanda, cha sāraṇīye dhamme samādāya vatteyyātha, passatha no tumhe, ānanda, taṃ vacanapathaṃ aṇuṃ vā thūlaṃ vā yaṃ tumhe nādhivāseyyāthā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Tasmātihānanda, ime cha sāraṇīye dhamme samādāya vattatha. Taṃ vo bhavissati dīgharattaṃ hitāya sukhāyā’’ti.

Ānanda, if you were to undertake and maintain these six principles of cordiality, would you perceive any utterance, whether minor or major, that you could not tolerate?" "No, venerable sir." "Therefore, Ānanda, undertake and maintain these six principles of cordiality. That will be for your long-term welfare and happiness.

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamano āyasmā ānando bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandīti.

Thus spoke the Blessed One. The Venerable Ānanda, pleased, rejoiced at the Blessed One's words.

Sāmagāmasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ catutthaṃ.

The Sāmagāma Discourse, the fourth, is finished.
