# 99. Subhasuttaṃ

## 99. The Subha Discourse

462. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tena kho pana samayena subho māṇavo todeyyaputto sāvatthiyaṃ paṭivasati aññatarassa gahapatissa nivesane kenacideva karaṇīyena. Atha kho subho māṇavo todeyyaputto yassa gahapatissa nivesane paṭivasati taṃ gahapatiṃ etadavoca – ‘‘sutaṃ metaṃ, gahapati – ‘avivittā sāvatthī arahantehī’ti. Kaṃ nu khvajja samaṇaṃ vā brāhmaṇaṃ vā payirupāseyyāmā’’ti? ‘‘Ayaṃ, bhante, bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Taṃ, bhante, bhagavantaṃ payirupāsassū’’ti. Atha kho subho māṇavo todeyyaputto tassa gahapatissa paṭissutvā yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavatā saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘brāhmaṇā, bho gotama, evamāhaṃsu – ‘gahaṭṭho ārādhako hoti ñāyaṃ dhammaṃ kusalaṃ, na pabbajito ārādhako hoti ñāyaṃ dhammaṃ kusala’nti. Idha bhavaṃ gotamo kimāhā’’ti?

462.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. Now at that time, the young brahmin Subha, the son of Todeyya, was staying in Savatthi at the residence of a certain householder for some business or other. Then the young brahmin Subha, the son of Todeyya, said this to the householder at whose residence he was staying: "Householder, I have heard that Savatthi is not devoid of arahants. Which ascetic or brahmin should we attend on today?" "Sir, the Blessed One is dwelling at Savatthi in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. You should attend on the Blessed One, sir." Then the young brahmin Subha, the son of Todeyya, having agreed with the householder, approached the Blessed One. Having approached, he exchanged courteous greetings with the Blessed One. When the polite and amiable talk was finished, he sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the young brahmin Subha, the son of Todeyya, said to the Blessed One: "Master Gotama, the brahmins say this: ‘A householder is capable of achieving what is right, what is according to the Dhamma, what is skillful, but not a renunciant.’ What does Master Gotama say about this?"

463. ‘‘Vibhajjavādo kho ahamettha, māṇava; nāhamettha ekaṃsavādo. Gihissa vāhaṃ, māṇava, pabbajitassa vā micchāpaṭipattiṃ na vaṇṇemi. Gihī vā hi, māṇava, pabbajito vā micchāpaṭipanno micchāpaṭipattādhikaraṇahetu na ārādhako hoti ñāyaṃ dhammaṃ kusalaṃ. Gihissa vāhaṃ, māṇava, pabbajitassa vā sammāpaṭipattiṃ vaṇṇemi. Gihī vā hi, māṇava, pabbajito vā sammāpaṭipanno sammāpaṭipattādhikaraṇahetu ārādhako hoti ñāyaṃ dhammaṃ kusala’’nti.

463."I am one who speaks analytically, young man; I am not one who speaks one-sidedly in this matter. I do not praise wrong practice, whether in a householder or one gone forth. For, young man, whether a householder or one gone forth, if they are practicing wrongly, because of the cause inherent in that wrong practice, they do not achieve the right, the Dhamma, the wholesome. I praise right practice, whether in a householder or one gone forth. For, young man, whether a householder or one gone forth, if they are practicing rightly, because of the cause inherent in that right practice, they achieve the right, the Dhamma, the wholesome."

‘‘Brāhmaṇā, bho gotama, evamāhaṃsu – ‘mahaṭṭhamidaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ gharāvāsakammaṭṭhānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti; appaṭṭhamidaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ pabbajjā kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaphalaṃ hotī’ti. Idha bhavaṃ gotamo kimāhā’’ti.

"Brahmins, Master Gotama, say this: 'Living the household life is a great endeavor, a great task, a great undertaking, a great effort, and yields great fruit. The life of homelessness is a small endeavor, a small task, a small undertaking, a small effort, and yields little fruit.' What does Master Gotama say about this?"

‘‘Etthāpi kho ahaṃ, māṇava, vibhajjavādo; nāhamettha ekaṃsavādo. Atthi, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti; atthi, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti; atthi, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti; atthi, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti. Katamañca, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti? Kasi kho, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti. Katamañca, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti? Kasiyeva kho, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti. Katamañca, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti? Vaṇijjā kho, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti. Katamañca māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti? Vaṇijjāyeva kho, māṇava, kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti.

"Here too, young man, I speak analytically; I do not make a categorical statement. There is, young man, a pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit. There is, young man, a pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit. There is, young man, a pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit. There is, young man, a pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit. And what, young man, is the pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit? Farming, young man, is the pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit. And what, young man, is the pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit? Farming, young man, is the pursuit that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, great in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit. And what, young man, is the pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit? Trade, young man, is the pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it fails, it yields little fruit. And what, young man, is the pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit? Trade, young man, is the pursuit that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, small in undertaking—when it succeeds, it yields great fruit."

464. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, māṇava, kasi kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti; evameva kho, māṇava, gharāvāsakammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, kasiyeva kammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti; evameva kho, māṇava, gharāvāsakammaṭṭhānaṃ mahaṭṭhaṃ mahākiccaṃ mahādhikaraṇaṃ mahāsamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, vaṇijjā kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti; evameva kho, māṇava, pabbajjā kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ vipajjamānaṃ appaphalaṃ hoti. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, vaṇijjāyeva kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hoti; evameva kho, māṇava, pabbajjā kammaṭṭhānaṃ appaṭṭhaṃ appakiccaṃ appādhikaraṇaṃ appasamārambhaṃ sampajjamānaṃ mahapphalaṃ hotī’’ti.

464."Young man, just as farming is a work that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, and great in undertaking, but when it goes wrong, it yields little fruit; so too, young man, household life is a work that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, and great in undertaking, but when it goes wrong, it yields little fruit. Just as farming is a work that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, and great in undertaking, and when it goes right, it yields abundant fruit; so too, young man, household life is a work that is great in effort, great in task, great in responsibility, and great in undertaking, and when it goes right, it yields abundant fruit. Just as trade is a work that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, and small in undertaking, but when it goes wrong, it yields little fruit; so too, young man, the going forth is a work that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, and small in undertaking, but when it goes wrong, it yields little fruit. Just as trade is a work that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, and small in undertaking, and when it goes right, it yields abundant fruit; so too, young man, the going forth is a work that is small in effort, small in task, small in responsibility, and small in undertaking, and when it goes right, it yields abundant fruit."

‘‘Brāhmaṇā, bho gotama, pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāyā’’ti. ‘‘Ye te, māṇava, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya – sace te agaru – sādhu te pañca dhamme imasmiṃ parisati bhāsassū’’ti. ‘‘Na kho me, bho gotama, garu yatthassu bhavanto vā nisinno bhavantarūpo vā’’ti [nisinnā bhavantarūpā vāti (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)]. ‘‘Tena hi, māṇava, bhāsassū’’ti. ‘‘Saccaṃ kho, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā paṭhamaṃ dhammaṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya. Tapaṃ kho, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā dutiyaṃ dhammaṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya. Brahmacariyaṃ kho, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā tatiyaṃ dhammaṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya. Ajjhenaṃ kho, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā catutthaṃ dhammaṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya. Cāgaṃ kho, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā pañcamaṃ dhammaṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāya. Brāhmaṇā, bho gotama, ime pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya, kusalassa ārādhanāyāti. Idha bhavaṃ gotamo kimāhā’’ti?

"Venerable Gotama, brahmins describe five things as conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful." "Young man, if it is not a burden for you, please speak about those five things that brahmins describe as conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful, in this assembly.” "It is not a burden for me, Venerable Gotama, wherever the venerable ones or those like the venerable ones are seated." "Then, young man, please speak." "Indeed, Venerable Gotama, brahmins describe truthfulness as the first thing conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. They describe austerity as the second thing conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. They describe celibacy as the third thing conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. They describe study as the fourth thing conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. They describe generosity as the fifth thing conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. Venerable Gotama, brahmins describe these five things as conducive to making merit, for the cultivation of what is skillful. What does the Venerable Gotama say about this?"

465. ‘‘Kiṃ pana, māṇava, atthi koci brāhmaṇānaṃ ekabrāhmaṇopi yo evamāha – ‘ahaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemī’’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho gotama’’. ‘‘Kiṃ pana, māṇava, atthi koci brāhmaṇānaṃ ekācariyopi ekācariyapācariyopi yāva sattamā ācariyamahayugāpi yo evamāha – ‘ahaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemī’’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho gotama’’. ‘‘Kiṃ pana, māṇava, yepi te brāhmaṇānaṃ pubbakā isayo mantānaṃ kattāro mantānaṃ pavattāro yesamidaṃ etarahi brāhmaṇā porāṇaṃ mantapadaṃ gītaṃ pavuttaṃ samihitaṃ tadanugāyanti tadanubhāsanti bhāsitamanubhāsanti vācitamanuvācenti, seyyathidaṃ – aṭṭhako vāmako vāmadevo vessāmitto yamataggi aṅgīraso bhāradvājo vāseṭṭho kassapo bhagu, tepi evamāhaṃsu – ‘mayaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemā’’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho gotama’’.

465.“But, young man, is there even a single brahmin who says: ‘Having directly known and realized for myself these five things, I declare their outcome’? ” “No, indeed, Master Gotama.” “But, young man, is there even a single teacher among the brahmins, or a teacher of teachers, up to the seventh generation of teachers, who says: ‘Having directly known and realized for myself these five things, I declare their outcome’? ” “No, indeed, Master Gotama.” “But, young man, what about those ancient seers of the brahmins, the makers of mantra-verses, the propounders of mantra-verses, whose ancient mantra-verses the brahmins of today chant, recite, and compile, following along and repeating what has been spoken and recited—such as Aṭṭhaka, Vāmaka, Vāmadeva, Vessāmitta, Yamataggi, Aṅgīrasa, Bhāradvāja, Vāseṭṭha, Kassapa, and Bhagu—did they too say: ‘We declare the outcome of these five things after having directly known and realized them for ourselves’? ” “No, indeed, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Iti kira, māṇava, natthi koci brāhmaṇānaṃ ekabrāhmaṇopi yo evamāha – ‘ahaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemī’ti; natthi koci brāhmaṇānaṃ ekācariyopi ekācariyapācariyopi yāva sattamā ācariyamahayugāpi yo evamāha – ‘ahaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemī’ti; yepi te brāhmaṇānaṃ pubbakā isayo mantānaṃ kattāro mantānaṃ pavattāro, yesamidaṃ etarahi brāhmaṇā porāṇaṃ mantapadaṃ gītaṃ pavuttaṃ samihitaṃ, tadanugāyanti tadanubhāsanti bhāsitamanubhāsanti vācitamanuvācenti, seyyathidaṃ – aṭṭhako vāmako vāmadevo vessāmitto yamataggi aṅgīraso bhāradvājo vāseṭṭho kassapo bhagu. Tepi na evamāhaṃsu – ‘mayaṃ imesaṃ pañcannaṃ dhammānaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā vipākaṃ pavedemā’ti.

“So it is, young man, there is not a single brahmin who says: ‘I declare the outcome of these five things after having directly known and realized them for myself.’ There is not a single teacher among the brahmins, nor a teacher of teachers, even up to the seventh generation of teachers, who says: ‘I declare the outcome of these five things after having directly known and realized them for myself.’ Even those ancient seers of the brahmins, the makers of mantra-verses, the propounders of mantra-verses, whose ancient mantra-verses the brahmins of today chant, recite, and compile, following along and repeating what has been spoken and recited—such as Aṭṭhaka, Vāmaka, Vāmadeva, Vessāmitta, Yamataggi, Aṅgīrasa, Bhāradvāja, Vāseṭṭha, Kassapa, and Bhagu—they too did not say: ‘We declare the outcome of these five things after having directly known and realized them for ourselves.’ ”

‘‘Seyyathāpi, māṇava, andhaveṇi paramparāsaṃsattā purimopi na passati majjhimopi na passati pacchimopi na passati; evameva kho, māṇava, andhaveṇūpamaṃ maññe brāhmaṇānaṃ bhāsitaṃ sampajjati – purimopi na passati majjhimopi na passati pacchimopi na passatī’’ti.

“Just as, young man, a string of blind men, each clinging to the one in front—the first one does not see, the middle one does not see, the last one does not see—so too, young man, I say that the brahmins’ statement is like a string of blind men: the first one does not see, the middle one does not see, the last one does not see.”

466. Evaṃ vutte, subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavatā andhaveṇūpamena vuccamāno kupito anattamano bhagavantaṃyeva khuṃsento bhagavantaṃyeva vambhento bhagavantaṃyeva vadamāno – ‘samaṇo gotamo pāpito bhavissatī’ti bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘brāhmaṇo, bho gotama, pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko evamāha – ‘evameva panidhekacce [panimeke (sabbattha)] samaṇabrāhmaṇā uttarimanussadhammā alamariyañāṇadassanavisesaṃ paṭijānanti. Tesamidaṃ bhāsitaṃ hassakaṃyeva sampajjati, nāmakaṃyeva sampajjati, rittakaṃyeva sampajjati, tucchakaṃyeva sampajjati. Kathañhi nāma manussabhūto uttarimanussadhammā alamariyañāṇadassanavisesaṃ ñassati vā dakkhati vā sacchi vā karissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjatī’’’ti?

466.When this was said, the young brahmin Subha, Todeyya’s son, being spoken to by the Blessed One with the simile of the string of blind men, became angry and displeased, and reviling the Blessed One, disparaging the Blessed One, and speaking ill of the Blessed One, said to the Blessed One: “The ascetic Gotama will be brought to error.” He then said to the Blessed One: “The brahmin Pokkharasāti, Master Gotama, of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, says thus: ‘Indeed, some ascetics and brahmins here claim a superior human state, a distinction of noble knowledge and vision. But what they say is merely laughable, merely a name, empty, hollow. For how could a human being know or see or realize a superior human state, a distinction of noble knowledge and vision? Such a thing is impossible.’”

‘‘Kiṃ pana, māṇava, brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko sabbesaṃyeva samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ cetasā ceto paricca pajānātī’’ti? ‘‘Sakāyapi hi, bho gotama, puṇṇikāya dāsiyā brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko cetasā ceto paricca na pajānāti, kuto pana sabbesaṃyeva samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ cetasā ceto paricca pajānissatī’’ti?

“But, young man, does the brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, know the minds of all ascetics and brahmins by encompassing their minds with his own mind?” “The brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, does not even know the mind of his own slave woman Puṇṇikā by encompassing her mind with his own mind, so how could he know the minds of all ascetics and brahmins?”

‘‘Seyyathāpi, māṇava, jaccandho puriso na passeyya kaṇhasukkāni rūpāni, na passeyya nīlakāni rūpāni, na passeyya pītakāni rūpāni, na passeyya lohitakāni rūpāni, na passeyya mañjiṭṭhakāni rūpāni, na passeyya samavisamaṃ, na passeyya tārakarūpāni, na passeyya candimasūriye. So evaṃ vadeyya – ‘natthi kaṇhasukkāni rūpāni, natthi kaṇhasukkānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi nīlakāni rūpāni, natthi nīlakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi pītakāni rūpāni, natthi pītakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi lohitakāni rūpāni, natthi lohitakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi mañjiṭṭhakāni rūpāni, natthi mañjiṭṭhakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi samavisamaṃ, natthi samavisamassa dassāvī; natthi tārakarūpāni, natthi tārakarūpānaṃ dassāvī; natthi candimasūriyā, natthi candimasūriyānaṃ dassāvī. Ahametaṃ na jānāmi, ahametaṃ na passāmi; tasmā taṃ natthī’ti. Sammā nu kho so, māṇava, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti?

“Suppose, young man, there was a man blind from birth who could not see black and white forms, nor blue forms, nor yellow forms, nor red forms, nor crimson forms, nor even and uneven ground, nor star-forms, nor the moon and sun. He might say: ‘There are no black and white forms, and no one who sees black and white forms; there are no blue forms, and no one who sees blue forms; there are no yellow forms, and no one who sees yellow forms; there are no red forms, and no one who sees red forms; there are no crimson forms, and no one who sees crimson forms; there is no even and uneven ground, and no one who sees even and uneven ground; there are no star-forms, and no one who sees star-forms; there is no moon or sun, and no one who sees the moon or sun. I do not know this, I do not see this; therefore it does not exist.’ Would he be speaking rightly, young man?”

‘‘No hidaṃ, bho gotama. Atthi kaṇhasukkāni rūpāni, atthi kaṇhasukkānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi nīlakāni rūpāni, atthi nīlakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi pītakāni rūpāni, atthi pītakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi lohitakāni rūpāni, atthi lohitakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi mañjiṭṭhakāni rūpāni, atthi mañjiṭṭhakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi samavisamaṃ, atthi samavisamassa dassāvī; atthi tārakarūpāni, atthi tārakarūpānaṃ dassāvī; atthi candimasūriyā, atthi candimasūriyānaṃ dassāvī. ‘Ahametaṃ na jānāmi, ahametaṃ na passāmi; tasmā taṃ natthī’ti; na hi so, bho gotama, sammā vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti.

“No, Master Gotama. There are black and white forms, and one who sees them; there are blue forms, and one who sees blue forms; there are yellow forms, and one who sees yellow forms; there are red forms, and one who sees red forms; there are crimson forms, and one who sees crimson forms; there is even and uneven ground, and one who sees even and uneven ground; there are star-forms, and one who sees star-forms; there are the moon and sun, and one who sees the moon and sun. To say, ‘I do not know this, I do not see this; therefore it does not exist’—he would not be speaking correctly, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Evameva kho, māṇava, brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko andho acakkhuko. So vata uttarimanussadhammā alamariyañāṇadassanavisesaṃ ñassati vā dakkhati vā sacchi vā karissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati’’.

“Just so, young man, the brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, is blind and sightless. That he should know or see or realize a superior human state, a distinction of noble knowledge and vision—this is impossible.”

467. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, māṇava, ye te kosalakā brāhmaṇamahāsālā, seyyathidaṃ – caṅkī brāhmaṇo tārukkho brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti brāhmaṇo jāṇussoṇi brāhmaṇo pitā ca [vā (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] te todeyyo, katamā nesaṃ seyyo [seyyā (syā. kaṃ.)], yaṃ vā te sammuccā [sammusā (sī. pī.)] vācaṃ bhāseyyuṃ yaṃ vā asammuccā’’ti? ‘‘Sammuccā, bho gotama’’.

467.“What do you think, young man? Those Kosalan brahmins of great wealth—such as the brahmin Caṅkī, the brahmin Tārukkha, the brahmin Pokkharasāti, the brahmin Jānussoṇi, and your father Todeyya—which is better for them: that they speak deliberately or undeliberately?” “Deliberately, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Katamā nesaṃ seyyo, yaṃ vā te mantā vācaṃ bhāseyyuṃ yaṃ vā amantā’’ti? ‘‘Mantā, bho gotama’’.

“Which is better for them: that they speak after reflection or without reflection?” “After reflection, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Katamā nesaṃ seyyo, yaṃ vā te paṭisaṅkhāya vācaṃ bhāseyyuṃ yaṃ vā appaṭisaṅkhāyā’’ti? ‘‘Paṭisaṅkhāya, bho gotama’’.

“Which is better for them: that they speak after consideration or without consideration?” “After consideration, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Katamā nesaṃ seyyo, yaṃ vā te atthasaṃhitaṃ vācaṃ bhāseyyuṃ yaṃ vā anatthasaṃhita’’nti? ‘‘Atthasaṃhitaṃ, bho gotama’’.

“Which is better for them: that they speak words connected with benefit or words not connected with benefit?” “Connected with benefit, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, māṇava, yadi evaṃ sante, brāhmaṇena pokkharasātinā opamaññena subhagavanikena sammuccā vācā bhāsitā asammuccā’’ti [asammusā vāti (pī.) evamitarapañhattayepi vāsaddena saha dissati]? ‘‘Asammuccā, bho gotama’’.

“What do you think, young man? If that is the case, has the brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, spoken deliberately or undeliberately?” “Undeliberately, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Mantā vācā bhāsitā amantā vā’’ti? ‘‘Amantā, bho gotama’’.

“Has he spoken after reflection or without reflection?” “Without reflection, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Paṭisaṅkhāya vācā bhāsitā appaṭisaṅkhāyā’’ti? ‘‘Appaṭisaṅkhāya, bho gotama’’.

“Has he spoken after consideration or without consideration?” “Without consideration, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Atthasaṃhitā vācā bhāsitā anatthasaṃhitā’’ti? ‘‘Anatthasaṃhitā, bho gotama’’.

“Has he spoken words connected with benefit, or words unconnected with benefit?” “Unconnected with benefit, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Pañca kho ime, māṇava, nīvaraṇā. Katame pañca? Kāmacchandanīvaraṇaṃ, byāpādanīvaraṇaṃ, thīnamiddhanīvaraṇaṃ uddhaccakukkuccanīvaraṇaṃ, vicikicchānīvaraṇaṃ – ime kho, māṇava, pañca nīvaraṇā. Imehi kho māṇava, pañcahi nīvaraṇehi brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko āvuto nivuto ophuṭo [ovuto (sī.), ophuto (syā. kaṃ. pī.)] pariyonaddho. So vata uttarimanussadhammā alamariyañāṇadassanavisesaṃ ñassati vā dakkhati vā sacchi vā karissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati.

“There are these five hindrances, young man. Which five? The hindrance of sensual desire, the hindrance of ill will, the hindrance of sloth and drowsiness, the hindrance of restlessness and remorse, and the hindrance of doubt. These are the five hindrances. The brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, is shrouded, obstructed, covered, enveloped, and fettered by these five hindrances. That he should know or see or realize a superior human state, a distinction of noble knowledge and vision—this is impossible.”

468. ‘‘Pañca kho ime, māṇava, kāmaguṇā. Katame pañca? Cakkhuviññeyyā rūpā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā, sotaviññeyyā saddā…pe… ghānaviññeyyā gandhā… jivhā viññeyyā rasā… kāyaviññeyyā phoṭṭhabbā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā – ime kho, māṇava, pañca kāmaguṇā. Imehi kho, māṇava, pañcahi kāmaguṇehi brāhmaṇo pokkharasāti opamañño subhagavaniko gathito mucchito ajjhopanno anādīnavadassāvī anissaraṇapañño paribhuñjati. So vata uttarimanussadhammā alamariyañāṇadassanavisesaṃ ñassati vā dakkhati vā sacchi vā karissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati.

468.“There are these five strands of sensual pleasure, young man. Which five? Forms cognizable by the eye—desirable, lovely, pleasing, agreeable, connected with sensual pleasure, alluring; sounds cognizable by the ear… smells cognizable by the nose… tastes cognizable by the tongue… touches cognizable by the body—desirable, lovely, pleasing, agreeable, connected with sensual pleasure, alluring. These are the five strands of sensual pleasure. The brahmin Pokkharasāti of the Upamañña clan, from Subhagavana, is ensnared, infatuated, and immersed in these five strands of sensual pleasure, not seeing the danger in them, lacking the wisdom of escape. That he should know or see or realize a superior human state, a distinction of noble knowledge and vision—this is impossible.”

‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, māṇava, yaṃ vā tiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ paṭicca aggiṃ jāleyya yaṃ vā nissaṭṭhatiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ aggiṃ jāleyya, katamo nu khvāssa aggi accimā ceva vaṇṇavā ca pabhassaro cā’’ti? ‘‘Sace taṃ, bho gotama, ṭhānaṃ nissaṭṭhatiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ aggiṃ jāletuṃ, svāssa aggi accimā ceva vaṇṇavā ca pabhassaro cā’’ti. ‘‘Aṭṭhānaṃ kho etaṃ, māṇava, anavakāso yaṃ nissaṭṭhatiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ aggiṃ jāleyya aññatra iddhimatā. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, tiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ paṭicca aggi jalati tathūpamāhaṃ, māṇava, imaṃ pītiṃ vadāmi yāyaṃ pīti pañca kāmaguṇe paṭicca. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, nissaṭṭhatiṇakaṭṭhupādāno [nissaṭṭhatiṇakaṭṭhupādānaṃ paṭicca (sī. pī. ka.)] aggi jalati tathūpamāhaṃ, māṇava, imaṃ pītiṃ vadāmi yāyaṃ pīti aññatreva kāmehi aññatra akusalehi dhammehi.

“What do you think, young man? If one were to light a fire relying on grass and sticks, or if one were to light a fire not relying on grass and sticks, which of these two fires would be more radiant, glorious, and luminous?” “If it were possible, Master Gotama, to light a fire not relying on grass and sticks, that fire would be more radiant, glorious, and luminous.” “It is impossible, young man, it is not possible, to light a fire not relying on grass and sticks, except through supernatural power. Just as, young man, a fire burns relying on grass and sticks, in the same way, young man, I describe that rapture that arises dependent on the five strands of sensual pleasure. Just as, young man, a fire burns not relying on grass and sticks, in the same way, young man, I describe that rapture that is apart from sensual pleasures, apart from unwholesome states.

‘‘Katamā ca, māṇava, pīti aññatreva kāmehi aññatra akusalehi dhammehi? Idha, māṇava, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi…pe… paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, māṇava, pīti aññatreva kāmehi aññatra akusalehi dhammehi. Puna caparaṃ, māṇava, bhikkhu vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā…pe… dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, māṇava, pīti aññatreva kāmehi aññatra akusalehi dhammehi.

“And what, young man, is rapture apart from sensual pleasures, apart from unwholesome states? Here, young man, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures ... enters and remains in the first jhāna. This, young man, is rapture apart from sensual pleasures, apart from unwholesome states. Furthermore, young man, a bhikkhu, with the stilling of thought and examination ... enters and remains in the second jhāna. This too, young man, is rapture apart from sensual pleasures, apart from unwholesome states.

469. ‘‘Ye te, māṇava, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, katamettha [kamettha (ka. sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] brāhmaṇā dhammaṃ mahapphalataraṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāyā’’ti? ‘‘Yeme, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, cāgamettha brāhmaṇā dhammaṃ mahapphalataraṃ paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāyā’’ti.

469.“Those brahmins, young man, who describe five things for the making of merit, for the cultivation of the wholesome, which of these things do the brahmins describe as more fruitful for the making of merit, for the cultivation of the wholesome?” “Among those five things, Master Gotama, which the brahmins describe for the making of merit, for the cultivation of the wholesome, they describe generosity as more fruitful for the making of merit, for the cultivation of the wholesome.”

‘‘Taṃ ki maññasi, māṇava, idha aññatarassa brāhmaṇassa mahāyañño paccupaṭṭhito assa. Atha dve brāhmaṇā āgaccheyyuṃ – ‘itthannāmassa brāhmaṇassa mahāyaññaṃ anubhavissāmā’ti. Tatrekassa [tatthekassa (pī.)] brāhmaṇassa evamassa – ‘aho vata! Ahameva labheyyaṃ bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍaṃ, na añño brāhmaṇo labheyya bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍa’nti. Ṭhānaṃ kho panetaṃ, māṇava, vijjati yaṃ añño brāhmaṇo labheyya bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍaṃ, na so brāhmaṇo labheyya bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍaṃ. ‘Añño brāhmaṇo labhati bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍaṃ, nāhaṃ labhāmi bhattagge aggāsanaṃ aggodakaṃ aggapiṇḍa’nti – iti so kupito hoti anattamano. Imassa pana, māṇava, brāhmaṇā kiṃ vipākaṃ paññapentī’’ti? ‘‘Na khvettha, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā evaṃ dānaṃ denti – ‘iminā paro kupito hotu anattamano’ti. Atha khvettha brāhmaṇā anukampājātikaṃyeva [anukampajātikaṃyeva (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] dānaṃ dentī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ sante, kho, māṇava, brāhmaṇānaṃ idaṃ chaṭṭhaṃ puññakiriyavatthu hoti – yadidaṃ anukampājātika’’nti. ‘‘Evaṃ sante, bho gotama, brāhmaṇānaṃ idaṃ chaṭṭhaṃ puññakiriyavatthu hoti – yadidaṃ anukampājātika’’nti.

“What do you think, young man? Suppose a certain brahmin were preparing a great sacrifice. Then two brahmins would arrive, thinking, ‘We shall partake of the great sacrifice of such-and-such a brahmin.’ Now, one of those brahmins might think, ‘Oh, if only I could receive the best seat, the best water, and the best morsel of food in the refectory, and no other brahmin would receive the best seat, the best water, and the best morsel of food in the refectory!’ But it is possible, young man, that another brahmin would receive the best seat, the best water, and the best morsel of food in the refectory, and that brahmin would not. Then he would think, ‘Another brahmin receives the best seat, the best water, and the best morsel of food in the refectory, but I do not!’ And so he becomes angry and displeased. Now, young man, what result do the brahmins declare for this brahmin?” “Surely, Master Gotama, the brahmins do not give gifts thinking, ‘By this, may another become angry and displeased!’ Rather, the brahmins give gifts only out of compassion.” “If that is so, young man, then this becomes the sixth basis for making merit among the brahmins—that is, acting out of compassion.” “If that is so, Master Gotama, then this becomes the sixth basis for making merit among the brahmins—that is, acting out of compassion.”

‘‘Ye te, māṇava, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, ime tvaṃ pañca dhamme kattha bahulaṃ samanupassasi – gahaṭṭhesu vā pabbajitesu vā’’ti? ‘‘Yeme, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, imāhaṃ pañca dhamme pabbajitesu bahulaṃ samanupassāmi appaṃ gahaṭṭhesu. Gahaṭṭho hi, bho gotama, mahaṭṭho mahākicco mahādhikaraṇo mahāsamārambho, na satataṃ samitaṃ saccavādī hoti; pabbajito kho pana, bho gotama, appaṭṭho appakicco appādhikaraṇo appasamārambho, satataṃ samitaṃ saccavādī hoti. Gahaṭṭho hi, bho gotama, mahaṭṭho mahākicco mahādhikaraṇo mahāsamārambho na satataṃ samitaṃ tapassī hoti… brahmacārī hoti… sajjhāyabahulo hoti… cāgabahulo hoti; pabbajito kho pana, bho gotama, appaṭṭho appakicco appādhikaraṇo appasamārambho satataṃ samitaṃ tapassī hoti… brahmacārī hoti… sajjhāyabahulo hoti… cāgabahulo hoti. Yeme, bho gotama, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, imāhaṃ pañca dhamme pabbajitesu bahulaṃ samanupassāmi appaṃ gahaṭṭhesū’’ti.

“What do you think, young man? Those brahmins who prescribe five things for the making of merit and the cultivation of the wholesome—where do you mostly see these five things: among householders or among renunciants?” “Master Gotama, those five things that the brahmins prescribe for the making of merit and the cultivation of the wholesome—I mostly see these five things among renunciants, rarely among householders. For a householder, Master Gotama, is greatly occupied, with many duties, many responsibilities, and much involvement; they do not consistently and continually speak the truth. But a renunciant, Master Gotama, is unoccupied, with few duties, few responsibilities, and little involvement; they consistently and continually speak the truth. A householder, Master Gotama, is greatly occupied, with many duties, many responsibilities, and much involvement; they do not consistently and continually practice austerity… observe celibacy… engage much in recitation… engage much in generosity. But a renunciant, Master Gotama, is unoccupied, with few duties, few responsibilities, and little involvement; they consistently and continually practice austerity… observe celibacy… engage much in recitation… engage much in generosity. Master Gotama, those five things that the brahmins prescribe for the making of merit and the cultivation of the wholesome—I mostly see these five things among renunciants, rarely among householders.”

‘‘Ye te, māṇava, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya cittassāhaṃ ete parikkhāre vadāmi – yadidaṃ cittaṃ averaṃ abyābajjhaṃ tassa bhāvanāya. Idha, māṇava, bhikkhu saccavādī hoti. So ‘saccavādīmhī’ti labhati atthavedaṃ, labhati dhammavedaṃ, labhati dhammūpasaṃhitaṃ pāmojjaṃ. Yaṃ taṃ kusalūpasaṃhitaṃ pāmojjaṃ, cittassāhaṃ etaṃ parikkhāraṃ vadāmi – yadidaṃ cittaṃ averaṃ abyābajjhaṃ tassa bhāvanāya. Idha, māṇava, bhikkhu tapassī hoti…pe… brahmacārī hoti…pe… sajjhāyabahulo hoti…pe… cāgabahulo hoti. So ‘cāgabahulomhī’ti labhati atthavedaṃ, labhati dhammavedaṃ, labhati dhammūpasaṃhitaṃ pāmojjaṃ. Yaṃ taṃ kusalūpasaṃhitaṃ pāmojjaṃ, cittassāhaṃ etaṃ parikkhāraṃ vadāmi – yadidaṃ cittaṃ averaṃ abyābajjhaṃ tassa bhāvanāya. Ye te māṇava, brāhmaṇā, pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya, cittassāhaṃ ete parikkhāre vadāmi – yadidaṃ cittaṃ averaṃ abyābajjhaṃ tassa bhāvanāyā’’ti.

“Young man, those brahmins who prescribe five things for the making of merit and the cultivation of the wholesome, I say these are accessories for the mind—that is, a mind free from hostility and ill will, for its development. Here, young man, a bhikkhu speaks the truth. Thinking, ‘I speak the truth,’ he gains understanding of meaning, he gains understanding of the Dhamma, and he gains joy connected with the Dhamma. Whatever joy is connected with what is skillful, I say this is an accessory for the mind—that is, a mind free from hostility and ill will, for its development. Here, young man, a bhikkhu is austere… is celibate… is devoted to study… is generous. Thinking, ‘I am generous,’ he gains understanding of meaning, he gains understanding of the Dhamma, and he gains joy connected with the Dhamma. Whatever joy is connected with what is skillful, I say this is an accessory for the mind—that is, a mind free from hostility and ill will, for its development. Young man, those brahmins who prescribe five things for the making of merit and the cultivation of the wholesome, I say these are accessories for the mind—that is, a mind free from hostility and ill will, for its development.”

470. Evaṃ vutte, subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘sutaṃ metaṃ, bho gotama – ‘samaṇo gotamo brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggaṃ jānātī’’’ti.

470.When this was said, the young man Subha, the son of Todeyya, said to the Blessed One: “I have heard this, Master Gotama, that ‘the ascetic Gotama knows the path to union with Brahmā.’”

‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, māṇava, āsanne ito naḷakāragāmo, na yito dūre naḷakāragāmo’’ti?

“What do you think, young man, is the village of the reed-workers near here, or is the village of the reed-workers far from here?”

‘‘Evaṃ, bho, āsanne ito naḷakāragāmo, na yito dūre naḷakāragāmo’’ti.

“Indeed, sir, the reed-workers’ village is near here, not far from here.”

‘‘Taṃ, kiṃ maññasi māṇava, idhassa puriso naḷakāragāme jātavaddho [jātavaḍḍho (syā. kaṃ. ka.)]; tamenaṃ naḷakāragāmato tāvadeva avasaṭaṃ [apasakkaṃ (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] naḷakāragāmassa maggaṃ puccheyyuṃ; siyā nu kho, māṇava, tassa purisassa naḷakāragāme jātavaddhassa naḷakāragāmassa maggaṃ puṭṭhassa dandhāyitattaṃ vā vitthāyitattaṃ vā’’ti?

“Now, what do you think, young man? Suppose there were a man born and raised in a reed-worker’s village. Then, just after he had left the reed-worker’s village, they were to ask him the way to the reed-worker’s village. Would there be any slowness or hesitation in that man, born and raised in the reed-worker’s village, when asked about the way to the reed-worker’s village?”

‘‘No hidaṃ, bho gotama’’.

“No, Master Gotama.”

‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’?

“Why is that?”

‘‘Amu hi, bho gotama, puriso naḷakāragāme jātavaddho. Tassa sabbāneva naḷakāragāmassa maggāni suviditānī’’ti. ‘‘Siyā nu kho, māṇava, tassa purisassa naḷakāragāme jātavaddhassa naḷakāragāmassa maggaṃ puṭṭhassa dandhāyitattaṃ vā vitthāyitattaṃ vāti, na tveva tathāgatassa brahmalokaṃ vā brahmalokagāminiṃ vā paṭipadaṃ puṭṭhassa dandhāyitattaṃ vā vitthāyitattaṃ vā. Brahmānañcāhaṃ, māṇava, pajānāmi brahmalokañca brahmalokagāminiñca paṭipadaṃ; yathāpaṭipanno ca brahmalokaṃ upapanno tañca pajānāmī’’ti.

“Sir, that man was born and grew up in the village of Naḷakāra. He knows all the paths of the village of Naḷakāra very well.” “Young man, would there be any slowness or hesitation in that man, born and grown up in the village of Naḷakāra, when asked about the path to the village of Naḷakāra? No, there would not. In the same way, young man, when the Tathāgata is asked about the Brahma-world or the path leading to the Brahma-world, there is no slowness or hesitation. I understand Brahmā, young man, and the Brahma-world, and the path leading to the Brahma-world; and I understand how one who has practiced accordingly has arisen in the Brahma-world.”

‘‘Sutaṃ metaṃ, bho gotama – ‘samaṇo gotamo brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggaṃ desetī’ti. Sādhu me bhavaṃ gotamo brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggaṃ desetū’’ti.

“I have heard this, Master Gotama, that ‘the ascetic Gotama teaches the path to union with Brahmā.’ It would be good if Master Gotama would teach me the path to union with Brahmā.”

‘‘Tena hi, māṇava, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ bho’’ti kho subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

“Well then, young man, listen and attend closely, I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” replied Subha, the young man, son of Todeyya, to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

471. ‘‘Katamo ca, māṇava, brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggo? Idha, māṇava, bhikkhu mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā viharati, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā viharati. Evaṃ bhāvitāya kho, māṇava, mettāya cetovimuttiyā yaṃ pamāṇakataṃ kammaṃ na taṃ tatrāvasissati, na taṃ tatrāvatiṭṭhati. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, balavā saṅkhadhamo appakasireneva cātuddisā viññāpeyya [evameva kho māṇava evaṃ bhāvitāya mettāya (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī. dī. ni. 1.556) tathāpi idha pāṭhoyeva upamāya saṃsandiyamāno paripuṇṇo viya dissati]; evameva kho, māṇava…pe… evaṃ bhāvitāya kho, māṇava, mettāya [evameva kho māṇava evaṃ bhāvitāya mettāya (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī. dī. ni. 1.556) tathāpi idha pāṭhoyeva upamāya saṃsandiyamāno paripuṇṇo viya dissati] cetovimuttiyā yaṃ pamāṇakataṃ kammaṃ na taṃ tatrāvasissati, na taṃ tatrāvatiṭṭhati. Ayampi kho, māṇava, brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggo. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, māṇava, bhikkhu karuṇāsahagatena cetasā…pe… muditāsahagatena cetasā…pe… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā viharati, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā viharati. Evaṃ bhāvitāya kho, māṇava, upekkhāya cetovimuttiyā yaṃ pamāṇakataṃ kammaṃ na taṃ tatrāvasissati, na taṃ tatrāvatiṭṭhati. Seyyathāpi, māṇava, balavā saṅkhadhamo appakasireneva cātuddisā viññāpeyya; evameva kho, māṇava…pe… evaṃ bhāvitāya kho, māṇava, upekkhāya cetovimuttiyā yaṃ pamāṇakataṃ kammaṃ na taṃ tatrāvasissati, na taṃ tatrāvatiṭṭhati. Ayampi kho, māṇava, brahmānaṃ sahabyatāya maggo’’ti.

471.“And what, young man, is the path to companionship with Brahmā? Here, young man, a bhikkhu dwells pervading one direction with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, around, everywhere, in all ways, he dwells pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without hostility, without ill will. When, young man, the liberation of mind by loving-kindness is thus developed, any limited action does not remain there, does not persist there. Just as, young man, a strong conch-blower can easily make himself heard in the four directions; so too, young man, when the liberation of mind by loving-kindness is thus developed, any limited action does not remain there, does not persist there. This too, young man, is the path to companionship with Brahmā. Furthermore, young man, a bhikkhu dwells pervading one direction with a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with appreciative joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, around, everywhere, in all ways, he dwells pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without hostility, without ill will. When, young man, the liberation of mind by equanimity is thus developed, any limited action does not remain there, does not persist there. Just as, young man, a strong conch-blower can easily make himself heard in the four directions; so too, young man, when the liberation of mind by equanimity is thus developed, any limited action does not remain there, does not persist there. This too, young man, is the path to companionship with Brahmā.”

472. Evaṃ vutte, subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘abhikkantaṃ, bho gotama, abhikkantaṃ, bho gotama! Seyyathāpi, bho gotama, nikkujjitaṃ vā ukkujjeyya, paṭicchannaṃ vā vivareyya, mūḷhassa vā maggaṃ ācikkheyya, andhakāre vā telapajjotaṃ dhāreyya – cakkhumanto rūpāni dakkhantīti – evamevaṃ bhotā gotamena anekapariyāyena dhammo pakāsito. Esāhaṃ bhavantaṃ gotamaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi dhammañca bhikkhusaṅghañca. Upāsakaṃ maṃ bhavaṃ gotamo dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gataṃ. Handa, ca dāni mayaṃ, bho gotama, gacchāma; bahukiccā mayaṃ bahukaraṇīyā’’ti. ‘‘Yassadāni tvaṃ, māṇava, kālaṃ maññasī’’ti. Atha kho subho māṇavo todeyyaputto bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinanditvā anumoditvā uṭṭhāyāsanā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā padakkhiṇaṃ katvā pakkāmi.

472.When this was said, the young man Subha, Todeyya’s son, said to the Blessed One: “Excellent, Master Gotama! Excellent, Master Gotama! Just as one might set upright what was overturned, or reveal what was hidden, or show the way to one who was lost, or hold up a lamp in the dark so that those with eyes could see forms—in the same way the Dhamma has been made clear in many ways by Master Gotama. I go to Master Gotama for refuge, and to the Dhamma, and to the Sangha of bhikkhus. May Master Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge from this day forward, for as long as life lasts. Now, Master Gotama, we must go. We have much to do, many duties to attend to.” “You may go, young man, at your own convenience.” Then the young man Subha, Todeyya’s son, having delighted and rejoiced in the Blessed One’s words, rose from his seat, paid homage to the Blessed One, circumambulated him, keeping the right side towards him, and departed.

Tena kho pana samayena jāṇussoṇi brāhmaṇo sabbasetena vaḷavābhirathena [vaḷabhīrathena (sī.)] sāvatthiyā niyyāti divā divassa. Addasā kho jāṇussoṇi brāhmaṇo subhaṃ māṇavaṃ todeyyaputtaṃ dūratova āgacchantaṃ. Disvāna subhaṃ māṇavaṃ todeyyaputtaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘handa, kuto nu bhavaṃ bhāradvājo āgacchati divā divassā’’ti? ‘‘Ito hi kho ahaṃ, bho, āgacchāmi samaṇassa gotamassa santikā’’ti. ‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, bhavaṃ bhāradvājo, samaṇassa gotamassa paññāveyyattiyaṃ paṇḍito maññeti’’? ‘‘Ko cāhaṃ, bho, ko ca samaṇassa gotamassa paññāveyyattiyaṃ jānissāmi? Sopi nūnassa tādisova yo samaṇassa gotamassa paññāveyyattiyaṃ jāneyyā’’ti. ‘‘Uḷārāya khalu, bhavaṃ bhāradvājo, samaṇaṃ gotamaṃ pasaṃsāya pasaṃsatī’’ti. ‘‘Ko cāhaṃ, bho, ko ca samaṇaṃ gotamaṃ pasaṃsissāmi? Pasatthapasatthova so bhavaṃ gotamo seṭṭho devamanussānaṃ. Ye cime, bho, brāhmaṇā pañca dhamme paññapenti puññassa kiriyāya kusalassa ārādhanāya; cittassete samaṇo gotamo parikkhāre vadeti – yadidaṃ cittaṃ averaṃ abyābajjhaṃ tassa bhāvanāyā’’ti.

At that time, the brahmin Jāṇussoṇi was leaving Sāvatthī in the middle of the day in an all-white chariot drawn by mares. The brahmin Jāṇussoṇi saw the young man Subha, son of Todeyya, coming from a distance. Seeing him, he said to Subha, son of Todeyya: “Well now, where is Master Bhāradvāja coming from in the middle of the day?” “Just now, sir, I am coming from the presence of the ascetic Gotama.” “What do you think, Master Bhāradvāja? Is the ascetic Gotama considered a wise man with penetrating wisdom?” “Who am I, sir, to know the penetrating wisdom of the ascetic Gotama? Surely, only one like him could know the penetrating wisdom of the ascetic Gotama.” “Master Bhāradvāja praises the ascetic Gotama with exceedingly high praise.” “Who am I, sir, to praise the ascetic Gotama? That Blessed Gotama is praised by those worthy of praise, the best of gods and humans. Those brahmins, sir, who prescribe five things for doing meritorious deeds and achieving what is wholesome—the ascetic Gotama declares these to be the equipment for the mind: that is, the cultivation of a mind free from hatred and ill will.”

Evaṃ vutte, jāṇussoṇi brāhmaṇo sabbasetā vaḷavābhirathā orohitvā ekaṃsaṃ uttarāsaṅgaṃ karitvā yena bhagavā tenañjaliṃ paṇāmetvā udānaṃ udānesi – ‘‘lābhā rañño pasenadissa kosalassa, suladdhalābhā rañño pasenadissa kosalassa yassa vijite tathāgato viharati arahaṃ sammāsambuddho’’ti.

When this was said, the brahmin Jāṇussoṇi descended from his all-white chariot drawn by mares, arranged his upper robe over one shoulder, raised his joined palms towards the Blessed One, and uttered this inspired utterance: “It is a gain for King Pasenadi of Kosala, a great gain for King Pasenadi of Kosala, that in his realm the Tathāgata, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One, dwells.”

Subhasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ navamaṃ.

The Subha Sutta, the ninth, is finished.
