# 2. Sabbāsavasuttaṃ

## 2. All the Cankers

14. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tatra kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘bhikkhavo’’ti. ‘‘Bhadante’’ti te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘sabbāsavasaṃvarapariyāyaṃ vo, bhikkhave, desessāmi. Taṃ suṇātha, sādhukaṃ manasi karotha, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca –

14. Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi in Jeta's Grove, Anathapindika's park. There the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Monks." "Venerable sir," those monks replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this: "Monks, I will teach you the discourse on the restraint of all taints. Listen to that and pay close attention, I will speak." "Yes, venerable sir," those monks replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

15. ‘‘Jānato ahaṃ, bhikkhave, passato āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi, no ajānato no apassato. Kiñca, bhikkhave, jānato kiñca passato āsavānaṃ khayaṃ vadāmi? Yoniso ca manasikāraṃ ayoniso ca manasikāraṃ. Ayoniso, bhikkhave, manasikaroto anuppannā ceva āsavā uppajjanti, uppannā ca āsavā pavaḍḍhanti; yoniso ca kho, bhikkhave, manasikaroto anuppannā ceva āsavā na uppajjanti, uppannā ca āsavā pahīyanti.

15. "Monks, I declare the destruction of the taints for one who knows and sees, not for one who does not know or see. And what, monks, does one know and see for the destruction of the taints? Proper attention and improper attention. When one attends improperly, monks, unarisen taints arise and arisen taints increase. But when one attends properly, monks, unarisen taints do not arise, and arisen taints are abandoned."

16. ‘‘Atthi, bhikkhave, āsavā dassanā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā saṃvarā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā paṭisevanā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā adhivāsanā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā parivajjanā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā vinodanā pahātabbā, atthi āsavā bhāvanā pahātabbā.

16. Monks, there are taints to be abandoned by seeing; there are taints to be abandoned by restraint; there are taints to be abandoned by using; there are taints to be abandoned by enduring; there are taints to be abandoned by avoiding; there are taints to be abandoned by dispelling; there are taints to be abandoned by developing.

## Dassanā pahātabbāsavā

### Cankers to be abandoned by seeing.

17. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā dassanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, assutavā puthujjano – ariyānaṃ adassāvī ariyadhammassa akovido ariyadhamme avinīto, sappurisānaṃ adassāvī sappurisadhammassa akovido sappurisadhamme avinīto – manasikaraṇīye dhamme nappajānāti, amanasikaraṇīye dhamme nappajānāti. So manasikaraṇīye dhamme appajānanto amanasikaraṇīye dhamme appajānanto, ye dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā, te dhamme manasi karoti, ye dhammā manasikaraṇīyā te dhamme na manasi karoti.

17. "And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by seeing? Here, monks, an uninstructed ordinary person—who has not seen the noble ones, is unskilled and undisciplined in the Dhamma of the noble ones; who has not seen the good people, is unskilled and undisciplined in the Dhamma of the good people—does not understand what things are fit for attention and what things are unfit for attention. Not understanding what is fit for attention and what is unfit for attention, they attend to things that are unfit for attention and do not attend to things that are fit for attention.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme manasi karoti? Yassa, bhikkhave, dhamme manasikaroto anuppanno vā kāmāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā kāmāsavo pavaḍḍhati; anuppanno vā bhavāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā bhavāsavo pavaḍḍhati; anuppanno vā avijjāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā avijjāsavo pavaḍḍhati – ime dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme manasi karoti.

"And what, monks, are the things unfit for attention that they attend to? When one attends to things in such a way, monks, that the unarisen taint of sensual desire arises in him and the arisen taint of sensual desire increases; that the unarisen taint of existence arises and the arisen taint of existence increases; that the unarisen taint of ignorance arises and the arisen taint of ignorance increases—these are the things unfit for attention that they attend to."

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme na manasi karoti? Yassa, bhikkhave, dhamme manasikaroto anuppanno vā kāmāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā kāmāsavo pahīyati; anuppanno vā bhavāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā bhavāsavo pahīyati; anuppanno vā avijjāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā avijjāsavo pahīyati – ime dhammā manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme na manasi karoti.

"And what, monks, are the things fit for attention that they do not attend to? When one attends to things in such a way, monks, that the unarisen taint of sensual desire does not arise and the arisen taint of sensual desire is abandoned; that the unarisen taint of existence does not arise and the arisen taint of existence is abandoned; that the unarisen taint of ignorance does not arise and the arisen taint of ignorance is abandoned—these are the things fit for attention that they do not attend to."

‘‘Tassa amanasikaraṇīyānaṃ dhammānaṃ manasikārā manasikaraṇīyānaṃ dhammānaṃ amanasikārā anuppannā ceva āsavā uppajjanti uppannā ca āsavā pavaḍḍhanti.

Thus, through attending to things unfit for attention, and through not attending to things fit for attention, unarisen taints arise, and arisen taints increase.

18. ‘‘So evaṃ ayoniso manasi karoti – ‘ahosiṃ nu kho ahaṃ atītamaddhānaṃ? Na nu kho ahosiṃ atītamaddhānaṃ? Kiṃ nu kho ahosiṃ atītamaddhānaṃ? Kathaṃ nu kho ahosiṃ atītamaddhānaṃ? Kiṃ hutvā kiṃ ahosiṃ nu kho ahaṃ atītamaddhānaṃ? Bhavissāmi nu kho ahaṃ anāgatamaddhānaṃ? Na nu kho bhavissāmi anāgatamaddhānaṃ? Kiṃ nu kho bhavissāmi anāgatamaddhānaṃ? Kathaṃ nu kho bhavissāmi anāgatamaddhānaṃ? Kiṃ hutvā kiṃ bhavissāmi nu kho ahaṃ anāgatamaddhāna’nti? Etarahi vā paccuppannamaddhānaṃ [paccuppannamaddhānaṃ ārabbha (syā.)] ajjhattaṃ kathaṃkathī hoti – ‘ahaṃ nu khosmi? No nu khosmi? Kiṃ nu khosmi? Kathaṃ nu khosmi? Ayaṃ nu kho satto kuto āgato? So kuhiṃ gāmī bhavissatī’ti?

18. "He attends unwisely in this way: ‘Did I exist in the past? Did I not exist in the past? What was I in the past? How was I in the past? Having been what, what did I become in the past?’ ‘Will I exist in the future? Will I not exist in the future? What will I be in the future? How will I be in the future? Having been what, what will I become in the future?’ Or he is inwardly perplexed about the present thus: ‘Am I? Am I not? What am I? How am I? Where has this being come from? Where will it go?’"

19. ‘‘Tassa evaṃ ayoniso manasikaroto channaṃ diṭṭhīnaṃ aññatarā diṭṭhi uppajjati. ‘Atthi me attā’ti vā assa [vāssa (sī. syā. pī.)] saccato thetato diṭṭhi uppajjati; ‘natthi me attā’ti vā assa saccato thetato diṭṭhi uppajjati; ‘attanāva attānaṃ sañjānāmī’ti vā assa saccato thetato diṭṭhi uppajjati; ‘attanāva anattānaṃ sañjānāmī’ti vā assa saccato thetato diṭṭhi uppajjati; ‘anattanāva attānaṃ sañjānāmī’ti vā assa saccato thetato diṭṭhi uppajjati; atha vā panassa evaṃ diṭṭhi hoti – ‘yo me ayaṃ attā vado vedeyyo tatra tatra kalyāṇapāpakānaṃ kammānaṃ vipākaṃ paṭisaṃvedeti so kho pana me ayaṃ attā nicco dhuvo sassato avipariṇāmadhammo sassatisamaṃ tatheva ṭhassatī’ti. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, diṭṭhigataṃ diṭṭhigahanaṃ diṭṭhikantāraṃ diṭṭhivisūkaṃ diṭṭhivipphanditaṃ diṭṭhisaṃyojanaṃ. Diṭṭhisaṃyojanasaṃyutto, bhikkhave, assutavā puthujjano na parimuccati jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi; ‘na parimuccati dukkhasmā’ti vadāmi.

19. When he attends unwisely in this way, one of six views arises in him. The view 'I have a self' arises in him as true and established; or the view 'I have no self' arises in him as true and established; or the view 'It is precisely self that perceives self' arises in him as true and established; or the view 'It is precisely self that perceives not-self' arises in him as true and established; or the view 'It is precisely not-self that perceives self' arises in him as true and established; or else he has some such view as this: 'This self of mine is the speaker, the knower, who experiences the results of good and bad actions in various places; but this self of mine is permanent, stable, eternal, not subject to change, and will last forever and ever.' This is called a stand of views, a jungle of views, a wilderness of views, a distortion of views, a vacillation of views, a fetter of views. Fettered by the fetter of views, the uninstructed worldling is not freed from birth, aging, and death, from sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair; he is not freed from suffering, I say.

20. ‘‘Sutavā ca kho, bhikkhave, ariyasāvako – ariyānaṃ dassāvī ariyadhammassa kovido ariyadhamme suvinīto, sappurisānaṃ dassāvī sappurisadhammassa kovido sappurisadhamme suvinīto – manasikaraṇīye dhamme pajānāti amanasikaraṇīye dhamme pajānāti. So manasikaraṇīye dhamme pajānanto amanasikaraṇīye dhamme pajānanto ye dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā te dhamme na manasi karoti, ye dhammā manasikaraṇīyā te dhamme manasi karoti.

20. But, monks, a learned noble disciple—one who has seen the noble ones, is skilled in the noble Dhamma, well-trained in the noble Dhamma; one who has seen good people, is skilled in the Dhamma of the good, well-trained in the Dhamma of the good—understands what things are fit for attention and what things are not fit for attention. Understanding what things are fit for attention and what things are not fit for attention, he does not attend to those things that are not fit for attention, and he attends to those things that are fit for attention.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme na manasi karoti? Yassa, bhikkhave, dhamme manasikaroto anuppanno vā kāmāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā kāmāsavo pavaḍḍhati; anuppanno vā bhavāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā bhavāsavo pavaḍḍhati; anuppanno vā avijjāsavo uppajjati, uppanno vā avijjāsavo pavaḍḍhati – ime dhammā na manasikaraṇīyā, ye dhamme na manasi karoti.

And what, monks, are the things not fit for attention that he does not attend to? Monks, when one attends to certain things, the unarisen taint of sensual desire arises, and the arisen taint of sensual desire increases; the unarisen taint of becoming arises, and the arisen taint of becoming increases; the unarisen taint of ignorance arises, and the arisen taint of ignorance increases—these are the things not fit for attention, which he does not attend to.

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, dhammā manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme manasi karoti? Yassa, bhikkhave, dhamme manasikaroto anuppanno vā kāmāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā kāmāsavo pahīyati; anuppanno vā bhavāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā bhavāsavo pahīyati; anuppanno vā avijjāsavo na uppajjati, uppanno vā avijjāsavo pahīyati – ime dhammā manasikaraṇīyā ye dhamme manasi karoti.

And what, monks, are the things fit for attention that he attends to? Monks, when one attends to certain things, the unarisen taint of sensual desire does not arise, and the arisen taint of sensual desire is abandoned; the unarisen taint of becoming does not arise, and the arisen taint of becoming is abandoned; the unarisen taint of ignorance does not arise, and the arisen taint of ignorance is abandoned—these are the things fit for attention, which he attends to.

‘‘Tassa amanasikaraṇīyānaṃ dhammānaṃ amanasikārā manasikaraṇīyānaṃ dhammānaṃ manasikārā anuppannā ceva āsavā na uppajjanti, uppannā ca āsavā pahīyanti.

By his not attending to those things that are not fit for attention, and by his attending to those things that are fit for attention, unarisen taints do not arise in him, and arisen taints are abandoned.

21. ‘‘So ‘idaṃ dukkha’nti yoniso manasi karoti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhasamudayo’ti yoniso manasi karoti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodho’ti yoniso manasi karoti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodhagāminī paṭipadā’ti yoniso manasi karoti. Tassa evaṃ yoniso manasikaroto tīṇi saṃyojanāni pahīyanti – sakkāyadiṭṭhi, vicikicchā, sīlabbataparāmāso. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā dassanā pahātabbā.

21. He attends systematically to ‘This is suffering’; he attends systematically to ‘This is the origin of suffering’; he attends systematically to ‘This is the cessation of suffering’; he attends systematically to ‘This is the way leading to the cessation of suffering.’ As he attends systematically in this way, three fetters are abandoned in him: identity view, doubt, and adherence to rules and observances. These, monks, are called the taints to be abandoned by seeing.

## Saṃvarā pahātabbāsavā

### Taints to be Abandoned by Restraint

22. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā saṃvarā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso cakkhundriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati. Yañhissa, bhikkhave, cakkhundriyasaṃvaraṃ asaṃvutassa viharato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, cakkhundriyasaṃvaraṃ saṃvutassa viharato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Paṭisaṅkhā yoniso sotindriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati…pe… ghānindriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati…pe… jivhindriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati…pe… kāyindriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati…pe… manindriyasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati. Yañhissa, bhikkhave, manindriyasaṃvaraṃ asaṃvutassa viharato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, manindriyasaṃvaraṃ saṃvutassa viharato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti.

22. "And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by restraint? Here, monks, a monk, reflecting wisely, dwells restrained with the restraint of the eye faculty. For, monks, if he were to dwell unrestrained with the restraint of the eye faculty, taints, distress, and fever would arise in him; but by dwelling restrained with the restraint of the eye faculty, then such taints, distress, and fever do not arise in him. Reflecting wisely, he dwells restrained with the restraint of the ear faculty... the nose faculty... the tongue faculty... the body faculty... the mind faculty. For, monks, if he were to dwell unrestrained with the restraint of the mind faculty, taints, distress, and fever would arise in him; but by dwelling restrained with the restraint of the mind faculty, then such taints, distress, and fever do not arise in him."

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, saṃvaraṃ asaṃvutassa viharato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, saṃvaraṃ saṃvutassa viharato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā saṃvarā pahātabbā.

For, monks, if he were to dwell unrestrained with restraint, taints, distress, and fever would arise in him; but by dwelling restrained with restraint, then such taints, distress, and fever do not arise in him. These, monks, are called the taints to be abandoned by restraint.

## Paṭisevanā pahātabbāsavā

### Defilements to be Abandoned by Proper Use

23. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā paṭisevanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso cīvaraṃ paṭisevati – ‘yāvadeva sītassa paṭighātāya, uṇhassa paṭighātāya, ḍaṃsamakasavātātapasarīṃsapa- [siriṃsapa (sī. syā. pī.)] samphassānaṃ paṭighātāya, yāvadeva hirikopīnappaṭicchādanatthaṃ’.

23. And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by proper use? Here, a monk reflects wisely and uses robes—'only for the purpose of warding off cold, warding off heat, warding off the touch of flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and creeping things, and only for the purpose of covering the private parts.'

‘‘Paṭisaṅkhā yoniso piṇḍapātaṃ paṭisevati – ‘neva davāya, na madāya, na maṇḍanāya, na vibhūsanāya, yāvadeva imassa kāyassa ṭhitiyā yāpanāya, vihiṃsūparatiyā, brahmacariyānuggahāya, iti purāṇañca vedanaṃ paṭihaṅkhāmi navañca vedanaṃ na uppādessāmi, yātrā ca me bhavissati anavajjatā ca phāsuvihāro ca’ [cāti (sī.)].

He reflects wisely and uses almsfood—'not for amusement or intoxication or for adornment or beautification, but only for the maintenance and sustenance of this body, for ending discomfort, and for assisting the holy life, considering: ‘Thus I shall put an end to old feelings and avoid arousing new feelings, and my life will be sustained, blameless, and at ease.’"

‘‘Paṭisaṅkhā yoniso senāsanaṃ paṭisevati – ‘yāvadeva sītassa paṭighātāya, uṇhassa paṭighātāya, ḍaṃsamakasavātātapasarīṃsapasamphassānaṃ paṭighātāya, yāvadeva utuparissayavinodanapaṭisallānārāmatthaṃ’.

He reflects wisely and makes use of a dwelling—'only for the purpose of warding off cold, warding off heat, warding off the touch of flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and creeping things, and only for the purpose of dispelling the dangers of the seasons and for enjoying seclusion.'

‘‘Paṭisaṅkhā yoniso gilānappaccayabhesajjaparikkhāraṃ paṭisevati – ‘yāvadeva uppannānaṃ veyyābādhikānaṃ vedanānaṃ paṭighātāya, abyābajjhaparamatāya’ [abyāpajjhaparamatāya (sī. syā. pī.), abyāpajjaparamatāya (ka.)].

He reflects wisely and uses medicine and the requisites for the sick—'only for the purpose of warding off painful feelings that have arisen and for the sake of ultimate freedom from affliction.'

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, appaṭisevato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, paṭisevato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā paṭisevanā pahātabbā.

If, monks, taints, vexation, and fever would arise for him by not making use, but by making use of them in this way, those taints, vexation, and fever do not occur. These, monks, are called taints to be abandoned by proper use.

## Adhivāsanā pahātabbāsavā

### Taints to be Abandoned by Endurance

24. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā adhivāsanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso khamo hoti sītassa uṇhassa, jighacchāya pipāsāya. Ḍaṃsamakasavātātapasarīṃsapasamphassānaṃ, duruttānaṃ durāgatānaṃ vacanapathānaṃ, uppannānaṃ sārīrikānaṃ vedanānaṃ dukkhānaṃ tibbānaṃ [tippānaṃ (sī. syā. pī.)] kharānaṃ kaṭukānaṃ asātānaṃ amanāpānaṃ pāṇaharānaṃ adhivāsakajātiko hoti.

24.“And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by endurance? Here, monks, a bhikkhu, reflecting wisely, is capable of enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst; the touch of flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and creeping things; harsh, unwelcome words; and bodily feelings that are painful, sharp, severe, intense, unpleasant, disagreeable, and life-threatening. He is one who is by nature an endurer.

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, anadhivāsayato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, adhivāsayato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā adhivāsanā pahātabbā.

“If, monks, taints, distress, and fever would arise in one who does not endure, then in one who endures, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise. These, monks, are called taints to be abandoned by endurance.

## Parivajjanā pahātabbāsavā

### Taints to be Abandoned by Avoidance

25. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā parivajjanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso caṇḍaṃ hatthiṃ parivajjeti, caṇḍaṃ assaṃ parivajjeti, caṇḍaṃ goṇaṃ parivajjeti, caṇḍaṃ kukkuraṃ parivajjeti, ahiṃ khāṇuṃ kaṇṭakaṭṭhānaṃ sobbhaṃ papātaṃ candanikaṃ oḷigallaṃ. Yathārūpe anāsane nisinnaṃ yathārūpe agocare carantaṃ yathārūpe pāpake mitte bhajantaṃ viññū sabrahmacārī pāpakesu ṭhānesu okappeyyuṃ, so tañca anāsanaṃ tañca agocaraṃ te ca pāpake mitte paṭisaṅkhā yoniso parivajjeti.

25.“And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by avoidance? Here, monks, a bhikkhu, reflecting wisely, avoids a fierce elephant, a fierce horse, a fierce bull, a fierce dog, a snake, a stump, a thorny ground, a pit, a precipice, a refuse heap, or a swamp. He avoids sitting in unsuitable seats, wandering in unsuitable resorts, and associating with bad friends, considering that wise companions in the holy life might suspect him of wrong conduct. Reflecting wisely, he avoids such unsuitable seats, unsuitable resorts, and bad friends.

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, aparivajjayato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, parivajjayato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā parivajjanā pahātabbā.

“If, monks, taints, distress, and fever would arise in one who does not avoid, then in one who avoids, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise. These, monks, are called taints to be abandoned by avoidance.

## Vinodanā pahātabbāsavā

### Taints to be Abandoned by Dispelling

26. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā vinodanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso uppannaṃ kāmavitakkaṃ nādhivāseti pajahati vinodeti byantīkaroti anabhāvaṃ gameti, uppannaṃ byāpādavitakkaṃ…pe… uppannaṃ vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ…pe… uppannuppanne pāpake akusale dhamme nādhivāseti pajahati vinodeti byantīkaroti anabhāvaṃ gameti.

26.“And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by dispelling? Here, monks, a bhikkhu, reflecting wisely, does not tolerate an arisen thought of sensual desire; he abandons it, dispels it, obliterates it, and makes it cease to exist. He does not tolerate an arisen thought of ill-will… an arisen thought of cruelty… He does not tolerate any arisen evil unwholesome states; he abandons them, dispels them, obliterates them, and makes them cease to exist.

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, avinodayato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, vinodayato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā vinodanā pahātabbā.

“If, monks, taints, distress, and fever would arise in one who does not dispel, then in one who dispels, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise. These, monks, are called taints to be abandoned by dispelling.

## Bhāvanā pahātabbāsavā

### Taints to be Abandoned by Development

27. ‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, āsavā bhāvanā pahātabbā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu paṭisaṅkhā yoniso satisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ; paṭisaṅkhā yoniso dhammavicayasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti…pe… vīriyasambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… pītisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… passaddhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… samādhisambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti… upekkhāsambojjhaṅgaṃ bhāveti vivekanissitaṃ virāganissitaṃ nirodhanissitaṃ vossaggapariṇāmiṃ.

27.“And what, monks, are the taints to be abandoned by development? Here, monks, a bhikkhu, reflecting wisely, develops the mindfulness enlightenment factor, which is based on seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, and ripens to relinquishment. He develops the investigation of phenomena enlightenment factor… the energy enlightenment factor… the rapture enlightenment factor… the tranquility enlightenment factor… the samādhi enlightenment factor… the equanimity enlightenment factor, which is based on seclusion, dispassion, and cessation, and ripens to relinquishment.

‘‘Yañhissa, bhikkhave, abhāvayato uppajjeyyuṃ āsavā vighātapariḷāhā, bhāvayato evaṃsa te āsavā vighātapariḷāhā na honti. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, āsavā bhāvanā pahātabbā.

“If, monks, taints, distress, and fever would arise in one who does not develop, then in one who develops, those taints, distress, and fever do not arise. These, monks, are called taints to be abandoned by development.

28. ‘‘Yato kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno ye āsavā dassanā pahātabbā te dassanā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā saṃvarā pahātabbā te saṃvarā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā paṭisevanā pahātabbā te paṭisevanā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā adhivāsanā pahātabbā te adhivāsanā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā parivajjanā pahātabbā te parivajjanā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā vinodanā pahātabbā te vinodanā pahīnā honti, ye āsavā bhāvanā pahātabbā te bhāvanā pahīnā honti; ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave – ‘bhikkhu sabbāsavasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati, acchecchi [acchejji (ka.)] taṇhaṃ, vivattayi [vāvattayi (sī. pī.)] saṃyojanaṃ, sammā mānābhisamayā antamakāsi dukkhassā’’’ti.

28.“When, monks, a bhikkhu has abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by seeing, abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by restraint, abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by use, abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by endurance, abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by avoidance, abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by dispelling, and abandoned the taints that should be abandoned by development—then he is called, monks, ‘a bhikkhu who dwells restrained by the restraint of all taints, who has cut off craving, unravelled the fetters, and by the realization and abandonment of conceit, has made an end of suffering.’”

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

The Blessed One said this. The monks were pleased and rejoiced at the Blessed One’s words.

Sabbāsavasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ dutiyaṃ.

The Sabbasava Sutta is concluded. Second.
