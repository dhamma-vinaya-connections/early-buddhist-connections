# 43. Mahāvedallasuttaṃ

## 43. The Great Vedalla Sutta

449. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Atha kho āyasmā mahākoṭṭhiko sāyanhasamayaṃ paṭisallānā vuṭṭhito yenāyasmā sāriputto tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmatā sāriputtena saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho āyasmā mahākoṭṭhiko āyasmantaṃ sāriputtaṃ etadavoca –

449.Thus have I heard. On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta’s Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Park. Then, in the evening, the Venerable Mahākoṭṭhita emerged from seclusion and approached the Venerable Sāriputta. Having approached, he exchanged greetings with the Venerable Sāriputta, and when they had concluded their greetings and cordial talk, he sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the Venerable Mahākoṭṭhita said to the Venerable Sāriputta:

‘‘‘Duppañño duppañño’ti, āvuso, vuccati. Kittāvatā nu kho, āvuso, duppaññoti vuccatī’’ti?

'Lacking in wisdom, lacking in wisdom,' is said, friend. In what way, friend, is one said to be 'lacking in wisdom'?

‘‘‘Nappajānāti nappajānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā duppaññoti vuccati.

'He doesn’t understand, he doesn’t understand,' friend, therefore he is called 'lacking in wisdom.'

‘‘Kiñca nappajānāti? ‘Idaṃ dukkha’nti nappajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhasamudayo’ti nappajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodho’ti nappajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodhagāminī paṭipadā’ti nappajānāti. ‘Nappajānāti nappajānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā duppaññoti vuccatī’’ti.

And what does he not understand? He does not understand: ‘This is suffering.’ He does not understand: ‘This is the origin of suffering.’ He does not understand: ‘This is the cessation of suffering.’ He does not understand: ‘This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.’ 'He does not understand, he does not understand,' friend, therefore he is called 'lacking in wisdom.'

‘‘‘Sādhāvuso’ti kho āyasmā mahākoṭṭhiko āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ abhinanditvā anumoditvā āyasmantaṃ sāriputtaṃ uttariṃ pañhaṃ apucchi –

“Good, friend,” replied Venerable Mahākoṭṭhita. Having rejoiced in and approved of Venerable Sāriputta’s words, he asked Venerable Sāriputta a further question:

‘‘‘Paññavā paññavā’ti, āvuso, vuccati. Kittāvatā nu kho, āvuso, paññavāti vuccatī’’ti?

'Wise, wise,' is said, friend. But to what extent, friend, is one said to be wise?

‘‘‘Pajānāti pajānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā paññavāti vuccati.

'One understands, one understands,' friend, therefore one is called 'wise.'

‘‘Kiñca pajānāti? ‘Idaṃ dukkha’nti pajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhasamudayo’ti pajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodho’ti pajānāti, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodhagāminī paṭipadā’ti pajānāti. ‘Pajānāti pajānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā paññavāti vuccatī’’ti.

And what does one understand? One understands: ‘This is suffering.’ One understands: ‘This is the origin of suffering.’ One understands: ‘This is the cessation of suffering.’ One understands: ‘This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.’ 'One understands, one understands,' friend, therefore, friend, one is called wise.

‘‘‘Viññāṇaṃ viññāṇa’nti, āvuso, vuccati. Kittāvatā nu kho, āvuso, viññāṇanti vuccatī’’ti?

'Consciousness, consciousness' is said, friend. But to what extent, friend, is it called 'consciousness'?

‘‘‘Vijānāti vijānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā viññāṇanti vuccati.

'It cognizes, it cognizes,' friend, therefore it is called 'consciousness.'

‘‘Kiñca vijānāti? Sukhantipi vijānāti, dukkhantipi vijānāti, adukkhamasukhantipi vijānāti. ‘Vijānāti vijānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā viññāṇanti vuccatī’’ti.

And what does it cognize? It cognizes pleasant feeling, it cognizes painful feeling, and it cognizes neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. 'It cognizes, it cognizes,' friend, therefore it is called consciousness.

‘‘Yā cāvuso, paññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā udāhu visaṃsaṭṭhā? Labbhā ca panimesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā [vinibbhujjitvā vinibbhujjitvā (ka.)] vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetu’’nti? ‘‘Yā cāvuso, paññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā, no visaṃsaṭṭhā. Na ca labbhā imesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetuṃ. Yaṃ hāvuso [yañcāvuso (syā. kaṃ. ka.)], pajānāti taṃ vijānāti, yaṃ vijānāti taṃ pajānāti. Tasmā ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā, no visaṃsaṭṭhā. Na ca labbhā imesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetu’’nti.

'Friend, wisdom and consciousness—are these qualities conjoined or disjoined? And is it possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them?' 'Friend, wisdom and consciousness—these qualities are conjoined, not disjoined. And it is not possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them. For what one understands, that one cognizes; and what one cognizes, that one understands. Therefore, these qualities are conjoined, not disjoined. And it is not possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them.'

‘‘Yā cāvuso, paññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – imesaṃ dhammānaṃ saṃsaṭṭhānaṃ no visaṃsaṭṭhānaṃ kiṃ nānākaraṇa’’nti? ‘‘Yā cāvuso, paññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – imesaṃ dhammānaṃ saṃsaṭṭhānaṃ no visaṃsaṭṭhānaṃ paññā bhāvetabbā, viññāṇaṃ pariññeyyaṃ. Idaṃ nesaṃ nānākaraṇa’’nti.

'Friend, wisdom and consciousness—these phenomena are conjoined, not disjoined. What is the distinction between them?' 'Friend, wisdom and consciousness—these phenomena are conjoined, not disjoined. Wisdom is to be cultivated, consciousness is to be fully understood. This is the distinction between them.'

450. ‘‘‘Vedanā vedanā’ti, āvuso, vuccati. Kittāvatā nu kho, āvuso, vedanāti vuccatī’’ti?

450.'Feeling, feeling,' is said, friend. But in what respect, friend, is it called 'feeling'?

‘‘‘Vedeti vedetī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā vedanāti vuccati.

'It feels, it feels,' friend, therefore it is called feeling.

‘‘Kiñca vedeti? Sukhampi vedeti, dukkhampi vedeti, adukkhamasukhampi vedeti. ‘Vedeti vedetī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā vedanāti vuccatī’’ti.

And what does it feel? It feels pleasant feeling, it feels painful feeling, it feels neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. 'It feels, it feels,' friend, therefore it is called feeling.

‘‘‘Saññā saññā’ti, āvuso, vuccati. Kittāvatā nu kho, āvuso, saññāti vuccatī’’ti?

'Perception, perception,' is said, friend. Now, to what extent, friend, is it called 'perception'?

‘‘‘Sañjānāti sañjānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā saññāti vuccati.

'It perceives, it perceives,' friend, therefore it is called perception.

‘‘Kiñca sañjānāti? Nīlakampi sañjānāti, pītakampi sañjānāti, lohitakampi sañjānāti, odātampi sañjānāti. ‘Sañjānāti sañjānātī’ti kho, āvuso, tasmā saññāti vuccatī’’ti.

And what does it perceive? It perceives blue, it perceives yellow, it perceives red, it perceives white. 'It perceives, it perceives,' friend, therefore it is called perception.

‘‘Yā cāvuso, vedanā yā ca saññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā udāhu visaṃsaṭṭhā? Labbhā ca panimesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetu’’nti? ‘‘Yā cāvuso, vedanā yā ca saññā yañca viññāṇaṃ – ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā, no visaṃsaṭṭhā. Na ca labbhā imesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetuṃ. Yaṃ hāvuso [yañcāvuso (syā. kaṃ. ka.)], vedeti taṃ sañjānāti, yaṃ sañjānāti taṃ vijānāti. Tasmā ime dhammā saṃsaṭṭhā no visaṃsaṭṭhā. Na ca labbhā imesaṃ dhammānaṃ vinibbhujitvā vinibbhujitvā nānākaraṇaṃ paññāpetu’’nti.

'Friend, are feeling, perception, and consciousness—these qualities—conjoined or disjoined? And is it possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them?' 'Friend, feeling, perception, and consciousness—these qualities—are conjoined, not disjoined. And it is not possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them. For what one feels, that one perceives; and what one perceives, that one cognizes. Therefore, these qualities are conjoined, not disjoined. And it is not possible to analyze these qualities and describe the distinction between them.'

451. ‘‘Nissaṭṭhena hāvuso [nissaṭṭhena panāvuso (?)], pañcahi indriyehi parisuddhena manoviññāṇena kiṃ neyya’’nti?

451.“Friend, what is to be known by mind-consciousness, which is detached from the five faculties and purified?”

‘‘Nissaṭṭhena āvuso, pañcahi indriyehi parisuddhena manoviññāṇena ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ neyyaṃ, ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ neyyaṃ, ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ neyya’’nti.

“Friend, with mind-consciousness detached from the five faculties and purified, the base of the infinity of space is to be known as ‘infinite space’; the base of the infinity of consciousness is to be known as ‘infinite consciousness’; the base of nothingness is to be known as ‘there is nothing.’”

‘‘Neyyaṃ panāvuso, dhammaṃ kena pajānātī’’ti?

“But, friend, by what does one understand what is to be known?”

‘‘Neyyaṃ kho, āvuso, dhammaṃ paññācakkhunā pajānātī’’ti.

“Friend, one understands what is to be known with the eye of wisdom.”

‘‘Paññā panāvuso, kimatthiyā’’ti?

“But what, friend, is the purpose of wisdom?”

‘‘Paññā kho, āvuso, abhiññatthā pariññatthā pahānatthā’’ti.

“Wisdom, friend, is for direct knowing, for full understanding, for abandoning.”

452. ‘‘Kati panāvuso, paccayā sammādiṭṭhiyā uppādāyā’’ti?

452.“But how many, friend, are the conditions for the arising of right view?”

‘‘Dve kho, āvuso, paccayā sammādiṭṭhiyā uppādāya – parato ca ghoso, yoniso ca manasikāro. Ime kho, āvuso, dve paccayā sammādiṭṭhiyā uppādāyā’’ti.

“Friend, there are two conditions for the arising of right view: the voice of another and systematic attention. These, friend, are the two conditions for the arising of right view.”

‘‘Katihi panāvuso, aṅgehi anuggahitā sammādiṭṭhi cetovimuttiphalā ca hoti cetovimuttiphalānisaṃsā ca, paññāvimuttiphalā ca hoti paññāvimuttiphalānisaṃsā cā’’ti?

“But, friend, supported by how many factors does right view have liberation of mind as its fruit and benefit, and liberation by wisdom as its fruit and benefit?”

‘‘Pañcahi kho, āvuso, aṅgehi anuggahitā sammādiṭṭhi cetovimuttiphalā ca hoti cetovimuttiphalānisaṃsā ca, paññāvimuttiphalā ca hoti paññāvimuttiphalānisaṃsā ca. Idhāvuso, sammādiṭṭhi sīlānuggahitā ca hoti, sutānuggahitā ca hoti, sākacchānuggahitā ca hoti, samathānuggahitā ca hoti, vipassanānuggahitā ca hoti. Imehi kho, āvuso, pañcahaṅgehi anuggahitā sammādiṭṭhi cetovimuttiphalā ca hoti cetovimuttiphalānisaṃsā ca, paññāvimuttiphalā ca hoti paññāvimuttiphalānisaṃsā cā’’ti.

“Friend, right view supported by five factors is fruitful in the liberation of mind, and beneficial in the liberation of mind; fruitful in the liberation by wisdom, and beneficial in the liberation by wisdom. Here, friend, right view is supported by virtue, supported by learning, supported by discussion, supported by tranquility, and supported by insight. Friend, right view supported by these five factors is fruitful in the liberation of mind, and beneficial in the liberation of mind; fruitful in the liberation by wisdom, and beneficial in the liberation by wisdom.”

453. ‘‘Kati panāvuso, bhavā’’ti?

453.“But how many kinds of existence are there, friend?”

‘‘Tayome, āvuso, bhavā – kāmabhavo, rūpabhavo, arūpabhavo’’ti.

“Friend, there are these three kinds of existence: the sensual realm of existence, the form realm of existence, and the formless realm of existence.”

‘‘Kathaṃ panāvuso, āyatiṃ punabbhavābhinibbatti hotī’’ti?

“But how, friend, is there the production of renewed existence in the future?”

‘‘Avijjānīvaraṇānaṃ kho, āvuso, sattānaṃ taṇhāsaṃyojanānaṃ tatratatrābhinandanā – evaṃ āyatiṃ punabbhavābhinibbatti hotī’’ti.

“Friend, for beings hindered by ignorance and fettered by craving, delighting in this and that—thus there is the production of renewed existence in the future.”

‘‘Kathaṃ panāvuso, āyatiṃ punabbhavābhinibbatti na hotī’’ti?

“But, friend, how is there no production of renewed existence in the future?”

‘‘Avijjāvirāgā kho, āvuso, vijjuppādā taṇhānirodhā – evaṃ āyatiṃ punabbhavābhinibbatti na hotī’’ti.

“Friend, with the fading away of ignorance, with the arising of true knowledge, and with the cessation of craving—thus there is no production of renewed existence in the future.”

454. ‘‘Katamaṃ panāvuso, paṭhamaṃ jhāna’’nti?

454.“But what, friend, is the first jhāna?”

‘‘Idhāvuso, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati – idaṃ vuccati, āvuso, paṭhamaṃ jhāna’’nti.

“Here, friend, a monk, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome qualities, enters and dwells in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by thought and examination, and by joy and happiness born of seclusion. This, friend, is called the first jhāna.”

‘‘Paṭhamaṃ panāvuso, jhānaṃ katiaṅgika’’nti?

“But, friend, how many factors does the first jhāna have?”

‘‘Paṭhamaṃ kho, āvuso, jhānaṃ pañcaṅgikaṃ. Idhāvuso, paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa bhikkhuno vitakko ca vattati, vicāro ca pīti ca sukhañca cittekaggatā ca. Paṭhamaṃ kho, āvuso, jhānaṃ evaṃ pañcaṅgika’’nti.

“The first jhāna, friend, is five-factored. Here, friend, when a monk has attained the first jhāna, applied thought is present, sustained thought is present, rapture is present, pleasure is present, and one-pointedness of mind is present. Thus, friend, the first jhāna is five-factored.”

‘‘Paṭhamaṃ panāvuso, jhānaṃ kataṅgavippahīnaṃ kataṅgasamannāgata’’nti?

“But, friend, what factors are abandoned and with what factors is the first jhāna endowed?”

‘‘Paṭhamaṃ kho, āvuso, jhānaṃ pañcaṅgavippahīnaṃ, pañcaṅgasamannāgataṃ. Idhāvuso, paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa bhikkhuno kāmacchando pahīno hoti, byāpādo pahīno hoti, thīnamiddhaṃ pahīnaṃ hoti, uddhaccakukkuccaṃ pahīnaṃ hoti, vicikicchā pahīnā hoti; vitakko ca vattati, vicāro ca pīti ca sukhañca cittekaggatā ca. Paṭhamaṃ kho, āvuso, jhānaṃ evaṃ pañcaṅgavippahīnaṃ pañcaṅgasamannāgata’’nti.

The first jhāna, friend, is divested of five factors and possessed of five factors. Here, friend, when a bhikkhu has attained the first jhāna, sensual desire is abandoned, ill will is abandoned, sloth and torpor are abandoned, restlessness and remorse are abandoned, doubt is abandoned; and applied thought and sustained thought, rapture and pleasure, and unification of mind are present. Thus, friend, the first jhāna is divested of five factors and possessed of five factors.

455. ‘‘Pañcimāni, āvuso, indriyāni nānāvisayāni nānāgocarāni, na aññamaññassa gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhonti, seyyathidaṃ – cakkhundriyaṃ, sotindriyaṃ, ghānindriyaṃ, jivhindriyaṃ, kāyindriyaṃ. Imesaṃ kho, āvuso, pañcannaṃ indriyānaṃ nānāvisayānaṃ nānāgocarānaṃ, na aññamaññassa gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhontānaṃ, kiṃ paṭisaraṇaṃ, ko ca nesaṃ gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhotī’’ti?

455.Friend, these five faculties have separate ranges and separate pastures, and they do not partake of each other’s range and pasture. They are the eye faculty, the ear faculty, the nose faculty, the tongue faculty, and the body faculty. Of these five faculties, friend, which have separate ranges and separate pastures, and do not partake of each other’s range and pasture, what is their resort, and who partakes of their range and pasture?

‘‘Pañcimāni, āvuso, indriyāni nānāvisayāni nānāgocarāni, na aññamaññassa gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhonti, seyyathidaṃ – cakkhundriyaṃ, sotindriyaṃ, ghānindriyaṃ, jivhindriyaṃ, kāyindriyaṃ. Imesaṃ kho, āvuso, pañcannaṃ indriyānaṃ nānāvisayānaṃ nānāgocarānaṃ, na aññamaññassa gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhontānaṃ, mano paṭisaraṇaṃ, mano ca nesaṃ gocaravisayaṃ paccanubhotī’’ti.

Friend, these five faculties have separate ranges and separate pastures, and they do not partake of each other’s range and pasture. They are the eye faculty, the ear faculty, the nose faculty, the tongue faculty, and the body faculty. Of these five faculties, friend, which have separate ranges and separate pastures, and do not partake of each other’s range and pasture, the mind is their resort, and the mind partakes of their range and pasture.

456. ‘‘Pañcimāni, āvuso, indriyāni, seyyathidaṃ – cakkhundriyaṃ, sotindriyaṃ, ghānindriyaṃ, jivhindriyaṃ, kāyindriyaṃ. Imāni kho, āvuso, pañcindriyāni kiṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhantī’’ti?

456.Friend, there are these five faculties: the eye faculty, the ear faculty, the nose faculty, the tongue faculty, and the body faculty. Upon what, friend, do these five faculties depend?

‘‘Pañcimāni, āvuso, indriyāni, seyyathidaṃ – cakkhundriyaṃ, sotindriyaṃ, ghānindriyaṃ, jivhindriyaṃ, kāyindriyaṃ. Imāni kho, āvuso, pañcindriyāni āyuṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhantī’’ti.

Friend, there are these five faculties: the eye faculty, the ear faculty, the nose faculty, the tongue faculty, and the body faculty. These five faculties, friend, depend on vitality.

‘‘Āyu panāvuso, kiṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’’ti?

But upon what, friend, does vitality depend?

‘‘Āyu usmaṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’’ti.

Vitality depends on heat.

‘‘Usmā panāvuso, kiṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’’ti?

But upon what, friend, does heat depend?

‘‘Usmā āyuṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’’ti.

Heat depends on vitality.

‘‘Idāneva kho mayaṃ, āvuso, āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ evaṃ ājānāma – ‘āyu usmaṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’ti. Idāneva pana mayaṃ, āvuso, āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ evaṃ ājānāma – ‘usmā āyuṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’ti.

Just now, friend, we understood Venerable Sāriputta’s statement in this way: ‘Vitality depends on heat.’ And just now, friend, we understood Venerable Sāriputta’s statement in this way: ‘Heat depends on vitality.’

‘‘Yathā kathaṃ panāvuso, imassa bhāsitassa attho daṭṭhabbo’’ti?

But how, friend, should the meaning of this statement be understood?

‘‘Tena hāvuso, upamaṃ te karissāmi; upamāyapidhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Seyyathāpi, āvuso, telappadīpassa jhāyato acciṃ paṭicca ābhā paññāyati, ābhaṃ paṭicca acci paññāyati; evameva kho, āvuso, āyu usmaṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhati, usmā āyuṃ paṭicca tiṭṭhatī’’ti.

Friend, I shall give you a simile; for by means of a simile, some intelligent persons understand the meaning of what is said. Just as, friend, when an oil lamp is burning, a flame is discerned dependent on the radiance, and radiance is discerned dependent on the flame; in the same way, friend, vitality depends on heat, and heat depends on vitality.

457. ‘‘Teva nu kho, āvuso, āyusaṅkhārā, te vedaniyā dhammā udāhu aññe āyusaṅkhārā aññe vedaniyā dhammā’’ti? ‘‘Na kho, āvuso, teva āyusaṅkhārā te vedaniyā dhammā. Te ca hāvuso, āyusaṅkhārā abhaviṃsu te vedaniyā dhammā, na yidaṃ saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpannassa bhikkhuno vuṭṭhānaṃ paññāyetha. Yasmā ca kho, āvuso, aññe āyusaṅkhārā aññe vedaniyā dhammā, tasmā saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpannassa bhikkhuno vuṭṭhānaṃ paññāyatī’’ti.

457.But friend, are the life formations the same as the feeling factors, or are the life formations one thing and the feeling factors another?" "No, friend, the life formations are not the same as the feeling factors. If, friend, the life formations were the same as the feeling factors, then there would be no discernment of emergence for a bhikkhu who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling. But because, friend, the life formations are one thing and the feeling factors another, therefore emergence is discerned for a bhikkhu who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling.

‘‘Yadā nu kho, āvuso, imaṃ kāyaṃ kati dhammā jahanti; athāyaṃ kāyo ujjhito avakkhitto seti, yathā kaṭṭhaṃ acetana’’nti?

Friend, when how many factors abandon this body, then does this body lie discarded, cast aside, like a senseless piece of wood?

‘‘Yadā kho, āvuso, imaṃ kāyaṃ tayo dhammā jahanti – āyu usmā ca viññāṇaṃ; athāyaṃ kāyo ujjhito avakkhitto seti, yathā kaṭṭhaṃ acetana’’nti.

When, friend, three factors abandon this body—vitality, heat, and consciousness—then this body lies discarded, cast aside, like a senseless piece of wood.

‘‘Yvāyaṃ, āvuso, mato kālaṅkato, yo cāyaṃ bhikkhu saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpanno – imesaṃ kiṃ nānākaraṇa’’nti?

What, friend, is the difference between one who is dead, who has passed away, and a bhikkhu who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling?

‘‘Yvāyaṃ, āvuso, mato kālaṅkato tassa kāyasaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, vacīsaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, cittasaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, āyu parikkhīṇo, usmā vūpasantā, indriyāni paribhinnāni. Yo cāyaṃ bhikkhu saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpanno tassapi kāyasaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, vacīsaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, cittasaṅkhārā niruddhā paṭippassaddhā, āyu na parikkhīṇo, usmā avūpasantā, indriyāni vippasannāni. Yvāyaṃ, āvuso, mato kālaṅkato, yo cāyaṃ bhikkhu saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpanno – idaṃ nesaṃ nānākaraṇa’’nti.

Friend, in one who is dead, who has passed away, bodily volitional formations have ceased and subsided, verbal volitional formations have ceased and subsided, mental volitional formations have ceased and subsided, vitality is exhausted, heat has subsided, and the faculties are broken down. But in a bhikkhu who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling, bodily volitional formations have ceased and subsided, verbal volitional formations have ceased and subsided, and mental volitional formations have ceased and subsided, but vitality is not exhausted, heat has not subsided, and the faculties are clear. This, friend, is the difference between one who is dead, who has passed away, and a bhikkhu who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling.

458. ‘‘Kati panāvuso, paccayā adukkhamasukhāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā’’ti?

458.But how many conditions, friend, are there for the attainment of the neither-painful-nor-pleasant liberation of mind?

‘‘Cattāro kho, āvuso, paccayā adukkhamasukhāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā. Idhāvuso, bhikkhu sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ime kho, āvuso, cattāro paccayā adukkhamasukhāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā’’ti.

There are these four conditions, friend, for the attainment of the neither-painful-nor-pleasant liberation of mind. Here, friend, a bhikkhu, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the prior disappearance of joy and sorrow, enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which is neither painful nor pleasant, with the purification of mindfulness by equanimity. These, friend, are the four conditions for the attainment of the neither-painful-nor-pleasant liberation of mind.

‘‘Kati panāvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā’’ti?

But how many conditions, friend, are there for the attainment of the signless liberation of mind?

‘‘Dve kho, āvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā – sabbanimittānañca amanasikāro, animittāya ca dhātuyā manasikāro. Ime kho, āvuso, dve paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā samāpattiyā’’ti.

There are two conditions, friend, for the attainment of the signless liberation of mind: non-attention to all signs, and adversion to the signless element. These, friend, are the two conditions for the attainment of the signless liberation of mind.

‘‘Kati panāvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā ṭhitiyā’’ti?

But how many conditions, friend, are there for the stability of signless liberation of mind?

‘‘Tayo kho, āvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā ṭhitiyā – sabbanimittānañca amanasikāro, animittāya ca dhātuyā manasikāro, pubbe ca abhisaṅkhāro. Ime kho, āvuso, tayo paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā ṭhitiyā’’ti.

Friend, there are three conditions for the stability of the signless liberation of mind: non-attention to all signs, adversion to the signless element, and prior volitional formation. These, friend, are the three conditions for the stability of signless liberation of mind.

‘‘Kati panāvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā vuṭṭhānāyā’’ti?

But, friend, how many conditions are there for emerging from the signless liberation of mind?

‘‘Dve kho, āvuso, paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā vuṭṭhānāya – sabbanimittānañca manasikāro, animittāya ca dhātuyā amanasikāro. Ime kho, āvuso, dve paccayā animittāya cetovimuttiyā vuṭṭhānāyā’’ti.

There are two conditions, friend, for emerging from the signless liberation of mind: adversion to all signs, and non-attention to the signless element. These, friend, are the two conditions for emerging from the signless liberation of mind.

459. ‘‘Yā cāyaṃ, āvuso, appamāṇā cetovimutti, yā ca ākiñcaññā cetovimutti, yā ca suññatā cetovimutti, yā ca animittā cetovimutti – ime dhammā nānātthā ceva nānābyañjanā ca udāhu ekatthā byañjanameva nāna’’nti?

459.But friend, this boundless liberation of mind, this liberation of mind of nothingness, this liberation of mind of emptiness, and this signless liberation of mind—are these factors different in meaning and different in designation, or are they the same in meaning, only the designation is different?

‘‘Yā cāyaṃ, āvuso, appamāṇā cetovimutti, yā ca ākiñcaññā cetovimutti, yā ca suññatā cetovimutti, yā ca animittā cetovimutti – atthi kho, āvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā nānātthā ceva nānābyañjanā ca; atthi ca kho, āvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā ekatthā, byañjanameva nānaṃ’’.

Friend, this boundless liberation of mind, this liberation of mind of nothingness, this liberation of mind of emptiness, and this signless liberation of mind—there is a way, friend, whereby these factors are different in meaning and different in designation; and there is a way, friend, whereby these factors are the same in meaning, only the designation is different.

‘‘Katamo cāvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā nānātthā ceva nānābyañjanā ca’’?

And what, friend, is the way whereby these factors are different in meaning and different in designation?

‘‘Idhāvuso, bhikkhu mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā viharati, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ. Iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā viharati. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā…pe… muditāsahagatena cetasā… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā viharati, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ. Iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā viharati. Ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, appamāṇā cetovimutti’’.

Here, friend, a bhikkhu dwells pervading one direction with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth. Thus above, below, across, everywhere, in every way, he dwells pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, exalted, immeasurable, without hostility, without ill will. With a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with altruistic joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, he dwells pervading one direction, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth. Thus above, below, across, everywhere, in every way, he dwells pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, exalted, immeasurable, without hostility, without ill will. This, friend, is called the immeasurable liberation of mind.

‘‘Katamā cāvuso, ākiñcaññā cetovimutti’’?

And what, friend, is the liberation of mind of nothingness?

‘‘Idhāvuso, bhikkhu sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma natthi kiñcīti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, ākiñcaññā cetovimutti’’.

Here, friend, a bhikkhu, having entirely transcended the base of infinite consciousness, thinking: ‘There is nothing,’ enters and dwells in the base of nothingness. This, friend, is called the liberation of mind of nothingness.

‘‘Katamā cāvuso, suññatā cetovimutti’’?

And what, friend, is the liberation of mind of emptiness?

‘‘Idhāvuso, bhikkhu araññagato vā rukkhamūlagato vā suññāgāragato vā iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘suññamidaṃ attena vā attaniyena vā’ti. Ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, suññatā cetovimutti’’.

Here, friend, a bhikkhu, gone to the forest, to the root of a tree, or to an empty dwelling, reflects thus: ‘This is empty of self or of what belongs to self.’ This, friend, is called the liberation of mind of emptiness.

‘‘Katamā cāvuso, animittā cetovimutti’’?

And what, friend, is the signless liberation of mind?

‘‘Idhāvuso, bhikkhu sabbanimittānaṃ amanasikārā animittaṃ cetosamādhiṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, animittā cetovimutti. Ayaṃ kho, āvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā nānātthā ceva nānābyañjanā ca’’.

Here, friend, a bhikkhu, not attending to any signs, enters and dwells in the signless samādhi of mind. This, friend, is called the signless liberation of mind. This, friend, is the way by resorting to which these phenomena are different in meaning and different in expression.

‘‘Katamo cāvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā ekatthā byañjanameva nānaṃ’’?

But what, friend, is the way by resorting to which these phenomena are of one meaning, though the expressions are different?

‘‘Rāgo kho, āvuso, pamāṇakaraṇo, doso pamāṇakaraṇo, moho pamāṇakaraṇo. Te khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno pahīnā ucchinnamūlā tālāvatthukatā anabhāvaṃkatā āyatiṃ anuppādadhammā. Yāvatā kho, āvuso, appamāṇā cetovimuttiyo, akuppā tāsaṃ cetovimutti aggamakkhāyati. Sā kho panākuppā cetovimutti suññā rāgena, suññā dosena, suññā mohena. Rāgo kho, āvuso, kiñcano, doso kiñcano, moho kiñcano. Te khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno pahīnā ucchinnamūlā tālāvatthukatā anabhāvaṃkatā āyatiṃ anuppādadhammā. Yāvatā kho, āvuso, ākiñcaññā cetovimuttiyo, akuppā tāsaṃ cetovimutti aggamakkhāyati. Sā kho panākuppā cetovimutti suññā rāgena, suññā dosena, suññā mohena. Rāgo kho, āvuso, nimittakaraṇo, doso nimittakaraṇo, moho nimittakaraṇo. Te khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno pahīnā ucchinnamūlā tālāvatthukatā anabhāvaṃkatā āyatiṃ anuppādadhammā. Yāvatā kho, āvuso, animittā cetovimuttiyo, akuppā tāsaṃ cetovimutti aggamakkhāyati. Sā kho panākuppā cetovimutti suññā rāgena, suññā dosena, suññā mohena. Ayaṃ kho, āvuso, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma ime dhammā ekatthā byañjanameva nāna’’nti.

Friend, lust is a measure-maker, hatred is a measure-maker, delusion is a measure-maker. For a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, these have been abandoned, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, and are no more subject to future arising. As far as the immeasurable liberations of mind are concerned, friend, the unshakable liberation of mind is declared the highest among them. And this unshakable liberation of mind is empty of lust, empty of hatred, empty of delusion. Friend, lust is a defilement, hatred is a defilement, delusion is a defilement. For a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, these have been abandoned, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, and are no more subject to future arising. As far as the liberations of mind of nothingness are concerned, friend, the unshakable liberation of mind is declared the highest among them. And this unshakable liberation of mind is empty of lust, empty of hatred, empty of delusion. Friend, lust is a sign-maker, hatred is a sign-maker, delusion is a sign-maker. For a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, these have been abandoned, cut off at the root, made like a palm stump, obliterated, and are no more subject to future arising. As far as the signless liberations of mind are concerned, friend, the unshakable liberation of mind is declared the highest among them. And this unshakable liberation of mind is empty of lust, empty of hatred, empty of delusion. This, friend, is the way by resorting to which these phenomena are of one meaning, though the expressions are different.

Idamavocāyasmā sāriputto. Attamano āyasmā mahākoṭṭhiko āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ abhinandīti.

This is what Venerable Sāriputta said. Venerable Mahākoṭṭhika was pleased and delighted in Venerable Sāriputta’s words.

Mahāvedallasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ tatiyaṃ.

The Great Vedalla Sutta: The Third.
