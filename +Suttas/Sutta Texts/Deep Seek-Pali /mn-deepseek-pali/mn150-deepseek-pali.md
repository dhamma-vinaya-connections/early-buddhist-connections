# 150. Indriyabhāvanāsuttaṃ

## 150. The Discourse on the Development of the Faculties

453. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā gajaṅgalāyaṃ [kajaṅgalāyaṃ (sī. pī.), kajjaṅgalāyaṃ (syā. kaṃ.)] viharati suveḷuvane [veḷuvane (syā. kaṃ.), mukheluvane (sī. pī.)]. Atha kho uttaro māṇavo pārāsiviyantevāsī [pārāsariyantevāsī (sī. pī.), pārāsiriyantevāsī (syā. kaṃ.)] yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavatā saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinnaṃ kho uttaraṃ māṇavaṃ pārāsiviyantevāsiṃ bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘deseti, uttara, pārāsiviyo brāhmaṇo sāvakānaṃ indriyabhāvana’’nti? ‘‘Deseti, bho gotama, pārāsiviyo brāhmaṇo sāvakānaṃ indriyabhāvana’’nti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ pana, uttara, deseti pārāsiviyo brāhmaṇo sāvakānaṃ indriyabhāvana’’nti? ‘‘Idha, bho gotama, cakkhunā rūpaṃ na passati, sotena saddaṃ na suṇāti – evaṃ kho, bho gotama, deseti pārāsiviyo brāhmaṇo sāvakānaṃ indriyabhāvana’’nti. ‘‘Evaṃ sante kho, uttara, andho bhāvitindriyo bhavissati, badhiro bhāvitindriyo bhavissati; yathā pārāsiviyassa brāhmaṇassa vacanaṃ. Andho hi, uttara, cakkhunā rūpaṃ na passati, badhiro sotena saddaṃ na suṇātī’’ti. Evaṃ vutte, uttaro māṇavo pārāsiviyantevāsī tuṇhībhūto maṅkubhūto pattakkhandho adhomukho pajjhāyanto appaṭibhāno nisīdi.

453.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Gajaṅgala in the Suveḷu Grove. Then the young man Uttara, a student of the Brahmin Pārāsiva, approached the Blessed One. Having approached, he exchanged greetings with the Blessed One. After exchanging polite and amiable conversation, he sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the Blessed One said to the young man Uttara, the student of the Brahmin Pārāsiva: "Uttara, does the Brahmin Pārāsiva teach his disciples the development of the faculties?" "Yes, Master Gotama, the Brahmin Pārāsiva teaches his disciples the development of the faculties." "But how, Uttara, does the Brahmin Pārāsiva teach his disciples the development of the faculties?" "Here, Master Gotama, one does not see a form with the eye, does not hear a sound with the ear—thus, Master Gotama, the Brahmin Pārāsiva teaches his disciples the development of the faculties." "If that is so, Uttara, then a blind person would have developed faculties, a deaf person would have developed faculties, according to the Brahmin Pārāsiva’s teaching. For a blind person, Uttara, does not see a form with the eye, and a deaf person does not hear a sound with the ear." When this was said, the young man Uttara, the student of the Brahmin Pārāsiva, sat silent, dismayed, with drooping shoulders, downcast, brooding, unable to respond.

Atha kho bhagavā uttaraṃ māṇavaṃ pārāsiviyantevāsiṃ tuṇhībhūtaṃ maṅkubhūtaṃ pattakkhandhaṃ adhomukhaṃ pajjhāyantaṃ appaṭibhānaṃ viditvā āyasmantaṃ ānandaṃ āmantesi – ‘‘aññathā kho, ānanda, deseti pārāsiviyo brāhmaṇo sāvakānaṃ indriyabhāvanaṃ, aññathā ca panānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā hotī’’ti. ‘‘Etassa, bhagavā, kālo; etassa, sugata, kālo yaṃ bhagavā ariyassa vinaye anuttaraṃ indriyabhāvanaṃ deseyya. Bhagavato sutvā bhikkhū dhāressantī’’ti. ‘‘Tenahānanda, suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho āyasmā ānando bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

Then the Blessed One, knowing that the young man Uttara, the disciple of the Brahmin Pārāsiva, was silent, crestfallen, his shoulders drooping, head down, brooding, and unresponsive, addressed the Venerable Ānanda: "Ānanda, the Brahmin Pārāsiva teaches his disciples the development of the faculties in one way, but in the Noble One's Discipline, the unsurpassed development of the faculties is otherwise." "Now is the time, Blessed One; now is the time, Sugata, for the Blessed One to teach the unsurpassed development of the faculties in the Noble One's Discipline. Having heard it from the Blessed One, the bhikkhus will remember it." "Then listen, Ānanda, pay close attention, I will speak." "Yes, Venerable Sir," the Venerable Ānanda replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

454. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā hoti? Idhānanda, bhikkhuno cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, cakkhumā puriso ummīletvā vā nimīleyya, nimīletvā vā ummīleyya; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā cakkhuviññeyyesu rūpesu.

454."And how, Ananda, in the Noble One's Discipline is there the unsurpassed development of the faculties? Here, Ananda, when a monk sees a form with the eye, there arises what is agreeable, there arises what is disagreeable, there arises what is neither agreeable nor disagreeable. He understands thus: 'This agreeable thing has arisen in me, this disagreeable thing has arisen, this neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing has arisen. But that is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. This is peaceful, this is sublime, that is, equanimity.' That which has arisen as agreeable, disagreeable, or neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases in him, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ananda, a man with good eyes might open or close his eyes, or close or open his eyes; just so, Ananda, for whomever what has arisen as agreeable, what has arisen as disagreeable, what has arisen as neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases so quickly, so readily, so effortlessly, and equanimity is established—this, Ananda, is called in the Noble One's Discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties regarding forms cognizable by the eye.

455. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno sotena saddaṃ sutvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, balavā puriso appakasireneva accharaṃ [accharikaṃ (syā. kaṃ. pī. ka.)] pahareyya; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā sotaviññeyyesu saddesu.

455."Furthermore, Ananda, when a bhikkhu hears a sound with the ear, there arises what is agreeable, there arises what is disagreeable, there arises what is neither agreeable nor disagreeable. He understands thus: 'This agreeable thing has arisen in me, this disagreeable thing has arisen, this neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing has arisen. But that is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. This is peaceful, this is sublime, that is, equanimity.' That agreeable thing, that disagreeable thing, that neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing which has arisen ceases in him, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ananda, a strong man might snap his fingers with little effort, so too, Ananda, in whomsoever that which arises as agreeable, that which arises as disagreeable, that which arises as neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases so quickly, so readily, with so little effort, and equanimity is established—this, Ananda, is called in the Noble One's Discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties regarding sounds cognizable by the ear."

456. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, īsakaṃpoṇe [īsakapoṇe (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), īsakaphaṇe (sī. aṭṭha.), ‘‘majjhe uccaṃ hutvā’’ti ṭīkāya saṃsanditabbā] padumapalāse [paduminipatte (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] udakaphusitāni pavattanti, na saṇṭhanti; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā ghānaviññeyyesu gandhesu.

456."Furthermore, Ānanda, when a bhikkhu smells an odor with the nose, there arises what is agreeable, there arises what is disagreeable, there arises what is neither agreeable nor disagreeable. He understands thus: ‘This agreeable thing has arisen in me, this disagreeable thing has arisen, this neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing has arisen. But that is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. This is peaceful, this is sublime, that is, equanimity.’ That which has arisen as agreeable, disagreeable, or neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases in him, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ānanda, drops of water roll off a slightly raised lotus leaf and do not remain, so too, Ānanda, for whomever what has arisen as agreeable, disagreeable, or neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases so quickly, so readily, so effortlessly, and equanimity is established—this, Ānanda, is called in the Noble One's Discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties with regard to odors cognizable by the nose."

457. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, balavā puriso jivhagge kheḷapiṇḍaṃ saṃyūhitvā appakasirena vameyya [sandhameyya (ka.)]; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā jivhāviññeyyesu rasesu.

457."Furthermore, Ānanda, when a monk experiences a taste with the tongue, there arises what is agreeable, what is disagreeable, and what is neither agreeable nor disagreeable. He understands thus: ‘This agreeable thing has arisen in me, this disagreeable thing has arisen, this neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing has arisen. And this is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. But this is peaceful, this is sublime—that is, equanimity.’ For him, that arisen agreeable thing, disagreeable thing, and neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing ceases, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ānanda, a strong man might gather up a ball of saliva on the tip of his tongue and spit it out with little effort, so too, Ānanda, for anyone—so quickly, so swiftly, with so little effort—the arisen agreeable thing, disagreeable thing, and neither agreeable nor disagreeable thing ceases, and equanimity is established. This, Ānanda, is called in the Noble One’s Discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties regarding tastes cognizable by the tongue."

458. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, balavā puriso samiñjitaṃ vā bāhaṃ pasāreyya, pasāritaṃ vā bāhaṃ samiñjeyya; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā kāyaviññeyyesu phoṭṭhabbesu.

458."Furthermore, Ānanda, when a monk experiences a tactile sensation with the body, there arises the agreeable, the disagreeable, and the neither agreeable nor disagreeable. He understands thus: ‘This agreeable has arisen in me, this disagreeable has arisen, this neither agreeable nor disagreeable has arisen. And that is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. But this is peaceful, this is sublime—that is, equanimity.’ For him, that arisen agreeable, disagreeable, and neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ānanda, a strong man might extend his drawn-in arm or draw in his extended arm, so too, Ānanda, for anyone—so quickly, so swiftly, with so little effort—the arisen agreeable, disagreeable, and neither agreeable nor disagreeable ceases, and equanimity is established. This, Ānanda, is called in the Noble One’s Discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties regarding tactile objects cognizable by the body."

459. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno manasā dhammaṃ viññāya uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannaṃ kho me idaṃ manāpaṃ, uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ, uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ. Tañca kho saṅkhataṃ oḷārikaṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ. Etaṃ santaṃ etaṃ paṇītaṃ yadidaṃ – upekkhā’ti. Tassa taṃ uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati; upekkhā saṇṭhāti. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, balavā puriso divasaṃsantatte [divasasantette (sī.)] ayokaṭāhe dve vā tīṇi vā udakaphusitāni nipāteyya. Dandho, ānanda, udakaphusitānaṃ nipāto, atha kho naṃ khippameva parikkhayaṃ pariyādānaṃ gaccheyya; evameva kho, ānanda, yassa kassaci evaṃsīghaṃ evaṃtuvaṭaṃ evaṃappakasirena uppannaṃ manāpaṃ uppannaṃ amanāpaṃ uppannaṃ manāpāmanāpaṃ nirujjhati, upekkhā saṇṭhāti – ayaṃ vuccatānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā manoviññeyyesu dhammesu. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā hoti.

459.Furthermore, Ānanda, when a bhikkhu, through the mind, cognizes a mental object, there arises the agreeable, the disagreeable, and the neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable. He understands thus: ‘This agreeable has arisen in me, this disagreeable has arisen, this neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable has arisen. And that is conditioned, gross, dependently arisen. But this is peaceful, this is sublime—that is, equanimity.’ For him, that arisen agreeable, disagreeable, and neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable ceases, and equanimity is established. Just as, Ānanda, a strong man might drop two or three drops of water onto an iron pan heated all day. Slow, Ānanda, is the falling of the drops, yet they would quickly go to destruction and exhaustion. So too, Ānanda, for anyone—so quickly, so swiftly, with so little difficulty—the arisen agreeable, disagreeable, and neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable ceases, and equanimity is established. This, Ānanda, is called in the Noble One’s discipline the unsurpassed development of the faculties with regard to mental objects knowable by the mind. Thus, Ānanda, in the Noble One’s discipline, there is the unsurpassed development of the faculties.

460. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, sekho hoti pāṭipado? Idhānanda, bhikkhuno cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So tena uppannena manāpena uppannena amanāpena uppannena manāpāmanāpena aṭṭīyati harāyati jigucchati. Sotena saddaṃ sutvā…pe… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā…, jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So tena uppannena manāpena uppannena amanāpena uppannena manāpāmanāpena aṭṭīyati harāyati jigucchati. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, sekho hoti pāṭipado.

460.And how, Ānanda, is one a trainee on the path? Here, Ānanda, a bhikkhu, seeing a form with the eye, the agreeable arises, the disagreeable arises, the neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable arises. He is repelled, humiliated, and disgusted by that arisen agreeable, that arisen disagreeable, and that arisen neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable. Hearing a sound with the ear… smelling an odour with the nose… tasting a flavour with the tongue… touching a tangible object with the body… cognizing a mental object with the mind, the agreeable arises, the disagreeable arises, the neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable arises. He is repelled, humiliated, and disgusted by that arisen agreeable, that arisen disagreeable, and that arisen neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable. Thus, Ānanda, is one a trainee on the path.

461. ‘‘Kathañcānanda, ariyo hoti bhāvitindriyo? Idhānanda, bhikkhuno cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūle [paṭikkūle (sabbattha)] appaṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, appaṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘appaṭikūle paṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, paṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūle ca appaṭikūle ca appaṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, appaṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘appaṭikūle ca paṭikūle ca paṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, paṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūlañca appaṭikūlañca tadubhayaṃ abhinivajjetvā upekkhako vihareyyaṃ sato sampajāno’ti, upekkhako tattha viharati sato sampajāno.

461.And how, Ānanda, is one a noble one with developed faculties? Here, Ānanda, a bhikkhu sees a form with the eye, and there arises in him the agreeable, the disagreeable, and the neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the unrepulsive in the repulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the unrepulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the repulsive in the unrepulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the repulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the unrepulsive in both the repulsive and the unrepulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the unrepulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the repulsive in both the unrepulsive and the repulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the repulsive there. If he wishes: ‘Avoiding both the repulsive and the unrepulsive, may I dwell equanimous, mindful, and fully aware,’ he dwells equanimous, mindful, and fully aware there.

462. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, bhikkhuno sotena saddaṃ sutvā…pe… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya uppajjati manāpaṃ, uppajjati amanāpaṃ, uppajjati manāpāmanāpaṃ. So sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūle appaṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, appaṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘appaṭikūle paṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, paṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūle ca appaṭikūle ca appaṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, appaṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘appaṭikūle ca paṭikūle ca paṭikūlasaññī vihareyya’nti, paṭikūlasaññī tattha viharati. Sace ākaṅkhati – ‘paṭikūlañca appaṭikūlañca tadubhayampi abhinivajjetvā upekkhako vihareyyaṃ sato sampajāno’ti, upekkhako tattha viharati sato sampajāno. Evaṃ kho, ānanda, ariyo hoti bhāvitindriyo.

462.Furthermore, Ānanda, a bhikkhu hears a sound with the ear… smells an odour with the nose… tastes a flavour with the tongue… touches a tangible object with the body… cognizes a mental object with the mind, and there arises in him the agreeable, the disagreeable, and the neither-agreeable-nor-disagreeable. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the unrepulsive in the repulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the unrepulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the repulsive in the unrepulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the repulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the unrepulsive in both the repulsive and the unrepulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the unrepulsive there. If he wishes: ‘May I dwell perceiving the repulsive in both the unrepulsive and the repulsive,’ he dwells perceiving the repulsive there. If he wishes: ‘Avoiding both the repulsive and the unrepulsive, may I dwell equanimous, mindful, and fully aware,’ he dwells equanimous, mindful, and fully aware there. Thus, Ānanda, is one a noble one with developed faculties.

463. ‘‘Iti kho, ānanda, desitā mayā ariyassa vinaye anuttarā indriyabhāvanā, desito sekho pāṭipado, desito ariyo bhāvitindriyo. Yaṃ kho, ānanda, satthārā karaṇīyaṃ sāvakānaṃ hitesinā anukampakena anukampaṃ upādāya, kataṃ vo taṃ mayā. Etāni, ānanda, rukkhamūlāni, etāni suññāgārāni, jhāyathānanda, mā pamādattha, mā pacchā vippaṭisārino ahuvattha. Ayaṃ vo amhākaṃ anusāsanī’’ti.

463.Thus, Ānanda, I have taught the unsurpassed development of the faculties in the Noble One’s Discipline; I have taught the trainee on the path; I have taught the Noble One with developed faculties. Whatever should be done, Ānanda, by a teacher out of compassion, seeking the welfare of his disciples, that has been done by me for you. These, Ānanda, are the roots of trees; these are empty dwellings. Meditate, Ānanda, do not be negligent, do not regret it later. This is our instruction to you.

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamano āyasmā ānando bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandīti.

Thus spoke the Blessed One. The Venerable Ānanda, pleased, rejoiced at the Blessed One’s words.

Indriyabhāvanāsuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ dasamaṃ.

The Indriyabhāvanā Sutta, the tenth, is concluded.

Saḷāyatanavaggo niṭṭhito pañcamo.

The Division of the Six Sense Bases, the fifth, is concluded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its summary:

Anāthapiṇḍiko channo, puṇṇo nandakarāhulā;Chachakkaṃ saḷāyatanikaṃ, nagaravindeyyasuddhikā;Indriyabhāvanā cāpi, vaggo ovādapañcamoti.

Anāthapiṇḍika, Channa, Puṇṇa, Nandaka, Rāhula; Six Sixes, the Six Sense Bases, Purity in Nagaravindeyya; The Development of the Faculties, and the fifth admonitory division.

Idaṃ vaggānamuddānaṃ –

This is the summary of the divisions:

Devadahonupado ca, suññato ca vibhaṅgako;Saḷāyatanoti vaggā, uparipaṇṇāsake ṭhitāti.

Devadaha, Anupada, Suññata, and Vibhaṅga; and the Six Sense Bases—these are the divisions that stand in the Uparipaṇṇāsaka.

Uparipaṇṇāsakaṃ samattaṃ.

The Uparipaṇṇāsaka is completed.

Tīhi paṇṇāsakehi paṭimaṇḍito sakalo

Adorned with three fifties, the entire (collection)

Majjhimanikāyo samatto.

The Middle Length Discourses are completed.
