# 28. Mahāhatthipadopamasuttaṃ

## 28. The Greater Discourse on the Simile of the Elephant's Footprint

300. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tatra kho āyasmā sāriputto bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘āvuso bhikkhave’’ti. ‘‘Āvuso’’ti kho te bhikkhū āyasmato sāriputtassa paccassosuṃ. Āyasmā sāriputto etadavoca – ‘‘seyyathāpi, āvuso, yāni kānici jaṅgalānaṃ pāṇānaṃ padajātāni sabbāni tāni hatthipade samodhānaṃ gacchanti, hatthipadaṃ tesaṃ aggamakkhāyati yadidaṃ mahantattena; evameva kho, āvuso, ye keci kusalā dhammā sabbete catūsu ariyasaccesu saṅgahaṃ gacchanti. Katamesu catūsu? Dukkhe ariyasacce, dukkhasamudaye ariyasacce, dukkhanirodhe ariyasacce, dukkhanirodhagāminiyā paṭipadāya ariyasacce’’.

300.Thus have I heard: At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi in Jeta's Grove, Anathapindika's Park. There, the Venerable Sariputta addressed the bhikkhus: "Friends, bhikkhus!" "Friends," those bhikkhus replied to the Venerable Sariputta. The Venerable Sariputta said this: "Just as, friends, whatever footprints of creatures that live on the earth's surface there are, all of them are included within the elephant's footprint, and the elephant's footprint is reckoned the foremost among them because of its great size; in the same way, friends, all wholesome qualities are included within the four noble truths. Which four? The noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering."

301. ‘‘Katamañcāvuso, dukkhaṃ ariyasaccaṃ? Jātipi dukkhā, jarāpi dukkhā, maraṇampi dukkhaṃ, sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsāpi dukkhā, yampicchaṃ na labhati tampi dukkhaṃ; saṃkhittena, pañcupādānakkhandhā dukkhā. Katame cāvuso, pañcupādānakkhandhā? Seyyathidaṃ – rūpupādānakkhandho, vedanupādānakkhandho, saññupādānakkhandho, saṅkhārupādānakkhandho, viññāṇupādānakkhandho.

301."And what, friend, is the noble truth of suffering? Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, death is suffering; sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair are suffering; not getting what one wants is suffering. In brief, the five aggregates subject to clinging are suffering. And what, friend, are the five aggregates subject to clinging? They are: the form aggregate subject to clinging, the feeling aggregate subject to clinging, the perception aggregate subject to clinging, the mental formations aggregate subject to clinging, and the consciousness aggregate subject to clinging.

‘‘Katamo cāvuso, rūpupādānakkhandho? Cattāri ca mahābhūtāni, catunnañca mahābhūtānaṃ upādāya rūpaṃ.

"And what, friends, is the form aggregate of clinging? It is the four great elements and the form derived from the four great elements."

‘‘Katamā cāvuso, cattāro mahābhūtā? Pathavīdhātu, āpodhātu, tejodhātu, vāyodhātu.

"And what, friends, are the four great elements? They are the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element."

302. ‘‘Katamā cāvuso, pathavīdhātu? Pathavīdhātu siyā ajjhattikā, siyā bāhirā. Katamā cāvuso, ajjhattikā pathavīdhātu? Yaṃ ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ kakkhaḷaṃ kharigataṃ upādinnaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – kesā lomā nakhā dantā taco maṃsaṃ nhāru aṭṭhi aṭṭhimiñjaṃ vakkaṃ hadayaṃ yakanaṃ kilomakaṃ pihakaṃ papphāsaṃ antaṃ antaguṇaṃ udariyaṃ karīsaṃ, yaṃ vā panaññampi kiñci ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ kakkhaḷaṃ kharigataṃ upādinnaṃ. Ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, ajjhattikā pathavīdhātu. Yā ceva kho pana ajjhattikā pathavīdhātu, yā ca bāhirā pathavīdhātu, pathavīdhāturevesā. ‘Taṃ netaṃ mama, nesohamasmi, na meso attā’ti – evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya daṭṭhabbaṃ. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā pathavīdhātuyā nibbindati, pathavīdhātuyā cittaṃ virājeti.

302."And what, friends, is the earth element? The earth element may be internal or external. And what, friends, is the internal earth element? Whatever internal, belonging to oneself, is hard, solid, and clung to—that is, head hairs, body hairs, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, diaphragm, spleen, lungs, intestines, mesentery, stomach contents, feces, or whatever else internal, belonging to oneself, is hard, solid, and clung to—this, friends, is called the internal earth element. Both the internal earth element and the external earth element are simply the earth element. This should be seen as it really is with proper wisdom: ‘This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self.’ When one sees this as it really is with proper wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with the earth element and detaches the mind from the earth element."

‘‘Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ bāhirā āpodhātu pakuppati [pathavīdhātu pakuppati (ka.)]. Antarahitā tasmiṃ samaye bāhirā pathavīdhātu hoti. Tassā hi nāma, āvuso, bāhirāya pathavīdhātuyā tāva mahallikāya aniccatā paññāyissati, khayadhammatā paññāyissati, vayadhammatā paññāyissati, vipariṇāmadhammatā paññāyissati. Kiṃ panimassa mattaṭṭhakassa kāyassa taṇhupādinnassa ‘ahanti vā mamanti vā asmī’ti vā? Atha khvāssa notevettha hoti.

"There comes a time, friend, when the external water element is disturbed. At that time the external earth element disappears. Even this external earth element, so vast, will be seen as impermanent, subject to decay, vanishing, and change. What then of this body, sustained by craving, which lasts but a little while? How could there be any considering it as ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’?"

‘‘Tañce, āvuso, bhikkhuṃ pare akkosanti paribhāsanti rosenti vihesenti, so evaṃ pajānāti – ‘uppannā kho me ayaṃ sotasamphassajā dukkhavedanā. Sā ca kho paṭicca, no apaṭicca. Kiṃ paṭicca? Phassaṃ paṭicca’. So [sopikho (syā.), sopi (ka.)] phasso aniccoti passati, vedanā aniccāti passati, saññā aniccāti passati, saṅkhārā aniccāti passati, viññāṇaṃ aniccanti passati. Tassa dhātārammaṇameva cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati adhimuccati.

"If, friend, others insult, revile, provoke, or harass a monk, he understands thus: ‘This painful feeling born of ear-contact has arisen in me. And it is dependent, not independent. Dependent on what? Dependent on contact.’ He sees that contact is impermanent, feeling is impermanent, perception is impermanent, volitional formations are impermanent, consciousness is impermanent. His mind leaps forward, settles down, becomes serene, steady, and released, with the element as its object."

‘‘Tañce, āvuso, bhikkhuṃ pare aniṭṭhehi akantehi amanāpehi samudācaranti – pāṇisamphassenapi leḍḍusamphassenapi daṇḍasamphassenapi satthasamphassenapi. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘tathābhūto kho ayaṃ kāyo yathābhūtasmiṃ kāye pāṇisamphassāpi kamanti, leḍḍusamphassāpi kamanti, daṇḍasamphassāpi kamanti, satthasamphassāpi kamanti. Vuttaṃ kho panetaṃ bhagavatā kakacūpamovāde – ‘‘ubhatodaṇḍakena cepi, bhikkhave, kakacena corā ocarakā aṅgamaṅgāni okanteyyuṃ, tatrāpi yo mano padūseyya na me so tena sāsanakaro’’ti. Āraddhaṃ kho pana me vīriyaṃ bhavissati asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ. Kāmaṃ dāni imasmiṃ kāye pāṇisamphassāpi kamantu, leḍḍusamphassāpi kamantu, daṇḍasamphassāpi kamantu, satthasamphassāpi kamantu, karīyati hidaṃ buddhānaṃ sāsana’nti.

"If, friends, others address a monk with unpleasant, disagreeable, and displeasing words—or treat him with blows from fists, clods, sticks, or knives—he understands thus: 'This body is of such a nature that blows from fists, clods, sticks, or knives will come upon it. The Blessed One has said in the simile of the saw: "bhikkhus, even if bandits were to sever you limb from limb with a two-handled saw, anyone who harbors hate in his heart on that account would not be following my teaching." My energy shall be aroused and unflagging, my mindfulness established and unconfused, my body calmed and untroubled, my mind collected and unified. Let blows from fists, clods, sticks, or knives now come upon this body—for this is how the Buddhas' teaching is done.'"

‘‘Tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhāti. So tena saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati – ‘alābhā vata me, na vata me lābhā, dulladdhaṃ vata me, na vata me suladdhaṃ, yassa me evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhātī’ti. Seyyathāpi, āvuso, suṇisā sasuraṃ disvā saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati; evameva kho, āvuso, tassa ce bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhāti, so tena saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati – ‘alābhā vata me na vata me lābhā, dulladdhaṃ vata me, na vata me suladdhaṃ, yassa me evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhātī’ti. Tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā saṇṭhāti, so tena attamano hoti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hoti.

“Friend, if, when a monk recollects the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in him, he is stirred and agitated by that: ‘It is a loss for me, not a gain; it is ill-gotten for me, not well-gotten, that when I recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in me.’ Just as, friend, a daughter-in-law is stirred and agitated upon seeing her father-in-law, so too, friend, if, when a monk recollects the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in him, he is stirred and agitated by that: ‘It is a loss for me, not a gain; it is ill-gotten for me, not well-gotten, that when I recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in me.’ But if, friend, when a monk recollects the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome becomes established in him, he is gratified by that. To this extent, friend, much has been accomplished by that monk.

303. ‘‘Katamā cāvuso, āpodhātu? Āpodhātu siyā ajjhattikā, siyā bāhirā. Katamā cāvuso ajjhattikā āpodhātu? Yaṃ ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ āpo āpogataṃ upādinnaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – pittaṃ semhaṃ pubbo lohitaṃ sedo medo assu vasā kheḷo siṅghāṇikā lasikā muttaṃ, yaṃ vā panaññampi kiñci ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ āpo āpogataṃ upādinnaṃ – ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, ajjhattikā āpodhātu. Yā ceva kho pana ajjhattikā āpodhātu yā ca bāhirā āpodhātu, āpodhāturevesā. ‘Taṃ netaṃ mama, nesohamasmi, na meso attā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya daṭṭhabbaṃ. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā āpodhātuyā nibbindati, āpodhātuyā cittaṃ virājeti.

303.“And what, friend, is the water element? The water element may be internal or external. And what, friend, is the internal water element? Whatever internal, belonging to oneself, is water, watery, and clung to—that is, bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, fat, tears, grease, saliva, mucus, synovial fluid, urine, or whatever else internal, belonging to oneself, is water, watery, and clung to—this, friend, is called the internal water element. Both the internal water element and the external water element are simply the water element. ‘This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self’—this should be seen as it really is with right wisdom. Having seen it thus as it really is with right wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with the water element, and one’s mind is detached from the water element.

‘‘Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ bāhirā āpodhātu pakuppati. Sā gāmampi vahati, nigamampi vahati, nagarampi vahati, janapadampi vahati, janapadapadesampi vahati. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ mahāsamudde yojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, dviyojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, tiyojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, catuyojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, pañcayojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, chayojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti, sattayojanasatikānipi udakāni ogacchanti. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ mahāsamudde sattatālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, chattālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, pañcatālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, catuttālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, titālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, dvitālampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, tālamattampi [tālaṃpi (sī.)] udakaṃ saṇṭhāti. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ mahāsamudde sattaporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, chapporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, pañcaporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, catupporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, tiporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, dviporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, porisamattampi [porisaṃpi (sī.)] udakaṃ saṇṭhāti. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ mahāsamudde aḍḍhaporisampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, kaṭimattampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, jāṇukamattampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti, gopphakamattampi udakaṃ saṇṭhāti. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo, yaṃ mahāsamudde aṅgulipabbatemanamattampi udakaṃ na hoti. Tassā hi nāma, āvuso, bāhirāya āpodhātuyā tāva mahallikāya aniccatā paññāyissati, khayadhammatā paññāyissati, vayadhammatā paññāyissati, vipariṇāmadhammatā paññāyissati. Kiṃ panimassa mattaṭṭhakassa kāyassa taṇhupādinnassa ‘ahanti vā mamanti vā asmīti’ vā? Atha khvāssa notevettha hoti…pe… tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā saṇṭhāti. So tena attamano hoti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hoti.

“Friend, there comes a time when the external water element becomes agitated. It carries away villages, towns, cities, districts, and regions of districts. There comes a time when the waters of the great ocean recede a hundred leagues, two hundred leagues, three hundred leagues, four hundred leagues, five hundred leagues, six hundred leagues, seven hundred leagues. There comes a time when the waters of the great ocean stand at a depth of seven palm-trees, six palm-trees, five palm-trees, four palm-trees, three palm-trees, two palm-trees, even just one palm-tree. There comes a time when the waters of the great ocean stand at a depth of seven fathoms, six fathoms, five fathoms, four fathoms, three fathoms, two fathoms, even just one fathom. There comes a time when the waters of the great ocean stand at a depth of half a fathom, waist-deep, knee-deep, ankle-deep. There comes a time when there is not even enough water in the great ocean to wet the joint of a finger. Thus, friend, even this so vast external water element is seen to be impermanent, subject to decay, vanishing, and change. How much more so this trifling body, sustained by craving, which one regards as ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’? Then, for him, there is no ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’ here. When a monk recollects the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome becomes established in him, he is pleased. Even this much, friend, is a great achievement for a monk.

304. ‘‘Katamā cāvuso, tejodhātu? Tejodhātu siyā ajjhattikā, siyā bāhirā. Katamā cāvuso, ajjhattikā tejodhātu? Yaṃ ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ tejo tejogataṃ upādinnaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – yena ca santappati, yena ca jīrīyati, yena ca pariḍayhati, yena ca asitapītakhāyitasāyitaṃ sammā pariṇāmaṃ gacchati, yaṃ vā panaññampi kiñci ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ tejo tejogataṃ upādinnaṃ – ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, ajjhattikā tejodhātu. Yā ceva kho pana ajjhattikā tejodhātu yā ca bāhirā tejodhātu, tejodhāturevesā. ‘Taṃ netaṃ mama, nesohamasmi, na meso attā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya daṭṭhabbaṃ. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā tejodhātuyā nibbindati, tejodhātuyā cittaṃ virājeti.

304.“And what, friend, is the fire element? The fire element may be internal or external. What, friend, is the internal fire element? Whatever internally, belonging to oneself, is fire, fiery, and clung to—that is to say, that by which one is heated, ages, and is burnt up, and that by which what is eaten, drunk, chewed, and tasted is properly digested, or whatever else internally, belonging to oneself, is fire, fiery, and clung to—this, friend, is called the internal fire element. Both the internal fire element and the external fire element are simply the fire element. This should be seen as it really is with right wisdom: ‘This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self.’ Having seen this thus as it really is with right wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with the fire element and detaches the mind from the fire element.

‘‘Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ bāhirā tejodhātu pakuppati. Sā gāmampi dahati, nigamampi dahati, nagarampi dahati, janapadampi dahati, janapadapadesampi dahati. Sā haritantaṃ vā panthantaṃ vā selantaṃ vā udakantaṃ vā ramaṇīyaṃ vā bhūmibhāgaṃ āgamma anāhārā nibbāyati. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ kukkuṭapattenapi nhārudaddulenapi aggiṃ gavesanti. Tassā hi nāma, āvuso, bāhirāya tejodhātuyā tāva mahallikāya aniccatā paññāyissati, khayadhammatā paññāyissati, vayadhammatā paññāyissati, vipariṇāmadhammatā paññāyissati. Kiṃ panimassa mattaṭṭhakassa kāyassa taṇhupādinnassa ‘ahanti vā mamanti vā asmī’ti vā? Atha khvāssa notevettha hoti…pe… tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā saṇṭhāti, so tena attamano hoti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hoti.

“Friend, there comes a time when the external fire element becomes agitated. It burns villages, towns, cities, districts, and regions of districts. Reaching a green stretch, a road, a rocky area, a watery area, or a pleasant land area, it goes out for lack of sustenance. Friend, there comes a time when they search for fire even with a chicken’s wing or a scrap of hide. Indeed, friend, such great impermanence will be evident in that external fire element; liability to destruction will be evident, liability to decay will be evident, liability to alteration will be evident. What then of this frail body, sustained by craving, that one thinks of as ‘I,’ ‘mine,’ or ‘I am’? Then, for him, there is no ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’ here. When, friend, a bhikkhu recollects the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Sangha in this way, equanimity based on the wholesome becomes established, he is gratified by that. Even this much, friend, is much done by a bhikkhu.

305. ‘‘Katamā cāvuso, vāyodhātu? Vāyodhātu siyā ajjhattikā, siyā bāhirā. Katamā cāvuso, ajjhattikā vāyodhātu? Yaṃ ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ vāyo vāyogataṃ upādinnaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – uddhaṅgamā vātā, adhogamā vātā, kucchisayā vātā, koṭṭhāsayā [koṭṭhasayā (sī. pī.)] vātā, aṅgamaṅgānusārino vātā, assāso passāso iti, yaṃ vā panaññampi kiñci ajjhattaṃ paccattaṃ vāyo vāyogataṃ upādinnaṃ – ayaṃ vuccatāvuso, ajjhattikā vāyodhātu. Yā ceva kho pana ajjhattikā vāyodhātu, yā ca bāhirā vāyodhātu, vāyodhāturevesā. ‘Taṃ netaṃ mama nesohamasmi na meso attā’ti evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya daṭṭhabbaṃ. Evametaṃ yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya disvā vāyodhātuyā nibbindati vāyodhātuyā cittaṃ virājeti.

305.“And what, friend, is the air element? The air element may be internal or external. And what, friend, is the internal air element? Whatever internal, belonging to oneself, is air, airy, and clung to, such as up-going winds, down-going winds, winds in the belly, winds in the bowels, winds that course through the limbs, inhalation and exhalation, or whatever else internal, belonging to oneself, is air, airy, and clung to—this, friend, is called the internal air element. Both the internal air element and the external air element are simply the air element. ‘This is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self’—thus this should be seen as it really is with right wisdom. Seeing this as it really is with right wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with the air element and detaches the mind from the air element.

‘‘Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ bāhirā vāyodhātu pakuppati. Sā gāmampi vahati, nigamampi vahati, nagarampi vahati, janapadampi vahati, janapadapadesampi vahati. Hoti kho so, āvuso, samayo yaṃ gimhānaṃ pacchime māse tālavaṇṭenapi vidhūpanenapi vātaṃ pariyesanti, ossavanepi tiṇāni na icchanti. Tassā hi nāma, āvuso, bāhirāya vāyodhātuyā tāva mahallikāya aniccatā paññāyissati, khayadhammatā paññāyissati, vayadhammatā paññāyissati, vipariṇāmadhammatā paññāyissati. Kiṃ panimassa mattaṭṭhakassa kāyassa taṇhupādinnassa ‘ahanti vā mamanti vā asmī’ti vā? Atha khvāssa notevettha hoti.

“Friend, there comes a time when the external air element becomes agitated. It sweeps away villages, market towns, cities, districts, and even areas within districts. There comes a time, friend, in the last month of summer when people seek a breeze even with a palm-leaf fan or a winnowing fan, and they do not even want the grass to be wet from a drip. Indeed, friend, even in such a great and mighty external air element, its impermanence will be evident, its liability to decay will be evident, its liability to vanish will be evident, its liability to change will be evident. How much more so for this slight body, maintained by craving, which one thinks of as ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’? Then, for him, there is no ‘I’ or ‘mine’ or ‘I am’ here.

‘‘Tañce, āvuso, bhikkhuṃ pare akkosanti paribhāsanti rosenti vihesenti. So evaṃ pajānāti, uppannā kho me ayaṃ sotasamphassajā dukkhā vedanā. Sā ca kho paṭicca, no apaṭicca. Kiṃ paṭicca? Phassaṃ paṭicca. Sopi phasso aniccoti passati, vedanā aniccāti passati, saññā aniccāti passati, saṅkhārā aniccāti passati, viññāṇaṃ aniccanti passati. Tassa dhātārammaṇameva cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati adhimuccati.

“If, friend, others abuse, revile, scold, or harass that monk, he understands thus: ‘This painful feeling born of ear-contact has arisen in me. And that is dependent, not independent. Dependent on what? Dependent on contact.’ He sees that contact is impermanent, feeling is impermanent, perception is impermanent, formations are impermanent, consciousness is impermanent. His mind leaps forward, becomes serene, settles, and becomes resolute on that element as its object.

‘‘Tañce, āvuso, bhikkhuṃ pare aniṭṭhehi akantehi amanāpehi samudācaranti, pāṇisamphassenapi leḍḍusamphassenapi daṇḍasamphassenapi satthasamphassenapi. So evaṃ pajānāti ‘tathābhūto kho ayaṃ kāyo yathābhūtasmiṃ kāye pāṇisamphassāpi kamanti, leḍḍusamphassāpi kamanti, daṇḍasamphassāpi kamanti, satthasamphassāpi kamanti. Vuttaṃ kho panetaṃ bhagavatā kakacūpamovāde ‘‘ubhatodaṇḍakena cepi, bhikkhave, kakacena corā ocarakā aṅgamaṅgāni okanteyyuṃ. Tatrāpi yo mano padūseyya, na me so tena sāsanakaro’’ti. Āraddhaṃ kho pana me vīriyaṃ bhavissati asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ. Kāmaṃ dāni imasmiṃ kāye pāṇisamphassāpi kamantu, leḍḍusamphassāpi kamantu, daṇḍasamphassāpi kamantu, satthasamphassāpi kamantu. Karīyati hidaṃ buddhānaṃ sāsana’nti.

“Friend, if others assail that monk with undesirable, unlikable, and disagreeable things—whether with blows of the hand, with clods, with sticks, or with knives—he understands thus: ‘This body is of such a nature that blows of the hand impinge upon it, clods impinge upon it, sticks impinge upon it, and knives impinge upon it. Indeed, the Blessed One has said in the Exhortation on the Saw: “bhikkhus, even if bandits were to carve you up limb by limb with a two-handled saw, anyone who held hate in their mind on that account would not be following my instructions.” My energy will be roused and unwavering, mindfulness present and unconfused, the body tranquil and unperturbed, the mind collected and unified. Let blows of the hand now impinge upon this body, let clods impinge upon it, let sticks impinge upon it, let knives impinge upon it—for this is how the Buddhas’ teaching is put into practice.’

‘‘Tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhāti. So tena saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati – ‘alābhā vata me, na vata me lābhā, dulladdhaṃ vata me, na vata me suladdhaṃ. Yassa me evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhātī’ti. Seyyathāpi, āvuso, suṇisā sasuraṃ disvā saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati; evameva kho, āvuso, tassa ce bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhāti. So tena saṃvijjati saṃvegaṃ āpajjati – ‘alābhā vata me, na vata me lābhā, dulladdhaṃ vata me, na vata me suladdhaṃ. Yassa me evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā na saṇṭhātī’ti. Tassa ce, āvuso, bhikkhuno evaṃ buddhaṃ anussarato, evaṃ dhammaṃ anussarato, evaṃ saṅghaṃ anussarato, upekkhā kusalanissitā saṇṭhāti, so tena attamano hoti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hoti.

“Friend, if while recollecting the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in that monk, he is stirred by that and experiences a sense of urgency: ‘Alas, it is a loss for me, not a gain; it is ill-gained for me, not well-gained, that while I recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in me!’ Just as, friend, a daughter-in-law, upon seeing her father-in-law, is stirred and experiences a sense of urgency, in the same way, friend, if while recollecting the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in that monk, he is stirred by that and experiences a sense of urgency: ‘Alas, it is a loss for me, not a gain; it is ill-gained for me, not well-gained, that while I recollect the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome does not become established in me!’ But friend, if while recollecting the Buddha, the Dhamma, and the Saṅgha in this way, equanimity rooted in the wholesome becomes established in that monk, he is gratified by that. To this extent, friend, much has been done by that monk.

306. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, āvuso, kaṭṭhañca paṭicca valliñca paṭicca tiṇañca paṭicca mattikañca paṭicca ākāso parivārito agāraṃ tveva saṅkhaṃ gacchati; evameva kho, āvuso, aṭṭhiñca paṭicca nhāruñca paṭicca maṃsañca paṭicca cammañca paṭicca ākāso parivārito rūpaṃ tveva saṅkhaṃ gacchati. Ajjhattikañceva, āvuso, cakkhuṃ aparibhinnaṃ hoti, bāhirā ca rūpā na āpāthaṃ āgacchanti, no ca tajjo samannāhāro hoti, neva tāva tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Ajjhattikañceva [ajjhattikañce (sī. syā. pī.), ajjhattikañcepi (?)], āvuso, cakkhuṃ aparibhinnaṃ hoti bāhirā ca rūpā āpāthaṃ āgacchanti, no ca tajjo samannāhāro hoti, neva tāva tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Yato ca kho, āvuso, ajjhattikañceva cakkhuṃ aparibhinnaṃ hoti, bāhirā ca rūpā āpāthaṃ āgacchanti, tajjo ca samannāhāro hoti. Evaṃ tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Yaṃ tathābhūtassa rūpaṃ taṃ rūpupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, yā tathābhūtassa vedanā sā vedanupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, yā tathābhūtassa saññā sā saññupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, ye tathābhūtassa saṅkhārā te saṅkhārupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchanti, yaṃ tathābhūtassa viññāṇaṃ taṃ viññāṇupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati.

306.“Just as, friend, dependent on wood, vines, grass, and clay, the surrounding space is called a house; in the same way, friend, dependent on bones, sinews, flesh, and skin, the surrounding space is called form. If, friend, the internal eye is intact but external forms do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding consciousness does not occur. If, friend, the internal eye is intact and external forms come into range, but there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding consciousness does not occur. But when, friend, the internal eye is intact, external forms come into range, and there is appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding consciousness occurs. Such form is included in the form aggregate of clinging. Such feeling is included in the feeling aggregate of clinging. Such perception is included in the perception aggregate of clinging. Such formations are included in the formations aggregate of clinging. Such consciousness is included in the consciousness aggregate of clinging.”

‘‘So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘evañhi kira imesaṃ pañcannaṃ upādānakkhandhānaṃ saṅgaho sannipāto samavāyo hoti. Vuttaṃ kho panetaṃ bhagavatā – ‘yo paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passati so dhammaṃ passati; yo dhammaṃ passati so paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passatīti. Paṭiccasamuppannā kho panime yadidaṃ pañcupādānakkhandhā. Yo imesu pañcasu upādānakkhandhesu chando ālayo anunayo ajjhosānaṃ so dukkhasamudayo. Yo imesu pañcasu upādānakkhandhesu chandarāgavinayo chandarāgappahānaṃ, so dukkhanirodho’ti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hoti.

“He understands thus: ‘Indeed, this is how the collection, convergence, and union of these five aggregates subject to clinging occur. For the Blessed One has said: “One who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; one who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination.” These five aggregates subject to clinging are dependently arisen. Whatever desire, lingering attachment, inclination, and obsession there is toward these five aggregates subject to clinging: this is the origin of suffering. The abandoning of desire-passion and the elimination of desire-passion in regard to these five aggregates subject to clinging: this is the cessation of suffering.’ Even to this extent, friend, much has been done by a monk.”

‘‘Ajjhattikañceva, āvuso, sotaṃ aparibhinnaṃ hoti…pe… ghānaṃ aparibhinnaṃ hoti… jivhā aparibhinnā hoti… kāyo aparibhinno hoti… mano aparibhinno hoti, bāhirā ca dhammā na āpāthaṃ āgacchanti no ca tajjo samannāhāro hoti, neva tāva tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Ajjhattiko ceva, āvuso, mano aparibhinno hoti, bāhirā ca dhammā āpāthaṃ āgacchanti, no ca tajjo samannāhāro hoti, neva tāva tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Yato ca kho, āvuso, ajjhattiko ceva mano aparibhinno hoti, bāhirā ca dhammā āpāthaṃ āgacchanti, tajjo ca samannāhāro hoti, evaṃ tajjassa viññāṇabhāgassa pātubhāvo hoti. Yaṃ tathābhūtassa rūpaṃ taṃ rūpupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, yā tathābhūtassa vedanā sā vedanupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, yā tathābhūtassa saññā sā saññupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati, ye tathābhūtassa saṅkhārā te saṅkhārupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchanti, yaṃ tathābhūtassa viññāṇaṃ taṃ viññāṇupādānakkhandhe saṅgahaṃ gacchati. So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘evañhi kira imesaṃ pañcannaṃ upādānakkhandhānaṃ saṅgaho sannipāto samavāyo hoti. Vuttaṃ kho panetaṃ bhagavatā – ‘‘yo paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passati so dhammaṃ passati; yo dhammaṃ passati so paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passatī’’ti. Paṭiccasamuppannā kho panime yadidaṃ pañcupādānakkhandhā. Yo imesu pañcasu upādānakkhandhesu chando ālayo anunayo ajjhosānaṃ so dukkhasamudayo. Yo imesu pañcasu upādānakkhandhesu chandarāgavinayo chandarāgappahānaṃ so dukkhanirodho’ti. Ettāvatāpi kho, āvuso, bhikkhuno bahukataṃ hotī’’ti.

“Friend, if the internal ear is intact but external sounds do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding ear-consciousness does not occur… Friend, if the internal nose is intact but external smells do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding nose-consciousness does not occur… If the internal tongue is intact but external tastes do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding tongue-consciousness does not occur… If the internal body is intact but external touches do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding body-consciousness does not occur… If the internal mind is intact but external mental phenomena do not come into range, and there is no appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding mind-consciousness does not occur. But, friend, when the internal mind is intact, external mental phenomena come into range, and there is appropriate advertence, then the appearance of the corresponding mind-consciousness occurs. Such form is included in the form aggregate of clinging. Such feeling is included in the feeling aggregate of clinging. Such perception is included in the perception aggregate of clinging. Such formations are included in the formations aggregate of clinging. Such consciousness is included in the consciousness aggregate of clinging. One understands thus: ‘Indeed, this is how the collection, convergence, and union of these five aggregates of clinging occur.’ The Blessed One has said: ‘One who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma; one who sees the Dhamma sees dependent origination.’ These five aggregates of clinging are dependently arisen. Whatever desire, lingering attachment, inclination, and obsession there is toward these five aggregates of clinging: this is the origin of suffering. The abandoning of desire-passion and the elimination of desire-passion in regard to these five aggregates of clinging: this is the cessation of suffering.’ At this point, friend, much has been done by a monk.”

Idamavoca āyasmā sāriputto. Attamanā te bhikkhū āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

This was said by the Venerable Sāriputta. Those bhikkhus were delighted and rejoiced at the words of the Venerable Sāriputta.

Mahāhatthipadopamasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ aṭṭhamaṃ.

The Great Elephant Footprint Simile Discourse, the eighth, is concluded.
