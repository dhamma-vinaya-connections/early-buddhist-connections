# 30. Cūḷasāropamasuttaṃ

## 30. The Shorter Discourse on the Simile of the Corewood

312. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Atha kho piṅgalakoccho brāhmaṇo yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavatā saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho piṅgalakoccho brāhmaṇo bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘yeme, bho gotama, samaṇabrāhmaṇā saṅghino gaṇino gaṇācariyā ñātā yasassino titthakarā sādhusammatā, bahujanassa, seyyathidaṃ – pūraṇo kassapo, makkhali gosālo, ajito kesakambalo, pakudho kaccāyano, sañcayo [sañjayo (sī. syā. pī. ka.)] belaṭṭhaputto, nigaṇṭho nāṭaputto, sabbete sakāya paṭiññāya abbhaññaṃsu sabbeva nābbhaññaṃsu, udāhu ekacce abbhaññaṃsu ekacce nābbhaññaṃsū’’ti? ‘‘Alaṃ, brāhmaṇa, tiṭṭhatetaṃ – sabbete sakāya paṭiññāya abbhaññaṃsu sabbeva nābbhaññaṃsu, udāhu ekacce abbhaññaṃsu ekacce nābbhaññaṃsūti. Dhammaṃ te, brāhmaṇa, desessāmi, taṃ suṇāhi, sādhukaṃ manasi karohi, bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’ti kho piṅgalakoccho brāhmaṇo bhagavato paccassosi. Bhagavā etadavoca –

312.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Savatthi in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's Monastery. Then the brahmin Piṅgalakoccha approached the Blessed One; having approached, he exchanged greetings with the Blessed One. After exchanging pleasant and courteous conversation, he sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the brahmin Piṅgalakoccha said to the Blessed One: “Venerable Gotama, those ascetics and brahmins who are leaders of orders, heads of groups, and teachers of groups, well-known and famous, founders of sects, regarded as worthy by many people—such as Pūraṇa Kassapa, Makkhali Gosāla, Ajita Kesakambala, Pakudha Kaccāyana, Sañjaya Belaṭṭhaputta, and Nigaṇṭha Nāṭaputta—did all of them, by their own declaration, attain full knowledge, or did none of them attain it? Or did some attain it and some not?” “Enough, brahmin, let this be: ‘Did all of them, by their own declaration, attain full knowledge, or did none of them attain it? Or did some attain it and some not?’ I shall teach you the Dhamma, brahmin. Listen and pay close attention, I will speak.” “Yes, sir,” the brahmin Piṅgalakoccha replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said:

313. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ atikkamma papaṭikaṃ, sākhāpalāsaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkameyya ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Tamenaṃ cakkhumā puriso disvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘na vatāyaṃ bhavaṃ puriso aññāsi sāraṃ, na aññāsi phegguṃ, na aññāsi tacaṃ, na aññāsi papaṭikaṃ, na aññāsi sākhāpalāsaṃ. Tathā hayaṃ bhavaṃ puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ atikkamma papaṭikaṃ, sākhāpalāsaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissatī’ti.

313.“Suppose, brahmin, a man needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, should come to a large tree standing, endowed with heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, passing over the outer bark, he would cut off the twigs and leaves and take them away, thinking, ‘This is heartwood.’ A discerning man, seeing him, would say: ‘Surely, this good man does not know heartwood, does not know sapwood, does not know inner bark, does not know outer bark, and does not know twigs and leaves. Thus, this good man, needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, came to a large tree standing, endowed with heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, passing over the outer bark, he cut off the twigs and leaves and has taken them away, thinking, ‘This is heartwood.’ And whatever purpose he had for the heartwood, that he will not attain.’”

317. ‘‘Seyyathāpi vā pana, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato sāraññeva chetvā ādāya pakkameyya ‘sāra’nti jānamāno. Tamenaṃ cakkhumā puriso disvā evaṃ vadeyya – ‘aññāsi vatāyaṃ bhavaṃ puriso sāraṃ, aññāsi phegguṃ, aññāsi tacaṃ, aññāsi papaṭikaṃ, aññāsi sākhāpalāsaṃ. 317.“Suppose, brahmin, a man needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, should come to a large tree standing, endowed with heartwood. He would cut off just the heartwood and take it away, knowing, ‘This is heartwood.’ A discerning man, seeing him, would say: ‘Surely, this good man knows heartwood, knows sapwood, knows inner bark, knows outer bark, and knows twigs and leaves. Thus, this good man, needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, came to a large tree standing, endowed with heartwood. He cut off just the heartwood and has taken it away, knowing, ‘This is heartwood.’ And whatever purpose he had for the heartwood, that he will attain.’”

318. ‘‘Evameva kho, brāhmaṇa, idhekacco puggalo saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attamano hoti paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena attānukkaṃseti, paraṃ vambheti – ‘ahamasmi lābhasakkārasilokavā, ime panaññe bhikkhū appaññātā appesakkhā’ti. Lābhasakkārasilokena ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya na chandaṃ janeti, na vāyamati, olīnavuttiko ca hoti sāthaliko. Seyyathāpi so, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ atikkamma papaṭikaṃ, sākhāpalāsaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissati. Tathūpamāhaṃ, brāhmaṇa, imaṃ puggalaṃ vadāmi.

318.“In the same way, brahmin, a certain person here goes forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness, thinking: ‘I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair; I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps the ending of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.’ Having gone forth, he generates gain, honor, and renown. He is pleased with that gain, honor, and renown, and he considers his aim accomplished. On account of that gain, honor, and renown, he exalts himself and disparages others: ‘I am one who has gain, honor, and renown, but these other bhikkhus are unknown, of little influence.’ Because of gain, honor, and renown, he does not generate desire or make an effort for the realization of those other qualities that are higher and more sublime. He lives a life of laxity and slackness. Just as that man, brahmin, needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, passed over the heartwood of a large tree standing, endowed with heartwood, and also passed over the sapwood, the inner bark, and the outer bark, and cut off the twigs and leaves, took them away, and departed thinking, ‘This is heartwood’; and whatever purpose he had for the heartwood, that he would not attain; so too, I declare this person to be comparable to that man.”

319. ‘‘Idha pana, brāhmaṇa, ekacco puggalo saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. Lābhasakkārasilokena ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya chandaṃ janeti, vāyamati, anolīnavuttiko ca hoti asāthaliko. So sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti, paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya attānukkaṃseti, paraṃ vambheti – ‘ahamasmi sīlavā kalyāṇadhammo, ime panaññe bhikkhū dussīlā pāpadhammā’ti. Sīlasampadāya ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya na chandaṃ janeti, na vāyamati, olīnavuttiko ca hoti sāthaliko. Seyyathāpi so, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ atikkamma phegguṃ atikkamma tacaṃ, papaṭikaṃ chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ, tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissati. Tathūpamāhaṃ, brāhmaṇa, imaṃ puggalaṃ vadāmi.

319.Here, brahmin, some person goes forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness, thinking: 'I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair. I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps the end of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.' Having gone forth, he generates gain, honor, and fame. He is not pleased with that gain, honor, and fame, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. On account of that gain, honor, and fame, he does not exalt himself or disparage others. He generates desire and makes an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond gain, honor, and fame; and he is unsluggish in his conduct and not lax. He achieves perfection in virtue. He is pleased with that perfection in virtue, and his aspiration is fulfilled. On account of that perfection in virtue, he exalts himself and disparages others: 'I am virtuous, of good character, but these other bhikkhus are immoral, of bad character.' He does not generate desire or make an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond perfection in virtue; and he is sluggish in his conduct and lax. Suppose, brahmin, a man in need of heartwood, searching for heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes across a great standing tree with heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, passing over the sapwood, passing over the inner bark, he cuts off the outer bark and takes it away, thinking, 'This is heartwood!' Whatever purpose could be served by heartwood, he will not experience that benefit. So too, brahmin, do I declare this person to be.

Ñāṇadassanena ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya na chandaṃ janeti, na vāyamati, olīnavuttiko ca hoti sāthaliko. Seyyathāpi so, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato atikkammeva sāraṃ, phegguṃ chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti maññamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ nānubhavissati. Tathūpamāhaṃ, brāhmaṇa, imaṃ puggalaṃ vadāmi.

321.Here, brahmin, some person goes forth out of faith from the household life into homelessness, thinking: 'I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair. I am overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps the end of this entire mass of suffering might be discerned.' Having gone forth, he generates gain, honor, and fame. He is not pleased with that gain, honor, and fame, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. On account of that gain, honor, and fame, he does not exalt himself or disparage others. He generates desire and makes an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond gain, honor, and fame; and he is unsluggish in his conduct and not lax. He achieves perfection in virtue. He is pleased with that perfection in virtue, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. On account of that perfection in virtue, he does not exalt himself or disparage others. He generates desire and makes an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond perfection in virtue; and he is unsluggish in his conduct and not lax. He achieves perfection in samādhi. He is pleased with that perfection in samādhi, but his aspiration is not yet fulfilled. On account of that perfection in samādhi, he does not exalt himself or disparage others. He generates desire and makes an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond perfection in samādhi; and he is unsluggish in his conduct and not lax. He achieves knowledge and vision. He is pleased with that knowledge and vision, and his aspiration is fulfilled. On account of that knowledge and vision, he exalts himself and disparages others, thinking: 'I dwell knowing and seeing, but these other bhikkhus dwell unknowing and unseeing.' He does not generate desire or make an effort for the realization of those higher, more sublime qualities beyond knowledge and vision; and he is sluggish in his conduct and lax. Suppose, brahmin, a man in need of heartwood, searching for heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, comes across a great standing tree with heartwood. Passing over the heartwood, he cuts off the sapwood and takes it away, thinking, 'This is heartwood!' Whatever purpose could be served by heartwood, he will not experience that benefit. So too, brahmin, do I declare this person to be.

322. ‘‘Idha pana, brāhmaṇa, ekacco puggalo saddhā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito hoti – ‘otiṇṇomhi jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi, dukkhotiṇṇo dukkhapareto, appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethā’ti. So evaṃ pabbajito samāno lābhasakkārasilokaṃ abhinibbatteti. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attamano hoti, na paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena lābhasakkārasilokena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. Lābhasakkārasilokena ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya chandaṃ janeti, vāyamati, anolīnavuttiko ca hoti asāthaliko. So sīlasampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya sīlasampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya sīlasampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. Sīlasampadāya ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya chandaṃ janeti, vāyamati, anolīnavuttiko ca hoti asāthaliko. So samādhisampadaṃ ārādheti. So tāya samādhisampadāya attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tāya samādhisampadāya na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. Samādhisampadāya ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya chandaṃ janeti, vāyamati, anolīnavuttiko ca hoti asāthaliko. So ñāṇadassanaṃ ārādheti. So tena ñāṇadassanena attamano hoti, no ca kho paripuṇṇasaṅkappo. So tena ñāṇadassanena na attānukkaṃseti, na paraṃ vambheti. Ñāṇadassanena ca ye aññe dhammā uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca tesaṃ dhammānaṃ sacchikiriyāya chandaṃ janeti, vāyamati, anolīnavuttiko ca hoti asāthaliko.

322.‘‘Here, brahmin, a certain person goes forth from the household life into homelessness out of faith, thinking: ‘I am overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair—overwhelmed by suffering, afflicted by suffering. Perhaps an end to this whole mass of suffering can be discerned.’ Having gone forth, he obtains gains, honor, and renown. He is not pleased with those gains, honor, and renown, nor is his aspiration fulfilled. He does not exalt himself or disparage others because of those gains, honor, and renown. Instead, he generates desire, makes an effort, and is unflagging in energy and not negligent in striving to realize those qualities that are higher and more sublime than gains, honor, and renown. He attains perfection in virtue. He is pleased with that perfection in virtue, but his aspiration is not fulfilled. He does not exalt himself or disparage others because of that perfection in virtue. Instead, he generates desire, makes an effort, and is unflagging in energy and not negligent in striving to realize those qualities that are higher and more sublime than perfection in virtue. He attains perfection in samādhi. He is pleased with that perfection in samādhi, but his aspiration is not fulfilled. He does not exalt himself or disparage others because of that perfection in samādhi. Instead, he generates desire, makes an effort, and is unflagging in energy and not negligent in striving to realize those qualities that are higher and more sublime than perfection in samādhi. He attains knowledge and vision. He is pleased with that knowledge and vision, but his aspiration is not fulfilled. He does not exalt himself or disparage others because of that knowledge and vision. Instead, he generates desire, makes an effort, and is unflagging in energy and not negligent in striving to realize those qualities that are higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

323. ‘‘Katame ca, brāhmaṇa, dhammā ñāṇadassanena uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca? Idha, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

323.‘‘And what, brahmin, are those qualities higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision? Here, brahmin, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome qualities, enters and dwells in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by thought and examination, with rapture and pleasure born of seclusion. This, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā ajjhattaṃ sampasādanaṃ cetaso ekodibhāvaṃ avitakkaṃ avicāraṃ samādhijaṃ pītisukhaṃ dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, with the subsiding of thought and examination, a bhikkhu enters and dwells in the second jhāna, which has internal confidence and unification of mind, is without thought and examination, and has rapture and happiness born of samādhi. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu pītiyā ca virāgā upekkhako ca viharati, sato ca sampajāno sukhañca kāyena paṭisaṃvedeti, yaṃ taṃ ariyā ācikkhanti – ‘upekkhako satimā sukhavihārī’ti tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, with the fading away of rapture, a bhikkhu dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, and experiences happiness with the body. He enters and dwells in the third jhāna, which the noble ones declare to be: ‘Equanimous, mindful, one who dwells happily.’ This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the previous passing away of joy and sorrow, enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which has neither-pain-nor-pleasure and purity of mindfulness due to equanimity. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, with the complete transcending of perceptions of form, with the passing away of perceptions of resistance, not attending to perceptions of diversity, (thinking:) ‘Space is infinite,’ enters and dwells in the dimension of infinite space. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, having entirely transcended the dimension of infinite space, thinking: ‘Consciousness is infinite,’ enters and dwells in the dimension of infinite consciousness. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, having entirely transcended the dimension of infinite consciousness, thinking: ‘There is nothing,’ enters and dwells in the dimension of nothingness. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sabbaso ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, having entirely transcended the dimension of nothingness, enters and dwells in the dimension of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu sabbaso nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ upasampajja viharati, paññāya cassa disvā āsavā parikkhīṇā honti. Ayampi kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammo ñāṇadassanena uttaritaro ca paṇītataro ca. Ime kho, brāhmaṇa, dhammā ñāṇadassanena uttaritarā ca paṇītatarā ca.

‘‘Furthermore, brahmin, a bhikkhu, having entirely transcended the dimension of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, enters and dwells in the cessation of perception and feeling; and his taints are destroyed by seeing with wisdom. This, too, brahmin, is a quality higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision. These, brahmin, are qualities higher and more sublime than knowledge and vision.

324. ‘‘Seyyathāpi so, brāhmaṇa, puriso sāratthiko sāragavesī sārapariyesanaṃ caramāno mahato rukkhassa tiṭṭhato sāravato sāraṃyeva chetvā ādāya pakkanto ‘sāra’nti jānamāno. Yañcassa sārena sārakaraṇīyaṃ tañcassa atthaṃ anubhavissati. Tathūpamāhaṃ, brāhmaṇa, imaṃ puggalaṃ vadāmi.

324.‘‘Just as, brahmin, a man needing heartwood, seeking heartwood, wandering in search of heartwood, might cut and take the heartwood from a large, standing tree endowed with heartwood, knowing it to be heartwood, and depart. And whatever purpose he might accomplish with that heartwood, that purpose would be fulfilled for him. So too, brahmin, is the simile I state concerning this person.

‘‘Iti kho, brāhmaṇa, nayidaṃ brahmacariyaṃ lābhasakkārasilokānisaṃsaṃ, na sīlasampadānisaṃsaṃ, na samādhisampadānisaṃsaṃ, na ñāṇadassanānisaṃsaṃ. Yā ca kho ayaṃ, brāhmaṇa, akuppā cetovimutti – etadatthamidaṃ, brāhmaṇa, brahmacariyaṃ, etaṃ sāraṃ etaṃ pariyosāna’’nti.

‘‘So, brahmin, this spiritual life is not for the benefit of gains, honor, and renown, nor for the benefit of the accomplishment of virtue, nor for the benefit of the accomplishment of samādhi, nor for the benefit of knowledge and vision. But that unshakeable freedom of mind—this, brahmin, is the purpose of the spiritual life, this is its essence, this is its final goal.

Evaṃ vutte, piṅgalakoccho brāhmaṇo bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘abhikkantaṃ, bho gotama, abhikkantaṃ, bho gotama…pe… upāsakaṃ maṃ bhavaṃ gotamo dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gata’’nti.

When this was said, Piṅgalakoccha the brahmin said to the Blessed One: “Excellent, Master Gotama! Excellent, Master Gotama! … From today, may Master Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge for life.”

Cūḷasāropamasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ dasamaṃ.

The Shorter Discourse on the Simile of the Corewood is finished, the tenth.

Opammavaggo niṭṭhito tatiyo.

The Chapter of Similes, the Third, is Finished.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its summary: -

Moḷiyaphaggunariṭṭhañca nāmo, andhavane kathipuṇṇaṃ nivāpo;Rāsikaṇerumahāgajanāmo, sārūpamo [sāravaro (syā.), sāravano (ka.)] puna piṅgalakoccho.

Moḷiyaphagguna, Ariṭṭha, and Nāma; in the Blind Men's Grove, Kathipuṇṇa's dwelling; Rāsika, Neru, the great elephant Nāma; the simile of the heartwood, and again Piṅgalakoccha.
