# 19. Dvedhāvitakkasuttaṃ

## 19. The Discourse on Two Sorts of Thinking

206. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sāvatthiyaṃ viharati jetavane anāthapiṇḍikassa ārāme. Tatra kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘bhikkhavo’’ti. ‘‘Bhadante’’ti te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca –

206.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was staying at Savatthi, in Jeta's Grove, Anathapindika's monastery. There the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus, saying: "bhikkhus." "Venerable sir," those bhikkhus replied to the Blessed One. The Blessed One said this:

‘‘Pubbeva me, bhikkhave, sambodhā anabhisambuddhassa bodhisattasseva sato etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ dvidhā katvā dvidhā katvā vitakke vihareyya’nti. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, yo cāyaṃ kāmavitakko yo ca byāpādavitakko yo ca vihiṃsāvitakko – imaṃ ekaṃ bhāgamakāsiṃ; yo cāyaṃ nekkhammavitakko yo ca abyāpādavitakko yo ca avihiṃsāvitakko – imaṃ dutiyaṃ bhāgamakāsiṃ.

"Before my enlightenment, bhikkhus, while I was still an unenlightened Bodhisatta, the thought occurred to me: ‘Suppose I were to divide my thoughts into two, and dwell accordingly.’ So I, bhikkhus, made one part of those thoughts of sensual desire, thoughts of ill will, and thoughts of violence; and I made another part of those thoughts of renunciation, thoughts of non-ill will, and thoughts of non-violence."

207. ‘‘Tassa mayhaṃ, bhikkhave, evaṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato uppajjati kāmavitakko. So evaṃ pajānāmi – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ kāmavitakko. So ca kho attabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, parabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, ubhayabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, paññānirodhiko vighātapakkhiko anibbānasaṃvattaniko’ [anibbānasaṃvattaniko’’ti (?)]. ‘Attabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘parabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘ubhayabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘paññānirodhiko vighātapakkhiko anibbānasaṃvattaniko’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, uppannuppannaṃ kāmavitakkaṃ pajahameva [atītakālikakiriyāpadāniyeva] vinodameva [atītakālikakiriyāpadāniyeva] byantameva [byanteva (sī. syā. pī.)] naṃ akāsiṃ.

207."While I was dwelling thus, diligent, ardent, and resolute, bhikkhus, a thought of sensual desire arose in me. I understood thus: 'This thought of sensual desire has arisen in me. And it leads to my own affliction, to others' affliction, and to the affliction of both; it obstructs wisdom, is on the side of vexation, and does not lead to Nibbāna.' 'It leads to my own affliction'—reflecting thus, bhikkhus, it went to its ending in me. 'It leads to others' affliction'—reflecting thus, bhikkhus, it went to its ending in me. 'It leads to the affliction of both'—reflecting thus, bhikkhus, it went to its ending in me. 'It obstructs wisdom, is on the side of vexation, and does not lead to Nibbāna'—reflecting thus, bhikkhus, it went to its ending in me. Thus, bhikkhus, I abandoned, dispelled, eliminated, and made an end of each arisen thought of sensual desire."

208. ‘‘Tassa mayhaṃ, bhikkhave, evaṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato uppajjati byāpādavitakko…pe… uppajjati vihiṃsāvitakko. So evaṃ pajānāmi – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ vihiṃsāvitakko. So ca kho attabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, parabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, ubhayabyābādhāyapi saṃvattati, paññānirodhiko vighātapakkhiko anibbānasaṃvattaniko’. ‘Attabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘parabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘ubhayabyābādhāya saṃvattatī’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati; ‘paññānirodhiko vighātapakkhiko anibbānasaṃvattaniko’tipi me, bhikkhave, paṭisañcikkhato abbhatthaṃ gacchati. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, uppannuppannaṃ vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ pajahameva vinodameva byantameva naṃ akāsiṃ.

208."While I was dwelling thus, diligent, ardent, and resolute, bhikkhus, a thought of ill will arose in me...a thought of harming arose in me. I understood thus: ‘This thought of harming has arisen in me. And it leads to my own affliction, to others’ affliction, and to the affliction of both; it obstructs wisdom, is on the side of vexation, and does not lead to Nibbāna.’ As I reflected, bhikkhus, ‘It leads to my own affliction,’ it went to its ending in me. As I reflected, bhikkhus, ‘It leads to others’ affliction,’ it went to its ending in me. As I reflected, bhikkhus, ‘It leads to the affliction of both,’ it went to its ending in me. As I reflected, bhikkhus, ‘It obstructs wisdom, is on the side of vexation, and does not lead to Nibbāna,’ it went to its ending in me. Thus, bhikkhus, I abandoned, dispelled, eliminated, and made an end of each arisen thought of harming."

‘‘Yaññadeva, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, tathā tathā nati hoti cetaso. Kāmavitakkaṃ ce, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, pahāsi nekkhammavitakkaṃ, kāmavitakkaṃ bahulamakāsi, tassa taṃ kāmavitakkāya cittaṃ namati. Byāpādavitakkaṃ ce, bhikkhave…pe… vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ ce, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, pahāsi avihiṃsāvitakkaṃ, vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ bahulamakāsi, tassa taṃ vihiṃsāvitakkāya cittaṃ namati. Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, vassānaṃ pacchime māse saradasamaye kiṭṭhasambādhe gopālako gāvo rakkheyya. So tā gāvo tato tato daṇḍena ākoṭeyya paṭikoṭeyya sannirundheyya sannivāreyya. Taṃ kissa hetu? Passati hi so, bhikkhave, gopālako tatonidānaṃ vadhaṃ vā bandhanaṃ vā jāniṃ vā garahaṃ vā. Evameva kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, addasaṃ akusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ ādīnavaṃ okāraṃ saṃkilesaṃ, kusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ nekkhamme ānisaṃsaṃ vodānapakkhaṃ.

"Whatever, bhikkhus, a monk frequently thinks and considers, his mind is inclined in that way. If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of sensual pleasure, he abandons thoughts of renunciation, cultivates thoughts of sensual pleasure, and his mind inclines towards thoughts of sensual pleasure. If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of ill will... If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of harm, he abandons thoughts of non-harm, cultivates thoughts of harm, and his mind inclines towards thoughts of harm. Just as, bhikkhus, in the last month of the rainy season, in autumn, when the crops are dense, a cowherd would guard his cows. He would strike them, drive them back, restrain them, and keep them in check here and there with a stick. Why is that? Because, bhikkhus, the cowherd sees that from this cause could come death, imprisonment, punishment, or censure. In the same way, bhikkhus, I saw the drawback, the inferiority, and the defilement in unwholesome qualities, and the benefit of renunciation, the aspect of cleansing in wholesome qualities."

209. ‘‘Tassa mayhaṃ, bhikkhave, evaṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato uppajjati nekkhammavitakko. So evaṃ pajānāmi – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ nekkhammavitakko. So ca kho nevattabyābādhāya saṃvattati, na parabyābādhāya saṃvattati, na ubhayabyābādhāya saṃvattati, paññāvuddhiko avighātapakkhiko nibbānasaṃvattaniko’. Rattiṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Divasaṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Rattindivaṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Api ca kho me aticiraṃ anuvitakkayato anuvicārayato kāyo kilameyya. Kāye kilante [kilamante (ka.)] cittaṃ ūhaññeyya. Ūhate citte ārā cittaṃ samādhimhāti. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, ajjhattameva cittaṃ saṇṭhapemi sannisādemi ekodiṃ karomi [ekodi karomi (pī.)] samādahāmi. Taṃ kissa hetu? ‘Mā me cittaṃ ūhaññī’ti [ugghāṭīti (syā. ka.), ūhanīti (pī.)].

209."While I was dwelling thus, diligent, ardent, and resolute, bhikkhus, a thought of renunciation arose in me. I understood it thus: 'This thought of renunciation has arisen in me. And it does not conduce to my own affliction, nor to the affliction of others, nor to the affliction of both. It promotes wisdom, is on the side of non-affliction, and conduces to Nibbāna.' If I were to think about and ponder it at night, bhikkhus, I would see no fear as a result of that. If I were to think about and ponder it during the day, bhikkhus, I would see no fear as a result of that. If I were to think about and ponder it night and day, bhikkhus, I would see no fear as a result of that. But if I were to think about and ponder it for too long, my body would become weary. When the body is weary, the mind would be agitated. When the mind is agitated, it would be far from samādhi.' Therefore, bhikkhus, I steadied my mind internally, settled it, made it one-pointed, and collected it. Why is that? 'Lest my mind be agitated.'"

210. ‘‘Tassa mayhaṃ, bhikkhave, evaṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato uppajjati abyāpādavitakko…pe… uppajjati avihiṃsāvitakko. So evaṃ pajānāmi – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ avihiṃsāvitakko. So ca kho nevattabyābādhāya saṃvattati, na parabyābādhāya saṃvattati, na ubhayabyābādhāya saṃvattati, paññāvuddhiko avighātapakkhiko nibbānasaṃvattaniko’. Rattiṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Divasaṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Rattindivaṃ cepi naṃ, bhikkhave, anuvitakkeyyaṃ anuvicāreyyaṃ, neva tatonidānaṃ bhayaṃ samanupassāmi. Api ca kho me aticiraṃ anuvitakkayato anuvicārayato kāyo kilameyya. Kāye kilante cittaṃ ūhaññeyya. Ūhate citte ārā cittaṃ samādhimhāti. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, ajjhattameva cittaṃ saṇṭhapemi, sannisādemi, ekodiṃ karomi samādahāmi. Taṃ kissa hetu? ‘Mā me cittaṃ ūhaññī’ti.

210.“While I was dwelling thus, diligent, ardent, and resolute, bhikkhus, a thought of non-ill-will arose in me… a thought of non-cruelty arose in me. I understood thus: ‘This thought of non-cruelty has arisen in me. And it does not lead to self-affliction, nor to the affliction of others, nor to the affliction of both. It increases wisdom, favors the side of non-affliction, and leads to Nibbāna.’ Even if, bhikkhus, I were to think about it and ponder it at night, I would see no fear arising from that. Even if, bhikkhus, I were to think about it and ponder it during the day, I would see no fear arising from that. Even if, bhikkhus, I were to think about it and ponder it night and day, I would see no fear arising from that. But if I were to think about it and ponder it for too long, my body would become weary. With the body weary, the mind would become agitated. With the mind agitated, it would be far from samādhi.’ Therefore, bhikkhus, I settled my mind internally, quieted it, made it one-pointed, and collected it. Why is that? So that my mind would not become agitated.”

‘‘Yaññadeva, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, tathā tathā nati hoti cetaso. Nekkhammavitakkañce, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, pahāsi kāmavitakkaṃ, nekkhammavitakkaṃ bahulamakāsi, tassaṃ taṃ nekkhammavitakkāya cittaṃ namati. Abyāpādavitakkañce, bhikkhave…pe… avihiṃsāvitakkañce, bhikkhave, bhikkhu bahulamanuvitakketi anuvicāreti, pahāsi vihiṃsāvitakkaṃ, avihiṃsāvitakkaṃ bahulamakāsi, tassa taṃ avihiṃsāvitakkāya cittaṃ namati. Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, gimhānaṃ pacchime māse sabbasassesu gāmantasambhatesu gopālako gāvo rakkheyya, tassa rukkhamūlagatassa vā abbhokāsagatassa vā satikaraṇīyameva hoti – ‘etā [ete (ka.)] gāvo’ti. Evamevaṃ kho, bhikkhave, satikaraṇīyameva ahosi – ‘ete dhammā’ti.

“bhikkhus, whatever a monk frequently thinks and considers, his mind is inclined in that way. If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of renunciation, he abandons thoughts of sensual pleasure, develops thoughts of renunciation, and his mind inclines towards those thoughts of renunciation. If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of goodwill… If a monk frequently thinks and considers thoughts of harmlessness, he abandons thoughts of harmfulness, develops thoughts of harmlessness, and his mind inclines towards those thoughts of harmlessness. Just as, bhikkhus, in the last month of the hot season, when all the crops have been gathered to the edge of the village, a cowherd would guard the cows. Staying at the foot of a tree or in the open, he just needs to maintain mindfulness: ‘These are the cows.’ Just so, bhikkhus, I just needed to maintain mindfulness: ‘These are the mental qualities.’”

211. ‘‘Āraddhaṃ kho pana me, bhikkhave, vīriyaṃ ahosi asallīnaṃ, upaṭṭhitā sati asammuṭṭhā, passaddho kāyo asāraddho, samāhitaṃ cittaṃ ekaggaṃ. So kho ahaṃ, bhikkhave, vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsiṃ. Vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā ajjhattaṃ sampasādanaṃ cetaso ekodibhāvaṃ avitakkaṃ avicāraṃ samādhijaṃ pītisukhaṃ dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsiṃ. Pītiyā ca virāgā upekkhako ca vihāsiṃ sato ca sampajāno, sukhañca kāyena paṭisaṃvedesiṃ, yaṃ taṃ ariyā ācikkhanti ‘upekkhako satimā sukhavihārī’ti, tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsiṃ. Sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsiṃ.

211.“And then, bhikkhus, my energy was roused and unflagging, my mindfulness was established and unforgetful, my body was tranquil and unaroused, my mind was collected and unified. ![[Jhanas]].

212. ‘‘So evaṃ samāhite citte parisuddhe pariyodāte anaṅgaṇe vigatūpakkilese mudubhūte kammaniye ṭhite āneñjappatte pubbenivāsānussatiñāṇāya cittaṃ abhininnāmesiṃ. So anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarāmi. Seyyathidaṃ, ekampi jātiṃ…pe… iti sākāraṃ sauddesaṃ anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarāmi. Ayaṃ kho me, bhikkhave, rattiyā paṭhame yāme paṭhamā vijjā adhigatā; avijjā vihatā vijjā uppannā; tamo vihato āloko uppanno; yathā taṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato.

212.“When my mind was thus collected, purified, bright, spotless, rid of defilements, pliant, workable, steady, and attained to imperturbability, I directed it to the knowledge of recollecting past lives. I recollected many kinds of past lives; that is, one birth, two births… so with their aspects and details I recollected many kinds of past lives. This, bhikkhus, was the first knowledge I attained in the first watch of the night. Ignorance was destroyed, knowledge arose; darkness was destroyed, light arose, as happens in one who dwells diligent, ardent, and resolute.”

213. ‘‘So evaṃ samāhite citte parisuddhe pariyodāte anaṅgaṇe vigatūpakkilese mudubhūte kammaniye ṭhite āneñjappatte sattānaṃ cutūpapātañāṇāya cittaṃ abhininnāmesiṃ. So dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena satte passāmi cavamāne upapajjamāne…pe… ime vata bhonto sattā kāyaduccaritena samannāgatā…pe… iti dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena satte passāmi cavamāne upapajjamāne hīne paṇīte suvaṇṇe dubbaṇṇe sugate duggate, yathākammūpage satte pajānāmi. Ayaṃ kho me, bhikkhave, rattiyā majjhime yāme dutiyā vijjā adhigatā; avijjā vihatā vijjā uppannā; tamo vihato āloko uppanno; yathā taṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato.

213.“When my mind was thus collected, purified, bright, unblemished, rid of defilement, malleable, workable, steady, and attained to imperturbability, I directed it to the knowledge of the passing away and reappearance of beings. With the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, I saw beings passing away and reappearing—inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate—and I understood beings passing on according to their actions: ‘These beings who were endowed with bad conduct of body…’ Thus, with the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, I saw beings passing away and reappearing, inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate, and I understood beings passing on according to their deeds. This, bhikkhus, was the second knowledge I attained in the middle watch of the night. Ignorance was destroyed, knowledge arose; darkness was destroyed, light arose, as happens in one who dwells diligent, ardent, and resolute.”

214. ‘‘So evaṃ samāhite citte parisuddhe pariyodāte anaṅgaṇe vigatūpakkilese mudubhūte kammaniye ṭhite āneñjappatte āsavānaṃ khayañāṇāya cittaṃ abhininnāmesiṃ. So ‘idaṃ dukkha’nti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ dukkhasamudayo’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodho’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ dukkhanirodhagāminī paṭipadā’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ. ‘Ime āsavā’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ āsavasamudayo’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ āsavanirodho’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ, ‘ayaṃ āsavanirodhagāminī paṭipadā’ti yathābhūtaṃ abbhaññāsiṃ. Tassa me evaṃ jānato evaṃ passato kāmāsavāpi cittaṃ vimuccittha, bhavāsavāpi cittaṃ vimuccittha, avijjāsavāpi cittaṃ vimuccittha, vimuttasmiṃ vimuttamiti ñāṇaṃ ahosi – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti abbhaññāsiṃ. Ayaṃ kho me, bhikkhave, rattiyā pacchime yāme tatiyā vijjā adhigatā; avijjā vihatā vijjā uppannā; tamo vihato āloko uppanno; yathā taṃ appamattassa ātāpino pahitattassa viharato.

214.“With my mind thus collected, purified, and cleansed, unblemished, rid of defilements, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakeable, I directed it to the knowledge of the destruction of the taints. I truly understood: ‘This is suffering.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the origin of suffering.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the cessation of suffering.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.’ I truly understood: ‘These are the taints.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the origin of the taints.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the cessation of the taints.’ I truly understood: ‘This is the path leading to the cessation of the taints.’ As I knew and saw thus, my mind was liberated from the taint of sensual desire, the taint of being, and the taint of ignorance. When liberated, there was the knowledge that I was liberated. I knew directly: ‘Birth is destroyed, the spiritual journey has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more returning to this state of being.’ This, bhikkhus, was the third knowledge I attained in the last watch of the night. Ignorance was destroyed, knowledge arose; darkness was destroyed, light arose, as happens in one who dwells diligent, ardent, and resolute.”

215. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, araññe pavane mahantaṃ ninnaṃ pallalaṃ. Tamenaṃ mahāmigasaṅgho upanissāya vihareyya. Tassa kocideva puriso uppajjeyya anatthakāmo ahitakāmo ayogakkhemakāmo. So yvāssa maggo khemo sovatthiko pītigamanīyo taṃ maggaṃ pidaheyya, vivareyya kummaggaṃ, odaheyya okacaraṃ, ṭhapeyya okacārikaṃ. Evañhi so, bhikkhave, mahāmigasaṅgho aparena samayena anayabyasanaṃ [anayabyasanaṃ tanuttaṃ (sī. syā. pī.)] āpajjeyya. Tasseva kho pana, bhikkhave, mahato migasaṅghassa kocideva puriso uppajjeyya atthakāmo hitakāmo yogakkhemakāmo. So yvāssa maggo khemo sovatthiko pītigamanīyo taṃ maggaṃ vivareyya, pidaheyya kummaggaṃ, ūhaneyya okacaraṃ, nāseyya okacārikaṃ. Evañhi so, bhikkhave, mahāmigasaṅgho aparena samayena vuddhiṃ virūḷhiṃ vepullaṃ āpajjeyya.

215.“Suppose, bhikkhus, in a forest grove there were a large low-lying marsh. And a great herd of deer lived depending on it. Then a certain man would appear, wishing for their disadvantage, harm, and insecurity. He would block off the safe, secure, and pleasant path they used, open up a wrong path, set up a decoy, and place a decoy doe. In time, that great herd of deer would come to ruin and disaster. But then another man would appear, wishing for their welfare, benefit, and security. He would open up the safe, secure, and pleasant path they used, block off the wrong path, remove the decoy, and destroy the decoy doe. In time, that great herd of deer would flourish, grow, and increase.”

‘‘Upamā kho me ayaṃ, bhikkhave, katā atthassa viññāpanāya. Ayaṃ cevettha attho – mahantaṃ ninnaṃ pallalanti kho, bhikkhave, kāmānametaṃ adhivacanaṃ. Mahāmigasaṅghoti kho, bhikkhave, sattānametaṃ adhivacanaṃ. Puriso anatthakāmo ahitakāmo ayogakkhemakāmoti kho, bhikkhave, mārassetaṃ pāpimato adhivacanaṃ. Kummaggoti kho, bhikkhave, aṭṭhaṅgikassetaṃ micchāmaggassa adhivacanaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – micchādiṭṭhiyā micchāsaṅkappassa micchāvācāya micchākammantassa micchāājīvassa micchāvāyāmassa micchāsatiyā micchāsamādhissa. Okacaroti kho, bhikkhave, nandīrāgassetaṃ adhivacanaṃ. Okacārikāti kho, bhikkhave, avijjāyetaṃ adhivacanaṃ. Puriso atthakāmo hitakāmo yogakkhemakāmoti kho, bhikkhave, tathāgatassetaṃ adhivacanaṃ arahato sammāsambuddhassa. Khemo maggo sovatthiko pītigamanīyoti kho, bhikkhave, ariyassetaṃ aṭṭhaṅgikassa maggassa adhivacanaṃ, seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhiyā sammāsaṅkappassa sammāvācāya sammākammantassa sammāājīvassa sammāvāyāmassa sammāsatiyā sammāsamādhissa.

Bhikkhus, I have given this simile to make the meaning clear. Here is the meaning: ‘A great deep marsh’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for sensual pleasures. ‘A great herd of deer’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for beings. ‘A man who desires their harm, their misfortune, their lack of security’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for Māra, the Evil One. ‘The wrong path’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for the eight-factored wrong path, namely: wrong view, wrong intention, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, and wrong samādhi. ‘The snare’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for delight and lust. ‘The hunter’s range’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for ignorance. ‘A man who desires their welfare, their happiness, their security’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for the Tathāgata, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One. ‘The secure path, auspicious and leading to joy’—this, bhikkhus, is a term for the ![[Noble Path]].

‘‘Iti kho, bhikkhave, vivaṭo mayā khemo maggo sovatthiko pītigamanīyo, pihito kummaggo, ūhato okacaro, nāsitā okacārikā. Yaṃ, bhikkhave, satthārā karaṇīyaṃ sāvakānaṃ hitesinā anukampakena anukampaṃ upādāya, kataṃ vo taṃ mayā. Etāni, bhikkhave, rukkhamūlāni, etāni suññāgārāni; jhāyatha, bhikkhave, mā pamādattha; mā pacchā vippaṭisārino ahuvattha. Ayaṃ vo amhākaṃ anusāsanī’’ti.

Thus, bhikkhus, I have opened up the secure path, auspicious and leading to joy; the wrong path is blocked; the snare is uprooted; the hunter’s range is destroyed. That which should be done by a teacher who seeks the welfare of his disciples, out of compassion, that I have done for you. These are the roots of trees, these are empty dwellings. Meditate, bhikkhus, do not be negligent, lest you regret it later. This is our instruction to you.

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamanā te bhikkhū bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

This the Blessed One said. The bhikkhus were pleased and rejoiced at the Blessed One’s words.

Dvedhāvitakkasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ navamaṃ.

The Discourse on the Two Kinds of Thought, the ninth, is concluded.
