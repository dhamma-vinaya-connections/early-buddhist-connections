# 83. Maghadevasuttaṃ

## 83. The Maghadeva Sutta

308. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā mithilāyaṃ viharati maghadevaambavane [makhādevaambavane (sī. pī.), magghadevaambavane (ka.)]. Atha kho bhagavā aññatarasmiṃ padese sitaṃ pātvākāsi. Atha kho āyasmato ānandassa etadahosi – ‘‘ko nu kho hetu, ko paccayo bhagavato sitassa pātukammāya? Na akāraṇena tathāgatā sitaṃ pātukarontī’’ti. Atha kho āyasmā ānando ekaṃsaṃ cīvaraṃ katvā yena bhagavā tenañjaliṃ paṇāmetvā bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘ko nu kho, bhante, hetu, ko paccayo bhagavato sitassa pātukammāya? Na akāraṇena tathāgatā sitaṃ pātukarontī’’ti. ‘‘Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, imissāyeva mithilāyaṃ rājā ahosi maghadevo nāma dhammiko dhammarājā dhamme ṭhito mahārājā; dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassa. Atha kho, ānanda, rājā maghadevo bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena kappakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yadā me, samma kappaka, passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha me āroceyyāsī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako rañño maghadevassa paccassosi. Addasā kho, ānanda, kappako bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena rañño maghadevassa sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni. Disvāna rājānaṃ maghadevaṃ etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho devassa devadūtā, dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātānī’ti. ‘Tena hi, samma kappaka, tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā mama añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpehī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako rañño maghadevassa paṭissutvā tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā rañño maghadevassa añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpesi.

308.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling in Mithilā, in the Maghadeva Mango Grove. Then the Blessed One revealed a smile in a certain place. Then it occurred to Venerable Ānanda: “What is the cause, what is the reason for the Blessed One’s revealing a smile? Tathāgatas do not reveal a smile without a reason.” Then Venerable Ānanda arranged his robe over one shoulder, raised his joined hands in salutation towards the Blessed One, and said: “What is the cause, Venerable Sir, what is the reason for the Blessed One’s revealing a smile? Tathāgatas do not reveal a smile without a reason.” “Once upon a time, Ānanda, in this very Mithilā, there was a king named Maghadeva, a righteous king, a king of Dhamma, a great king established in the Dhamma; he practiced the Dhamma among brahmins and householders, townspeople and country folk; and he observed the Uposatha on the eighth, fourteenth, and fifteenth days of the half-month. Then, Ānanda, after many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, King Maghadeva addressed his barber: ‘When you, dear barber, see grey hairs appearing on my head, then you should inform me.’ ‘Yes, Your Majesty,’ replied the barber to King Maghadeva. Then, Ānanda, after many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, the barber saw grey hairs appearing on King Maghadeva’s head. Seeing them, he said to King Maghadeva: ‘The divine messengers have appeared, Your Majesty; grey hairs are seen appearing on your head.’ ‘Well then, dear barber, carefully pluck out those grey hairs with tweezers and place them carefully in my cupped hands.’ ‘Yes, Your Majesty,’ replied the barber to King Maghadeva. Having agreed, he carefully plucked out those grey hairs with tweezers and placed them carefully in King Maghadeva’s cupped hands.

309. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rājā maghadevo kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ āmantāpetvā etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho me, tāta kumāra, devadūtā; dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni; bhuttā kho pana me mānusakā kāmā; samayo dibbe kāme pariyesituṃ. Ehi tvaṃ, tāta kumāra, imaṃ rajjaṃ paṭipajja. Ahaṃ pana kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajissāmi. Tena hi, tāta kumāra, yadā tvampi passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajeyyāsi. Yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosi. Yasmiṃ kho, tāta kumāra, purisayuge vattamāne evarūpassa kalyāṇassa vattassa samucchedo hoti so tesaṃ antimapuriso hoti. Taṃ tāhaṃ, tāta kumāra, evaṃ vadāmi – yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosī’ti. Atha kho, ānanda, rājā maghadevo kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbaji. So mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena [abyāpajjhena (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), abyāpajjena (ka.)] pharitvā vihāsi. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā… muditāsahagatena cetasā… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihāsi.

309.Then, Ānanda, King Maghadeva, having given a chief village to the barber, summoned his eldest son, the prince, and said: ‘Dear prince, divine messengers have appeared to me; grey hairs are seen growing on my head; I have enjoyed human pleasures; it is time to seek divine pleasures. Come, dear prince, take charge of this kingdom. I will shave off my hair and beard, put on the ochre robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. Therefore, dear prince, when you too see grey hairs growing on your head, then after giving a chief village to the barber and carefully instructing the eldest son, the prince, in the kingship, shave off your hair and beard, put on the ochre robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. Continue this good practice that I have established, do not be the last of my line. For, dear prince, in whatever generation of men this good practice is discontinued, that one is the last of them. Therefore, dear prince, I say to you: continue this good practice that I have established, do not be the last of my line.’ Then, Ānanda, King Maghadeva, having given a chief village to the barber and carefully instructing the eldest son, the prince, in the kingship, shaved off his hair and beard, put on the ochre robes, and went forth from the household life into homelessness right there in Maghadeva’s Mango Grove. He dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, around, everywhere, in every way, he dwelt pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity, without ill will. He dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with appreciative joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, around, everywhere, in every way, he dwelt pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity, without ill will.

‘‘Rājā kho panānanda, maghadevo caturāsītivassasahassāni kumārakīḷitaṃ kīḷi, caturāsītivassasahassāni oparajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni rajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito brahmacariyamacari. So cattāro brahmavihāre bhāvetvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā brahmalokūpago ahosi.

Furthermore, Ānanda, King Maghadeva played children’s games for eighty-four thousand years, acted as viceroy for eighty-four thousand years, ruled as king for eighty-four thousand years, and then, in this very Maghadeva Mango Grove, he went forth from the household life into homelessness and lived the holy life. Having developed the four divine abidings, upon the breakup of the body, after death, he was reborn in the Brahma world.

310. ‘‘Atha kho rañño, ānanda, maghadevassa putto bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena kappakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yadā me, samma kappaka, passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha kho āroceyyāsī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako rañño maghadevassa puttassa paccassosi. Addasā kho, ānanda, kappako bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena rañño maghadevassa puttassa sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni. Disvāna rañño maghadevassa puttaṃ etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho devassa devadūtā; dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātānī’ti. ‘Tena hi, samma kappaka, tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā mama añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpehī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako rañño maghadevassa puttassa paṭissutvā tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā rañño maghadevassa puttassa añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpesi.

310.Then, Ānanda, after many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, the son of King Maghadeva addressed his barber: ‘When you, dear barber, see grey hairs appearing on my head, then you should inform me.’ ‘Yes, Your Majesty,’ replied the barber to the son of King Maghadeva. Then, Ānanda, after many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, the barber saw grey hairs appearing on the head of the son of King Maghadeva. Seeing them, he said to the son of King Maghadeva: ‘The divine messengers have appeared, Your Majesty; grey hairs are seen appearing on your head.’ ‘Well then, dear barber, carefully pluck out those grey hairs with tweezers and place them carefully in my cupped hands.’ ‘Yes, Your Majesty,’ replied the barber to the son of King Maghadeva. Having agreed, he carefully plucked out those grey hairs with tweezers and placed them carefully in the cupped hands of the son of King Maghadeva.

‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño maghadevassa putto kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ āmantāpetvā etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho, me, tāta kumāra, devadūtā; dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni; bhuttā kho pana me mānusakā kāmā; samayo dibbe kāme pariyesituṃ. Ehi tvaṃ, tāta kumāra, imaṃ rajjaṃ paṭipajja. Ahaṃ pana kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajissāmi. Tena hi, tāta kumāra, yadā tvampi passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajeyyāsi. Yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosi. Yasmiṃ kho, tāta kumāra, purisayuge vattamāne evarūpassa kalyāṇassa vattassa samucchedo hoti so tesaṃ antimapuriso hoti. Taṃ tāhaṃ, tāta kumāra, evaṃ vadāmi – yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosī’ti. Atha kho, ānanda, rañño maghadevassa putto kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbaji. So mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihāsi. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā… muditāsahagatena cetasā… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihāsi. Rañño kho panānanda, maghadevassa putto caturāsītivassasahassāni kumārakīḷitaṃ kīḷi, caturāsītivassasahassāni oparajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni rajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito brahmacariyamacari. So cattāro brahmavihāre bhāvetvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā brahmalokūpago ahosi.

"Then, Ānanda, King Maghadeva’s son, having given the chief village to the barber, summoned his eldest son, the prince, and said to him: ‘My dear prince, the deva-messengers have appeared to me; grey hairs are seen to have arisen on my head; I have enjoyed human sensual pleasures; it is time to seek divine sensual pleasures. Come, dear prince, undertake this kingship. I will shave off my hair and beard, don the saffron robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. Therefore, dear prince, when you too see grey hairs arisen on your head, then after giving the chief village to the barber and properly instructing your eldest son, the prince, in kingship, shave off your hair and beard, don the saffron robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. You should continue this noble practice established by me, and do not be the last person. For, dear prince, in whatever generation this noble practice is discontinued, that person becomes the last of them. Therefore, dear prince, I say to you: you should continue this noble practice established by me, and do not be the last person.’
Then, Ānanda, King Maghadeva’s son, having given the chief village to the barber and properly instructing his eldest son, the prince, in kingship, shaved off his hair and beard, donned the saffron robes, and went forth from the household life into homelessness in this very Maghadeva mango grove. Dwelling with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, he pervaded one direction, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, across, everywhere, all-encompassing, he pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity and without ill will. Dwelling with a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with sympathetic joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, he pervaded one direction, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, across, everywhere, all-encompassing, he pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity and without ill will.
Now, Ānanda, King Maghadeva’s son played as a prince for eighty-four thousand years, exercised viceregency for eighty-four thousand years, exercised kingship for eighty-four thousand years, and having gone forth from the household life into homelessness in this very Maghadeva mango grove, he practiced the holy life for eighty-four thousand years. Having developed the four divine abidings, with the breakup of the body, after death, he reappeared in the Brahma-world."

311. ‘‘Rañño kho panānanda, maghadevassa puttapaputtakā tassa paramparā caturāsītirājasahassāni [caturāsītikhattiyasahassāni (sī. pī.), caturāsītisahassāni (syā. kaṃ.)] imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajiṃsu. Te mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihariṃsu, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihariṃsu. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā… muditāsahagatena cetasā… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihariṃsu, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihariṃsu. Caturāsītivassasahassāni kumārakīḷitaṃ kīḷiṃsu, caturāsītivassasahassāni oparajjaṃ kāresuṃ, caturāsītivassasahassāni rajjaṃ kāresuṃ, caturāsītivassasahassāni imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajitā brahmacariyamacariṃsu. Te cattāro brahmavihāre bhāvetvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā brahmalokūpagā ahesuṃ. Nimi tesaṃ rājā [rājānaṃ (sī. pī.)] pacchimako ahosi dhammiko dhammarājā dhamme ṭhito mahārājā; dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassa.

311."Ānanda, the sons and grandsons of King Maghadeva, his lineage, eighty-four thousand kings, in this very Maghadeva mango grove, shaved off their hair and beards, donned the saffron robes, and went forth from the household life into homelessness. They dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, across, everywhere, all-encompassing, they pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, exalted, measureless, without hostility, without ill will. They dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with sympathetic joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, likewise the second, likewise the third, likewise the fourth; so above, below, across, everywhere, all-encompassing, they pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, exalted, measureless, without hostility, without ill will. For eighty-four thousand years they played as princes, for eighty-four thousand years they exercised viceregency, for eighty-four thousand years they exercised kingship, and for eighty-four thousand years they practiced the holy life in this very Maghadeva mango grove after going forth from the household life into homelessness. Having developed the four divine abodes, with the breakup of the body, after death, they reappeared in the Brahma-world. Nimi was the last king among them, a righteous king, a king by Dhamma, a great king established in Dhamma; he practiced Dhamma among brahmins and householders, among townspeople and country folk. And he observed the Uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth days of the fortnight."

312. ‘‘Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sudhammāyaṃ sabhāyaṃ sannisinnānaṃ sannipatitānaṃ ayamantarākathā udapādi – ‘lābhā vata, bho, videhānaṃ, suladdhaṃ vata, bho, videhānaṃ, yesaṃ nimi rājā dhammiko dhammarājā dhamme ṭhito mahārājā; dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassā’ti. Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo deve tāvatiṃse āmantesi – ‘iccheyyātha no tumhe, mārisā, nimiṃ rājānaṃ daṭṭhu’nti? ‘Icchāma mayaṃ, mārisa, nimiṃ rājānaṃ daṭṭhu’nti. Tena kho pana, ānanda, samayena nimi rājā tadahuposathe pannarase sīsaṃnhāto [sasīsaṃ nahāto (sī.), sīsanhāto (syā. kaṃ.)] uposathiko uparipāsādavaragato nisinno hoti. Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo – seyyathāpi nāma balavā puriso samiñjitaṃ vā bāhaṃ pasāreyya, pasāritaṃ vā bāhaṃ samiñjeyya, evameva – devesu tāvatiṃsesu antarahito nimissa rañño pamukhe pāturahosi. Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo nimiṃ rājānaṃ etadavoca – ‘lābhā te, mahārāja, suladdhaṃ te, mahārāja. Devā, mahārāja, tāvatiṃsā sudhammāyaṃ sabhāyaṃ kittayamānarūpā sannisinnā – ‘‘lābhā vata, bho, videhānaṃ, suladdhaṃ vata, bho, videhānaṃ, yesaṃ nimi rājā dhammiko dhammarājā dhamme ṭhito mahārājā; dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassā’’ti. Devā te, mahārāja, tāvatiṃsā dassanakāmā. Tassa te ahaṃ, mahārāja, sahassayuttaṃ ājaññarathaṃ pahiṇissāmi; abhiruheyyāsi, mahārāja, dibbaṃ yānaṃ avikampamāno’ti. Adhivāsesi kho, ānanda, nimi rājā tuṇhībhāvena.

312."Once, Ānanda, while the Tāvatiṃsa devas were seated together in the Sudhammā assembly hall, this discussion arose among them: ‘Indeed, the Videhans are fortunate; indeed, the Videhans have gained much, who have Nimi as king, a righteous king, a king by Dhamma, a great king established in Dhamma; who practices Dhamma among brahmins and householders, among townspeople and country folk; and who observes the Uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth days of the fortnight.’ Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of devas, addressed the Tāvatiṃsa devas: ‘Sirs, would you like to see King Nimi?’ ‘Sir, we would like to see King Nimi.’ At that time, Ānanda, King Nimi, on that Uposatha day, the fifteenth, having bathed his head and observing the Uposatha, was seated in the excellent upper chamber of his palace. Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of devas—just as a strong man might extend his flexed arm or flex his extended arm, in the same way—disappeared from among the Tāvatiṃsa devas and reappeared before King Nimi. Then Sakka, the lord of devas, said this to King Nimi: ‘Great king, you are fortunate; great king, you have gained much. Great king, the Tāvatiṃsa devas, seated in the Sudhammā assembly hall, were praising you, saying: "Indeed, the Videhans are fortunate; indeed, the Videhans have gained much, who have Nimi as king, a righteous king, a king by Dhamma, a great king established in Dhamma; who practices Dhamma among brahmins and householders, among townspeople and country folk; and who observes the Uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth days of the fortnight." Great king, the Tāvatiṃsa devas wish to see you. Great king, I will send you a divine chariot yoked to a thousand thoroughbred horses. Great king, you should mount the divine vehicle without wavering.’ King Nimi, Ānanda, consented by his silence."

313. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo nimissa rañño adhivāsanaṃ viditvā – seyyathāpi nāma balavā puriso samiñjitaṃ vā bāhaṃ pasāreyya, pasāritaṃ vā bāhaṃ samiñjeyya, evameva – nimissa rañño pamukhe antarahito devesu tāvatiṃsesu pāturahosi. Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo mātaliṃ saṅgāhakaṃ āmantesi – ‘ehi tvaṃ, samma mātali, sahassayuttaṃ ājaññarathaṃ yojetvā nimiṃ rājānaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evaṃ vadehi – ayaṃ te, mahārāja, sahassayutto ājaññaratho sakkena devānamindena pesito; abhiruheyyāsi, mahārāja, dibbaṃ yānaṃ avikampamāno’ti. ‘Evaṃ, bhaddantavā’ti kho, ānanda, mātali saṅgāhako sakkassa devānamindassa paṭissutvā sahassayuttaṃ ājaññarathaṃ yojetvā nimiṃ rājānaṃ upasaṅkamitvā etadavoca – ‘ayaṃ te, mahārāja, sahassayutto ājaññaratho sakkena devānamindena pesito; abhiruha, mahārāja, dibbaṃ yānaṃ avikampamāno. Api ca, mahārāja, katamena taṃ nemi, yena vā pāpakammā pāpakānaṃ kammānaṃ vipākaṃ paṭisaṃvedenti, yena vā kalyāṇakammā kalyāṇakammānaṃ vipākaṃ paṭisaṃvedentī’ti? ‘Ubhayeneva maṃ, mātali, nehī’ti. Sampavesesi [sampāpesi (sī. pī.)] kho, ānanda, mātali, saṅgāhako nimiṃ rājānaṃ sudhammaṃ sabhaṃ. Addasā kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo nimiṃ rājānaṃ dūratova āgacchantaṃ. Disvāna nimiṃ rājānaṃ etadavoca – ‘ehi kho, mahārāja. Svāgataṃ, mahārāja. Devā te dassanakāmā, mahārāja, tāvatiṃsā sudhammāyaṃ sabhāyaṃ kittayamānarūpā sannisinnā – ‘‘lābhā vata, bho, videhānaṃ, suladdhaṃ vata, bho, videhānaṃ, yesaṃ nimi rājā dhammiko dhammarājā dhamme ṭhito mahārājā; dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassā’’ti. Devā te, mahārāja, tāvatiṃsā dassanakāmā. Abhirama, mahārāja, devesu devānubhāvenā’ti. ‘Alaṃ, mārisa, tattheva maṃ mithilaṃ paṭinetu. Tathāhaṃ dhammaṃ carissāmi brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca; uposathañca upavasāmi cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassā’ti.

313."Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of the gods, knowing King Nimi’s assent—just as a strong man might bend or stretch his arm—so he vanished from the presence of King Nimi and reappeared among the Tāvatiṃsa gods. Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of the gods, addressed his charioteer Mātali: ‘Come, good Mātali, harness the chariot with its team of a thousand thoroughbreds, approach King Nimi, and say to him: “Great king, this chariot with its team of a thousand thoroughbreds has been sent by Sakka, the lord of the gods. Mount, great king, this divine vehicle without hesitation.”’ ‘Yes, good sir,’ replied Mātali the charioteer to Sakka, the lord of the gods. Then he harnessed the chariot with its team of a thousand thoroughbreds, approached King Nimi, and said: ‘Great king, this chariot with its team of a thousand thoroughbreds has been sent by Sakka, the lord of the gods. Mount, great king, this divine vehicle without hesitation. Moreover, great king, by which way shall I take you—the way where evildoers experience the results of their evil deeds, or the way where the virtuous experience the results of their good deeds?’ ‘Take me by both ways, Mātali.’ Then, Ānanda, Mātali the charioteer led King Nimi into the Sudhamma assembly hall. Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of the gods, saw King Nimi coming from afar. Seeing him, he said: ‘Come, great king. Welcome, great king. The Tāvatiṃsa gods in the Sudhamma assembly hall, seated together, wishing to see you, are praising you: “How fortunate, sirs, are the Videhans! How blessed, sirs, are the Videhans, who have King Nimi, a righteous king, a king who rules by righteousness, established in righteousness; who practices the Dhamma among brahmins and householders, merchants and country people; and who observes the uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth of the fortnight.” The Tāvatiṃsa gods, great king, wish to see you. Enjoy, great king, the divine majesty among the gods.’ ‘Enough, good sir. Take me back to Mithilā. Thus, I will practice the Dhamma among brahmins and householders, merchants and country people, and observe the uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth of the fortnight.’"

314. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo mātaliṃ saṅgāhakaṃ āmantesi – ‘ehi tvaṃ, samma mātali, sahassayuttaṃ ājaññarathaṃ yojetvā nimiṃ rājānaṃ tattheva mithilaṃ paṭinehī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, bhaddantavā’ti kho, ānanda, mātali saṅgāhako sakkassa devānamindassa paṭissutvā sahassayuttaṃ ājaññarathaṃ yojetvā nimiṃ rājānaṃ tattheva mithilaṃ paṭinesi. Tatra sudaṃ, ānanda, nimi rājā dhammaṃ carati brāhmaṇagahapatikesu negamesu ceva jānapadesu ca, uposathañca upavasati cātuddasiṃ pañcadasiṃ aṭṭhamiñca pakkhassāti. Atha kho, ānanda, nimi rājā bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena kappakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yadā me, samma kappaka, passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha me āroceyyāsī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako nimissa rañño paccassosi. Addasā kho, ānanda, kappako bahūnaṃ vassānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahūnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena nimissa rañño sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni. Disvāna nimiṃ rājānaṃ etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho devassa devadūtā; dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātānī’ti. ‘Tena hi, samma kappaka, tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā mama añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpehī’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, kappako nimissa rañño paṭissutvā tāni palitāni sādhukaṃ saṇḍāsena uddharitvā nimissa rañño añjalismiṃ patiṭṭhāpesi. Atha kho, ānanda, nimi rājā kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ āmantāpetvā etadavoca – ‘pātubhūtā kho me, tāta kumāra, devadūtā; dissanti sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni; bhuttā kho pana me mānusakā kāmā; samayo dibbe kāme pariyesituṃ. Ehi tvaṃ, tāta kumāra, imaṃ rajjaṃ paṭipajja. Ahaṃ pana kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajissāmi. Tena hi, tāta kumāra, yadā tvampi passeyyāsi sirasmiṃ palitāni jātāni, atha kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajeyyāsi. Yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosi. Yasmiṃ kho, tāta kumāra, purisayuge vattamāne evarūpassa kalyāṇassa vattassa samucchedo hoti so tesaṃ antimapuriso hoti. Taṃ tāhaṃ, tāta kumāra, evaṃ vadāmi – ‘yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyāsi, mā kho me tvaṃ antimapuriso ahosī’ti.

314."Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of the gods, addressed Mātali the charioteer: 'Come, good Mātali, harness the chariot with a thousand thoroughbreds and bring King Nimi back to Mithilā right there.' ‘Yes, good sir,’ replied Mātali the charioteer to Sakka, the lord of the gods. He harnessed the chariot with a thousand thoroughbreds and brought King Nimi back to Mithilā right there. There, Ānanda, King Nimi ruled righteously among the brahmins and householders, in town and country alike, and observed the uposatha on the fourteenth, fifteenth, and eighth days of the fortnight."Then, Ānanda, after many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, King Nimi addressed his barber: 'When you see, dear barber, that grey hairs have appeared on my head, then you should inform me.' 'Yes, Your Majesty,' replied the barber to King Nimi. After many years, many hundreds of years, many thousands of years had passed, the barber saw grey hairs appearing on King Nimi’s head. Seeing this, he said to King Nimi: 'The divine messengers have appeared for Your Majesty; grey hairs are seen on your head.' 'Well then, dear barber, carefully pluck out those grey hairs with tweezers and place them in my cupped hands.' 'Yes, Your Majesty,' replied the barber to King Nimi. He carefully plucked out the grey hairs with tweezers and placed them in King Nimi’s cupped hands."Then, Ānanda, King Nimi gave an excellent village to the barber and summoned his eldest prince. He said: 'My dear prince, the divine messengers have appeared for me; grey hairs are seen on my head. I have enjoyed human pleasures; now is the time to seek divine pleasures. Come, my dear prince, take charge of this kingdom. I will shave off my hair and beard, put on the ochre robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. Therefore, my dear prince, when you too see grey hairs appearing on your head, then give a village to the barber, instruct your eldest prince well in kingship, shave off your hair and beard, put on the ochre robes, and go forth from the household life into homelessness. Continue this good practice that I have established; do not be my last man. For, my dear prince, in whatever generation of men this practice is discontinued, that man is their last man. Therefore, my dear prince, I say to you: "Continue this good practice that I have established; do not be my last man."'"

315. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, nimi rājā kappakassa gāmavaraṃ datvā jeṭṭhaputtaṃ kumāraṃ sādhukaṃ rajje samanusāsitvā imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane kesamassuṃ ohāretvā kāsāyāni vatthāni acchādetvā agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbaji. So mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihāsi. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā… muditāsahagatena cetasā… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi, tathā dutiyaṃ, tathā tatiyaṃ, tathā catutthaṃ; iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyābajjhena pharitvā vihāsi. Nimi kho, panānanda, rājā caturāsītivassasahassāni kumārakīḷitaṃ kīḷi, caturāsītivassasahassāni oparajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni rajjaṃ kāresi, caturāsītivassasahassāni imasmiṃyeva maghadevaambavane agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbajito brahmacariyamacari. So cattāro brahmavihāre bhāvetvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā brahmalokūpago ahosi. Nimissa kho panānananda, rañño kaḷārajanako nāma putto ahosi. Na so agārasmā anagāriyaṃ pabbaji. So taṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ samucchindi. So tesaṃ antimapuriso ahosi.

315."Then, Ānanda, King Nimi, having given an excellent village to the barber, carefully instructed his eldest prince in kingship, shaved off his hair and beard in this very Maghadeva mango grove, donned the ochre robes, and went forth from the household life into homelessness. He dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with loving-kindness, likewise the second, the third, and the fourth direction; just so above, below, and all around, everywhere, identifying himself with all, he dwelt pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity, without ill will. He dwelt pervading one direction with a mind imbued with compassion... with a mind imbued with sympathetic joy... with a mind imbued with equanimity, likewise the second, the third, and the fourth direction; just so above, below, and all around, everywhere, identifying himself with all, he dwelt pervading the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—vast, expansive, immeasurable, without enmity, without ill will. King Nimi, Ānanda, enjoyed the play of a prince for eighty-four thousand years, acted as viceroy for eighty-four thousand years, ruled as king for eighty-four thousand years, and then, in this very Maghadeva mango grove, he went forth from the household life into homelessness and lived the holy life for eighty-four thousand years. Having developed the four divine abodes, upon the breakup of the body after death, he was reborn in the Brahma-world. Now, Ānanda, King Nimi had a son named Kaḷārajanaka. He did not go forth from the household life into homelessness. He cut off that wholesome course. He was the last of them."

316. ‘‘Siyā kho pana te, ānanda, evamassa – ‘añño nūna tena samayena rājā maghadevo ahosi, yena taṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihita’nti [yo taṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihinīti (sī.)]. Na kho panetaṃ, ānanda, evaṃ daṭṭhabbaṃ. Ahaṃ tena samayena rājā maghadevo ahosiṃ. (Ahaṃ taṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihiniṃ,) [( ) natthi (ka.)] mayā taṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ; pacchimā janatā anuppavattesi. Taṃ kho panānanda, kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ na nibbidāya na virāgāya na nirodhāya na upasamāya na abhiññāya na sambodhāya na nibbānāya saṃvattati, yāvadeva brahmalokūpapattiyā. Idaṃ kho panānanda, etarahi mayā kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ ekantanibbidāya virāgāya nirodhāya upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattati. Katamañcānanda, etarahi mayā kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ ekantanibbidāya virāgāya nirodhāya upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattati? Ayameva ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo, seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi, sammāsaṅkappo, sammāvācā, sammākammanto, sammāājīvo, sammāvāyāmo, sammāsati, sammāsamādhi. Idaṃ kho, ānanda, etarahi mayā kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ ekantanibbidāya virāgāya nirodhāya upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattati. Taṃ vo ahaṃ, ānanda, evaṃ vadāmi – ‘yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyātha, mā kho me tumhe antimapurisā ahuvattha’. Yasmiṃ kho, ānanda, purisayuge vattamāne evarūpassa kalyāṇassa vattassa samucchedo hoti so tesaṃ antimapuriso hoti. Taṃ vo ahaṃ, ānanda, evaṃ vadāmi – ‘yena me idaṃ kalyāṇaṃ vattaṃ nihitaṃ anuppavatteyyātha, mā kho me tumhe antimapurisā ahuvatthā’’’ti.

316.“Perhaps, Ānanda, you might think: ‘Surely there was another King Maghadeva at that time by whom this good practice was established.’ But you should not see it thus, Ānanda. I myself was King Maghadeva at that time. I laid down that good practice; later generations continued it. But, Ānanda, that good practice did not lead to disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, or to Nibbāna—it only led as far as rebirth in the Brahmā world. Now, Ānanda, this good practice that I have established leads solely to disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, and to Nibbāna. And what, Ānanda, is this good practice that I have now established, which leads solely to disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, and to Nibbāna? It is this ![[Noble Path]]. This, Ānanda, is the good practice I have now established, which leads solely to disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to awakening, and to Nibbāna. Therefore, Ānanda, I say to you: ‘Continue this good practice that I have established, and do not be the last men.’ For, Ānanda, when in an age of men, such a good practice comes to be cut off, they are the last men. Thus, Ānanda, I say to you: ‘Continue this good practice that I have established, and do not be the last men.’”

Idamavoca bhagavā. Attamano āyasmā ānando bhagavato bhāsitaṃ abhinandīti.

Thus spoke the Blessed One. The Venerable Ānanda, pleased, rejoiced at the Blessed One's words.

Maghadevasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ tatiyaṃ.

The Maghadeva Discourse, the Third, is finished.
