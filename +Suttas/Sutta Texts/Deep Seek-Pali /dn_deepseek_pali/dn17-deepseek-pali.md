# Mahāsudassanasuttaṃ

## The Discourse on Mahāsudassana

241. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā kusinārāyaṃ viharati upavattane mallānaṃ sālavane antarena yamakasālānaṃ parinibbānasamaye. Atha kho āyasmā ānando yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho āyasmā ānando bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘mā, bhante, bhagavā imasmiṃ khuddakanagarake ujjaṅgalanagarake sākhānagarake parinibbāyi. Santi, bhante, aññāni mahānagarāni. Seyyathidaṃ – campā, rājagahaṃ, sāvatthi, sāketaṃ, kosambī, bārāṇasī; ettha bhagavā parinibbāyatu. Ettha bahū khattiyamahāsālā brāhmaṇamahāsālā gahapatimahāsālā tathāgate abhippasannā, te tathāgatassa sarīrapūjaṃ karissantī’’ti.

241.Thus have I heard: At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Kusinārā in the Upavattana, the Mallans’ sal-grove, between the twin sal trees, at the time of his final passing away. Then the Venerable Ānanda approached the Blessed One; having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, the Venerable Ānanda said to the Blessed One: “Venerable sir, let not the Blessed One pass away in this small town, this wilderness town, this branch town. There are, venerable sir, other great cities such as Campā, Rājagaha, Sāvatthī, Sāketa, Kosambī, and Bārāṇasī; let the Blessed One pass away there. There are many wealthy nobles, brahmins, and householders there who are deeply devoted to the Tathāgata; they will perform the obsequies for the Tathāgata’s body.”

242. ‘‘Mā hevaṃ, ānanda, avaca; mā hevaṃ, ānanda, avaca – khuddakanagarakaṃ ujjaṅgalanagarakaṃ sākhānagaraka’’nti.

242.“Do not say that, Ānanda; do not say that, Ānanda—a small town, a wilderness town, a branch town.”

## Kusāvatīrājadhānī

### Kusāvatī, the Royal Capital

‘‘Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano nāma ahosi khattiyo muddhāvasitto [khattiyo muddhābhisitto (ka.), cakkavattīdhammiko dhammarājā (mahāparinibbānasutta)] cāturanto vijitāvī janapadatthāvariyappatto. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa ayaṃ kusinārā kusāvatī nāma rājadhānī ahosi. Puratthimena ca pacchimena ca dvādasayojanāni āyāmena, uttarena ca dakkhiṇena ca sattayojanāni vitthārena. Kusāvatī, ānanda, rājadhānī iddhā ceva ahosi phītā ca bahujanā ca ākiṇṇamanussā ca subhikkhā ca. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, devānaṃ āḷakamandā nāma rājadhānī iddhā ceva hoti phītā ca [iddhā ceva ahosi phītā ca (syā.)] bahujanā ca ākiṇṇayakkhā ca subhikkhā ca; evameva kho, ānanda, kusāvatī rājadhānī iddhā ceva ahosi phītā ca bahujanā ca ākiṇṇamanussā ca subhikkhā ca. Kusāvatī, ānanda, rājadhānī dasahi saddehi avivittā ahosi divā ceva rattiñca, seyyathidaṃ – hatthisaddena assasaddena rathasaddena bherisaddena mudiṅgasaddena vīṇāsaddena gītasaddena saṅkhasaddena sammasaddena pāṇitāḷasaddena ‘asnātha pivatha khādathā’ti dasamena saddena.

“Once upon a time, Ānanda, there was a king named Mahāsudassana, a khattiya, a head-anointed king, a universal monarch, victorious, with his territory stable. The capital of King Mahāsudassana, Ānanda, was this very Kusinārā, then called Kusāvatī. The city of Kusāvatī, Ānanda, was twelve yojanas in length from east to west, and seven yojanas in breadth from north to south. Kusāvatī, Ānanda, the royal capital, was prosperous, flourishing, populous, crowded with people, and well-supplied with food. Just as, Ānanda, the royal capital of the devas named Āḷakamandā is prosperous, flourishing, populous, crowded with yakkhas, and well-supplied with food, so too, Ānanda, the royal capital of Kusāvatī was prosperous, flourishing, populous, crowded with people, and well-supplied with food. Kusāvatī, Ānanda, the royal capital, was never free of ten sounds by day or night—namely, the sound of elephants, the sound of horses, the sound of chariots, the sound of kettle drums, the sound of tabors, the sound of lutes, the sound of singing, the sound of conches, the sound of cymbals, the sound of hand-clapping, and the tenth sound of people crying, ‘Eat, drink, consume!’”

‘‘Kusāvatī, ānanda, rājadhānī sattahi pākārehi parikkhittā ahosi. Eko pākāro sovaṇṇamayo, eko rūpiyamayo, eko veḷuriyamayo, eko phalikamayo, eko lohitaṅkamayo [lohitaṅgamayo (ka.), lohitakamayo (byākaraṇesu)], eko masāragallamayo, eko sabbaratanamayo. Kusāvatiyā, ānanda, rājadhāniyā catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ dvārāni ahesuṃ. Ekaṃ dvāraṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ, ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ, ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ, ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ. Ekekasmiṃ dvāre satta satta esikā nikhātā ahesuṃ tiporisaṅgā tiporisanikhātā dvādasaporisā ubbedhena. Ekā esikā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā, ekā lohitaṅkamayā, ekā masāragallamayā, ekā sabbaratanamayā. Kusāvatī, ānanda, rājadhānī sattahi tālapantīhi parikkhittā ahosi. Ekā tālapanti sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā, ekā lohitaṅkamayā, ekā masāragallamayā, ekā sabbaratanamayā. Sovaṇṇamayassa tālassa sovaṇṇamayo khandho ahosi, rūpiyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Rūpiyamayassa tālassa rūpiyamayo khandho ahosi, sovaṇṇamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Veḷuriyamayassa tālassa veḷuriyamayo khandho ahosi, phalikamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Phalikamayassa tālassa phalikamayo khandho ahosi, veḷuriyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Lohitaṅkamayassa tālassa lohitaṅkamayo khandho ahosi, masāragallamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Masāragallamayassa tālassa masāragallamayo khandho ahosi, lohitaṅkamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Sabbaratanamayassa tālassa sabbaratanamayo khandho ahosi, sabbaratanamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Tāsaṃ kho panānanda, tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo [kamanīyo (sī. syā. pī.)] ca madanīyo ca. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, pañcaṅgikassa tūriyassa suvinītassa suppaṭitāḷitassa sukusalehi samannāhatassa saddo hoti vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca, evameva kho, ānanda, tāsaṃ tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca. Ye kho panānanda, tena samayena kusāvatiyā rājadhāniyā dhuttā ahesuṃ soṇḍā pipāsā, te tāsaṃ tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddena paricāresuṃ.

“Ānanda, the royal capital of Kusāvatī was surrounded by seven ramparts. One rampart was made of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, one of crystal, one of ruby, one of coral, and one of all kinds of gems. In the royal capital of Kusāvatī, Ānanda, there were four gates for the four castes. At each gate, seven pillars were erected, each fifteen cubits in circumference, buried three men’s height deep, and sixty cubits in height. One pillar was made of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, one of crystal, one of ruby, one of coral, and one of all kinds of gems. Ānanda, the royal capital of Kusāvatī was surrounded by seven rows of palm trees. One row was made of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, one of crystal, one of ruby, one of coral, and one of all kinds of gems. The trunk of the golden palm tree was made of gold, its leaves and fruits of silver. The trunk of the silver palm tree was made of silver, its leaves and fruits of gold. The trunk of the beryl palm tree was made of beryl, its leaves and fruits of crystal. The trunk of the crystal palm tree was made of crystal, its leaves and fruits of beryl. The trunk of the ruby palm tree was made of ruby, its leaves and fruits of coral. The trunk of the coral palm tree was made of coral, its leaves and fruits of ruby. The trunk of the gem palm tree was made of all kinds of gems, its leaves and fruits also of all kinds of gems. Ānanda, when these rows of palm trees were shaken by the wind, the sound was delightful, charming, agreeable, and intoxicating. Just as, Ānanda, the sound of a five-piece orchestra, well-tuned, well-struck, and skillfully played by experts, is delightful, charming, agreeable, and intoxicating, so too, Ānanda, was the sound of these palm trees when shaken by the wind. And those who were gamblers, drunkards, and addicts in the royal capital of Kusāvatī at that time, they amused themselves with the sound of these palm trees when shaken by the wind.”

## Cakkaratanaṃ

### The Wheel Treasure

243. ‘‘Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano sattahi ratanehi samannāgato ahosi catūhi ca iddhīhi. Katamehi sattahi? Idhānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa tadahuposathe pannarase sīsaṃnhātassa uposathikassa uparipāsādavaragatassa dibbaṃ cakkaratanaṃ pāturahosi sahassāraṃ sanemikaṃ sanābhikaṃ sabbākāraparipūraṃ. Disvā rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘sutaṃ kho panetaṃ – ‘‘yassa rañño khattiyassa muddhāvasittassa tadahuposathe pannarase sīsaṃnhātassa uposathikassa uparipāsādavaragatassa dibbaṃ cakkaratanaṃ pātubhavati sahassāraṃ sanemikaṃ sanābhikaṃ sabbākāraparipūraṃ, so hoti rājā cakkavattī’’ti. Assaṃ nu kho ahaṃ rājā cakkavattī’ti.

243.“Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana possessed seven treasures and four powers. What are the seven? Here, Ānanda, on the fifteenth, the Uposatha day, when King Mahāsudassana, having bathed his head, was observing the Uposatha, having gone to the best upper palace chamber, a divine wheel-treasure appeared, with a thousand spokes, a rim, a hub, complete in every way. Seeing it, King Mahāsudassana thought: ‘I have heard that to whatever anointed Khattiya king, on the fifteenth Uposatha day, having bathed his head, while observing the Uposatha, having gone to the best upper palace chamber, a divine wheel-treasure appears, with a thousand spokes, a rim, a hub, complete in every way, he becomes a wheel-turning monarch.’ Could I be a wheel-turning monarch?”

244. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano uṭṭhāyāsanā ekaṃsaṃ uttarāsaṅgaṃ karitvā vāmena hatthena suvaṇṇabhiṅkāraṃ gahetvā dakkhiṇena hatthena cakkaratanaṃ abbhukkiri – ‘pavattatu bhavaṃ cakkaratanaṃ, abhivijinātu bhavaṃ cakkaratana’nti. Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, cakkaratanaṃ puratthimaṃ disaṃ pavatti [pavattati (syā. ka.)], anvadeva [anudeva (syā.)] rājā mahāsudassano saddhiṃ caturaṅginiyā senāya, yasmiṃ kho panānanda, padese cakkaratanaṃ patiṭṭhāsi, tattha rājā mahāsudassano vāsaṃ upagacchi saddhiṃ caturaṅginiyā senāya. Ye kho panānanda, puratthimāya disāya paṭirājāno, te rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘ehi kho mahārāja, svāgataṃ te mahārāja, sakaṃ te mahārāja, anusāsa mahārājā’ti. Rājā mahāsudassano evamāha – ‘pāṇo na hantabbo, adinnaṃ na ādātabbaṃ, kāmesu micchā na caritabbā, musā na bhaṇitabbā, majjaṃ na pātabbaṃ, yathābhuttañca bhuñjathā’ti. Ye kho panānanda, puratthimāya disāya paṭirājāno, te rañño mahāsudassanassa anuyantā ahesuṃ. Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, cakkaratanaṃ puratthimaṃ samuddaṃ ajjhogāhetvā paccuttaritvā dakkhiṇaṃ disaṃ pavatti…pe… dakkhiṇaṃ samuddaṃ ajjhogāhetvā paccuttaritvā pacchimaṃ disaṃ pavatti…pe… pacchimaṃ samuddaṃ ajjhogāhetvā paccuttaritvā uttaraṃ disaṃ pavatti, anvadeva rājā mahāsudassano saddhiṃ caturaṅginiyā senāya. Yasmiṃ kho panānanda, padese cakkaratanaṃ patiṭṭhāsi, tattha rājā mahāsudassano vāsaṃ upagacchi saddhiṃ caturaṅginiyā senāya. Ye kho panānanda, uttarāya disāya paṭirājāno, te rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘ehi kho mahārāja, svāgataṃ te mahārāja, sakaṃ te mahārāja, anusāsa mahārājā’ti. Rājā mahāsudassano evamāha – ‘pāṇo na hantabbo, adinnaṃ na ādātabbaṃ, kāmesu micchā na caritabbā, musā na bhaṇitabbā, majjaṃ na pātabbaṃ, yathābhuttañca bhuñjathā’ti. Ye kho panānanda, uttarāya disāya paṭirājāno, te rañño mahāsudassanassa anuyantā ahesuṃ.

244.“Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana rose from his seat, arranged his upper robe over one shoulder, took a golden vessel in his left hand, and sprinkled the Wheel-Treasure with his right hand, saying: ‘Roll on, venerable Wheel-Treasure! Conquer, venerable Wheel-Treasure!’ Then, Ānanda, the Wheel-Treasure rolled eastward, followed by King Mahāsudassana with his fourfold army. And wherever, Ānanda, the Wheel-Treasure stopped, there King Mahāsudassana took up residence with his fourfold army. The opposing kings of the eastern region came to King Mahāsudassana and said: ‘Come, great king! Welcome, great king! It is yours, great king! Instruct us, great king!’ King Mahāsudassana replied: ‘No living thing should be killed; nothing that is not given should be taken; no sexual misconduct should be committed; no false speech should be spoken; no intoxicants should be drunk; and enjoy what has been enjoyed.’ And so, Ānanda, the opposing kings of the eastern region became followers of King Mahāsudassana. Then, Ānanda, the Wheel-Treasure plunged into the eastern ocean and reemerged, rolling southward… It plunged into the southern ocean and reemerged, rolling westward… It plunged into the western ocean and reemerged, rolling northward, followed by King Mahāsudassana with his fourfold army. And wherever, Ānanda, the Wheel-Treasure stopped, there King Mahāsudassana took up residence with his fourfold army. The opposing kings of the northern region came to King Mahāsudassana and said: ‘Come, great king! Welcome, great king! It is yours, great king! Instruct us, great king!’ King Mahāsudassana replied: ‘No living thing should be killed; nothing that is not given should be taken; no sexual misconduct should be committed; no false speech should be spoken; no intoxicants should be drunk; and enjoy what has been enjoyed.’ And so, Ānanda, the opposing kings of the northern region became followers of King Mahāsudassana.”

245. ‘‘Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, cakkaratanaṃ samuddapariyantaṃ pathaviṃ abhivijinitvā kusāvatiṃ rājadhāniṃ paccāgantvā rañño mahāsudassanassa antepuradvāre atthakaraṇapamukhe akkhāhataṃ maññe aṭṭhāsi rañño mahāsudassanassa antepuraṃ upasobhayamānaṃ. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ cakkaratanaṃ pāturahosi.

245.“Then, Ānanda, that wheel-treasure, having conquered the earth up to the ocean’s edge, returned to the capital city Kusāvatī and, I think, stood at the gate of King Mahāsudassana’s inner palace, as if fixed to the axle at the place for deciding disputes, beautifying King Mahāsudassana’s inner palace. Such a wheel-treasure, Ānanda, appeared to King Mahāsudassana.”

## Hatthiratanaṃ

### The Elephant Treasure.

246. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa hatthiratanaṃ pāturahosi sabbaseto sattappatiṭṭho iddhimā vehāsaṅgamo uposatho nāma nāgarājā. Taṃ disvā rañño mahāsudassanassa cittaṃ pasīdi – ‘bhaddakaṃ vata bho hatthiyānaṃ, sace damathaṃ upeyyā’ti. Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, hatthiratanaṃ – seyyathāpi nāma gandhahatthājāniyo dīgharattaṃ suparidanto, evameva damathaṃ upagacchi. Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tameva hatthiratanaṃ vīmaṃsamāno pubbaṇhasamayaṃ abhiruhitvā samuddapariyantaṃ pathaviṃ anuyāyitvā kusāvatiṃ rājadhāniṃ paccāgantvā pātarāsamakāsi. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ hatthiratanaṃ pāturahosi.

246.Furthermore, Ānanda, the elephant treasure appeared to King Mahāsudassana—all white, seven-limbed, endowed with supernormal power, able to travel through the sky, named Uposatha, the Nāga king. Upon seeing it, King Mahāsudassana's heart was pleased: ‘Truly excellent is this elephant vehicle, if only it would submit to training.’ Then, Ānanda, that elephant treasure submitted to training, just as a thoroughbred elephant, long trained, would do. Formerly, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana, testing that very elephant treasure, mounted it in the morning, traveled around the earth to the ocean's edge, returned to the capital city of Kusāvati, and took his breakfast. Such, Ānanda, was the elephant treasure that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

## Assaratanaṃ

### The Horse Treasure.

247. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa assaratanaṃ pāturahosi sabbaseto kāḷasīso muñjakeso iddhimā vehāsaṅgamo valāhako nāma assarājā. Taṃ disvā rañño mahāsudassanassa cittaṃ pasīdi – ‘bhaddakaṃ vata bho assayānaṃ sace damathaṃ upeyyā’ti. Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, assaratanaṃ seyyathāpi nāma bhaddo assājāniyo dīgharattaṃ suparidanto, evameva damathaṃ upagacchi. Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tameva assaratanaṃ vīmaṃsamāno pubbaṇhasamayaṃ abhiruhitvā samuddapariyantaṃ pathaviṃ anuyāyitvā kusāvatiṃ rājadhāniṃ paccāgantvā pātarāsamakāsi. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ assaratanaṃ pāturahosi.

247.Furthermore, Ānanda, the horse treasure appeared to King Mahāsudassana—all white, with a black head, a mane like muñja grass, endowed with supernormal power, able to travel through the sky, named Valāhaka, the king of horses. Upon seeing it, King Mahāsudassana's heart was pleased: ‘Truly excellent is this horse vehicle, if only it would submit to training.’ Then, Ānanda, that horse treasure submitted to training, just as a thoroughbred horse, long trained, would do. Formerly, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana, testing that very horse treasure, mounted it in the morning, traveled around the earth to the ocean's edge, returned to the capital city of Kusāvati, and took his breakfast. Such, Ānanda, was the horse treasure that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

## Maṇiratanaṃ

### The Jewel Treasure.

248. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa maṇiratanaṃ pāturahosi. So ahosi maṇi veḷuriyo subho jātimā aṭṭhaṃso suparikammakato accho vippasanno anāvilo sabbākārasampanno. Tassa kho panānanda, maṇiratanassa ābhā samantā yojanaṃ phuṭā ahosi. Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tameva maṇiratanaṃ vīmaṃsamāno caturaṅginiṃ senaṃ sannayhitvā maṇiṃ dhajaggaṃ āropetvā rattandhakāratimisāya pāyāsi. Ye kho panānanda, samantā gāmā ahesuṃ, te tenobhāsena kammante payojesuṃ divāti maññamānā. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ maṇiratanaṃ pāturahosi.

248.Furthermore, Ānanda, the jewel treasure appeared to King Mahāsudassana. It was a beryl gem, beautiful, of fine quality, eight-faceted, excellently wrought, clear, bright, unclouded, perfect in every way. The radiance of that jewel treasure, Ānanda, shone out a league in all directions. Formerly, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana, testing that very jewel treasure, assembled his fourfold army, placed the jewel atop his banner, and set out in the darkness of night. The villages all around, Ānanda, by that light, applied themselves to their work, thinking it was day. Such, Ānanda, was the jewel treasure that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

## Itthiratanaṃ

### The Woman Treasure.

249. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa itthiratanaṃ pāturahosi abhirūpā dassanīyā pāsādikā paramāya vaṇṇapokkharatāya samannāgatā nātidīghā nātirassā nātikisā nātithūlā nātikāḷikā nāccodātā atikkantā mānusivaṇṇaṃ [mānussivaṇṇaṃ (syā.)] appattā dibbavaṇṇaṃ. Tassa kho panānanda, itthiratanassa evarūpo kāyasamphasso hoti, seyyathāpi nāma tūlapicuno vā kappāsapicuno vā. Tassa kho panānanda, itthiratanassa sīte uṇhāni gattāni honti, uṇhe sītāni. Tassa kho panānanda, itthiratanassa kāyato candanagandho vāyati, mukhato uppalagandho. Taṃ kho panānanda, itthiratanaṃ rañño mahāsudassanassa pubbuṭṭhāyinī ahosi pacchānipātinī kiṅkārapaṭissāvinī manāpacārinī piyavādinī. Taṃ kho panānanda, itthiratanaṃ rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ manasāpi no aticari [aticarī (ka.), aticārī (sī. syā. pī.)], kuto pana kāyena. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ itthiratanaṃ pāturahosi.

249.Furthermore, Ānanda, the woman treasure appeared to King Mahāsudassana—beautiful, lovely, and charming, possessing supreme beauty of complexion, neither too tall nor too short, neither too thin nor too fat, neither too dark nor too fair, surpassing human complexion but not reaching divine complexion. The touch of this woman treasure, Ānanda, was like cotton or cotton down. In the cold, her limbs were warm; in the heat, they were cool. From her body wafted the scent of sandalwood, and from her mouth, the scent of a lotus. This woman treasure, Ānanda, rose before King Mahāsudassana and retired after him, responsive to his needs, pleasing in conduct, and amiable in speech. This woman treasure, Ānanda, never transgressed against King Mahāsudassana even in thought—how much less in deed! Such was the woman treasure that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

## Gahapatiratanaṃ

### The Householder-Jewel

250. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa gahapatiratanaṃ pāturahosi. Tassa kammavipākajaṃ dibbacakkhu pāturahosi yena nidhiṃ passati sassāmikampi assāmikampi. So rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāha – ‘appossukko tvaṃ, deva, hohi, ahaṃ te dhanena dhanakaraṇīyaṃ karissāmī’ti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tameva gahapatiratanaṃ vīmaṃsamāno nāvaṃ abhiruhitvā majjhe gaṅgāya nadiyā sotaṃ ogāhitvā gahapatiratanaṃ etadavoca – ‘attho me, gahapati, hiraññasuvaṇṇenā’ti. ‘Tena hi, mahārāja, ekaṃ tīraṃ nāvā upetū’ti. ‘Idheva me, gahapati, attho hiraññasuvaṇṇenā’ti. Atha kho taṃ, ānanda, gahapatiratanaṃ ubhohi hatthehi udakaṃ omasitvā pūraṃ hiraññasuvaṇṇassa kumbhiṃ uddharitvā rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ etadavoca – ‘alamettāvatā mahārāja, katamettāvatā mahārāja, pūjitamettāvatā mahārājā’ti? Rājā mahāsudassano evamāha – ‘alamettāvatā gahapati, katamettāvatā gahapati, pūjitamettāvatā gahapatī’ti. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ gahapatiratanaṃ pāturahosi.

250.Furthermore, Ananda, the Householder-Jewel appeared to King Mahāsudassana. Through the ripening of his kamma, a divine eye arose in him, by which he could see treasure, whether owned or ownerless. He approached King Mahāsudassana and said, ‘Be without anxiety, sire, I will attend to your financial affairs.’ Once, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana, wishing to test that Householder-Jewel, boarded a boat and, having entered the current in the middle of the Ganges River, said to the Householder-Jewel, ‘I need gold and silver, householder.’ ‘Then, great king, let the boat approach one shore.’ ‘Right here, householder, I need gold and silver.’ Then, Ananda, that Householder-Jewel, dipping both hands into the water, drew up a pot full of gold and silver and said to King Mahāsudassana, ‘Is this sufficient, great king? Is this accomplished, great king? Is this honored, great king?’ King Mahāsudassana replied, ‘It is sufficient, householder. It is accomplished, householder. It is honored, householder.’ Such was the Householder-Jewel that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

## Pariṇāyakaratanaṃ

### The Guiding-Jewel

251. ‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa pariṇāyakaratanaṃ pāturahosi paṇḍito viyatto medhāvī paṭibalo rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upayāpetabbaṃ upayāpetuṃ, apayāpetabbaṃ apayāpetuṃ, ṭhapetabbaṃ ṭhapetuṃ. So rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāha – ‘appossukko tvaṃ, deva, hohi, ahamanusāsissāmī’ti. Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa evarūpaṃ pariṇāyakaratanaṃ pāturahosi.

251.Furthermore, Ananda, the Guiding-Jewel appeared to King Mahāsudassana: wise, astute, intelligent, and capable of persuading King Mahāsudassana to adopt what should be adopted, to reject what should be rejected, and to establish what should be established. He approached King Mahāsudassana and said, ‘Be without anxiety, sire, I will instruct you.’ Such was the Guiding-Jewel that appeared to King Mahāsudassana.

‘‘Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imehi sattahi ratanehi samannāgato ahosi.

Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with these seven jewels.

## Catuiddhisamannāgato

### Endowed with the Four Accomplishments

252. ‘‘Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano catūhi iddhīhi samannāgato ahosi. Katamāhi catūhi iddhīhi? Idhānanda, rājā mahāsudassano abhirūpo ahosi dassanīyo pāsādiko paramāya vaṇṇapokkharatāya samannāgato ativiya aññehi manussehi. Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imāya paṭhamāya iddhiyā samannāgato ahosi.

252.Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with four accomplishments. What four? Here, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was beautiful, lovely, and charming, endowed with surpassing loveliness, excelling other men in appearance. Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with this first accomplishment.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano dīghāyuko ahosi ciraṭṭhitiko ativiya aññehi manussehi. Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imāya dutiyāya iddhiyā samannāgato ahosi.

Furthermore, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was long-lived and long-enduring, far more so than other human beings. Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with this second accomplishment.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano appābādho ahosi appātaṅko samavepākiniyā gahaṇiyā samannāgato nātisītāya nāccuṇhāya ativiya aññehi manussehi. Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imāya tatiyāya iddhiyā samannāgato ahosi.

Furthermore, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was free from illness and affliction, endowed with a balanced digestion that was neither too cold nor too hot, excelling that of other men. Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with this third accomplishment.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano brāhmaṇagahapatikānaṃ piyo ahosi manāpo. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, pitā puttānaṃ piyo hoti manāpo, evameva kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano brāhmaṇagahapatikānaṃ piyo ahosi manāpo. Raññopi, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa brāhmaṇagahapatikā piyā ahesuṃ manāpā. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, pitu puttā piyā honti manāpā, evameva kho, ānanda, raññopi mahāsudassanassa brāhmaṇagahapatikā piyā ahesuṃ manāpā.

Furthermore, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was liked and agreeable to the brahmins and householders. Just as, Ananda, a father is liked and agreeable to his sons, so too, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was liked and agreeable to the brahmins and householders. And the brahmins and householders, Ananda, were liked and agreeable to King Mahāsudassana. Just as, Ananda, sons are liked and agreeable to their father, so too, Ananda, the brahmins and householders were liked and agreeable to King Mahāsudassana.

‘‘Bhūtapubbaṃ, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano caturaṅginiyā senāya uyyānabhūmiṃ niyyāsi. Atha kho, ānanda, brāhmaṇagahapatikā rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘ataramāno, deva, yāhi, yathā taṃ mayaṃ cirataraṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Rājāpi, ānanda, mahāsudassano sārathiṃ āmantesi – ‘ataramāno, sārathi, rathaṃ pesehi, yathā ahaṃ brāhmaṇagahapatike cirataraṃ passeyya’nti. Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imāya catutthiyā [catutthāya (syā.)] iddhiyā samannāgato ahosi. Rājā, ānanda, mahāsudassano imāhi catūhi iddhīhi samannāgato ahosi.

Once, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana went out to the pleasure grounds with his fourfold army. Then, Ananda, the brahmins and householders approached King Mahāsudassana and said: ‘Go slowly, lord, so that we may see you for longer.’ King Mahāsudassana, Ananda, addressed his charioteer: ‘Drive the chariot slowly, charioteer, so that I may see the brahmins and householders for longer.’ Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with this fourth accomplishment. Ananda, King Mahāsudassana was endowed with these four accomplishments.

## Dhammapāsādapokkharaṇī

### The Lotus Pond of the Dhamma Palace

253. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ imāsu tālantarikāsu dhanusate dhanusate pokkharaṇiyo māpeyya’nti.

253."Then, Ānanda, it occurred to King Mahāsudassana: 'Suppose I were to have lotus ponds constructed at every hundred bow-lengths in these palm-tree-lined avenues.'"

‘‘Māpesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tāsu tālantarikāsu dhanusate dhanusate pokkharaṇiyo. Tā kho panānanda, pokkharaṇiyo catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ iṭṭhakāhi citā ahesuṃ – ekā iṭṭhakā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā.

"King Mahāsudassana, Ānanda, had lotus ponds constructed at every hundred bow-lengths along those avenues. And those lotus ponds, Ānanda, were built of bricks of four colors: one of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal."

‘‘Tāsu kho panānanda, pokkharaṇīsu cattāri cattāri sopānāni ahesuṃ catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ, ekaṃ sopānaṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ. Sovaṇṇamayassa sopānassa sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayassa sopānassa rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Veḷuriyamayassa sopānassa veḷuriyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, phalikamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Phalikamayassa sopānassa phalikamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, veḷuriyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Tā kho panānanda, pokkharaṇiyo dvīhi vedikāhi parikkhittā ahesuṃ ekā vedikā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā. Sovaṇṇamayāya vedikāya sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayāya vedikāya rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ imāsu pokkharaṇīsu evarūpaṃ mālaṃ ropāpeyyaṃ uppalaṃ padumaṃ kumudaṃ puṇḍarīkaṃ sabbotukaṃ sabbajanassa anāvaṭa’nti. Ropāpesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tāsu pokkharaṇīsu evarūpaṃ mālaṃ uppalaṃ padumaṃ kumudaṃ puṇḍarīkaṃ sabbotukaṃ sabbajanassa anāvaṭaṃ.

"Now, Ananda, in those lotus ponds there were four stairways of four different kinds: one stairway made of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal. For the golden stairway, the pillars were golden, with silver crossbars and coping. For the silver stairway, the pillars were silver, with golden crossbars and coping. For the beryl stairway, the pillars were beryl, with crystal crossbars and coping. For the crystal stairway, the pillars were crystal, with beryl crossbars and coping. Moreover, Ananda, those lotus ponds were enclosed by two railings: one railing was golden, and the other was silver. For the golden railing, the pillars were golden, with silver crossbars and coping. For the silver railing, the pillars were silver, with golden crossbars and coping. Then, Ananda, it occurred to King Mahāsudassana: ‘Suppose I were to have planted in these lotus ponds such flowers as lotuses, water lilies, red water lilies, and white water lilies, blooming in every season, accessible to all people.’ So, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana had planted in these lotus ponds such flowers as lotuses, water lilies, red water lilies, and white water lilies, blooming in every season, accessible to all people."

254. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ imāsaṃ pokkharaṇīnaṃ tīre nhāpake purise ṭhapeyyaṃ, ye āgatāgataṃ janaṃ nhāpessantī’ti. Ṭhapesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tāsaṃ pokkharaṇīnaṃ tīre nhāpake purise, ye āgatāgataṃ janaṃ nhāpesuṃ.

254.Then, Ananda, it occurred to King Mahāsudassana: 'Suppose now I were to station bath attendants on the banks of these lotus ponds to bathe people as they arrive.' And indeed, Ananda, King Mahāsudassana stationed bath attendants on the banks of those lotus ponds, who bathed people as they arrived.

‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ imāsaṃ pokkharaṇīnaṃ tīre evarūpaṃ dānaṃ paṭṭhapeyyaṃ – annaṃ annaṭṭhikassa [annatthitassa (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), evaṃ sabbattha pakabhirūpeneva dissati], pānaṃ pānaṭṭhikassa, vatthaṃ vatthaṭṭhikassa, yānaṃ yānaṭṭhikassa, sayanaṃ sayanaṭṭhikassa, itthiṃ itthiṭṭhikassa, hiraññaṃ hiraññaṭṭhikassa, suvaṇṇaṃ suvaṇṇaṭṭhikassā’ti. Paṭṭhapesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tāsaṃ pokkharaṇīnaṃ tīre evarūpaṃ dānaṃ – annaṃ annaṭṭhikassa, pānaṃ pānaṭṭhikassa, vatthaṃ vatthaṭṭhikassa, yānaṃ yānaṭṭhikassa, sayanaṃ sayanaṭṭhikassa, itthiṃ itthiṭṭhikassa, hiraññaṃ hiraññaṭṭhikassa, suvaṇṇaṃ suvaṇṇaṭṭhikassa.

"Then, Ānanda, it occurred to King Mahāsudassana: ‘Suppose I were to set up a gift of this kind on the banks of these lotus ponds: food for one who needs food, drink for one who needs drink, clothing for one who needs clothing, vehicles for one who needs a vehicle, beds for one who needs a bed, women for one who needs a woman, silver for one who needs silver, gold for one who needs gold.’ So, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana set up a gift of this kind on the banks of those lotus ponds: food for one who needs food, drink for one who needs drink, clothing for one who needs clothing, vehicles for one who needs a vehicle, beds for one who needs a bed, women for one who needs a woman, silver for one who needs silver, gold for one who needs gold."

255. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, brāhmaṇagahapatikā pahūtaṃ sāpateyyaṃ ādāya rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘idaṃ, deva, pahūtaṃ sāpateyyaṃ devaññeva uddissa ābhataṃ, taṃ devo paṭiggaṇhatū’ti. ‘Alaṃ bho, mamapidaṃ pahūtaṃ sāpateyyaṃ dhammikena balinā abhisaṅkhataṃ, tañca vo hotu, ito ca bhiyyo harathā’ti. Te raññā paṭikkhittā ekamantaṃ apakkamma evaṃ samacintesuṃ – ‘na kho etaṃ amhākaṃ patirūpaṃ, yaṃ mayaṃ imāni sāpateyyāni punadeva sakāni gharāni paṭihareyyāma. Yaṃnūna mayaṃ rañño mahāsudassanassa nivesanaṃ māpeyyāmā’ti. Te rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘nivesanaṃ te deva, māpessāmā’ti. Adhivāsesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tuṇhībhāvena.

255."Then, Ananda, brahmin householders, taking abundant valuables, approached King Mahāsudassana and said: 'This, lord, is abundant valuables brought specifically for you. May the lord accept it.' 'Enough, sirs, I also have abundant valuables righteously accumulated by strength. Let that be yours, and bring even more from here.' Being refused by the king, they stepped aside and considered thus: 'It is not fitting for us to take these valuables back to our own homes. What if we were to build a palace for King Mahāsudassana?' They approached King Mahāsudassana and said: 'Lord, we will build a palace for you.' King Mahāsudassana consented with silence.

256. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, sakko devānamindo rañño mahāsudassanassa cetasā cetoparivitakkamaññāya vissakammaṃ [visukammaṃ (ka.)] devaputtaṃ āmantesi – ‘ehi tvaṃ, samma vissakamma, rañño mahāsudassanassa nivesanaṃ māpehi dhammaṃ nāma pāsāda’nti. ‘Evaṃ bhaddantavā’ti kho, ānanda, vissakammo devaputto sakkassa devānamindassa paṭissutvā seyyathāpi nāma balavā puriso samiñjitaṃ vā bāhaṃ pasāreyya pasāritaṃ vā bāhaṃ samiñjeyya, evameva devesu tāvatiṃsesu antarahito rañño mahāsudassanassa purato pāturahosi. Atha kho, ānanda, vissakammo devaputto rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ etadavoca – ‘nivesanaṃ te deva, māpessāmi dhammaṃ nāma pāsāda’nti. Adhivāsesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano tuṇhībhāvena.

256."Then, Ānanda, Sakka, the lord of the devas, knowing with his mind the reflection in King Mahāsudassana's mind, addressed the deva Vissakamma: 'Come, good Vissakamma, build a mansion named Dhamma for King Mahāsudassana.' 'Yes, lord,' replied Vissakamma the deva to Sakka, the lord of the devas. Then, just as a strong man might stretch out his bent arm or bend his outstretched arm, so Vissakamma vanished from among the Tāvatiṃsa devas and appeared before King Mahāsudassana. Then, Ānanda, Vissakamma the deva said to King Mahāsudassana: 'Lord, I will build a mansion named Dhamma for you.' King Mahāsudassana consented by remaining silent."

‘‘Māpesi kho, ānanda, vissakammo devaputto rañño mahāsudassanassa nivesanaṃ dhammaṃ nāma pāsādaṃ. Dhammo, ānanda, pāsādo puratthimena pacchimena ca yojanaṃ āyāmena ahosi. Uttarena dakkhiṇena ca aḍḍhayojanaṃ vitthārena. Dhammassa, ānanda, pāsādassa tiporisaṃ uccatarena vatthu citaṃ ahosi catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ iṭṭhakāhi – ekā iṭṭhakā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā.

"Then, Ananda, Vissakamma, the deva's son, built the mansion named Dhamma for King Mahāsudassana. The Dhamma mansion, Ananda, was a league in length from east to west and half a league in width from north to south. The foundation of the Dhamma mansion, Ananda, was three purisa high, built with bricks of four colors—one brick of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal."

‘‘Dhammassa, ānanda, pāsādassa caturāsīti thambhasahassāni ahesuṃ catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ – eko thambho sovaṇṇamayo, eko rūpiyamayo, eko veḷuriyamayo, eko phalikamayo. Dhammo, ānanda, pāsādo catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ phalakehi santhato ahosi – ekaṃ phalakaṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ, ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ, ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ, ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ.

"The Dhamma palace, Ananda, had eighty-four thousand pillars of four colors—one pillar of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal. The Dhamma palace, Ananda, was covered with planks of four colors—one plank of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal."

‘‘Dhammassa, ānanda, pāsādassa catuvīsati sopānāni ahesuṃ catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ – ekaṃ sopānaṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ, ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ, ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ, ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ. Sovaṇṇamayassa sopānassa sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayassa sopānassa rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Veḷuriyamayassa sopānassa veḷuriyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ phalikamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Phalikamayassa sopānassa phalikamayā thambhā ahesuṃ veḷuriyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca.

"Ānanda, the palace of the Dhamma had twenty-four steps of four different materials: one step of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal. The golden step had golden pillars with silver crossbars and coping. The silver step had silver pillars with golden crossbars and coping. The beryl step had beryl pillars with crystal crossbars and coping. The crystal step had crystal pillars with beryl crossbars and coping."

‘‘Dhamme, ānanda, pāsāde caturāsīti kūṭāgārasahassāni ahesuṃ catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ – ekaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ, ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ, ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ, ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ. Sovaṇṇamaye kūṭāgāre rūpiyamayo pallaṅko paññatto ahosi, rūpiyamaye kūṭāgāre sovaṇṇamayo pallaṅko paññatto ahosi, veḷuriyamaye kūṭāgāre dantamayo pallaṅko paññatto ahosi, phalikamaye kūṭāgāre sāramayo pallaṅko paññatto ahosi. Sovaṇṇamayassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre rūpiyamayo tālo ṭhito ahosi, tassa rūpiyamayo khandho sovaṇṇamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Rūpiyamayassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre sovaṇṇamayo tālo ṭhito ahosi, tassa sovaṇṇamayo khandho, rūpiyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Veḷuriyamayassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre phalikamayo tālo ṭhito ahosi, tassa phalikamayo khandho, veḷuriyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Phalikamayassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre veḷuriyamayo tālo ṭhito ahosi, tassa veḷuriyamayo khandho, phalikamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca.

"In the Dhamma palace, Ananda, there were eighty-four thousand pinnacled mansions of four kinds—one mansion was made of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal. In the golden mansion, a silver couch was prepared; in the silver mansion, a golden couch was prepared; in the beryl mansion, an ivory couch was prepared; and in the crystal mansion, a sandalwood couch was prepared. At the door of the golden mansion stood a silver palm tree, its silver trunk having golden leaves and fruits. At the door of the silver mansion stood a golden palm tree, its golden trunk having silver leaves and fruits. At the door of the beryl mansion stood a crystal palm tree, its crystal trunk having beryl leaves and fruits. At the door of the crystal mansion stood a beryl palm tree, its beryl trunk having crystal leaves and fruits."

257. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ mahāviyūhassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre sabbasovaṇṇamayaṃ tālavanaṃ māpeyyaṃ, yattha divāvihāraṃ nisīdissāmī’ti. Māpesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano mahāviyūhassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre sabbasovaṇṇamayaṃ tālavanaṃ, yattha divāvihāraṃ nisīdi. Dhammo, ānanda, pāsādo dvīhi vedikāhi parikkhitto ahosi, ekā vedikā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā. Sovaṇṇamayāya vedikāya sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayāya vedikāya rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ, sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca.

257."Then, Ānanda, it occurred to King Mahāsudassana: ‘I should have an all-golden palm grove made at the entrance of the Great Array Hall, where I can sit for the day’s rest.’ So, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana had an all-golden palm grove made at the entrance of the Great Array Hall, where he would sit for the day’s rest. The Dhamma palace, Ānanda, was surrounded by two railings: one railing was made of gold, the other of silver. The golden railing had golden pillars with silver crossbars and coping. The silver railing had silver pillars with golden crossbars and coping."

258. ‘‘Dhammo, ānanda, pāsādo dvīhi kiṅkiṇikajālehi [kiṅkaṇikajālehi (syā. ka.)] parikkhitto ahosi – ekaṃ jālaṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ. Sovaṇṇamayassa jālassa rūpiyamayā kiṅkiṇikā ahesuṃ, rūpiyamayassa jālassa sovaṇṇamayā kiṅkiṇikā ahesuṃ. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, kiṅkiṇikajālānaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, pañcaṅgikassa tūriyassa suvinītassa suppaṭitāḷitassa sukusalehi [kusalehi (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] samannāhatassa saddo hoti, vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca, evameva kho, ānanda, tesaṃ kiṅkiṇikajālānaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca. Ye kho panānanda, tena samayena kusāvatiyā rājadhāniyā dhuttā ahesuṃ soṇḍā pipāsā, te tesaṃ kiṅkiṇikajālānaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddena paricāresuṃ. Niṭṭhito kho panānanda, dhammo pāsādo duddikkho ahosi musati cakkhūni. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, vassānaṃ pacchime māse saradasamaye viddhe vigatavalāhake deve ādicco nabhaṃ abbhussakkamāno [abbhuggamamāno (sī. pī. ka.)] duddikkho [dudikkho (pī.)] hoti musati cakkhūni; evameva kho, ānanda, dhammo pāsādo duddikkho ahosi musati cakkhūni.

258."Ānanda, the Dhamma Palace was surrounded by two nets of tinkling bells—one net was made of gold, and one of silver. The golden net had silver bells, and the silver net had golden bells. When these nets of tinkling bells were stirred by the wind, Ānanda, the sound was pleasing, delightful, agreeable, and intoxicating. Just as, Ānanda, the sound of a well-tuned five-piece orchestra, expertly played and skillfully struck, is pleasing, delightful, agreeable, and intoxicating, so too, Ānanda, was the sound of those nets of tinkling bells when stirred by the wind—pleasing, delightful, agreeable, and intoxicating. And at that time, Ānanda, the gamblers, drunkards, and those overcome by thirst in the royal city of Kusāvatī would attend to the sound of those tinkling bells. The Dhamma Palace, Ānanda, being completely finished, was so dazzling that it hurt the eyes. Just as, Ānanda, in the last month of the rainy season, in autumn, when the sky is clear and cloudless, the sun, rising up into the sky, is dazzling and hurts the eyes, so too, Ānanda, was the Dhamma Palace—dazzling and hurting the eyes."

259. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ dhammassa pāsādassa purato dhammaṃ nāma pokkharaṇiṃ māpeyya’nti. Māpesi kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano dhammassa pāsādassa purato dhammaṃ nāma pokkharaṇiṃ. Dhammā, ānanda, pokkharaṇī puratthimena pacchimena ca yojanaṃ āyāmena ahosi, uttarena dakkhiṇena ca aḍḍhayojanaṃ vitthārena. Dhammā, ānanda, pokkharaṇī catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ iṭṭhakāhi citā ahosi – ekā iṭṭhakā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā.

259."Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana thought: ‘Why don’t I have a pond named Dhamma built in front of the Dhamma Palace?’ And so, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana had a pond named Dhamma built in front of the Dhamma Palace. The Dhamma pond, Ānanda, was a league in length from east to west and half a league in width from north to south. The Dhamma pond, Ānanda, was constructed with bricks of four kinds—one of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal.

‘‘Dhammāya, ānanda, pokkharaṇiyā catuvīsati sopānāni ahesuṃ catunnaṃ vaṇṇānaṃ – ekaṃ sopānaṃ sovaṇṇamayaṃ, ekaṃ rūpiyamayaṃ, ekaṃ veḷuriyamayaṃ, ekaṃ phalikamayaṃ. Sovaṇṇamayassa sopānassa sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayassa sopānassa rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Veḷuriyamayassa sopānassa veḷuriyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ phalikamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Phalikamayassa sopānassa phalikamayā thambhā ahesuṃ veḷuriyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca.

"Ānanda, the Dhamma pond had twenty-four staircases of four kinds—one of gold, one of silver, one of beryl, and one of crystal. The golden staircase had golden pillars with silver spikes and finials. The silver staircase had silver pillars with golden spikes and finials. The beryl staircase had beryl pillars with crystal spikes and finials. The crystal staircase had crystal pillars with beryl spikes and finials.

‘‘Dhammā, ānanda, pokkharaṇī dvīhi vedikāhi parikkhittā ahosi – ekā vedikā sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā. Sovaṇṇamayāya vedikāya sovaṇṇamayā thambhā ahesuṃ rūpiyamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca. Rūpiyamayāya vedikāya rūpiyamayā thambhā ahesuṃ sovaṇṇamayā sūciyo ca uṇhīsañca.

"Ānanda, the Dhamma pond was surrounded by two railings—one of gold and one of silver. The golden railing had golden pillars, silver crossbars, and a silver coping. The silver railing had silver pillars, golden crossbars, and a golden coping."

‘‘Dhammā, ānanda, pokkharaṇī sattahi tālapantīhi parikkhittā ahosi – ekā tālapanti sovaṇṇamayā, ekā rūpiyamayā, ekā veḷuriyamayā, ekā phalikamayā, ekā lohitaṅkamayā, ekā masāragallamayā, ekā sabbaratanamayā. Sovaṇṇamayassa tālassa sovaṇṇamayo khandho ahosi rūpiyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Rūpiyamayassa tālassa rūpiyamayo khandho ahosi sovaṇṇamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Veḷuriyamayassa tālassa veḷuriyamayo khandho ahosi phalikamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Phalikamayassa tālassa phalikamayo khandho ahosi veḷuriyamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Lohitaṅkamayassa tālassa lohitaṅkamayo khandho ahosi masāragallamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Masāragallamayassa tālassa masāragallamayo khandho ahosi lohitaṅkamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Sabbaratanamayassa tālassa sabbaratanamayo khandho ahosi, sabbaratanamayāni pattāni ca phalāni ca. Tāsaṃ kho panānanda, tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi, vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca. Seyyathāpi, ānanda, pañcaṅgikassa tūriyassa suvinītassa suppaṭitāḷitassa sukusalehi samannāhatassa saddo hoti vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca, evameva kho, ānanda, tāsaṃ tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddo ahosi vaggu ca rajanīyo ca khamanīyo ca madanīyo ca. Ye kho panānanda, tena samayena kusāvatiyā rājadhāniyā dhuttā ahesuṃ soṇḍā pipāsā, te tāsaṃ tālapantīnaṃ vāteritānaṃ saddena paricāresuṃ.

"Ānanda, the Dhamma pond was surrounded by seven rows of palm trees—one row of golden palms, one of silver, one of beryl, one of crystal, one of red coral, one of emerald, and one of all precious gems. For the golden palm trees, the trunks were golden, while the leaves and fruits were silver. For the silver palm trees, the trunks were silver, while the leaves and fruits were golden. For the beryl palm trees, the trunks were beryl, while the leaves and fruits were crystal. For the crystal palm trees, the trunks were crystal, while the leaves and fruits were beryl. For the red coral palm trees, the trunks were red coral, while the leaves and fruits were emerald. For the emerald palm trees, the trunks were emerald, while the leaves and fruits were red coral. For the palm trees made of all precious gems, the trunks were of all precious gems, as were the leaves and fruits. When the wind stirred those rows of palm trees, Ānanda, the sound was sweet, enchanting, pleasing, and intoxicating. Just as the sound of a well-tuned five-piece orchestra, well-trained, skillfully played by experts, is sweet, enchanting, pleasing, and intoxicating, so too was the sound of those rows of palm trees stirred by the wind—sweet, enchanting, pleasing, and intoxicating. And at that time, Ānanda, the gamblers, drunkards, and those parched with thirst in the royal city of Kusāvatī attended to the sound of those wind-stirred rows of palm trees."

‘‘Niṭṭhite kho panānanda, dhamme pāsāde niṭṭhitāya dhammāya ca pokkharaṇiyā rājā mahāsudassano ‘ye [ye ko panānanda (syā. ka.)] tena samayena samaṇesu vā samaṇasammatā brāhmaṇesu vā brāhmaṇasammatā’, te sabbakāmehi santappetvā dhammaṃ pāsādaṃ abhiruhi.

Then, Ānanda, when the Dhamma Palace and the Dhamma pond were completed, King Mahāsudassana, having satisfied with all desires those who were considered recluses or brahmins at that time, ascended the Dhamma Palace.

## Paṭhamabhāṇavāro.

### The First Recitation Section.

## Jhānasampatti

### Attainment of Jhana

260. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘kissa nu kho me idaṃ kammassa phalaṃ kissa kammassa vipāko, yenāhaṃ etarahi evaṃmahiddhiko evaṃmahānubhāvo’ti? Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘tiṇṇaṃ kho me idaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ tiṇṇaṃ kammānaṃ vipāko, yenāhaṃ etarahi evaṃmahiddhiko evaṃmahānubhāvo, seyyathidaṃ dānassa damassa saṃyamassā’ti.

260.Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana had this thought: ‘What is the fruit of what action, the result of what action, that I am now so mighty and majestic?’ Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana had this thought: ‘This is the fruit of three actions, the result of three actions, that I am now so mighty and majestic, namely, giving, self-control, and restraint.’

‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano yena mahāviyūhaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā mahāviyūhassa kūṭāgārassa dvāre ṭhito udānaṃ udānesi – ‘tiṭṭha, kāmavitakka, tiṭṭha, byāpādavitakka, tiṭṭha, vihiṃsāvitakka. Ettāvatā kāmavitakka, ettāvatā byāpādavitakka, ettāvatā vihiṃsāvitakkā’ti.

Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana approached the Great Array Hall. Having approached, he stood at the door of the Great Array Hall and uttered this inspired utterance: ‘Stop, sensual thought! Stop, malevolent thought! Stop, harmful thought! Thus far sensual thought, thus far malevolent thought, thus far harmful thought.’

261. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano mahāviyūhaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ pavisitvā sovaṇṇamaye pallaṅke nisinno vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsi. Vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā ajjhattaṃ sampasādanaṃ cetaso ekodibhāvaṃ avitakkaṃ avicāraṃ samādhijaṃ pītisukhaṃ dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsi. Pītiyā ca virāgā upekkhako ca vihāsi, sato ca sampajāno sukhañca kāyena paṭisaṃvedesi, yaṃ taṃ ariyā ācikkhanti – ‘upekkhako satimā sukhavihārī’ti tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsi. Sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja vihāsi.

261.Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana, having entered the Great Array Hall, sat on the golden couch. Quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome states, he entered and dwelled in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by thought and examination, born of seclusion, filled with rapture and joy. With the subsiding of thought and examination, having attained internal serenity and singleness of mind, he entered and dwelled in the second jhāna, which is without thought, without examination, born of samādhi, filled with rapture and joy. With the fading away of rapture, he remained equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending, experiencing pleasure with the body; that which the noble ones announce, 'Equanimous and mindful, he dwells pleasantly'—he entered and dwelled in the third jhāna. With the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the previous disappearance of joy and sorrow, he entered and dwelled in the fourth jhāna, which is neither painful nor pleasant, purified by equanimity and mindfulness.

262. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano mahāviyūhā kūṭāgārā nikkhamitvā sovaṇṇamayaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ pavisitvā rūpiyamaye pallaṅke nisinno mettāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi. Tathā dutiyaṃ tathā tatiyaṃ tathā catutthaṃ. Iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ mettāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyāpajjena pharitvā vihāsi. Karuṇāsahagatena cetasā…pe… muditāsahagatena cetasā…pe… upekkhāsahagatena cetasā ekaṃ disaṃ pharitvā vihāsi tathā dutiyaṃ tathā tatiyaṃ tathā catutthaṃ. Iti uddhamadho tiriyaṃ sabbadhi sabbattatāya sabbāvantaṃ lokaṃ upekkhāsahagatena cetasā vipulena mahaggatena appamāṇena averena abyāpajjena pharitvā vihāsi.

262.Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana, emerging from the Great Array Hall, entered the golden hall and sat on the silver couch. With a mind imbued with loving-kindness, he pervaded one direction and remained. Likewise, the second direction, likewise the third direction, likewise the fourth direction. Thus, above, below, across, everywhere, in every way, he pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with loving-kindness—abundant, exalted, immeasurable, without hostility, and without ill will, and remained. With a mind imbued with compassion… with a mind imbued with appreciative joy… with a mind imbued with equanimity, he pervaded one direction and remained. Likewise, the second direction, likewise the third direction, likewise the fourth direction. Thus, above, below, across, everywhere, in every way, he pervaded the entire world with a mind imbued with equanimity—abundant, exalted, immeasurable, without hostility, and without ill will, and remained.

## Caturāsīti nagarasahassādi

### Eighty-four thousand cities and so forth

263. ‘‘Rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa caturāsīti nagarasahassāni ahesuṃ kusāvatīrājadhānippamukhāni; caturāsīti pāsādasahassāni ahesuṃ dhammapāsādappamukhāni; caturāsīti kūṭāgārasahassāni ahesuṃ mahāviyūhakūṭāgārappamukhāni; caturāsīti pallaṅkasahassāni ahesuṃ sovaṇṇamayāni rūpiyamayāni dantamayāni sāramayāni gonakatthatāni paṭikatthatāni paṭalikatthatāni kadalimigapavarapaccattharaṇāni sauttaracchadāni ubhatolohitakūpadhānāni; caturāsīti nāgasahassāni ahesuṃ sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni uposathanāgarājappamukhāni; caturāsīti assasahassāni ahesuṃ sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni valāhakaassarājappamukhāni; caturāsīti rathasahassāni ahesuṃ sīhacammaparivārāni byagghacammaparivārāni dīpicammaparivārāni paṇḍukambalaparivārāni sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni vejayantarathappamukhāni; caturāsīti maṇisahassāni ahesuṃ maṇiratanappamukhāni; caturāsīti itthisahassāni ahesuṃ subhaddādevippamukhāni; caturāsīti gahapatisahassāni ahesuṃ gahapatiratanappamukhāni; caturāsīti khattiyasahassāni ahesuṃ anuyantāni pariṇāyakaratanappamukhāni; caturāsīti dhenusahassāni ahesuṃ duhasandanāni [dukūlasandanāni(pī.)] dukūlasandānāni [dukūlasandanāni (pī.) dukūlasandānāni (saṃ. ni. 3.96)] kaṃsūpadhāraṇāni; caturāsīti vatthakoṭisahassāni ahesuṃ khomasukhumānaṃ kappāsikasukhumānaṃ koseyyasukhumānaṃ kambalasukhumānaṃ; (rañño, ānanda, mahāsudassanassa) [( ) sī. ipotthakesu natthi] caturāsīti thālipākasahassāni ahesuṃ sāyaṃ pātaṃ bhattābhihāro abhihariyittha.

263.Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana had eighty-four thousand cities, with Kusāvatī as the foremost capital; eighty-four thousand palaces, with the Dhamma Palace as the foremost; eighty-four thousand halls, with the Great Array Hall as the foremost; eighty-four thousand couches, made of gold, silver, ivory, and sandalwood, covered with long-fleeced woolen blankets, embroidered coverlets, patchwork coverlets, choice antelope-skin rugs, with canopies above and red cushions at both ends; eighty-four thousand elephants, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden netting, with Uposatha the elephant king as the foremost; eighty-four thousand horses, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden netting, with Valāhaka the horse king as the foremost; eighty-four thousand chariots, draped with lion skins, tiger skins, leopard skins, and blankets of saffron color, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden netting, with the Vejayanta chariot as the foremost; eighty-four thousand jewels, with the jewel treasure as the foremost; eighty-four thousand women, with Queen Subhaddā as the foremost; eighty-four thousand householder chiefs, with the householder treasure as the foremost; eighty-four thousand noble warriors, attendants, with the chief minister as the foremost; eighty-four thousand milk cows, easily milked, with milk pails of dukūla cloth and bronze milk pails; eighty-four thousand crores of cloths, of fine linen, fine cotton, fine silk, and fine wool; eighty-four thousand dishes of rice were offered to King Mahāsudassana morning and evening.

264. ‘‘Tena kho panānanda, samayena rañño mahāsudassanassa caturāsīti nāgasahassāni sāyaṃ pātaṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgacchanti. Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa etadahosi – ‘imāni kho me caturāsīti nāgasahassāni sāyaṃ pātaṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgacchanti, yaṃnūna vassasatassa vassasatassa accayena dvecattālīsaṃ dvecattālīsaṃ nāgasahassāni sakiṃ sakiṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgaccheyyu’nti. Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano pariṇāyakaratanaṃ āmantesi – ‘imāni kho me, samma pariṇāyakaratana, caturāsīti nāgasahassāni sāyaṃ pātaṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgacchanti, tena hi, samma pariṇāyakaratana, vassasatassa vassasatassa accayena dvecattālīsaṃ dvecattālīsaṃ nāgasahassāni sakiṃ sakiṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgacchantū’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devā’ti kho, ānanda, pariṇāyakaratanaṃ rañño mahāsudassanassa paccassosi. Atha kho, ānanda, rañño mahāsudassanassa aparena samayena vassasatassa vassasatassa accayena dvecattālīsaṃ dvecattālīsaṃ nāgasahassāni sakiṃ sakiṃ upaṭṭhānaṃ āgamaṃsu.

264.At that time, Ānanda, eighty-four thousand elephants came to attend upon King Mahāsudassana morning and evening. Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana thought: ‘These eighty-four thousand elephants attend upon me morning and evening. What if forty-two thousand elephants were to attend upon me just once at the end of every hundred years?’ Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana addressed his chief minister: ‘My dear chief minister, these eighty-four thousand elephants attend upon me morning and evening. Therefore, my dear chief minister, arrange for forty-two thousand elephants to attend upon me just once at the end of every hundred years.’ ‘Yes, lord,’ replied the chief minister to King Mahāsudassana. And so, Ānanda, after some time, at the end of every hundred years, forty-two thousand elephants attended upon the king just once.

## Subhaddādeviupasaṅkamanaṃ

### The approach to Queen Subhaddā.

265. ‘‘Atha kho, ānanda, subhaddāya deviyā bahunnaṃ vassānaṃ bahunnaṃ vassasatānaṃ bahunnaṃ vassasahassānaṃ accayena etadahosi – ‘ciraṃ diṭṭho kho me rājā mahāsudassano. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkameyya’nti. Atha kho, ānanda, subhaddā devī itthāgāraṃ āmantesi – ‘etha tumhe sīsāni nhāyatha pītāni vatthāni pārupatha. Ciraṃ diṭṭho no rājā mahāsudassano, rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkamissāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ, ayye’ti kho, ānanda, itthāgāraṃ subhaddāya deviyā paṭissutvā sīsāni nhāyitvā pītāni vatthāni pārupitvā yena subhaddā devī tenupasaṅkami. Atha kho, ānanda, subhaddā devī pariṇāyakaratanaṃ āmantesi – ‘kappehi, samma pariṇāyakaratana, caturaṅginiṃ senaṃ, ciraṃ diṭṭho no rājā mahāsudassano, rājānaṃ mahāsudassanaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkamissāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ, devī’ti kho, ānanda, pariṇāyakaratanaṃ subhaddāya deviyā paṭissutvā caturaṅginiṃ senaṃ kappāpetvā subhaddāya deviyā paṭivedesi – ‘kappitā kho, devi, caturaṅginī senā, yassadāni kālaṃ maññasī’ti. Atha kho, ānanda, subhaddā devī caturaṅginiyā senāya saddhiṃ itthāgārena yena dhammo pāsādo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā dhammaṃ pāsādaṃ abhiruhitvā yena mahāviyūhaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ tenupasaṅkami. Upasaṅkamitvā mahāviyūhassa kūṭāgārassa dvārabāhaṃ ālambitvā aṭṭhāsi. Atha kho, ānanda, rājā mahāsudassano saddaṃ sutvā – ‘kiṃ nu kho mahato viya janakāyassa saddo’ti mahāviyūhā kūṭāgārā nikkhamanto addasa subhaddaṃ deviṃ dvārabāhaṃ ālambitvā ṭhitaṃ, disvāna subhaddaṃ deviṃ etadavoca – ‘ettheva, devi, tiṭṭha mā pāvisī’ti. So cattāro brahmavihāre bhāvetvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā brahmalokūpago ahosi.

269.Then, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana not long after passed away. Just as, Ānanda, a householder or a householder’s son might experience food-intoxication after eating a delightful meal, so too, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana’s death-agony was like a food-intoxication. And when he passed away, Ānanda, King Mahāsudassana reappeared in a good destination, in the Brahmā world. King Mahāsudassana, Ānanda, played as a prince for eighty-four thousand years. He exercised viceregency for eighty-four thousand years. He exercised kingship for eighty-four thousand years. And for eighty-four thousand years, as a householder, he lived the holy life in the Dhamma Palace. Having developed the four brahmavihāras, with the breakup of the body, after death, he reappeared in the Brahmā world.

270. ‘‘Siyā kho panānanda, evamassa – ‘añño nūna tena samayena rājā mahāsudassano ahosī’ti, na kho panetaṃ, ānanda, evaṃ daṭṭhabbaṃ. Ahaṃ tena samayena rājā mahāsudassano ahosiṃ. Mama tāni caturāsīti nagarasahassāni kusāvatīrājadhānippamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti pāsādasahassāni dhammapāsādappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti kūṭāgārasahassāni mahāviyūhakūṭāgārappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti pallaṅkasahassāni sovaṇṇamayāni rūpiyamayāni dantamayāni sāramayāni gonakatthatāni paṭikatthatāni paṭalikatthatāni kadalimigapavarapaccattharaṇāni sauttaracchadāni ubhatolohitakūpadhānāni, mama tāni caturāsīti nāgasahassāni sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni uposathanāgarājappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti assasahassāni sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni valāhakaassarājappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti rathasahassāni sīhacammaparivārāni byagghacammaparivārāni dīpicammaparivārāni paṇḍukambalaparivārāni sovaṇṇālaṅkārāni sovaṇṇadhajāni hemajālapaṭicchannāni vejayantarathappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti maṇisahassāni maṇiratanappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti itthisahassāni subhaddādevippamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti gahapatisahassāni gahapatiratanappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti khattiyasahassāni anuyantāni pariṇāyakaratanappamukhāni, mama tāni caturāsīti dhenusahassāni duhasandanāni kaṃsūpadhāraṇāni, mama tāni caturāsīti vatthakoṭisahassāni khomasukhumānaṃ kappāsikasukhumānaṃ koseyyasukhumānaṃ kambalasukhumānaṃ, mama tāni caturāsīti thālipākasahassāni sāyaṃ pātaṃ bhattābhihāro abhihariyittha.

270."Now, Ānanda, it might occur to you: ‘Surely, someone else was King Mahāsudassana at that time.’ But, Ānanda, it should not be seen in this way. I myself was King Mahāsudassana at that time. Those eighty-four thousand cities, with Kusāvatī, the royal capital, as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand palaces, with the Dhamma Palace as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand gabled mansions, with the Great Hall of Variety as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand couches, made of gold, silver, ivory, and heartwood, spread with long-fleeced coverlets, variegated coverlets, patchwork coverlets, deer skins, and choice antelope hides, with canopies above and red cushions at both ends, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand elephants, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden nets, with Uposatha, the elephant king, as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand horses, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden nets, with Valāhaka, the horse king, as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand chariots, equipped with lion skins, tiger skins, leopard skins, and yellow woolen blankets, adorned with gold ornaments and gold banners, covered with golden nets, with the Vejayanta chariot as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand jewels, with the jewel treasure as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand women, with Subhaddādevī as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand householders, with the householder treasure as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand nobles, followers, with the advisor treasure as the foremost, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand cows, easy to milk, with bronze milk pails, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand kotis of cloths – fine linen, fine cotton, fine silk, and fine wool, were mine. Those eighty-four thousand dishes of rice were offered to me, evening and morning, as my food."

271. ‘‘Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītinagarasahassānaṃ ekaññeva taṃ nagaraṃ hoti, yaṃ tena samayena ajjhāvasāmi yadidaṃ kusāvatī rājadhānī. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītipāsādasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so pāsādo hoti, yaṃ tena samayena ajjhāvasāmi yadidaṃ dhammo pāsādo. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītikūṭāgārasahassānaṃ ekaññeva taṃ kūṭāgāraṃ hoti, yaṃ tena samayena ajjhāvasāmi yadidaṃ mahāviyūhaṃ kūṭāgāraṃ. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītipallaṅkasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so pallaṅko hoti, yaṃ tena samayena paribhuñjāmi yadidaṃ sovaṇṇamayo vā rūpiyamayo vā dantamayo vā sāramayo vā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītināgasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so nāgo hoti, yaṃ tena samayena abhiruhāmi yadidaṃ uposatho nāgarājā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītiassasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so asso hoti, yaṃ tena samayena abhiruhāmi yadidaṃ valāhako assarājā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītirathasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so ratho hoti, yaṃ tena samayena abhiruhāmi yadidaṃ vejayantaratho. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītiitthisahassānaṃ ekāyeva sā itthī hoti, yā tena samayena paccupaṭṭhāti khattiyānī vā vessinī [vessāyinī (syā.), velāmikānī (ka. sī. pī.) velāmikā (saṃ. ni. 3.96)] vā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, vā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītivatthakoṭisahassānaṃ ekaṃyeva taṃ dussayugaṃ hoti, yaṃ tena samayena paridahāmi khomasukhumaṃ vā kappāsikasukhumaṃ vā koseyyasukhumaṃ vā kambalasukhumaṃ vā. Tesaṃ kho panānanda, caturāsītithālipākasahassānaṃ ekoyeva so thālipāko hoti, yato nāḷikodanaparamaṃ bhuñjāmi tadupiyañca sūpeyyaṃ.

271."And, Ānanda, of those eighty-four thousand cities, there was only one city where I dwelt at that time, namely, Kusāvatī, the royal capital. Of those eighty-four thousand palaces, there was only one palace where I dwelt, namely, the Dhamma Palace. Of those eighty-four thousand gabled mansions, there was only one gabled mansion where I dwelt, namely, the Great Hall of Variety. Of those eighty-four thousand couches, there was only one couch that I used, whether made of gold, or silver, or ivory, or heartwood. Of those eighty-four thousand elephants, there was only one elephant that I mounted, namely, Uposatha, the elephant king. Of those eighty-four thousand horses, there was only one horse that I mounted, namely, Valāhaka, the horse king. Of those eighty-four thousand chariots, there was only one chariot that I rode, namely, the Vejayanta Chariot. Of those eighty-four thousand women, there was only one woman who attended me, whether a noblewoman or a merchant’s daughter. Of those eighty-four thousand kotis of cloths, there was only one pair of cloths that I wore, whether of fine linen, fine cotton, fine silk, or fine wool. Of those eighty-four thousand dishes of cooked rice, there was only one dish from which I ate, at most a nāḷi measure of rice along with suitable curries."

272. ‘‘Passānanda, sabbete saṅkhārā atītā niruddhā vipariṇatā. Evaṃ aniccā kho, ānanda, saṅkhārā; evaṃ addhuvā kho, ānanda, saṅkhārā; evaṃ anassāsikā kho, ānanda, saṅkhārā! Yāvañcidaṃ, ānanda, alameva sabbasaṅkhāresu nibbindituṃ, alaṃ virajjituṃ, alaṃ vimuccituṃ.

272."See, Ānanda, all those conditioned things have passed, ceased, and undergone change. Thus impermanent are conditioned things, Ānanda; thus unstable are conditioned things, Ānanda; thus unreliable are conditioned things, Ānanda! Truly, Ānanda, it is quite enough to become disenchanted with all conditioned things, enough to become dispassionate, enough to be liberated."

‘‘Chakkhattuṃ kho panāhaṃ, ānanda, abhijānāmi imasmiṃ padese sarīraṃ nikkhipitaṃ, tañca kho rājāva samāno cakkavattī dhammiko dhammarājā cāturanto vijitāvī janapadatthāvariyapatto sattaratanasamannāgato, ayaṃ sattamo sarīranikkhepo. Na kho panāhaṃ, ānanda, taṃ padesaṃ samanupassāmi sadevake loke samārake sabrahmake sassamaṇabrāhmaṇiyā pajāya sadevamanussāya yattha tathāgato aṭṭhamaṃ sarīraṃ nikkhipeyyā’’ti. Idamavoca bhagavā, idaṃ vatvāna sugato athāparaṃ etadavoca satthā –

"Six times, Ānanda, I recall having laid down my body in this very place—each time as a wheel-turning monarch, a righteous king who ruled by the Dhamma, a conqueror of the four quarters, who had established stability in the country, and was endowed with the seven treasures. This is the seventh laying down of the body. But, Ānanda, I do not see any place in this world with its devas, Māras, and Brahmās, among this generation with its recluses and brahmins, its devas and humans, where the Tathāgata would lay down his body an eighth time." The Blessed One said this. Having said this, the Fortunate One, the Teacher, then spoke further:

‘‘Aniccā vata saṅkhārā, uppādavayadhammino;Uppajjitvā nirujjhanti, tesaṃ vūpasamo sukho’’ti.

"Impermanent, alas, are formations; their nature is to arise and pass away. Having arisen, they cease; their appeasement is bliss."

## Mahāsudassanasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ catutthaṃ.

### The Discourse on Mahāsudassana, the fourth, is concluded.
