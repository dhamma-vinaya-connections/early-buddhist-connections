# Pāyāsisuttaṃ

## The Pāyāsi Sutta

406. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ āyasmā kumārakassapo kosalesu cārikaṃ caramāno mahatā bhikkhusaṅghena saddhiṃ pañcamattehi bhikkhusatehi yena setabyā nāma kosalānaṃ nagaraṃ tadavasari. Tatra sudaṃ āyasmā kumārakassapo setabyāyaṃ viharati uttarena setabyaṃ siṃsapāvane [sīsapāvane (syā.)]. Tena kho pana samayena pāyāsi rājañño setabyaṃ ajjhāvasati sattussadaṃ satiṇakaṭṭhodakaṃ sadhaññaṃ rājabhoggaṃ raññā pasenadinā kosalena dinnaṃ rājadāyaṃ brahmadeyyaṃ.

406.Thus have I heard. At one time the Venerable Kumārakassapa was wandering in the Kosalan country with a large Saṅgha of monks, with about five hundred monks, and arrived at Setabyā, a Kosalan town. There the Venerable Kumārakassapa was dwelling north of Setabyā in the Siṃsapā Grove. Now at that time the chieftain Pāyāsi was dwelling in Setabyā, which was populous, well-supplied with grass, timber, and water, rich in grain, a royal possession given by King Pasenadi of Kosala, a royal gift, a brahminical grant.

## Pāyāsirājaññavatthu

### The Account of Chieftain Pāyāsi

407. Tena kho pana samayena pāyāsissa rājaññassa evarūpaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ uppannaṃ hoti – ‘‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ [sukaṭakkaṭānaṃ (sī. pī.)] kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. Assosuṃ kho setabyakā brāhmaṇagahapatikā – ‘‘samaṇo khalu bho kumārakassapo samaṇassa gotamassa sāvako kosalesu cārikaṃ caramāno mahatā bhikkhusaṅghena saddhiṃ pañcamattehi bhikkhusatehi setabyaṃ anuppatto setabyāyaṃ viharati uttarena setabyaṃ siṃsapāvane. Taṃ kho pana bhavantaṃ kumārakassapaṃ evaṃ kalyāṇo kittisaddo abbhuggato – ‘paṇḍito byatto medhāvī bahussuto cittakathī kalyāṇapaṭibhāno vuddho [buddho (syā. ka.)] ceva arahā ca. Sādhu kho pana tathārūpānaṃ arahataṃ dassanaṃ hotī’’’ti. Atha kho setabyakā brāhmaṇagahapatikā setabyāya nikkhamitvā saṅghasaṅghī gaṇībhūtā uttarenamukhā gacchanti yena siṃsapāvanaṃ [yena siṃsapāvanaṃ, tenupasaṅkamanti (sī. pī.)].

407.Now at that time such an evil pernicious view had arisen in the chieftain Pāyāsi: “There is no other world; there are no spontaneously arisen beings; there is no fruit or result of good and bad actions.” The brahmins and householders of Setabyā heard: “The ascetic Kumārakassapa, a disciple of the ascetic Gotama, while wandering in the Kosalan country with a large Saṅgha of monks, with about five hundred monks, has arrived at Setabyā and is dwelling north of Setabyā in the Siṃsapā Grove. Now concerning the Venerable Kumārakassapa, such a good report has spread: ‘He is wise, competent, intelligent, very learned, a good speaker, with an excellent turn of phrase, mature, and an arahant.’ Indeed, it is good to see such arahants.” Then the brahmins and householders of Setabyā, in groups and companies, went out of Setabyā heading north toward the Siṃsapā Grove.

408. Tena kho pana samayena pāyāsi rājañño uparipāsāde divāseyyaṃ upagato hoti. Addasā kho pāyāsi rājañño setabyake brāhmaṇagahapatike setabyāya nikkhamitvā saṅghasaṅghī gaṇībhūte uttarenamukhe gacchante yena siṃsapāvanaṃ [yena siṃsapāvanaṃ, tenupasaṅkamante (sī. pī.)], disvā khattaṃ āmantesi – ‘‘kiṃ nu kho, bho khatte, setabyakā brāhmaṇagahapatikā setabyāya nikkhamitvā saṅghasaṅghī gaṇībhūtā uttarenamukhā gacchanti yena siṃsapāvana’’nti [ettha pana sabbatthapi evameva dissati, natthi pāṭhantaraṃ]?

408.Now at that time the chieftain Pāyāsi had gone to the upper story of his palace for his midday rest. Seeing the brahmins and householders of Setabyā leaving Setabyā in groups and companies, heading north toward the Siṃsapā Grove, he addressed his attendant: “Why, good attendant, are the brahmins and householders of Setabyā leaving Setabyā in groups and companies, heading north toward the Siṃsapā Grove?”

‘‘Atthi kho, bho, samaṇo kumārakassapo, samaṇassa gotamassa sāvako kosalesu cārikaṃ caramāno mahatā bhikkhusaṅghena saddhiṃ pañcamattehi bhikkhusatehi setabyaṃ anuppatto setabyāyaṃ viharati uttarena setabyaṃ siṃsapāvane. Taṃ kho pana bhavantaṃ kumārakassapaṃ evaṃ kalyāṇo kittisaddo abbhuggato – ‘paṇḍito byatto medhāvī bahussuto cittakathī kalyāṇapaṭibhāno vuddho ceva arahā cā’ti [arahā ca (syā. ka.)]. Tamete [tamenaṃ te (sī. ka.), tamenaṃ (pī.)] bhavantaṃ kumārakassapaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkamantī’’ti. ‘‘Tena hi, bho khatte, yena setabyakā brāhmaṇagahapatikā tenupasaṅkama; upasaṅkamitvā setabyake brāhmaṇagahapatike evaṃ vadehi – ‘pāyāsi, bho, rājañño evamāha – āgamentu kira bhavanto, pāyāsipi rājañño samaṇaṃ kumārakassapaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkamissatī’ti. Purā samaṇo kumārakassapo setabyake brāhmaṇagahapatike bāle abyatte saññāpeti – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti. Natthi hi, bho khatte, paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ bho’’ti kho so khattā pāyāsissa rājaññassa paṭissutvā yena setabyakā brāhmaṇagahapatikā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā setabyake brāhmaṇagahapatike etadavoca – ‘‘pāyāsi, bho, rājañño evamāha, āgamentu kira bhavanto, pāyāsipi rājañño samaṇaṃ kumārakassapaṃ dassanāya upasaṅkamissatī’’ti.

“Indeed, sir, there is the ascetic Kumārakassapa, a disciple of the ascetic Gotama, who is wandering in the Kosalan country with a large Saṅgha of monks, with about five hundred monks. He has arrived at Setabyā and is dwelling north of Setabyā in the Siṃsapā Grove. And concerning the Venerable Kumārakassapa, such a good report has spread: ‘He is wise, competent, intelligent, very learned, a good speaker, with an excellent turn of phrase, mature, and an arahant.’ They are going to see the Venerable Kumārakassapa.”“Then, good attendant, go to the brahmins and householders of Setabyā. Having gone, tell the brahmins and householders of Setabyā: ‘The chieftain Pāyāsi says this: “Let the venerable ones wait, for the chieftain Pāyāsi will also go to see the ascetic Kumārakassapa.”’ Before the ascetic Kumārakassapa convinces the foolish and unwise brahmins and householders of Setabyā, saying: ‘Thus there is another world, there are spontaneously arisen beings, there is fruit and result of good and bad actions.’ For indeed, good attendant, there is no other world, no spontaneously arisen beings, no fruit and result of good and bad actions.”“Yes, sir,” replied that attendant to the chieftain Pāyāsi. He then went to the brahmins and householders of Setabyā and said: “The chieftain Pāyāsi says this: ‘Let the venerable ones wait, for the chieftain Pāyāsi will also go to see the ascetic Kumārakassapa.’”

409. Atha kho pāyāsi rājañño setabyakehi brāhmaṇagahapatikehi parivuto yena siṃsapāvanaṃ yenāyasmā kumārakassapo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmatā kumārakassapena saddhiṃ sammodi, sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Setabyakāpi kho brāhmaṇagahapatikā appekacce āyasmantaṃ kumārakassapaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu; appekacce āyasmatā kumārakassapena saddhiṃ sammodiṃsu; sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Appekacce yenāyasmā kumārakassapo tenañjaliṃ paṇāmetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Appekacce nāmagottaṃ sāvetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Appekacce tuṇhībhūtā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu.

409.Then the chieftain Pāyāsi, surrounded by the brahmins and householders of Setabyā, went to the Siṃsapā Grove, to where the Venerable Kumārakassapa was. Having approached, he exchanged courteous greetings with the Venerable Kumārakassapa. When the courteous and amiable talk was concluded, he sat down to one side. Some of the brahmins and householders of Setabyā, having greeted the Venerable Kumārakassapa, sat down to one side. Some exchanged courteous greetings with the Venerable Kumārakassapa; when the courteous and amiable talk was concluded, they sat down to one side. Some, toward the Venerable Kumārakassapa, extended their joined palms in reverence and sat down to one side. Some announced their name and clan and sat down to one side. Some remained silent and sat down to one side.

## Natthikavādo

### The Doctrine of Non-existence.

410. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho pāyāsi rājañño āyasmantaṃ kumārakassapaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘ahañhi, bho kassapa, evaṃvādī evaṃdiṭṭhī – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Nāhaṃ, rājañña, evaṃvādiṃ evaṃdiṭṭhiṃ addasaṃ vā assosiṃ vā. Kathañhi nāma evaṃ vadeyya – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti?

410.Seated to one side, the chieftain Pāyāsi said to Venerable Kumāra Kassapa: "Venerable Kassapa, I hold such a view and declare: ‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’""I have never, chieftain, seen or heard anyone who holds such a view and declares it. How could anyone say: ‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds’?"

## Candimasūriyaupamā

### The Simile of the Moon and Sun

411. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, taññevettha paṭipucchissāmi, yathā te khameyya, tathā naṃ byākareyyāsi. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, rājañña, ime candimasūriyā imasmiṃ vā loke parasmiṃ vā, devā vā te manussā vā’’ti? ‘‘Ime, bho kassapa, candimasūriyā parasmiṃ loke, na imasmiṃ; devā te na manussā’’ti. ‘‘Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti.

411."Well then, chieftain, I will question you about this very matter. Answer as you see fit. What do you think, chieftain—are these sun and moon in this world or another world, and are they gods or humans?" "These, Venerable Kassapa, are in another world, not in this one; they are gods, not humans." "By this reasoning too, chieftain, let it be thus for you: 'There is another world, there are beings of spontaneous birth, and there is a fruit and result of good and bad deeds.'"

412. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo, yena te pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti? ‘‘Atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ viya, rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātī adinnādāyī kāmesumicchācārī musāvādī pisuṇavācā pharusavācā samphappalāpī abhijjhālū byāpannacittā micchādiṭṭhī. Te aparena samayena ābādhikā honti dukkhitā bāḷhagilānā. Yadāhaṃ jānāmi – ‘na dānime imamhā ābādhā vuṭṭhahissantī’ti tyāhaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evaṃ vadāmi – ‘santi kho, bho, eke samaṇabrāhmaṇā evaṃvādino evaṃdiṭṭhino – ye te pāṇātipātī adinnādāyī kāmesumicchācārī musāvādī pisuṇavācā pharusavācā samphappalāpī abhijjhālū byāpannacittā micchādiṭṭhī, te kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ nirayaṃ upapajjantī’ti. Bhavanto kho pāṇātipātī adinnādāyī kāmesumicchācārī musāvādī pisuṇavācā pharusavācā samphappalāpī abhijjhālū byāpannacittā micchādiṭṭhī. Sace tesaṃ bhavataṃ samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ saccaṃ vacanaṃ, bhavanto kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ nirayaṃ upapajjissanti. Sace, bho, kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ nirayaṃ upapajjeyyātha, yena me āgantvā āroceyyātha – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti. Bhavanto kho pana me saddhāyikā paccayikā, yaṃ bhavantehi diṭṭhaṃ, yathā sāmaṃ diṭṭhaṃ evametaṃ bhavissatī’ti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā neva āgantvā ārocenti, na pana dūtaṃ pahiṇanti. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

412."Although you, Venerable Kassapa, say this, still this is how it occurs to me: ‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’"
"Is there, chieftain, any way in which you think this way—‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds’?"
"There is, Venerable Kassapa, a way in which I think this way—‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.’"
"How so, chieftain?"
"Here, Venerable Kassapa, I have friends, companions, relatives, and kin who kill living beings, take what is not given, commit sexual misconduct, lie, speak divisively, speak harshly, gossip, are covetous, have malevolent minds, and hold wrong view. After some time, they become sick, suffering, gravely ill. When I know that ‘they will not recover from this illness,’ I approach them and say:
"‘There are, sirs, some ascetics and brahmins who say this and hold this view: "Those who kill living beings, take what is not given, commit sexual misconduct, lie, speak divisively, speak harshly, gossip, are covetous, have malevolent minds, and hold wrong view—after death, when the body breaks up, they are reborn in a state of misery, a bad destination, the lower realms, even hell." You, sirs, kill living beings, take what is not given, commit sexual misconduct, lie, speak divisively, speak harshly, gossip, are covetous, have malevolent minds, and hold wrong view. If what those ascetics and brahmins say is true, then after death, when the body breaks up, you will be reborn in a state of misery, a bad destination, the lower realms, even hell.
"‘If, sirs, after death, when the body breaks up, you are reborn in a state of misery, a bad destination, the lower realms, even hell, then come and inform me: "There is another world, there are beings of spontaneous birth, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds." You, sirs, are credible and reliable to me. What is seen by you will be just as if I saw it myself.’
"They agree with ‘Very well,’ but they neither come to inform me nor send a messenger. This, Venerable Kassapa, is the way in which I think: ‘There is no other world, there are no beings of spontaneous birth, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.’"

## Coraupamā

### The Robber Simile

413. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, taññevettha paṭipucchissāmi. Yathā te khameyya tathā naṃ byākareyyāsi. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, rājañña, idha te purisā coraṃ āgucāriṃ gahetvā dasseyyuṃ – ‘ayaṃ te, bhante, coro āgucārī; imassa yaṃ icchasi, taṃ daṇḍaṃ paṇehī’ti. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ daḷhāya rajjuyā pacchābāhaṃ gāḷhabandhanaṃ bandhitvā khuramuṇḍaṃ karitvā [kāretvā (syā. ka.)] kharassarena paṇavena rathikāya rathikaṃ [rathiyāya rathiyaṃ (bahūsū)] siṅghāṭakena siṅghāṭakaṃ parinetvā dakkhiṇena dvārena nikkhamitvā dakkhiṇato nagarassa āghātane sīsaṃ chindathā’ti. Te ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā taṃ purisaṃ daḷhāya rajjuyā pacchābāhaṃ gāḷhabandhanaṃ bandhitvā khuramuṇḍaṃ karitvā kharassarena paṇavena rathikāya rathikaṃ siṅghāṭakena siṅghāṭakaṃ parinetvā dakkhiṇena dvārena nikkhamitvā dakkhiṇato nagarassa āghātane nisīdāpeyyuṃ. Labheyya nu kho so coro coraghātesu – ‘āgamentu tāva bhavanto coraghātā, amukasmiṃ me gāme vā nigame vā mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā, yāvāhaṃ tesaṃ uddisitvā āgacchāmī’ti, udāhu vippalapantasseva coraghātā sīsaṃ chindeyyu’’nti? ‘‘Na hi so, bho kassapa, coro labheyya coraghātesu – ‘āgamentu tāva bhavanto coraghātā amukasmiṃ me gāme vā nigame vā mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā, yāvāhaṃ tesaṃ uddisitvā āgacchāmī’ti. Atha kho naṃ vippalapantasseva coraghātā sīsaṃ chindeyyu’’nti. ‘‘So hi nāma, rājañña, coro manusso manussabhūtesu coraghātesu na labhissati – ‘āgamentu tāva bhavanto coraghātā, amukasmiṃ me gāme vā nigame vā mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā, yāvāhaṃ tesaṃ uddisitvā āgacchāmī’ti. Kiṃ pana te mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātī adinnādāyī kāmesumicchācārī musāvādī pisuṇavācā pharusavācā samphappalāpī abhijjhālū byāpannacittā micchādiṭṭhī, te kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ nirayaṃ upapannā labhissanti nirayapālesu – ‘āgamentu tāva bhavanto nirayapālā, yāva mayaṃ pāyāsissa rājaññassa gantvā ārocema – ‘‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti? Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

413."Well then, chieftain, I will question you in return. Answer as you see fit. What do you think, chieftain? Suppose your men were to capture a thief, a criminal, and bring him before you, saying, ‘Sire, this is a thief, a criminal; punish him as you see fit.’ And you were to say, ‘Well then, men, tie this man’s arms tightly behind his back with a strong rope, shave his head, and lead him around street by street, crossroads by crossroads, to the beating of a harsh drum. Take him out through the southern gate and behead him at the southern execution ground.’ They would agree, tie the man’s arms tightly behind his back with a strong rope, shave his head, and lead him around street by street, crossroads by crossroads, to the beating of a harsh drum. They would take him out through the southern gate and seat him at the southern execution ground. Would that thief be able to say to the executioners, ‘Wait, sirs, executioners, let me go to such-and-such village or town where I have friends, companions, or relatives. Let me inform them and then return’? Or would the executioners behead him even as he pleaded?" "Certainly not, Master Kassapa. That thief would not be able to say to the executioners, ‘Wait, sirs, executioners, let me go to such-and-such village or town where I have friends, companions, or relatives. Let me inform them and then return.’ Instead, the executioners would behead him even as he pleaded." "If, chieftain, a mere human thief cannot persuade human executioners with such a plea, how much less could your friends, companions, or relatives—who are killers of living beings, takers of what is not given, those who misconduct themselves in sexual pleasures, liars, slanderers, harsh speakers, idle chatterers, covetous, malicious, and holders of wrong views—how much less could they, after the body’s breakup, after death, having been reborn in the realm of misery, the bad destination, the downfall, hell, persuade the hell-keepers, saying, ‘Wait, sirs, hell-keepers, let us go and inform the chieftain Pāyāsi: "Truly, there is another world, there are beings born spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds"’? By this reasoning too, chieftain, you should understand: ‘Truly, there is another world, there are beings born spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’"

414. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo yena te pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti? ‘‘Atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Yathā kathaṃ viya, rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā pisuṇāya vācāya paṭiviratā pharusāya vācāya paṭiviratā samphappalāpā paṭiviratā anabhijjhālū abyāpannacittā sammādiṭṭhī. Te aparena samayena ābādhikā honti dukkhitā bāḷhagilānā. Yadāhaṃ jānāmi – ‘na dānime imamhā ābādhā vuṭṭhahissantī’ti tyāhaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evaṃ vadāmi – ‘santi kho, bho, eke samaṇabrāhmaṇā evaṃvādino evaṃdiṭṭhino – ye te pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā pisuṇāya vācāya paṭiviratā pharusāya vācāya paṭiviratā samphappalāpā paṭiviratā anabhijjhālū abyāpannacittā sammādiṭṭhī te kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjantīti. Bhavanto kho pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā pisuṇāya vācāya paṭiviratā pharusāya vācāya paṭiviratā samphappalāpā paṭiviratā anabhijjhālū abyāpannacittā sammādiṭṭhī. Sace tesaṃ bhavataṃ samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ saccaṃ vacanaṃ, bhavanto kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjissanti. Sace, bho, kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjeyyātha, yena me āgantvā āroceyyātha – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti. Bhavanto kho pana me saddhāyikā paccayikā, yaṃ bhavantehi diṭṭhaṃ, yathā sāmaṃ diṭṭhaṃ evametaṃ bhavissatī’ti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā neva āgantvā ārocenti, na pana dūtaṃ pahiṇanti. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

414."Although you, Venerable Kassapa, say this, still it occurs to me thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings who are spontaneously reborn, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’" "But, chieftain, is there a way in which it occurs to you thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings who are spontaneously reborn, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds’?" "There is, Venerable Kassapa. There is a way in which it occurs to me thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings who are spontaneously reborn, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds’." "How so, chieftain?" "Here, Venerable Kassapa, I have friends, companions, relatives, and kin who abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, abstain from divisive speech, abstain from harsh speech, abstain from idle chatter, are uncovetous, have minds free from ill will, and have right view. After some time, they become sick, suffering, gravely ill. When I know that they will not recover from this illness, I approach them and say: ‘Sirs, there are some ascetics and brahmins who say this and hold this view: "Those who abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, abstain from divisive speech, abstain from harsh speech, abstain from idle chatter, are uncovetous, have minds free from ill will, and have right view—after death, when the body breaks up, they are reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world." You, sirs, abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, abstain from divisive speech, abstain from harsh speech, abstain from idle chatter, are uncovetous, have minds free from ill will, and have right view. If what those ascetics and brahmins say is true, then after death, when the body breaks up, you will be reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world. If, sirs, after death, when the body breaks up, you are reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world, come and tell me: "Indeed, there is another world, there are beings who are spontaneously reborn, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds." You, sirs, are trustworthy and reliable to me. What is seen by you will be just as if it was seen by myself.’ They agree, saying ‘Very well,’ but they neither come back to tell me nor send a messenger. This, Venerable Kassapa, is the way in which it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings who are spontaneously reborn, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’"

## Gūthakūpapurisaupamā

### The Simile of the Man in the Cesspool

415. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce [upamāyapidhekacce (sī. syā.), upamāyapiidhekacce (pī.)] viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Seyyathāpi, rājañña, puriso gūthakūpe sasīsakaṃ [sasīsako (syā.)] nimuggo assa. Atha tvaṃ purise āṇāpeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, taṃ purisaṃ tamhā gūthakūpā uddharathā’ti. Te ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā taṃ purisaṃ tamhā gūthakūpā uddhareyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, tassa purisassa kāyā veḷupesikāhi gūthaṃ sunimmajjitaṃ nimmajjathā’ti. Te ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā tassa purisassa kāyā veḷupesikāhi gūthaṃ sunimmajjitaṃ nimmajjeyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, tassa purisassa kāyaṃ paṇḍumattikāya tikkhattuṃ subbaṭṭitaṃ ubbaṭṭethā’ti [suppaṭṭitaṃ uppaṭṭethāti (ka.)]. Te tassa purisassa kāyaṃ paṇḍumattikāya tikkhattuṃ subbaṭṭitaṃ ubbaṭṭeyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, taṃ purisaṃ telena abbhañjitvā sukhumena cuṇṇena tikkhattuṃ suppadhotaṃ karothā’ti. Te taṃ purisaṃ telena abbhañjitvā sukhumena cuṇṇena tikkhattuṃ suppadhotaṃ kareyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, tassa purisassa kesamassuṃ kappethā’ti. Te tassa purisassa kesamassuṃ kappeyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, tassa purisassa mahagghañca mālaṃ mahagghañca vilepanaṃ mahagghāni ca vatthāni upaharathā’ti. Te tassa purisassa mahagghañca mālaṃ mahagghañca vilepanaṃ mahagghāni ca vatthāni upahareyyuṃ. Te tvaṃ evaṃ vadeyyāsi – ‘tena hi, bho, taṃ purisaṃ pāsādaṃ āropetvā pañcakāmaguṇāni upaṭṭhāpethā’ti. Te taṃ purisaṃ pāsādaṃ āropetvā pañcakāmaguṇāni upaṭṭhāpeyyuṃ.

415."Well then, chieftain, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Suppose, chieftain, a man were sunk headfirst in a cesspool. Then you would command your men: ‘Now then, sirs, lift that man out of that cesspool.’ They would agree and lift that man out of that cesspool. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, thoroughly scrape the filth from that man’s body with bamboo scrapers.’ They would agree and thoroughly scrape the filth from his body with bamboo scrapers. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, rub his body three times with yellow clay, thoroughly applied.’ They would rub his body three times with yellow clay, thoroughly applied. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, anoint that man with oil and cleanse him three times with fine powder.’ They would anoint him with oil and cleanse him three times with fine powder. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, cut that man’s hair and beard.’ They would cut his hair and beard. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, offer that man expensive garlands, expensive perfumes, and expensive clothes.’ They would offer him expensive garlands, expensive perfumes, and expensive clothes. Then you would say to them: ‘Now then, sirs, take that man up to a palace and provide him with the five kinds of sensual pleasure.’ They would take him up to a palace and provide him with the five kinds of sensual pleasure.

‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, rājañña, api nu tassa purisassa sunhātassa suvilittassa sukappitakesamassussa āmukkamālābharaṇassa odātavatthavasanassa uparipāsādavaragatassa pañcahi kāmaguṇehi samappitassa samaṅgībhūtassa paricārayamānassa punadeva tasmiṃ gūthakūpe nimujjitukāmatā [nimujjitukāmyatā (syā. ka.)] assā’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho kassapa’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Asuci, bho kassapa, gūthakūpo asuci ceva asucisaṅkhāto ca duggandho ca duggandhasaṅkhāto ca jeguccho ca jegucchasaṅkhāto ca paṭikūlo ca paṭikūlasaṅkhāto cā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, rājañña, manussā devānaṃ asucī ceva asucisaṅkhātā ca, duggandhā ca duggandhasaṅkhātā ca, jegucchā ca jegucchasaṅkhātā ca, paṭikūlā ca paṭikūlasaṅkhātā ca. Yojanasataṃ kho, rājañña, manussagandho deve ubbādhati. Kiṃ pana te mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā pisuṇāya vācāya paṭiviratā pharusāya vācāya paṭiviratā samphappalāpā paṭiviratā anabhijjhālū abyāpannacittā sammādiṭṭhī, kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapannā te āgantvā ārocessanti – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti? Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

"What do you think, chieftain? Suppose that a man—well-bathed, well-anointed, with hair and beard finely trimmed, adorned with garlands and ornaments, wearing white clothes, staying on the upper story of a palace—and is supplied and endowed with the five cords of sensual pleasure. Would he then wish to plunge back into that cesspool?" "No, Master Kassapa." "Why is that?" "Because, Master Kassapa, the cesspool is impure, considered impure; foul-smelling, considered foul-smelling; disgusting, considered disgusting; repulsive, considered repulsive." "Just so, chieftain, humans are impure to the devas, considered impure; foul-smelling, considered foul-smelling; disgusting, considered disgusting; repulsive, considered repulsive. The smell of humans, chieftain, afflicts the devas even from a hundred leagues away. What then of your friends, companions, relatives, or kin who abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, abstain from divisive speech, abstain from harsh speech, abstain from idle chatter, are without covetousness, with minds free from ill will, and possess right view? After the breakup of the body, after death, they are reborn in a good destination, the heavenly world. Would they come and inform you: ‘Truly, there is another world, there are beings reborn spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds’? By this means too, chieftain, you should know: ‘Truly, there is another world, there are beings reborn spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’"

416. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo …pe… ‘‘atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… ``yathā kathaṃ viya, rājaññāti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā paṭiviratā, te aparena samayena ābādhikā honti dukkhitā bāḷhagilānā. Yadāhaṃ jānāmi – ‘na dānime imamhā ābādhā vuṭṭhahissantī’ti tyāhaṃ upasaṅkamitvā evaṃ vadāmi – ‘santi kho, bho, eke samaṇabrāhmaṇā evaṃvādino evaṃdiṭṭhino – ye te pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā paṭiviratā, te kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjanti devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyatanti. Bhavanto kho pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā paṭiviratā. Sace tesaṃ bhavataṃ samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ saccaṃ vacanaṃ, bhavanto kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjissanti, devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyataṃ. Sace, bho, kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajjeyyātha devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyataṃ, yena me āgantvā āroceyyātha – `itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipākoti. Bhavanto kho pana me saddhāyikā paccayikā, yaṃ bhavantehi diṭṭhaṃ, yathā sāmaṃ diṭṭhaṃ evametaṃ bhavissatīti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā neva āgantvā ārocenti, na pana dūtaṃ pahiṇanti. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

416."Although you, Venerable Kassapa, say this, still this is what occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings born spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’" "Is there, chieftain, a way...?" "There is, Venerable Kassapa, a way... How so, chieftain?" "Here, Venerable Kassapa, I have friends, ministers, relatives, and kin who abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, and abstain from intoxicants that cause heedlessness. Later, they become sick, suffering, gravely ill. You, sirs, abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from false speech, and abstain from intoxicants that cause heedlessness. If the words of those ascetics and brahmins are true, then after death, at the breaking up of the body, you will be reborn in a good destination, in a heavenly world in companionship with the devas of the Thirty-Three. If, sirs, after death, at the breaking up of the body, you are reborn in a good destination, in a heavenly world in companionship with the devas of the Thirty-Three, please come back and tell me: "Indeed, there is another world, there are beings born spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds." You, sirs, are trustworthy and reliable to me. What you see will be just as if I saw it myself.’ They agree with ‘Very well,’ but they neither come back to tell me nor send a messenger. This, Venerable Kassapa, is the way by which it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings born spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.’"

## Tāvatiṃsadevaupamā

### The Simile of the Tāvatiṃsa Devas

417. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, taññevettha paṭipucchissāmi; yathā te khameyya, tathā naṃ byākareyyāsi. Yaṃ kho pana, rājañña, mānussakaṃ vassasataṃ, devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ eso eko rattindivo [rattidivo (ka.)], tāya rattiyā tiṃsarattiyo māso, tena māsena dvādasamāsiyo saṃvaccharo, tena saṃvaccharena dibbaṃ vassasahassaṃ devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ āyuppamāṇaṃ. Ye te mittāmaccā ñātisālohitā pāṇātipātā paṭiviratā adinnādānā paṭiviratā kāmesumicchācārā paṭiviratā musāvādā paṭiviratā surāmerayamajjapamādaṭṭhānā paṭiviratā, te kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapannā devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyataṃ. Sace pana tesaṃ evaṃ bhavissati – ‘yāva mayaṃ dve vā tīṇi vā rattindivā dibbehi pañcahi kāmaguṇehi samappitā samaṅgībhūtā paricārema, atha mayaṃ pāyāsissa rājaññassa gantvā āroceyyāma – ‘‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. Api nu te āgantvā āroceyyuṃ – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho kassapa. Api hi mayaṃ, bho kassapa, ciraṃ kālaṅkatāpi bhaveyyāma. Ko panetaṃ bhoto kassapassa āroceti – ‘atthi devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā ‘evaṃdīghāyukā devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā. Na mayaṃ bhoto kassapassa saddahāma – ‘atthi devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā ‘evaṃdīghāyukā devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā’’ti.

417."Then, chieftain, I will question you in return. Answer as you see fit. Chieftain, a hundred human years equal a single day and night for the Tāvatiṃsa devas. Thirty such nights make a month, twelve such months make a year, and a thousand such divine years is the life-span of the Tāvatiṃsa devas. Those friends, advisors, relatives, and kin of yours who abstain from killing living beings, abstain from taking what is not given, abstain from sexual misconduct, abstain from lying, and abstain from liquor, wine, and intoxicants, the basis for negligence—at the breaking up of the body, after death, they are reborn in a good destination, a heavenly world, in association with the Tāvatiṃsa devas. Now, if they were to think: ‘Let us enjoy divine pleasures for two or three days and nights, and then go to the chieftain Pāyāsi and tell him: "Indeed there is another world; there are beings born spontaneously; there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds,"’ would they come and tell you: ‘Indeed there is another world; there are beings born spontaneously; there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds’?" "No, Master Kassapa. For by then, Master Kassapa, we would have long since died. And who would report to Master Kassapa: ‘There are Tāvatiṃsa devas,’ or ‘The Tāvatiṃsa devas live so long’? We do not believe Master Kassapa when he says: ‘There are Tāvatiṃsa devas,’ or ‘The Tāvatiṃsa devas live so long.’"

## Jaccandhaupamā

### The Simile of the Man Born Blind

418. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, rājañña, jaccandho puriso na passeyya kaṇha – sukkāni rūpāni, na passeyya nīlakāni rūpāni, na passeyya pītakāni [mañjeṭṭhakāni (syā.)] rūpāni, na passeyya lohitakāni rūpāni, na passeyya mañjiṭṭhakāni rūpāni, na passeyya samavisamaṃ, na passeyya tārakāni rūpāni, na passeyya candimasūriye. So evaṃ vadeyya – ‘natthi kaṇhasukkāni rūpāni, natthi kaṇhasukkānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi nīlakāni rūpāni, natthi nīlakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi pītakāni rūpāni, natthi pītakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi lohitakāni rūpāni, natthi lohitakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi mañjiṭṭhakāni rūpāni, natthi mañjiṭṭhakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi samavisamaṃ, natthi samavisamassa dassāvī. Natthi tārakāni rūpāni, natthi tārakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Natthi candimasūriyā, natthi candimasūriyānaṃ dassāvī. Ahametaṃ na jānāmi, ahametaṃ na passāmi, tasmā taṃ natthī’ti. Sammā nu kho so, rājañña, vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho kassapa. Atthi kaṇhasukkāni rūpāni, atthi kaṇhasukkānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Atthi nīlakāni rūpāni, atthi nīlakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī…pe… atthi samavisamaṃ, atthi samavisamassa dassāvī. Atthi tārakāni rūpāni, atthi tārakānaṃ rūpānaṃ dassāvī. Atthi candimasūriyā, atthi candimasūriyānaṃ dassāvī. ‘Ahametaṃ na jānāmi, ahametaṃ na passāmi, tasmā taṃ natthī’ti. Na hi so, bho kassapa, sammā vadamāno vadeyyā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, jaccandhūpamo maññe paṭibhāsi yaṃ maṃ tvaṃ evaṃ vadesi’’.

418."Just as, chieftain, a man born blind would not see black and white forms, would not see blue forms, would not see yellow forms, would not see red forms, would not see madder-red forms, would not see uneven ground, would not see the stars, would not see the moon and sun. He might say: 'There are no black and white forms, no one who sees black and white forms. There are no blue forms, no one who sees blue forms. There are no yellow forms, no one who sees yellow forms. There are no red forms, no one who sees red forms. There are no madder-red forms, no one who sees madder-red forms. There is no uneven ground, no one who sees uneven ground. There are no stars, no one who sees stars. There is no moon or sun, no one who sees the moon or sun. I do not know this, I do not see this, therefore it does not exist.' Would he be speaking rightly, chieftain?" "No, Master Kassapa. There are black and white forms, there are those who see black and white forms. There are blue forms, there are those who see blue forms. There are yellow forms, there are those who see yellow forms. There are red forms, there are those who see red forms. There are madder-red forms, there are those who see madder-red forms. There is uneven ground, there are those who see uneven ground. There are stars, there are those who see stars. There are the moon and sun, there are those who see the moon and sun. 'I do not know this, I do not see this, therefore it does not exist'—he would not be speaking rightly, Master Kassapa." "In the same way, chieftain, I think you appear to me like a man born blind when you say such things to me."

‘‘Ko panetaṃ bhoto kassapassa āroceti – ‘atthi devā tāvatiṃsā’’ti vā, ‘evaṃdīghāyukā devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā? Na mayaṃ bhoto kassapassa saddahāma – ‘atthi devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā ‘evaṃdīghāyukā devā tāvatiṃsā’ti vā’’ti. ‘‘Na kho, rājañña, evaṃ paro loko daṭṭhabbo, yathā tvaṃ maññasi iminā maṃsacakkhunā. Ye kho te rājañña samaṇabrāhmaṇā araññavanapatthāni pantāni senāsanāni paṭisevanti, te tattha appamattā ātāpino pahitattā viharantā dibbacakkhuṃ visodhenti. Te dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena imaṃ ceva lokaṃ passanti parañca satte ca opapātike. Evañca kho, rājañña, paro loko daṭṭhabbo; natveva yathā tvaṃ maññasi iminā maṃsacakkhunā. Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

"But who reports this to Master Kassapa—'There are the devas of Tāvatiṃsa' or 'The devas of Tāvatiṃsa are of such a long life'? We do not believe Master Kassapa when he says, 'There are the devas of Tāvatiṃsa' or 'The devas of Tāvatiṃsa are of such a long life.'" "It is not, chieftain, that the other world should be seen as you suppose with this fleshy eye. Those ascetics and brahmins, chieftain, who frequent remote forest and wilderness dwellings—secluded lodgings—there, diligent, ardent, and resolute, they purify the divine eye. With the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, they see this world and the next, and spontaneously arisen beings. Thus, chieftain, the other world should be seen—not as you suppose with this fleshy eye. By this line of reasoning too, chieftain, it may be like this for you: 'Indeed, there is another world, there are spontaneously arisen beings, there is the fruit and result of good and bad actions.'"

419. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo…pe… atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… yathā kathaṃ viya, rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idhāhaṃ, bho kassapa, passāmi samaṇabrāhmaṇe sīlavante kalyāṇadhamme jīvitukāme amaritukāme sukhakāme dukkhapaṭikūle. Tassa mayhaṃ, bho kassapa, evaṃ hoti – sace kho ime bhonto samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā evaṃ jāneyyuṃ – ‘ito no matānaṃ seyyo bhavissatī’ti. Idānime bhonto samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā visaṃ vā khādeyyuṃ, satthaṃ vā āhareyyuṃ, ubbandhitvā vā kālaṅkareyyuṃ, papāte vā papateyyuṃ. Yasmā ca kho ime bhonto samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā na evaṃ jānanti – ‘ito no matānaṃ seyyo bhavissatī’ti, tasmā ime bhonto samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā jīvitukāmā amaritukāmā sukhakāmā dukkhapaṭikūlā attānaṃ na mārenti [( ) natthi (syā. pī.)]. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

419."Although Master Kassapa speaks thus, still this is how it seems to me: 'Indeed, there is no other world, there are no spontaneously arisen beings, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.'" "Is there, chieftain, a way?" "Yes, Master Kassapa, there is a way." "How so, chieftain?" "Here, Master Kassapa, I see ascetics and brahmins who are virtuous, of good moral qualities, desiring to live, not desiring to die, desiring happiness, averse to suffering. It occurs to me, Master Kassapa: 'If these venerable ascetics and brahmins, virtuous and of good moral qualities, knew thus: "After death, it will be better for us," then these venerable ascetics and brahmins, virtuous and of good moral qualities, would consume poison, take up a weapon, hang themselves, or throw themselves off a cliff. But because these venerable ascetics and brahmins, virtuous and of good moral qualities, do not know thus: "After death, it will be better for us," therefore these venerable ascetics and brahmins, virtuous and of good moral qualities, do not kill themselves, desiring to live, not desiring to die, desiring happiness, averse to suffering.' This too, Master Kassapa, is a way why it seems to me thus: 'Indeed, there is no other world, there are no spontaneously arisen beings, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.'"

## Gabbhinīupamā

### The simile of the pregnant woman.

420. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, aññatarassa brāhmaṇassa dve pajāpatiyo ahesuṃ. Ekissā putto ahosi dasavassuddesiko vā dvādasavassuddesiko vā, ekā gabbhinī upavijaññā. Atha kho so brāhmaṇo kālamakāsi. Atha kho so māṇavako mātusapattiṃ [mātusapatiṃ (syā.)] etadavoca – ‘yamidaṃ, bhoti, dhanaṃ vā dhaññaṃ vā rajataṃ vā jātarūpaṃ vā, sabbaṃ taṃ mayhaṃ; natthi tuyhettha kiñci. Pitu me [pitu me santako (syā.)] bhoti, dāyajjaṃ niyyādehī’ti [nīyyātehīti (sī. pī.)]. Evaṃ vutte sā brāhmaṇī taṃ māṇavakaṃ etadavoca – ‘āgamehi tāva, tāta, yāva vijāyāmi. Sace kumārako bhavissati, tassapi ekadeso bhavissati; sace kumārikā bhavissati, sāpi te opabhoggā [upabhoggā (syā.)] bhavissatī’ti. Dutiyampi kho so māṇavako mātusapattiṃ etadavoca – ‘yamidaṃ, bhoti, dhanaṃ vā dhaññaṃ vā rajataṃ vā jātarūpaṃ vā, sabbaṃ taṃ mayhaṃ; natthi tuyhettha kiñci. Pitu me, bhoti, dāyajjaṃ niyyādehī’ti. Dutiyampi kho sā brāhmaṇī taṃ māṇavakaṃ etadavoca – ‘āgamehi tāva, tāta, yāva vijāyāmi. Sace kumārako bhavissati, tassapi ekadeso bhavissati; sace kumārikā bhavissati sāpi te opabhoggā [upabhoggā (syā.)] bhavissatī’ti. Tatiyampi kho so māṇavako mātusapattiṃ etadavoca – ‘yamidaṃ, bhoti, dhanaṃ vā dhaññaṃ vā rajataṃ vā jātarūpaṃ vā, sabbaṃ taṃ mayhaṃ; natthi tuyhettha kiñci. Pitu me, bhoti, dāyajjaṃ niyyādehī’ti.

420."Well then, chieftain, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile, some wise people understand the meaning of what is said. Once upon a time, chieftain, a certain brahmin had two wives. One had a son who was about ten or twelve years old, while the other was pregnant and close to giving birth. Then that brahmin passed away. Thereupon, the young boy said to his stepmother, 'Whatever wealth, grain, silver, or gold there is, all that is mine; you have no share here. Madam, hand over my father’s inheritance to me.' When this was said, the brahmin woman replied to the boy, 'Wait, dear, until I give birth. If it is a boy, he too will have a share. If it is a girl, she will also be at your disposal.' A second time, the young boy said to his stepmother, 'Whatever wealth, grain, silver, or gold there is, all that is mine; you have no share here. Madam, hand over my father’s inheritance to me.' A second time, the brahmin woman replied to the boy, 'Wait, dear, until I give birth. If it is a boy, he too will have a share. If it is a girl, she will also be at your disposal.' A third time, the young boy said to his stepmother, 'Whatever wealth, grain, silver, or gold there is, all that is mine; you have no share here. Madam, hand over my father’s inheritance to me.'

‘‘Atha kho sā brāhmaṇī satthaṃ gahetvā ovarakaṃ pavisitvā udaraṃ opādesi [uppātesi (syā.)] – ‘yāva vijāyāmi yadi vā kumārako yadi vā kumārikā’ti. Sā attānaṃ ceva jīvitañca gabbhañca sāpateyyañca vināsesi. Yathā taṃ bālā abyattā anayabyasanaṃ āpannā ayoniso dāyajjaṃ gavesantī, evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, bālo abyatto anayabyasanaṃ āpajjissasi ayoniso paralokaṃ gavesanto; seyyathāpi sā brāhmaṇī bālā abyattā anayabyasanaṃ āpannā ayoniso dāyajjaṃ gavesantī. Na kho, rājañña, samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā apakkaṃ paripācenti; api ca paripākaṃ āgamenti. Paṇḍitānaṃ attho hi, rājañña, samaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ sīlavantānaṃ kalyāṇadhammānaṃ jīvitena. Yathā yathā kho, rājañña, samaṇabrāhmaṇā sīlavanto kalyāṇadhammā ciraṃ dīghamaddhānaṃ tiṭṭhanti, tathā tathā bahuṃ puññaṃ pasavanti, bahujanahitāya ca paṭipajjanti bahujanasukhāya lokānukampāya atthāya hitāya sukhāya devamanussānaṃ. Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

"Then that brahmin woman took a knife, entered her chamber, and cut open her belly, in order to give birth, whether it be a boy or a girl. Thus, she destroyed herself, her life, the fetus, and her possessions. Just as that foolish, unwise woman met with ruin and disaster by seeking an inheritance in the wrong way, so too, chieftain, you—foolish and unwise—will meet with ruin and disaster by seeking the next world in the wrong way, just like that foolish, unwise brahmin woman who sought an inheritance in the wrong way. Chieftain, virtuous ascetics and brahmins of good character do not ripen what is unripe; rather, they await what is ripe. Chieftain, the life of virtuous ascetics and brahmins of good character is indeed beneficial to the wise. The longer, chieftain, virtuous ascetics and brahmins of good character remain in the world for a long time, the more merit they generate. They act for the welfare and happiness of many, out of compassion for the world, for the benefit, welfare, and happiness of gods and humans. By this method too, chieftain, you should come to understand: 'Indeed, there is a next world, there are spontaneously reborn beings, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.'

421. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo…pe… atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… yathā kathaṃ viya, rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, purisā coraṃ āgucāriṃ gahetvā dassenti – ‘ayaṃ te, bhante, coro āgucārī; imassa yaṃ icchasi, taṃ daṇḍaṃ paṇehī’ti. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ jīvantaṃyeva kumbhiyā pakkhipitvā mukhaṃ pidahitvā allena cammena onandhitvā allāya mattikāya bahalāvalepanaṃ [bahalavilepanaṃ (syā. ka.)] karitvā uddhanaṃ āropetvā aggiṃ dethā’ti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā taṃ purisaṃ jīvantaṃyeva kumbhiyā pakkhipitvā mukhaṃ pidahitvā allena cammena onandhitvā allāya mattikāya bahalāvalepanaṃ karitvā uddhanaṃ āropetvā aggiṃ denti. Yadā mayaṃ jānāma ‘kālaṅkato so puriso’ti, atha naṃ kumbhiṃ oropetvā ubbhinditvā mukhaṃ vivaritvā saṇikaṃ nillokema [vilokema (syā.)] – ‘appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Nevassa mayaṃ jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passāma. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

421."Although you, Venerable Kassapa, say this, still it occurs to me thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no spontaneously reborn beings, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’"
"Is there, chieftain, any reasoning...?"
"There is, Venerable Kassapa, a reasoning... How is that, chieftain?"
"Here, Venerable Kassapa, men bring me a thief, a wrongdoer, saying, ‘This, lord, is a thief, a wrongdoer; impose on him whatever punishment you wish.’ Then I say to them, ‘Well then, sirs, take this man, put him alive into a jar, close the mouth, cover it with a wet hide, smear it thickly with wet clay, place it on a furnace, and light a fire.’ They agree, ‘Very well,’ and taking that man, they put him alive into a jar, close the mouth, cover it with a wet hide, smear it thickly with wet clay, place it on a furnace, and light a fire. When we know that the man has died, we take down the jar, break it open, and carefully look to the mouth, thinking, ‘Perhaps we might see his life escaping.’ But we do not see his life escaping.
"This, Venerable Kassapa, is the reasoning by which it occurs to me thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no spontaneously reborn beings, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’"

## Supinakaupamā

### The Simile of the Dream

422. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, taññevettha paṭipucchissāmi, yathā te khameyya, tathā naṃ byākareyyāsi. Abhijānāsi no tvaṃ, rājañña, divā seyyaṃ upagato supinakaṃ passitā ārāmarāmaṇeyyakaṃ vanarāmaṇeyyakaṃ bhūmirāmaṇeyyakaṃ pokkharaṇīrāmaṇeyyaka’’nti? ‘‘Abhijānāmahaṃ, bho kassapa, divāseyyaṃ upagato supinakaṃ passitā ārāmarāmaṇeyyakaṃ vanarāmaṇeyyakaṃ bhūmirāmaṇeyyakaṃ pokkharaṇīrāmaṇeyyaka’’nti. ‘‘Rakkhanti taṃ tamhi samaye khujjāpi vāmanakāpi velāsikāpi [celāvikāpi (syā.), keḷāyikāpi (sī.)] komārikāpī’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho kassapa, rakkhanti maṃ tamhi samaye khujjāpi vāmanakāpi velāsikāpi [celāvikāpi (syā.), keḷāyikāpi (sī.)] komārikāpī’’ti. ‘‘Api nu tā tuyhaṃ jīvaṃ passanti pavisantaṃ vā nikkhamantaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ, bho kassapa’’. ‘‘Tā hi nāma, rājañña, tuyhaṃ jīvantassa jīvantiyo jīvaṃ na passissanti pavisantaṃ vā nikkhamantaṃ vā. Kiṃ pana tvaṃ kālaṅkatassa jīvaṃ passissasi pavisantaṃ vā nikkhamantaṃ vā. Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

422."Well then, prince, I will ask you about that very thing. Answer as seems fit to you. Do you recall, prince, having gone to sleep during the day and seeing in a dream delightful parks, delightful forests, delightful grounds, and delightful lotus ponds?" "Yes, Master Kassapa, I recall having gone to sleep during the day and seeing in a dream delightful parks, delightful forests, delightful grounds, and delightful lotus ponds." "At that time were you guarded by hunchbacks, dwarfs, sportive women, or young women?" "Yes, Master Kassapa, at that time I was guarded by hunchbacks, dwarfs, sportive women, and young women." "But did they see your life entering or leaving?" "No, Master Kassapa." "Well then, prince, if those living persons did not see the life of a living person like you entering or leaving, how then will you see the life of a dead person entering or leaving? By this line of reasoning too, prince, let it be thus for you: ‘Indeed, there is another world, there are beings reborn spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’"

423. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo…pe… ‘‘atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… yathā kathaṃ viya rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, purisā coraṃ āgucāriṃ gahetvā dassenti – ‘ayaṃ te, bhante, coro āgucārī; imassa yaṃ icchasi, taṃ daṇḍaṃ paṇehī’ti. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ jīvantaṃyeva tulāya tuletvā jiyāya anassāsakaṃ māretvā punadeva tulāya tulethā’ti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā taṃ purisaṃ jīvantaṃyeva tulāya tuletvā jiyāya anassāsakaṃ māretvā punadeva tulāya tulenti. Yadā so jīvati, tadā lahutaro ca hoti mudutaro ca kammaññataro ca. Yadā pana so kālaṅkato hoti tadā garutaro ca hoti patthinnataro ca akammaññataro ca. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

423."Even though Master Kassapa says this, still it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’" "But is there, prince, a way by which it occurs to you that there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds?" "There is, Master Kassapa, a way by which it occurs to me that there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds." "How is that, prince?" "Here, Master Kassapa, men bring me a thief caught in the act, saying: ‘Sir, this is a thief caught in wrongdoing. Impose on him whatever punishment you wish.’ I say to them: ‘Well then, sirs, weigh this man alive on the scales, strangle him with a bowstring until he is breathless, and then weigh him again.’ They agree and weigh the man alive, strangle him with a bowstring until he is breathless, and then weigh him again. While he is alive, he is lighter, softer, and more workable. But when he is dead, he is heavier, stiffer, and less workable. This too, Master Kassapa, is a line of reasoning by which it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’"

## Santattaayoguḷaupamā

### The Simile of the Heated Iron Ball

424. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Seyyathāpi, rājañña, puriso divasaṃ santattaṃ ayoguḷaṃ ādittaṃ sampajjalitaṃ sajotibhūtaṃ tulāya tuleyya. Tamenaṃ aparena samayena sītaṃ nibbutaṃ tulāya tuleyya. Kadā nu kho so ayoguḷo lahutaro vā hoti mudutaro vā kammaññataro vā, yadā vā āditto sampajjalito sajotibhūto, yadā vā sīto nibbuto’’ti? ‘‘Yadā so, bho kassapa, ayoguḷo tejosahagato ca hoti vāyosahagato ca āditto sampajjalito sajotibhūto, tadā lahutaro ca hoti mudutaro ca kammaññataro ca. Yadā pana so ayoguḷo neva tejosahagato hoti na vāyosahagato sīto nibbuto, tadā garutaro ca hoti patthinnataro ca akammaññataro cā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, rājañña, yadāyaṃ kāyo āyusahagato ca hoti usmāsahagato ca viññāṇasahagato ca, tadā lahutaro ca hoti mudutaro ca kammaññataro ca. Yadā panāyaṃ kāyo neva āyusahagato hoti na usmāsahagato na viññāṇasahagato tadā garutaro ca hoti patthinnataro ca akammaññataro ca. Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

424."Well then, prince, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile, some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Suppose, prince, a man were to weigh a red-hot iron ball, burning, blazing, and glowing, in a balance. Then at another time he were to weigh that same iron ball when it is cold and extinguished in the balance. Now, when is that iron ball lighter, softer, and more workable—when it is burning, blazing, and glowing, or when it is cold and extinguished?" "Master Kassapa, when that iron ball is accompanied by heat and air, burning, blazing, and glowing, then it is lighter, softer, and more workable. But when that iron ball is no longer accompanied by heat or air, when it is cold and extinguished, then it is heavier, stiffer, and less workable." "Just so, prince, when this body is accompanied by life, heat, and consciousness, then it is lighter, softer, and more workable. But when this body is no longer accompanied by life, heat, or consciousness, then it is heavier, stiffer, and less workable. By this line of reasoning too, prince, let it be thus for you: ‘Indeed, there is another world, there are beings reborn spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’"

425. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo…pe… atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… yathā kathaṃ viya rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, purisā coraṃ āgucāriṃ gahetvā dassenti – ‘ayaṃ te, bhante, coro āgucārī; imassa yaṃ icchasi, taṃ daṇḍaṃ paṇehī’ti. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ anupahacca chaviñca cammañca maṃsañca nhāruñca aṭṭhiñca aṭṭhimiñjañca jīvitā voropetha, appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Te me ‘sādhū’ti paṭissutvā taṃ purisaṃ anupahacca chaviñca…pe… jīvitā voropenti. Yadā so āmato hoti, tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ uttānaṃ nipātetha, appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Te taṃ purisaṃ uttānaṃ nipātenti. Nevassa mayaṃ jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passāma. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imaṃ purisaṃ avakujjaṃ nipātetha… passena nipātetha… dutiyena passena nipātetha… uddhaṃ ṭhapetha… omuddhakaṃ ṭhapetha… pāṇinā ākoṭetha… leḍḍunā ākoṭetha… daṇḍena ākoṭetha… satthena ākoṭetha… odhunātha sandhunātha niddhunātha, appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Te taṃ purisaṃ odhunanti sandhunanti niddhunanti. Nevassa mayaṃ jīvaṃ nikkhamantaṃ passāma. Tassa tadeva cakkhu hoti te rūpā, tañcāyatanaṃ nappaṭisaṃvedeti. Tadeva sotaṃ hoti te saddā, tañcāyatanaṃ nappaṭisaṃvedeti. Tadeva ghānaṃ hoti te gandhā, tañcāyatanaṃ nappaṭisaṃvedeti. Sāva jivhā hoti te rasā, tañcāyatanaṃ nappaṭisaṃvedeti. Sveva kāyo hoti te phoṭṭhabbā, tañcāyatanaṃ nappaṭisaṃvedeti. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

425."Even though Master Kassapa says this, still it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds.’" "But is there, prince, a way by which it occurs to you that there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds?" "There is, Master Kassapa, a way by which it occurs to me that there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit and result of good and bad deeds." "How is that, prince?" "Here, Master Kassapa, men bring me a thief caught in the act, saying: ‘Sir, this is a thief caught in wrongdoing. Impose on him whatever punishment you wish.’ I say to them: ‘Well then, sirs, without harming his skin, hide, flesh, sinews, bones, or marrow, take this man's life, and let us see if his life departs.’ They agree, and without harming his skin, hide, flesh, sinews, bones, or marrow, they take the man's life. When he is dead, I say: ‘Now, sirs, lay this man on his back, and let us see if his life departs.’ They lay the man on his back. We do not see his life departing. I say: ‘Now, sirs, lay this man face down... on his side... on the other side... hold him upright... hold him upside down... strike him with the hand... strike him with a clod... strike him with a stick... strike him with a sword... shake him, stir him, jolt him, and let us see if his life departs.’ They shake him, stir him, jolt him. We do not see his life departing. He has the same eye and those forms, but he does not experience that sense base. He has the same ear and those sounds, but he does not experience that sense base. He has the same nose and those smells, but he does not experience that sense base. He has the same tongue and those tastes, but he does not experience that sense base. He has the same body and those touches, but he does not experience that sense base. This too, Master Kassapa, is a line of reasoning by which it occurs to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.’"

## Saṅkhadhamaupamā

### The Simile of the Conch-Blower

426. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, aññataro saṅkhadhamo saṅkhaṃ ādāya paccantimaṃ janapadaṃ agamāsi. So yena aññataro gāmo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā majjhe gāmassa ṭhito tikkhattuṃ saṅkhaṃ upalāpetvā saṅkhaṃ bhūmiyaṃ nikkhipitvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Atha kho, rājañña, tesaṃ paccantajanapadānaṃ [paccantajānaṃ (sī.)] manussānaṃ etadahosi – ‘ambho kassa nu kho [etadahosi ‘‘kissa dukho (pī.)] eso saddo evaṃrajanīyo evaṃkamanīyo evaṃmadanīyo evaṃbandhanīyo evaṃmucchanīyo’ti. Sannipatitvā taṃ saṅkhadhamaṃ etadavocuṃ – ‘ambho, kassa nu kho eso saddo evaṃrajanīyo evaṃkamanīyo evaṃmadanīyo evaṃbandhanīyo evaṃmucchanīyo’ti. ‘Eso kho, bho, saṅkho nāma yasseso saddo evaṃrajanīyo evaṃkamanīyo evaṃmadanīyo evaṃbandhanīyo evaṃmucchanīyo’ti. Te taṃ saṅkhaṃ uttānaṃ nipātesuṃ – ‘vadehi, bho saṅkha, vadehi, bho saṅkhā’ti. Neva so saṅkho saddamakāsi. Te taṃ saṅkhaṃ avakujjaṃ nipātesuṃ, passena nipātesuṃ, dutiyena passena nipātesuṃ, uddhaṃ ṭhapesuṃ, omuddhakaṃ ṭhapesuṃ, pāṇinā ākoṭesuṃ, leḍḍunā ākoṭesuṃ, daṇḍena ākoṭesuṃ, satthena ākoṭesuṃ, odhuniṃsu sandhuniṃsu niddhuniṃsu – ‘vadehi, bho saṅkha, vadehi, bho saṅkhā’ti. Neva so saṅkho saddamakāsi.

426."Well then, chieftain, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Once upon a time, chieftain, a certain conch-blower took a conch and went to a border region. He approached a certain village, and standing in the middle of the village, he blew the conch three times, then set the conch down on the ground and sat to one side. Then, chieftain, the people of that border region thought: 'Sirs, what is this sound, so entrancing, so delightful, so intoxicating, so captivating, so enthralling?' Gathering around the conch-blower, they asked him: 'Sirs, what is this sound, so entrancing, so delightful, so intoxicating, so captivating, so enthralling?' He replied: 'This, sirs, is a conch. It is this that makes such a sound, so entrancing, so delightful, so intoxicating, so captivating, so enthralling.' They laid the conch on its back and said: 'Speak, conch, speak!' But the conch made no sound. They turned the conch upside down, laid it on its side, laid it on the other side, stood it upright, stood it upside down, struck it with their hands, struck it with a clod, struck it with a stick, struck it with a knife, shook it, jostled it, and hammered it—saying: 'Speak, conch, speak!' But the conch made no sound.

‘‘Atha kho, rājañña, tassa saṅkhadhamassa etadahosi – ‘yāva bālā ime paccantajanapadāmanussā, kathañhi nāma ayoniso saṅkhasaddaṃ gavesissantī’ti. Tesaṃ pekkhamānānaṃ saṅkhaṃ gahetvā tikkhattuṃ saṅkhaṃ upalāpetvā saṅkhaṃ ādāya pakkāmi. Atha kho, rājañña, tesaṃ paccantajanapadānaṃ manussānaṃ etadahosi – ‘yadā kira, bho, ayaṃ saṅkho nāma purisasahagato ca hoti vāyāmasahagato [vāyosahagato (syā.)] ca vāyusahagato ca, tadāyaṃ saṅkho saddaṃ karoti, yadā panāyaṃ saṅkho neva purisasahagato hoti na vāyāmasahagato na vāyusahagato, nāyaṃ saṅkho saddaṃ karotī’ti. Evameva kho, rājañña, yadāyaṃ kāyo āyusahagato ca hoti usmāsahagato ca viññāṇasahagato ca, tadā abhikkamatipi paṭikkamatipi tiṭṭhatipi nisīdatipi seyyampi kappeti, cakkhunāpi rūpaṃ passati, sotenapi saddaṃ suṇāti, ghānenapi gandhaṃ ghāyati, jivhāyapi rasaṃ sāyati, kāyenapi phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusati, manasāpi dhammaṃ vijānāti. Yadā panāyaṃ kāyo neva āyusahagato hoti, na usmāsahagato, na viññāṇasahagato, tadā neva abhikkamati na paṭikkamati na tiṭṭhati na nisīdati na seyyaṃ kappeti, cakkhunāpi rūpaṃ na passati, sotenapi saddaṃ na suṇāti, ghānenapi gandhaṃ na ghāyati, jivhāyapi rasaṃ na sāyati, kāyenapi phoṭṭhabbaṃ na phusati, manasāpi dhammaṃ na vijānāti. Imināpi kho te, rājañña, pariyāyena evaṃ hotu – ‘itipi atthi paro loko, atthi sattā opapātikā, atthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti [vipākoti, paṭhamabhāṇavāraṃ (syā.)].

"Then, chieftain, that conch-blower had this thought: 'How ignorant are these people of the borderlands, that they seek the sound of the conch so improperly!' While they were watching, he took the conch, blew it three times, and then took it away. Then, chieftain, those people of the borderlands thought: 'When, sirs, this conch is associated with a person, associated with effort, and associated with wind, then it makes a sound. But when this conch is not associated with a person, not associated with effort, and not associated with wind, then it does not make a sound.' In the same way, chieftain, when this body is associated with life, associated with heat, and associated with consciousness, then it moves forward, moves back, stands, sits, lies down, sees forms with the eye, hears sounds with the ear, smells odors with the nose, tastes flavors with the tongue, feels touches with the body, and knows mental phenomena with the mind. But when this body is not associated with life, not associated with heat, and not associated with consciousness, then it does not move forward, does not move back, does not stand, does not sit, does not lie down, does not see forms with the eye, does not hear sounds with the ear, does not smell odors with the nose, does not taste flavors with the tongue, does not feel touches with the body, and does not know mental phenomena with the mind. By this reasoning too, chieftain, you should be convinced: 'So indeed, there is another world, there are beings reborn spontaneously, there is fruit and result of good and bad deeds.'"

427. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho evaṃ me ettha hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti. ‘‘Atthi pana, rājañña, pariyāyo…pe… atthi, bho kassapa, pariyāyo…pe… yathā kathaṃ viya rājaññā’’ti? ‘‘Idha me, bho kassapa, purisā coraṃ āgucāriṃ gahetvā dassenti – ‘ayaṃ te, bhante, coro āgucārī, imassa yaṃ icchasi, taṃ daṇḍaṃ paṇehī’ti. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imassa purisassa chaviṃ chindatha, appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Te tassa purisassa chaviṃ chindanti. Nevassa mayaṃ jīvaṃ passāma. Tyāhaṃ evaṃ vadāmi – ‘tena hi, bho, imassa purisassa cammaṃ chindatha, maṃsaṃ chindatha, nhāruṃ chindatha, aṭṭhiṃ chindatha, aṭṭhimiñjaṃ chindatha, appeva nāmassa jīvaṃ passeyyāmā’ti. Te tassa purisassa aṭṭhimiñjaṃ chindanti, nevassa mayaṃ jīvaṃ passeyyāma. Ayampi kho, bho kassapa, pariyāyo, yena me pariyāyena evaṃ hoti – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti.

427."Although the venerable Kassapa speaks thus, still this is how it seems to me: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’" "Is there, chieftain, any way... is there, venerable Kassapa, any way... how might it be, chieftain?" "Here, venerable Kassapa, men seize a thief, a wrongdoer, and bring him before me, saying: ‘This, lord, is a thief, a wrongdoer. Inflict upon him whatever punishment you wish.’ Then I say to them: ‘Well then, sirs, cut the skin off this man, so that perhaps we may see his life.’ They cut the skin off that man, but we do not see his life. Then I say: ‘Well then, sirs, cut away this man’s skin... his flesh... his sinews... his bones... his bone marrow, so that perhaps we may see his life.’ They cut away his bone marrow, but we do not see his life. "This too, venerable Kassapa, is a way why it seems to me thus: ‘Indeed, there is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.’"

## Aggikajaṭilaupamā

### The Simile of the Fire-Worshipping Ascetic

428. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, aññataro aggiko jaṭilo araññāyatane paṇṇakuṭiyā sammati [vasati (sī. pī.)]. Atha kho, rājañña, aññataro janapade sattho [sattho janapadapadesā (sī.), janapado satthavāso (syā.), janapadapadeso (pī.)] vuṭṭhāsi. Atha kho so sattho [satthavāso (syā.)] tassa aggikassa jaṭilassa assamassa sāmantā ekarattiṃ vasitvā pakkāmi. Atha kho, rājañña, tassa aggikassa jaṭilassa etadahosi – ‘yaṃnūnāhaṃ yena so satthavāso tenupasaṅkameyyaṃ, appeva nāmettha kiñci upakaraṇaṃ adhigaccheyya’nti. Atha kho so aggiko jaṭilo kālasseva vuṭṭhāya yena so satthavāso tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā addasa tasmiṃ satthavāse daharaṃ kumāraṃ mandaṃ uttānaseyyakaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ. Disvānassa etadahosi – ‘na kho me taṃ patirūpaṃ yaṃ me pekkhamānassa manussabhūto kālaṅkareyya; yaṃnūnāhaṃ imaṃ dārakaṃ assamaṃ netvā āpādeyyaṃ poseyyaṃ vaḍḍheyya’nti. Atha kho so aggiko jaṭilo taṃ dārakaṃ assamaṃ netvā āpādesi posesi vaḍḍhesi. Yadā so dārako dasavassuddesiko vā hoti [ahosi (?)] dvādasavassuddesiko vā, atha kho tassa aggikassa jaṭilassa janapade kañcideva karaṇīyaṃ uppajji. Atha kho so aggiko jaṭilo taṃ dārakaṃ etadavoca – ‘icchāmahaṃ, tāta, janapadaṃ [nagaraṃ (ka.)] gantuṃ; aggiṃ, tāta, paricareyyāsi. Mā ca te aggi nibbāyi. Sace ca te aggi nibbāyeyya, ayaṃ vāsī imāni kaṭṭhāni idaṃ araṇisahitaṃ, aggiṃ nibbattetvā aggiṃ paricareyyāsī’ti. Atha kho so aggiko jaṭilo taṃ dārakaṃ evaṃ anusāsitvā janapadaṃ agamāsi. Tassa khiḍḍāpasutassa aggi nibbāyi.

428.“Well then, Rājañña, I shall give you a simile. For by means of a simile some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Once upon a time, Rājañña, a certain fire-worshipping ascetic lived in a leaf hut in a forest dwelling. Then, Rājañña, a caravan set out in a certain district. That caravan stayed near the hermitage of that fire-worshipping ascetic for one night and then departed. Then, Rājañña, that fire-worshipping ascetic thought: ‘Why don’t I go to where that caravan stayed? Perhaps I might find something useful there.’ So that fire-worshipping ascetic rose early and went to where the caravan had stayed. Upon arriving, he saw in that caravan site a small boy, weak, lying on his back, abandoned. Seeing him, he thought: ‘It would not be proper for me, while looking on, for a human being to pass away. Why don’t I take this child to my hermitage and raise him, nourish him, and bring him up?’ So that fire-worshipping ascetic took the child to his hermitage and raised him, nourished him, and brought him up. When the boy was about ten or twelve years old, the fire-worshipping ascetic had some business to attend to in the district. So he said to the boy: ‘I wish, dear, to go to the district. You, dear, should tend the fire. Do not let the fire go out. If the fire does go out, here is an axe, here are the firesticks, and here is the fire-drill. You should rekindle the fire and tend it.’ Having thus instructed the boy, the fire-worshipping ascetic went to the district. While he was absorbed in play, the fire went out.”

‘‘Atha kho tassa dārakassa etadahosi – ‘pitā kho maṃ evaṃ avaca – ‘‘aggiṃ, tāta, paricareyyāsi. Mā ca te aggi nibbāyi. Sace ca te aggi nibbāyeyya, ayaṃ vāsī imāni kaṭṭhāni idaṃ araṇisahitaṃ, aggiṃ nibbattetvā aggiṃ paricareyyāsī’’ti. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ aggiṃ nibbattetvā aggiṃ paricareyya’nti. Atha kho so dārako araṇisahitaṃ vāsiyā tacchi – ‘appeva nāma aggiṃ adhigaccheyya’nti. Neva so aggiṃ adhigacchi. Araṇisahitaṃ dvidhā phālesi, tidhā phālesi, catudhā phālesi, pañcadhā phālesi, dasadhā phālesi, satadhā [vīsatidhā (syā.)] phālesi, sakalikaṃ sakalikaṃ akāsi, sakalikaṃ sakalikaṃ karitvā udukkhale koṭṭesi, udukkhale koṭṭetvā mahāvāte opuni [ophuni (syā. ka.)] – ‘appeva nāma aggiṃ adhigaccheyya’nti. Neva so aggiṃ adhigacchi.

“Then the boy thought: ‘My father told me: “You, dear, should tend the fire. Do not let the fire go out. If the fire does go out, here is an axe, here are the firesticks, and here is the fire-drill. You should kindle fire and tend it.” Why don’t I kindle fire and tend it?’ So the boy chipped away at the fire-drill with the axe, thinking: ‘Perhaps I might get fire.’ But he did not get fire. He split the fire-drill into two pieces, three pieces, four pieces, five pieces, ten pieces, a hundred pieces; he reduced it to fragments; having reduced it to fragments, he pounded it in a mortar; and having pounded it, he winnowed it in a strong wind, thinking: ‘Perhaps I might get fire.’ But he did not get fire.”

‘‘Atha kho so aggiko jaṭilo janapade taṃ karaṇīyaṃ tīretvā yena sako assamo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā taṃ dārakaṃ etadavoca – ‘kacci te, tāta, aggi na nibbuto’ti? ‘Idha me, tāta, khiḍḍāpasutassa aggi nibbāyi. Tassa me etadahosi – ‘‘pitā kho maṃ evaṃ avaca aggiṃ, tāta, paricareyyāsi. Mā ca te, tāta, aggi nibbāyi. Sace ca te aggi nibbāyeyya, ayaṃ vāsī imāni kaṭṭhāni idaṃ araṇisahitaṃ, aggiṃ nibbattetvā aggiṃ paricareyyāsīti. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ aggiṃ nibbattetvā aggiṃ paricareyya’’nti. Atha khvāhaṃ, tāta, araṇisahitaṃ vāsiyā tacchiṃ – ‘‘appeva nāma aggiṃ adhigaccheyya’’nti. Nevāhaṃ aggiṃ adhigacchiṃ. Araṇisahitaṃ dvidhā phālesiṃ, tidhā phālesiṃ, catudhā phālesiṃ, pañcadhā phālesiṃ, dasadhā phālesiṃ, satadhā phālesiṃ, sakalikaṃ sakalikaṃ akāsiṃ, sakalikaṃ sakalikaṃ karitvā udukkhale koṭṭesiṃ, udukkhale koṭṭetvā mahāvāte opuniṃ – ‘‘appeva nāma aggiṃ adhigaccheyya’’nti. Nevāhaṃ aggiṃ adhigacchi’’’nti. Atha kho tassa aggikassa jaṭilassa etadahosi – ‘yāva bālo ayaṃ dārako abyatto, kathañhi nāma ayoniso aggiṃ gavesissatī’ti. Tassa pekkhamānassa araṇisahitaṃ gahetvā aggiṃ nibbattetvā taṃ dārakaṃ etadavoca – ‘evaṃ kho, tāta, aggi nibbattetabbo. Na tveva yathā tvaṃ bālo abyatto ayoniso aggiṃ gavesī’ti. Evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, bālo abyatto ayoniso paralokaṃ gavesissasi. Paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ, paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ, mā te ahosi dīgharattaṃ ahitāya dukkhāyā’’ti.

“Then that fire-worshipping ascetic, having completed that business in the district, approached his own hermitage. Upon arriving, he said to the boy: ‘I hope, dear, your fire has not gone out?’ ‘Here, father, while I was absorbed in play, the fire went out. Then I thought: “My father told me: ‘Dear, tend the fire. Do not let the fire go out. If the fire goes out, here is an axe, here are the firesticks, and here is the fire-drill. You should kindle fire and tend it.’ Why don’t I kindle fire and tend it?” So, father, I chipped at the fire-drill with the axe, thinking, “Perhaps I’ll get fire.” But I didn’t get fire. I split the fire-drill into two, three, four, five, ten, a hundred pieces; I reduced it to fragments; having reduced it to fragments, I pounded it in a mortar; and having pounded it, I winnowed it in a strong wind, thinking, “Perhaps I’ll get fire.” But I didn’t get fire.’ Then the fire-worshipping ascetic thought: ‘How foolish and unskilled this boy is, seeking fire improperly!’ While watching him, he took the fire-drill, kindled a fire, and said to the boy: ‘This, dear, is how fire should be kindled. Not like you, foolish and unskilled, seeking fire improperly.’ In the same way, Rājañña, you are foolish and unskilled, seeking the next world improperly. Relinquish this harmful wrong view, Rājañña. Relinquish it, lest it be for your harm and suffering for a long time.”

429. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho nevāhaṃ sakkomi idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjituṃ. Rājāpi maṃ pasenadi kosalo jānāti tirorājānopi – ‘pāyāsi rājañño evaṃvādī evaṃdiṭṭhī – ‘‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’ti. Sacāhaṃ, bho kassapa, idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjissāmi, bhavissanti me vattāro – ‘yāva bālo pāyāsi rājañño abyatto duggahitagāhī’ti. Kopenapi naṃ harissāmi, makkhenapi naṃ harissāmi, palāsenapi naṃ harissāmī’’ti.

429.“Although Venerable Kassapa speaks thus, still I am unable to relinquish this evil wrong view. King Pasenadi of Kosala knows me, and so do kings in other countries: ‘Prince Pāyāsi is one who speaks thus and holds such a view: “There is no other world, there are no beings reborn spontaneously, there is no fruit or result of good or bad deeds.”’ If I, Venerable Kassapa, were to relinquish this evil wrong view, there would be those who would say of me: ‘How foolish Prince Pāyāsi is, how unskilled, how poorly he has grasped it.’ I would carry it off out of anger, out of disparagement, out of rivalry.”

## Dve satthavāhaupamā

### The Simile of the Two Caravan Leaders.

430. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, mahāsakaṭasattho sakaṭasahassaṃ puratthimā janapadā pacchimaṃ janapadaṃ agamāsi. So yena yena gacchi, khippaṃyeva pariyādiyati tiṇakaṭṭhodakaṃ haritakapaṇṇaṃ. Tasmiṃ kho pana satthe dve satthavāhā ahesuṃ eko pañcannaṃ sakaṭasatānaṃ, eko pañcannaṃ sakaṭasatānaṃ. Atha kho tesaṃ satthavāhānaṃ etadahosi – ‘ayaṃ kho mahāsakaṭasattho sakaṭasahassaṃ; te mayaṃ yena yena gacchāma, khippameva pariyādiyati tiṇakaṭṭhodakaṃ haritakapaṇṇaṃ. Yaṃnūna mayaṃ imaṃ satthaṃ dvidhā vibhajeyyāma – ekato pañca sakaṭasatāni ekato pañca sakaṭasatānī’ti. Te taṃ satthaṃ dvidhā vibhajiṃsu [vibhajesuṃ (ka.)] ekato pañca sakaṭasatāni, ekato pañca sakaṭasatāni. Eko satthavāho bahuṃ tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca āropetvā satthaṃ payāpesi [pāyāpesi (sī. pī.)]. Dvīhatīhapayāto kho pana so sattho addasa purisaṃ kāḷaṃ lohitakkhaṃ [lohitakkhiṃ (syā.)] sannaddhakalāpaṃ [āsannaddhakalāpaṃ (syā.)] kumudamāliṃ allavatthaṃ allakesaṃ kaddamamakkhitehi cakkehi bhadrena rathena paṭipathaṃ āgacchantaṃ’, disvā etadavoca – ‘kuto, bho, āgacchasī’ti? ‘Amukamhā janapadā’ti. ‘Kuhiṃ gamissasī’ti? ‘Amukaṃ nāma janapada’nti. ‘Kacci, bho, purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho’ti? ‘Evaṃ, bho, purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho, āsittodakāni vaṭumāni, bahu tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca. Chaḍḍetha, bho, purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, lahubhārehi sakaṭehi sīghaṃ sīghaṃ gacchatha, mā yoggāni kilamitthā’ti.

430.“Well then, chieftain, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Once, chieftain, a great caravan of a thousand carts went from the eastern district to the western district. Wherever it went, it quickly used up the grass, firewood, water, and green foliage. Now, in that caravan there were two caravan leaders—one for five hundred carts, and another for five hundred carts. Then those caravan leaders thought: ‘This great caravan has a thousand carts; as we go, we quickly use up the grass, firewood, water, and green foliage. What if we were to divide this caravan into two parts—five hundred carts in one and five hundred in the other?’ So they divided the caravan into two parts: five hundred carts in one and five hundred in the other. One caravan leader loaded up much grass, firewood, and water and set out with his caravan. After traveling for two or three days, that caravan saw a man: dark, with red eyes, equipped with a bow and quiver, wearing a garland of water lilies, with wet clothes and wet hair, approaching on the opposite path in a fine chariot with mud-smeared wheels. Seeing him, the caravan leader asked: ‘From where, sir, do you come?’ ‘From such-and-such a district.’ ‘Where are you going?’ ‘To such-and-such a district.’ ‘I hope, sir, a great cloudburst has occurred in the wilderness ahead?’ ‘Yes, sir, a great cloudburst has occurred in the wilderness ahead. The cart tracks are awash, and there is plenty of grass, firewood, and water. Throw away, sirs, the old grass, firewood, and water, and travel swiftly with lightly loaded carts, so that your draft animals do not get exhausted.’

‘‘Atha kho so satthavāho satthike āmantesi – ‘ayaṃ, bho, puriso evamāha – ‘‘purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho, āsittodakāni vaṭumāni, bahu tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca. Chaḍḍetha, bho, purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, lahubhārehi sakaṭehi sīghaṃ sīghaṃ gacchatha, mā yoggāni kilamitthā’’ti. Chaḍḍetha, bho, purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, lahubhārehi sakaṭehi satthaṃ payāpethā’ti. ‘Evaṃ, bho’ti kho te satthikā tassa satthavāhassa paṭissutvā chaḍḍetvā purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni lahubhārehi sakaṭehi satthaṃ payāpesuṃ. Te paṭhamepi satthavāse na addasaṃsu tiṇaṃ vā kaṭṭhaṃ vā udakaṃ vā. Dutiyepi satthavāse… tatiyepi satthavāse… catutthepi satthavāse… pañcamepi satthavāse… chaṭṭhepi satthavāse… sattamepi satthavāse na addasaṃsu tiṇaṃ vā kaṭṭhaṃ vā udakaṃ vā. Sabbeva anayabyasanaṃ āpajjiṃsu. Ye ca tasmiṃ satthe ahesuṃ manussā vā pasū vā, sabbe so yakkho amanusso bhakkhesi. Aṭṭhikāneva sesāni.

“Then the caravan leader addressed the merchants, saying, ‘Sirs, this man says: “Ahead in the wilderness a great cloudburst has occurred. The cart tracks are awash, and there is plenty of grass, firewood, and water. Discard, sirs, the old grass, firewood, and water. Travel swiftly with lightly loaded carts, lest your draft animals become exhausted.” Discard, sirs, the old grass, firewood, and water, and set out with lightly loaded carts.’ ‘Yes, sir,’ replied those merchants to the caravan leader. They discarded the old grass, firewood, and water and set out with lightly loaded carts. At the first encampment, they found no grass, firewood, or water. At the second encampment… At the third encampment… At the fourth encampment… At the fifth encampment… At the sixth encampment… At the seventh encampment, they found no grass, firewood, or water. They all met with ruin and disaster. Every human and animal in that caravan was devoured by that non-human yaksha. Only bones remained.

‘‘Yadā aññāsi dutiyo satthavāho – ‘bahunikkhanto kho, bho, dāni so sattho’ti bahuṃ tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca āropetvā satthaṃ payāpesi. Dvīhatīhapayāto kho pana so sattho addasa purisaṃ kāḷaṃ lohitakkhaṃ sannaddhakalāpaṃ kumudamāliṃ allavatthaṃ allakesaṃ kaddamamakkhitehi cakkehi bhadrena rathena paṭipathaṃ āgacchantaṃ, disvā etadavoca – ‘kuto, bho, āgacchasī’ti? ‘Amukamhā janapadā’ti. ‘Kuhiṃ gamissasī’ti? ‘Amukaṃ nāma janapada’nti. ‘Kacci, bho, purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho’ti? ‘Evaṃ, bho, purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho. Āsittodakāni vaṭumāni, bahu tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca. Chaḍḍetha, bho, purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, lahubhārehi sakaṭehi sīghaṃ sīghaṃ gacchatha, mā yoggāni kilamitthā’ti.

“When the second caravan leader realized, ‘Sir, that caravan has now gone far ahead,’ he loaded up the caravan with much grass, firewood, and water, and set out. After traveling for two or three days, that caravan saw a man: dark, with red eyes, equipped with a bow and quiver, wearing a garland of water lilies, with wet clothes and wet hair, approaching on the opposite path in a fine chariot with mud-smeared wheels. Seeing him, he asked: ‘From where, sir, do you come?’ ‘From such-and-such a district.’ ‘Where are you going?’ ‘To such-and-such a district.’ ‘I hope, sir, a great cloudburst has occurred in the wilderness ahead?’ ‘Yes, sir, a great cloudburst has occurred in the wilderness ahead. The cart tracks are awash, and there is plenty of grass, firewood, and water. Throw away, sirs, the old grass, firewood, and water, and travel swiftly with lightly loaded carts, so that your draft animals do not get exhausted.’

‘‘Atha kho so satthavāho satthike āmantesi – ‘ayaṃ, bho, ‘‘puriso evamāha – purato kantāre mahāmegho abhippavuṭṭho, āsittodakāni vaṭumāni, bahu tiṇañca kaṭṭhañca udakañca. Chaḍḍetha, bho, purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, lahubhārehi sakaṭehi sīghaṃ sīghaṃ gacchatha; mā yoggāni kilamitthā’’ti. Ayaṃ bho puriso neva amhākaṃ mitto, na ñātisālohito, kathaṃ mayaṃ imassa saddhāya gamissāma. Na vo chaḍḍetabbāni purāṇāni tiṇāni kaṭṭhāni udakāni, yathābhatena bhaṇḍena satthaṃ payāpetha. Na no purāṇaṃ chaḍḍessāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ, bho’ti kho te satthikā tassa satthavāhassa paṭissutvā yathābhatena bhaṇḍena satthaṃ payāpesuṃ. Te paṭhamepi satthavāse na addasaṃsu tiṇaṃ vā kaṭṭhaṃ vā udakaṃ vā. Dutiyepi satthavāse… tatiyepi satthavāse… catutthepi satthavāse… pañcamepi satthavāse… chaṭṭhepi satthavāse… sattamepi satthavāse na addasaṃsu tiṇaṃ vā kaṭṭhaṃ vā udakaṃ vā. Tañca satthaṃ addasaṃsu anayabyasanaṃ āpannaṃ. Ye ca tasmiṃ satthepi ahesuṃ manussā vā pasū vā, tesañca aṭṭhikāneva addasaṃsu tena yakkhena amanussena bhakkhitānaṃ.

“Then the caravan leader addressed the merchants: ‘Sirs, this man says, “Ahead in the wilderness a great cloudburst has occurred. The cart tracks are awash, and there is much grass, firewood, and water. Discard, sirs, the old grass, firewood, and water. Travel swiftly with lightly loaded carts, so that your draft animals do not get exhausted.” But this man is neither our friend nor our relative. How can we go by his word? You must not discard the old grass, firewood, and water. Set out with your caravan with the goods as you have them. We will not discard our old supplies.’ ‘Yes, sir,’ replied the merchants to the caravan leader, and they set out with their caravan with the goods as they had them. At the first encampment, they found no grass, firewood, or water. At the second encampment… At the third encampment… At the fourth encampment… At the fifth encampment… At the sixth encampment… At the seventh encampment, they found no grass, firewood, or water. And they saw that caravan had met with ruin and disaster. They saw only the bones of the people and animals in that caravan, devoured by that non-human yaksha.

‘‘Atha kho so satthavāho satthike āmantesi – ‘ayaṃ kho, bho, sattho anayabyasanaṃ āpanno, yathā taṃ tena bālena satthavāhena pariṇāyakena. Tena hi, bho, yānamhākaṃ satthe appasārāni paṇiyāni, tāni chaḍḍetvā, yāni imasmiṃ satthe mahāsārāni paṇiyāni, tāni ādiyathā’ti. ‘Evaṃ, bho’ti kho te satthikā tassa satthavāhassa paṭissutvā yāni sakasmiṃ satthe appasārāni paṇiyāni, tāni chaḍḍetvā yāni tasmiṃ satthe mahāsārāni paṇiyāni, tāni ādiyitvā sotthinā taṃ kantāraṃ nitthariṃsu, yathā taṃ paṇḍitena satthavāhena pariṇāyakena. Evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, bālo abyatto anayabyasanaṃ āpajjissasi ayoniso paralokaṃ gavesanto seyyathāpi so purimo satthavāho. Yepi tava [te (ka.)] sotabbaṃ saddhātabbaṃ [saddahātabbaṃ (pī. ka.)] maññissanti, tepi anayabyasanaṃ āpajjissanti, seyyathāpi te satthikā. Paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ; paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ. Mā te ahosi dīgharattaṃ ahitāya dukkhāyā’’ti.

“Then the caravan leader addressed the merchants: ‘Sirs, this caravan has met with ruin and disaster, as was to be expected with that foolish caravan leader as guide. So now, sirs, discard the goods of little value in your caravan and take up those of great value in this caravan.’ ‘Yes, sir,’ replied those merchants to the caravan leader. Having discarded the goods of little value in their own caravan and taken up those of great value in that caravan, they safely crossed that desert, as was to be expected with a wise caravan leader as guide. In the same way, chieftain, you—being foolish and unskilled—will meet with ruin and disaster by improperly seeking the next world, just like that first caravan leader. And those who consider you worth listening to and trusting will also meet with ruin and disaster, like those merchants. Relinquish this evil view, chieftain; relinquish this evil view. May it not be for your long-term harm and suffering.”

431. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho nevāhaṃ sakkomi idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjituṃ. Rājāpi maṃ pasenadi kosalo jānāti tirorājānopi – ‘pāyāsi rājañño evaṃvādī evaṃdiṭṭhī – ‘‘itipi natthi paro loko…pe… vipāko’’’ti. Sacāhaṃ, bho kassapa, idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjissāmi, bhavissanti me vattāro – ‘yāva bālo pāyāsi rājañño, abyatto duggahitagāhī’ti. Kopenapi naṃ harissāmi, makkhenapi naṃ harissāmi, palāsenapi naṃ harissāmī’’ti.

431.“Although you, Venerable Kassapa, speak thus, still I am not able to relinquish this evil view. King Pasenadi of Kosala knows me, and the tributary kings also know me—‘Prince Pāyāsi holds such a view, such a belief: “There is no other world... no result of actions.”’ If I, Venerable Kassapa, were to relinquish this evil view, there would be those who would say of me, ‘How foolish is Prince Pāyāsi, inexperienced and grasping wrongly!’ I would hold onto it out of anger; I would hold onto it out of spite; I would hold onto it out of envy.”

## Gūthabhārikaupamā

### The Simile of the Dung-Carrier

432. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi. Upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, aññataro sūkaraposako puriso sakamhā gāmā aññaṃ gāmaṃ agamāsi. Tattha addasa pahūtaṃ sukkhagūthaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ. Disvānassa etadahosi – ‘ayaṃ kho pahuto sukkhagūtho chaḍḍito, mama ca sūkarabhattaṃ [sūkarānaṃ bhakkho (syā.)]; yaṃnūnāhaṃ ito sukkhagūthaṃ hareyya’nti. So uttarāsaṅgaṃ pattharitvā pahūtaṃ sukkhagūthaṃ ākiritvā bhaṇḍikaṃ bandhitvā sīse ubbāhetvā [uccāropetvā (ka. sī. ka.)] agamāsi. Tassa antarāmagge mahāakālamegho pāvassi. So uggharantaṃ paggharantaṃ yāva agganakhā gūthena makkhito gūthabhāraṃ ādāya agamāsi. Tamenaṃ manussā disvā evamāhaṃsu – ‘kacci no tvaṃ, bhaṇe, ummatto, kacci viceto, kathañhi nāma uggharantaṃ paggharantaṃ yāva agganakhā gūthena makkhito gūthabhāraṃ harissasī’ti. ‘Tumhe khvettha, bhaṇe, ummattā, tumhe vicetā, tathā hi pana me sūkarabhatta’nti. Evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, gūthabhārikūpamo [gūthahārikūpamo (sī. pī.)] maññe paṭibhāsi. Paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ. Paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ. Mā te ahosi dīgharattaṃ ahitāya dukkhāyā’’ti.

432."Well then, prince, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile some discerning people understand the meaning of what is said. Once upon a time, prince, a pig-keeper went from his own village to another village. There he saw a large heap of dried dung discarded. Seeing it, he thought: 'Here is a large heap of dried dung discarded, and it is food for my pigs; why don’t I carry away this dried dung?' So he spread out his upper robe, gathered up a large heap of dried dung, tied it into a bundle, hoisted it onto his head, and went on his way. On the way, a great storm cloud burst. It rained heavily. He trudged on, carrying the load of dung, smeared with dung oozing and dripping down to his fingertips. People who saw him said: 'Surely, friend, you must be mad! Surely you must be crazy! How can you carry a load of dung, smeared with dung oozing and dripping down to your fingertips?' 'You are the ones who are mad, friends, you are the ones who are crazy! For this is food for my pigs!' In the same way, prince, you seem to me like the dung-carrier in this simile. Give up this evil viewpoint, prince! Give up this evil viewpoint! Do not let it bring you harm and suffering for a long time!"

433. ‘‘Kiñcāpi bhavaṃ kassapo evamāha, atha kho nevāhaṃ sakkomi idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjituṃ. Rājāpi maṃ pasenadi kosalo jānāti tirorājānopi – ‘pāyāsi rājañño evaṃvādī evaṃdiṭṭhī – ‘‘itipi natthi paro loko…pe… vipāko’’ti. Sacāhaṃ, bho kassapa, idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjissāmi, bhavissanti me vattāro – ‘yāva bālo pāyāsi rājañño abyatto duggahitagāhī’ti. Kopenapi naṃ harissāmi, makkhenapi naṃ harissāmi, palāsenapi naṃ harissāmī’’ti.

433."Although you, Venerable Kassapa, speak thus, still I am not able to relinquish this evil viewpoint. King Pasenadi of Kosala knows me, and the vassal kings also know me: 'Prince Pāyāsi holds such a doctrine, such a view: "There is no other world...no result of actions."' If I, Venerable Kassapa, were to relinquish this evil viewpoint, there will be those who will say of me: 'How foolish is Prince Pāyāsi, unskilled, grasping things wrongly!' I will bear it with anger, I will bear it with disparagement, I will bear it with contempt."

## Akkhadhuttakaupamā

### The Simile of the Gambler

434. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi, upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, dve akkhadhuttā akkhehi dibbiṃsu. Eko akkhadhutto āgatāgataṃ kaliṃ gilati. Addasā kho dutiyo akkhadhutto taṃ akkhadhuttaṃ āgatāgataṃ kaliṃ gilantaṃ, disvā taṃ akkhadhuttaṃ etadavoca – ‘tvaṃ kho, samma, ekantikena jināsi, dehi me, samma, akkhe pajohissāmī’ti. ‘Evaṃ sammā’ti kho so akkhadhutto tassa akkhadhuttassa akkhe pādāsi. Atha kho so akkhadhutto akkhe visena paribhāvetvā taṃ akkhadhuttaṃ etadavoca – ‘ehi kho, samma, akkhehi dibbissāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ sammā’ti kho so akkhadhutto tassa akkhadhuttassa paccassosi. Dutiyampi kho te akkhadhuttā akkhehi dibbiṃsu. Dutiyampi kho so akkhadhutto āgatāgataṃ kaliṃ gilati. Addasā kho dutiyo akkhadhutto taṃ akkhadhuttaṃ dutiyampi āgatāgataṃ kaliṃ gilantaṃ, disvā taṃ akkhadhuttaṃ etadavoca –

434."Well then, prince, I will give you a simile. For by means of a simile, some discerning people understand the meaning of what is said. Once, prince, two dice-gamblers were gambling with dice. One dice-gambler repeatedly swallowed the losing throw. The second dice-gambler saw that dice-gambler repeatedly swallowing the losing throw and said, 'Good sir, you are consistently winning. Give me the dice; I will shake them.' 'Very well, good sir,' the first dice-gambler replied and gave him the dice. 'Coated with supreme venom, a man does not realize what he is swallowing; Swallow, swallow, wicked gambler, later it will be bitter for you.'

Sacāhaṃ, bho kassapa, idaṃ pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ paṭinissajjissāmi, bhavissanti me vattāro – ‘yāva bālo pāyāsi rājañño abyatto duggahitagāhī’ti. Kopenapi naṃ harissāmi, makkhenapi naṃ harissāmi, palāsenapi naṃ harissāmī’’ti.

King Pasenadi of Kosala knows me, and even outlying kings know me: 'Prince Pāyāsi holds such a doctrine, such a view: "There is no other world… no result of actions."’ If I, Venerable Kassapa, relinquish this evil viewpoint, there will be those who will say of me, ‘How foolish is Prince Pāyāsi, unskilled, grasping things wrongly!’ I will bear it with anger, with disparagement, with contempt."

## Sāṇabhārikaupamā

### The Simile of the Burden of Hemp

436. ‘‘Tena hi, rājañña, upamaṃ te karissāmi, upamāya midhekacce viññū purisā bhāsitassa atthaṃ ājānanti. Bhūtapubbaṃ, rājañña, aññataro janapado vuṭṭhāsi. Atha kho sahāyako sahāyakaṃ āmantesi – ‘āyāma, samma, yena so janapado tenupasaṅkamissāma, appeva nāmettha kiñci dhanaṃ adhigaccheyyāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ sammā’ti kho sahāyako sahāyakassa paccassosi. Te yena so janapado, yena aññataraṃ gāmapaṭṭaṃ [gāmapajjaṃ (syā.), gāmapattaṃ (sī.)] tenupasaṅkamiṃsu, tattha addasaṃsu pahūtaṃ sāṇaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā sahāyako sahāyakaṃ āmantesi – ‘idaṃ kho, samma, pahūtaṃ sāṇaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, tena hi, samma, tvañca sāṇabhāraṃ bandha, ahañca sāṇabhāraṃ bandhissāmi, ubho sāṇabhāraṃ ādāya gamissāmā’ti. ‘Evaṃ sammā’ti kho sahāyako sahāyakassa paṭissutvā sāṇabhāraṃ bandhitvā te ubho sāṇabhāraṃ ādāya yena aññataraṃ gāmapaṭṭaṃ tenupasaṅkamiṃsu. Tattha addasaṃsu pahūtaṃ sāṇasuttaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā sahāyako sahāyakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yassa kho, samma, atthāya iccheyyāma sāṇaṃ, idaṃ pahūtaṃ sāṇasuttaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ. Tena hi, samma, tvañca sāṇabhāraṃ chaḍḍehi, ahañca sāṇabhāraṃ chaḍḍessāmi, ubho sāṇasuttabhāraṃ ādāya gamissāmā’ti. ‘Ayaṃ kho me, samma, sāṇabhāro dūrābhato ca susannaddho ca, alaṃ me tvaṃ pajānāhī’ti. Atha kho so sahāyako sāṇabhāraṃ chaḍḍetvā sāṇasuttabhāraṃ ādiyi.

436."Well then, chieftain, I will give you a simile, for by means of a simile some wise people here understand the meaning of what is said. Once upon a time, chieftain, a certain region became deserted. Then one friend said to another, ‘Come, friend, let us go to that region. Perhaps we may find some wealth there.’ ‘Very well, friend,’ the other replied. They went to that region and arrived at the outskirts of a certain village. There they saw a large heap of discarded hemp. Seeing it, one friend said to the other, ‘Friend, here is a large heap of discarded hemp. So, friend, you tie up a load of hemp, and I will tie up a load of hemp. Then we can both take the loads and go.’ ‘Very well, friend,’ the other replied. Having tied up the loads, they both took them and went to the outskirts of another village. There they saw a large heap of discarded hemp thread. Seeing it, one friend said to the other, ‘Friend, for whatever purpose we might want hemp, here is a large heap of discarded hemp thread. So, friend, you discard your load of hemp, and I will discard my load of hemp. Then we can both take loads of hemp thread and go.’ ‘But friend, this load of hemp has been brought from afar and is well-bound. It is enough for me—you may do as you see fit.’ Then that friend discarded his load of hemp and took up a load of hemp thread.

‘‘Te yena aññataraṃ gāmapaṭṭaṃ tenupasaṅkamiṃsu. Tattha addasaṃsu pahūtā sāṇiyo chaḍḍitā, disvā sahāyako sahāyakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yassa kho, samma, atthāya iccheyyāma sāṇaṃ vā sāṇasuttaṃ vā, imā pahūtā sāṇiyo chaḍḍitā. Tena hi, samma, tvañca sāṇabhāraṃ chaḍḍehi, ahañca sāṇasuttabhāraṃ chaḍḍessāmi, ubho sāṇibhāraṃ ādāya gamissāmā’ti. ‘Ayaṃ kho me, samma, sāṇabhāro dūrābhato ca susannaddho ca, alaṃ me, tvaṃ pajānāhī’ti. Atha kho so sahāyako sāṇasuttabhāraṃ chaḍḍetvā sāṇibhāraṃ ādiyi.

"They went to some village outskirts. There they saw a large heap of discarded hemp cloth. Seeing it, one friend said to the other, ‘Friend, if we wanted hemp or hemp thread for any purpose, here is a large heap of discarded hemp cloth. So, friend, discard your load of hemp, and I will discard my load of hemp thread. Then we can both take a load of hemp cloth and go.’ ‘But friend, this load of hemp has been brought from afar and is well-secured. It is enough for me—you decide.’ Then that friend discarded his load of hemp thread and took up a load of hemp cloth."

‘‘Te yena aññataraṃ gāmapaṭṭaṃ tenupasaṅkamiṃsu. Tattha addasaṃsu pahūtaṃ khomaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā…pe… pahūtaṃ khomasuttaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ khomadussaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ kappāsaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ kappāsikasuttaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ kappāsikadussaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ ayaṃ [ayasaṃ (syā.)] chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ lohaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ tipuṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ sīsaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ sajjhaṃ [sajjhuṃ (sī. syā. pī.)] chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā… pahūtaṃ suvaṇṇaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ, disvā sahāyako sahāyakaṃ āmantesi – ‘yassa kho, samma, atthāya iccheyyāma sāṇaṃ vā sāṇasuttaṃ vā sāṇiyo vā khomaṃ vā khomasuttaṃ vā khomadussaṃ vā kappāsaṃ vā kappāsikasuttaṃ vā kappāsikadussaṃ vā ayaṃ vā lohaṃ vā tipuṃ vā sīsaṃ vā sajjhaṃ vā, idaṃ pahūtaṃ suvaṇṇaṃ chaḍḍitaṃ. Tena hi, samma, tvañca sāṇabhāraṃ chaḍḍehi, ahañca sajjhabhāraṃ [sajjhubhāraṃ (sī. syā. pī.)] chaḍḍessāmi, ubho suvaṇṇabhāraṃ ādāya gamissāmā’ti. ‘Ayaṃ kho me, samma, sāṇabhāro dūrābhato ca susannaddho ca, alaṃ me tvaṃ pajānāhī’ti. Atha kho so sahāyako sajjhabhāraṃ chaḍḍetvā suvaṇṇabhāraṃ ādiyi.

"They approached a certain village outskirts. There they saw a large amount of discarded flax, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded flax thread, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded flax cloth, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded cotton, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded cotton thread, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded cotton cloth, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded iron, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded bronze, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded tin, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded lead, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded silver, and upon seeing it… a large amount of discarded gold, one friend said to the other: ‘Friend, for whatever purpose we might want hemp, hemp thread, or hemp cloth; flax, flax thread, or flax cloth; cotton, cotton thread, or cotton cloth; iron, bronze, tin, lead, or silver—here is a large amount of discarded gold. Come, friend, you discard your hemp bundle, and I will discard my silver bundle. Let us both take bundles of gold and go.’ ‘But friend, this hemp bundle of mine was brought from afar and is well-tied. It is enough for me—you understand.’ Then that friend discarded his silver bundle and took up a bundle of gold."

‘‘Te yena sako gāmo tenupasaṅkamiṃsu. Tattha yo so sahāyako sāṇabhāraṃ ādāya agamāsi, tassa neva mātāpitaro abhinandiṃsu, na puttadārā abhinandiṃsu, na mittāmaccā abhinandiṃsu, na ca tatonidānaṃ sukhaṃ somanassaṃ adhigacchi. Yo pana so sahāyako suvaṇṇabhāraṃ ādāya agamāsi, tassa mātāpitaropi abhinandiṃsu, puttadārāpi abhinandiṃsu, mittāmaccāpi abhinandiṃsu, tatonidānañca sukhaṃ somanassaṃ adhigacchi. ‘‘Evameva kho tvaṃ, rājañña, sāṇabhārikūpamo maññe paṭibhāsi. Paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ; paṭinissajjetaṃ, rājañña, pāpakaṃ diṭṭhigataṃ. Mā te ahosi dīgharattaṃ ahitāya dukkhāyā’’ti.

"They went to their own village. There, the friend who took the load of hemp, his parents did not welcome him, nor his wife and children, nor his friends and companions, and he did not gain any happiness or joy because of it. But the friend who took the load of gold, his parents welcomed him, and his wife and children, and his friends and companions, and he gained happiness and joy because of it."Just so, chieftain, you seem to me like one carrying a load of hemp. Renounce this evil viewpoint, chieftain; renounce this evil viewpoint. Let it not lead to your long-term harm and suffering."

## Saraṇagamanaṃ

### Going for refuge.

437. ‘‘Purimeneva ahaṃ opammena bhoto kassapassa attamano abhiraddho. Api cāhaṃ imāni vicitrāni pañhāpaṭibhānāni sotukāmo evāhaṃ bhavantaṃ kassapaṃ paccanīkaṃ kātabbaṃ amaññissaṃ. Abhikkantaṃ, bho kassapa, abhikkantaṃ, bho kassapa. Seyyathāpi, bho kassapa, nikkujjitaṃ vā ukkujjeyya, paṭicchannaṃ vā vivareyya, mūḷhassa vā maggaṃ ācikkheyya, andhakāre vā telapajjotaṃ dhāreyya ‘cakkhumanto rūpāni dakkhantī’ti evamevaṃ bhotā kassapena anekapariyāyena dhammo pakāsito. Esāhaṃ, bho kassapa, taṃ bhavantaṃ gotamaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi, dhammañca, bhikkhusaṅghañca. Upāsakaṃ maṃ bhavaṃ kassapo dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gataṃ.

437."Even before, I was pleased and delighted by the simile of the venerable Kassapa. Yet, wishing to hear these varied displays of answering, I thought to engage the venerable Kassapa in debate. Excellent, venerable Kassapa, excellent! Just as one might set upright what was overturned, reveal what was hidden, show the path to one who is lost, or hold up a lamp in the darkness so that those with eyes may see forms—so too has the Dhamma been illuminated by the venerable Kassapa in many ways. I go for refuge, venerable Kassapa, to the Blessed Gotama, to the Dhamma, and to the Sangha of monks. May the venerable Kassapa remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge from this day forward, for as long as life lasts."

‘‘Icchāmi cāhaṃ, bho kassapa, mahāyaññaṃ yajituṃ, anusāsatu maṃ bhavaṃ kassapo, yaṃ mamassa dīgharattaṃ hitāya sukhāyā’’ti.

"I wish, venerable Kassapa, to perform a great sacrifice. May the venerable Kassapa instruct me, so that it will be for my long-term welfare and happiness."

## Yaññakathā

### The discussion of sacrifices.

438. ‘‘Yathārūpe kho, rājañña, yaññe gāvo vā haññanti ajeḷakā vā haññanti, kukkuṭasūkarā vā haññanti, vividhā vā pāṇā saṃghātaṃ āpajjanti, paṭiggāhakā ca honti micchādiṭṭhī micchāsaṅkappā micchāvācā micchākammantā micchāājīvā micchāvāyāmā micchāsatī micchāsamādhī, evarūpo kho, rājañña, yañño na mahapphalo hoti na mahānisaṃso na mahājutiko na mahāvipphāro. Seyyathāpi, rājañña, kassako bījanaṅgalaṃ ādāya vanaṃ paviseyya. So tattha dukkhette dubbhūme avihatakhāṇukaṇṭake bījāni patiṭṭhāpeyya khaṇḍāni pūtīni vātātapahatāni asāradāni asukhasayitāni. Devo ca na kālena kālaṃ sammādhāraṃ anuppaveccheyya. Api nu tāni bījāni vuddhiṃ virūḷhiṃ [viruḷhiṃ (moggalāne)] vepullaṃ āpajjeyyuṃ, kassako vā vipulaṃ phalaṃ adhigaccheyyā’’ti? ‘‘No hidaṃ [na evaṃ (syā. ka.)] bho kassapa’’. ‘‘Evameva kho, rājañña, yathārūpe yaññe gāvo vā haññanti, ajeḷakā vā haññanti, kukkuṭasūkarā vā haññanti, vividhā vā pāṇā saṃghātaṃ āpajjanti, paṭiggāhakā ca honti micchādiṭṭhī micchāsaṅkappā micchāvācā micchākammantā micchāājīvā micchāvāyāmā micchāsatī micchāsamādhī, evarūpo kho, rājañña, yañño na mahapphalo hoti na mahānisaṃso na mahājutiko na mahāvipphāro.

438."In such a sacrifice, chieftain, where cattle are slain, goats and sheep are slain, fowl and pigs are slain, and various creatures meet their destruction, and the recipients are of wrong view, wrong intention, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, wrong samādhi—such a sacrifice, chieftain, is not of great fruit, not of great benefit, not of great splendor, not of great expansion. Just as, chieftain, a farmer might take plow and seed and enter a forest. There, in a poor field, on bad ground, beset with stumps and thorns, he might sow seeds that are broken, rotten, withered by wind and sun, pithless, and badly stored. And if the rain god does not send down proper rain in due season. Would those seeds attain growth, increase, and abundance, or would the farmer reap abundant fruit?" "No, venerable Kassapa." "Even so, chieftain, in such a sacrifice where cattle are slain, goats and sheep are slain, fowl and pigs are slain, and various creatures meet their destruction, and the recipients are of wrong view, wrong intention, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, wrong samādhi—such a sacrifice, chieftain, is not of great fruit, not of great benefit, not of great splendor, not of great expansion."

‘‘Yathārūpe ca kho, rājañña, yaññe neva gāvo haññanti, na ajeḷakā haññanti, na kukkuṭasūkarā haññanti, na vividhā vā pāṇā saṃghātaṃ āpajjanti, paṭiggāhakā ca honti sammādiṭṭhī sammāsaṅkappā sammāvācā sammākammantā sammāājīvā sammāvāyāmā sammāsatī sammāsamādhī, evarūpo kho, rājañña, yañño mahapphalo hoti mahānisaṃso mahājutiko mahāvipphāro. Seyyathāpi, rājañña, kassako bījanaṅgalaṃ ādāya vanaṃ paviseyya. So tattha sukhette subhūme suvihatakhāṇukaṇṭake bījāni patiṭṭhapeyya akhaṇḍāni apūtīni avātātapahatāni sāradāni sukhasayitāni. Devo ca kālena kālaṃ sammādhāraṃ anuppaveccheyya. Api nu tāni bījāni vuddhiṃ virūḷhiṃ vepullaṃ āpajjeyyuṃ, kassako vā vipulaṃ phalaṃ adhigaccheyyā’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho kassapa’’. ‘‘Evameva kho, rājañña, yathārūpe yaññe neva gāvo haññanti, na ajeḷakā haññanti, na kukkuṭasūkarā haññanti, na vividhā vā pāṇā saṃghātaṃ āpajjanti, paṭiggāhakā ca honti sammādiṭṭhī sammāsaṅkappā sammāvācā sammākammantā sammāājīvā sammāvāyāmā sammāsatī sammāsamādhī, evarūpo kho, rājañña, yañño mahapphalo hoti mahānisaṃso mahājutiko mahāvipphāro’’ti.

"Moreover, chieftain, in such a sacrifice where no cows are killed, no goats or sheep are killed, no chickens or pigs are killed, and no various living beings meet their destruction, and where the recipients are of right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right samādhi—such a sacrifice, chieftain, is of great fruit, great benefit, great splendor, and great expansion. Just as, chieftain, a farmer might take a plow and seeds and enter a forest. There, in a well-prepared field, with fertile soil, free from stumps and thorns, he would plant the seeds—unbroken, unspoiled, unharmed by wind or sun, fresh, and well-stored. And if the god were to send down rain in due season, would those seeds grow, flourish, and thrive, and would the farmer reap an abundant harvest?" "Yes, venerable Kassapa." "Similarly, chieftain, in such a sacrifice where no cows are killed, no goats or sheep are killed, no chickens or pigs are killed, and no various living beings meet their destruction, and where the recipients are of right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right samādhi—such a sacrifice, chieftain, is of great fruit, great benefit, great splendor, and great expansion."

## Uttaramāṇavavatthu

### The Story of Uttara, the Youth

439. Atha kho pāyāsi rājañño dānaṃ paṭṭhapesi samaṇabrāhmaṇakapaṇaddhikavaṇibbakayācakānaṃ. Tasmiṃ kho pana dāne evarūpaṃ bhojanaṃ dīyati kaṇājakaṃ bilaṅgadutiyaṃ, dhorakāni [thorakāni (sī. pī.), corakāni (syā.)] ca vatthāni guḷavālakāni [guḷagāḷakāni (ka.)]. Tasmiṃ kho pana dāne uttaro nāma māṇavo vāvaṭo [byāvaṭo (sī. pī.)] ahosi. So dānaṃ datvā evaṃ anuddisati – ‘‘imināhaṃ dānena pāyāsiṃ rājaññameva imasmiṃ loke samāgacchiṃ, mā parasmi’’nti. Assosi kho pāyāsi rājañño – ‘‘uttaro kira māṇavo dānaṃ datvā evaṃ anuddisati – ‘imināhaṃ dānena pāyāsiṃ rājaññameva imasmiṃ loke samāgacchiṃ, mā parasmi’’’nti. Atha kho pāyāsi rājañño uttaraṃ māṇavaṃ āmantāpetvā etadavoca – ‘‘saccaṃ kira tvaṃ, tāta uttara, dānaṃ datvā evaṃ anuddisasi – ‘imināhaṃ dānena pāyāsiṃ rājaññameva imasmiṃ loke samāgacchiṃ, mā parasmi’’’nti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’. ‘‘Kissa pana tvaṃ, tāta uttara, dānaṃ datvā evaṃ anuddisasi – ‘imināhaṃ dānena pāyāsiṃ rājaññameva imasmiṃ loke samāgacchiṃ, mā parasmi’nti? Nanu mayaṃ, tāta uttara, puññatthikā dānasseva phalaṃ pāṭikaṅkhino’’ti? ‘‘Bhoto kho dāne evarūpaṃ bhojanaṃ dīyati kaṇājakaṃ bilaṅgadutiyaṃ, yaṃ bhavaṃ pādāpi [pādāsi (ka.)] na iccheyya samphusituṃ [chupituṃ (pī. ka.)], kuto bhuñjituṃ, dhorakāni ca vatthāni guḷavālakāni, yāni bhavaṃ pādāpi [acittikataṃ (ka.)] na iccheyya samphusituṃ, kuto paridahituṃ. Bhavaṃ kho panamhākaṃ piyo manāpo, kathaṃ mayaṃ manāpaṃ amanāpena saṃyojemā’’ti? ‘‘Tena hi tvaṃ, tāta uttara, yādisāhaṃ bhojanaṃ bhuñjāmi, tādisaṃ bhojanaṃ paṭṭhapehi. Yādisāni cāhaṃ vatthāni paridahāmi, tādisāni ca vatthāni paṭṭhapehī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’ti kho uttaro māṇavo pāyāsissa rājaññassa paṭissutvā yādisaṃ bhojanaṃ pāyāsi rājañño bhuñjati, tādisaṃ bhojanaṃ paṭṭhapesi. Yādisāni ca vatthāni pāyāsi rājañño paridahati, tādisāni ca vatthāni paṭṭhapesi.

439.Then the chieftain Pāyāsi set up a donation for ascetics, brahmins, the poor, travelers, beggars, and the destitute. At that donation, such food was given: rice with husk and gruel, and coarse cloths with tangled fringes. At that donation, there was a young man named Uttara who was in charge. Having given the gift, he made this dedication: “By this gift, may I meet only the chieftain Pāyāsi in this world, not in the next.” The chieftain Pāyāsi heard: “It seems the young man Uttara, having given a gift, makes this dedication: ‘By this gift, may I meet only the chieftain Pāyāsi in this world, not in the next.’” Then the chieftain Pāyāsi summoned the young man Uttara and said: “Is it true, dear Uttara, that having given a gift, you make this dedication: ‘By this gift, may I meet only the chieftain Pāyāsi in this world, not in the next’?” “Yes, sir.” “But why, dear Uttara, do you make such a dedication? Don’t we, dear Uttara, seek merit and expect the fruit of giving?” “Sir, at your donation, such food is given: rice with husk and gruel—which you yourself would not even wish to touch with your foot, let alone eat—and coarse cloths with tangled fringes, which you yourself would not even wish to touch with your foot, let alone wear. Yet you are dear and beloved to us. How could we bind the beloved with the unpleasant?” “Well then, dear Uttara, arrange food like what I eat and clothes like what I wear.” “Yes, sir,” replied the young man Uttara to the chieftain Pāyāsi. Then he arranged food like what Pāyāsi ate and clothes like what Pāyāsi wore.

440. Atha kho pāyāsi rājañño asakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā asahatthā dānaṃ datvā acittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā apaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā cātumahārājikānaṃ devānaṃ sahabyataṃ upapajji suññaṃ serīsakaṃ vimānaṃ. Yo pana tassa dāne vāvaṭo ahosi uttaro nāma māṇavo. So sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā sahatthā dānaṃ datvā cittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapajji devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyataṃ.

440.Then, after the chieftain Pāyāsi had given gifts carelessly, not with his own hand, without respect, and as if discarded, upon the breakup of the body, after death, he was reborn in the company of the gods of the Four Great Kings—in an empty Serīsaka mansion. But the young man named Uttara, who was diligent in his giving of alms, having given gifts carefully, with his own hand, with respect, and without discarding, upon the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in a good destination, the heavenly world, in the company of the gods of the Thirty-Three.

## Pāyāsidevaputto

### Pāyāsi the Deva

441. Tena kho pana samayena āyasmā gavampati abhikkhaṇaṃ suññaṃ serīsakaṃ vimānaṃ divāvihāraṃ gacchati. Atha kho pāyāsi devaputto yenāyasmā gavampati tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmantaṃ gavampatiṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ aṭṭhāsi. Ekamantaṃ ṭhitaṃ kho pāyāsiṃ devaputtaṃ āyasmā gavampati etadavoca – ‘‘kosi tvaṃ, āvuso’’ti? ‘‘Ahaṃ, bhante, pāyāsi rājañño’’ti. ‘‘Nanu tvaṃ, āvuso, evaṃdiṭṭhiko ahosi – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’’’ti? ‘‘Saccāhaṃ, bhante, evaṃdiṭṭhiko ahosiṃ – ‘itipi natthi paro loko, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko’ti. Api cāhaṃ ayyena kumārakassapena etasmā pāpakā diṭṭhigatā vivecito’’ti. ‘‘Yo pana te, āvuso, dāne vāvaṭo ahosi uttaro nāma māṇavo, so kuhiṃ upapanno’’ti? ‘‘Yo me, bhante, dāne vāvaṭo ahosi uttaro nāma māṇavo, so sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā sahatthā dānaṃ datvā cittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapanno devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyataṃ. Ahaṃ pana, bhante, asakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā asahatthā dānaṃ datvā acittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā apaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā cātumahārājikānaṃ devānaṃ sahabyataṃ upapanno suññaṃ serīsakaṃ vimānaṃ. Tena hi, bhante gavampati, manussalokaṃ gantvā evamārocehi – ‘sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ detha, sahatthā dānaṃ detha, cittīkataṃ dānaṃ detha, anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ detha. Pāyāsi rājañño asakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā asahatthā dānaṃ datvā acittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā apaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā cātumahārājikānaṃ devānaṃ sahabyataṃ upapanno suññaṃ serīsakaṃ vimānaṃ. Yo pana tassa dāne vāvaṭo ahosi uttaro nāma māṇavo, so sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā sahatthā dānaṃ datvā cittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapanno devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyata’’’nti.

441.At that time, Venerable Gavampati frequently went to the empty Serīsaka mansion for his daytime abiding. Then Pāyāsi the Deva approached Venerable Gavampati, paid homage to him, and stood to one side. Standing to one side, Venerable Gavampati said to him: “Who are you, friend?” “I, venerable sir, am Pāyāsi the chieftain.” “Didn’t you, friend, hold such a view: ‘There is no other world, there are no spontaneously reborn beings, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds’?” “Indeed, venerable sir, I held such a view: ‘There is no other world, there are no spontaneously reborn beings, there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds.’ But I was freed from this evil wrong view by the venerable Kumārakassapa.” “And that young man Uttara, who was diligent in your giving of alms—where has he been reborn?” “That young man Uttara, venerable sir, who was diligent in my giving of alms, having given alms carefully, with his own hands, with respect, and without discarding, after the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in a good destination, in the heavenly world, in companionship with the Tāvatiṃsa gods. But I, venerable sir, having given alms carelessly, not with my own hands, without respect, and as if discarded, after the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in companionship with the Four Great Kings in an empty Serīsaka mansion. Therefore, Venerable Gavampati, go to the human world and announce this: ‘Give alms carefully, give alms with your own hands, give alms with respect, give alms without discarding. Pāyāsi the chieftain, having given alms carelessly, not with his own hands, without respect, and as if discarded, after the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in companionship with the Four Great Kings in an empty Serīsaka mansion. But that young man Uttara, who was diligent in his giving of alms, having given alms carefully, with his own hands, with respect, and without discarding, after the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in a good destination, in the heavenly world, in companionship with the Tāvatiṃsa gods.’

Atha kho āyasmā gavampati manussalokaṃ āgantvā evamārocesi – ‘‘sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ detha, sahatthā dānaṃ detha, cittīkataṃ dānaṃ detha, anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ detha. Pāyāsi rājañño asakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā asahatthā dānaṃ datvā acittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā apaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā cātumahārājikānaṃ devānaṃ sahabyataṃ upapanno suññaṃ serīsakaṃ vimānaṃ. Yo pana tassa dāne vāvaṭo ahosi uttaro nāma māṇavo, so sakkaccaṃ dānaṃ datvā sahatthā dānaṃ datvā cittīkataṃ dānaṃ datvā anapaviddhaṃ dānaṃ datvā kāyassa bhedā paraṃ maraṇā sugatiṃ saggaṃ lokaṃ upapanno devānaṃ tāvatiṃsānaṃ sahabyata’’nti.

Then Venerable Gavampati, having come to the human world, announced this: “Give alms carefully, give alms with your own hands, give alms with respect, give alms without discarding. Pāyāsi the chieftain, having given alms carelessly, not with his own hands, without respect, and as if discarded, upon the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn into the company of the Four Great Kings in an empty Serīsaka mansion. But the young man named Uttara, who was diligent in his almsgiving, having given alms carefully, with his own hands, with respect, and without discarding, upon the breakup of the body, after death, was reborn in a good destination, in a heavenly world, into the company of the Tāvatiṃsa gods.”

## Pāyāsisuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ dasamaṃ.

### The Pāyāsi Sutta is concluded, the tenth.

## Mahāvaggo niṭṭhito.

### The Great Chapter is concluded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its summary:

Mahāpadāna nidānaṃ, nibbānañca sudassanaṃ;Janavasabha govindaṃ, samayaṃ sakkapañhakaṃ;Mahāsatipaṭṭhānañca, pāyāsi dasamaṃ bhave [satipaṭṭhānapāyāsi, mahāvaggassa saṅgaho (sī. pī.) satipaṭṭhānapāyāsi, mahāvaggoti vuccatīti (syā.)].

The Great Lineage Discourse, the Root Discourse, the Great Discourse on Nibbāna, the Clear-Sighted Discourse; Janavasabha, Govinda, the Assembly Discourse, Sakka’s Questions; the Great Discourse on the Foundations of Mindfulness, and Pāyāsi as the tenth.

## Mahāvaggapāḷi niṭṭhitā.

### The Great Chapter is concluded.
