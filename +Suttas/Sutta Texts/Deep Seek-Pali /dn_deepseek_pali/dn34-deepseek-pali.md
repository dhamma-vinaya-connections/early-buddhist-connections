# Dasuttarasuttaṃ

## The Dasuttara Sutta

350. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā campāyaṃ viharati gaggarāya pokkharaṇiyā tīre mahatā bhikkhusaṅghena saddhiṃ pañcamattehi bhikkhusatehi. Tatra kho āyasmā sāriputto bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘āvuso bhikkhave’’ti! ‘‘Āvuso’’ti kho te bhikkhū āyasmato sāriputtassa paccassosuṃ. Āyasmā sāriputto etadavoca –

350.Thus have I heard. At one time the Blessed One was dwelling at Campā on the banks of the Gaggarā Lotus Pond with a large Saṅgha of monks, with about five hundred monks. There Venerable Sāriputta addressed the monks: "Friends, monks," "Friend," those monks replied to Venerable Sāriputta. Venerable Sāriputta said this:

‘‘Dasuttaraṃ pavakkhāmi, dhammaṃ nibbānapattiyā;Dukkhassantakiriyāya, sabbaganthappamocanaṃ’’.

I shall proclaim the Dhamma in tenfold order, leading to Nibbana, for the ending of suffering, for the release from all fetters.

## Eko dhammo

### One Quality

351. ‘‘Eko, āvuso, dhammo bahukāro, eko dhammo bhāvetabbo, eko dhammo pariññeyyo, eko dhammo pahātabbo, eko dhammo hānabhāgiyo, eko dhammo visesabhāgiyo, eko dhammo duppaṭivijjho, eko dhammo uppādetabbo, eko dhammo abhiññeyyo, eko dhammo sacchikātabbo.

351."Friend, one quality is very helpful, one quality should be developed, one quality should be fully understood, one quality should be abandoned, one quality leads to decline, one quality leads to special attainment, one quality is difficult to penetrate, one quality should be brought about, one quality should be directly known, one quality should be realized."

(Ka) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo bahukāro? Appamādo kusalesu dhammesu. Ayaṃ eko dhammo bahukāro.

(Ka) "What is the one quality that is very helpful? Heedfulness in wholesome qualities. This one quality is very helpful.

(Kha) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo bhāvetabbo? Kāyagatāsati sātasahagatā. Ayaṃ eko dhammo bhāvetabbo.

(Kha) "What is the one quality that should be developed? Mindfulness of the body, accompanied by pleasure. This one quality should be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo pariññeyyo? Phasso sāsavo upādāniyo. Ayaṃ eko dhammo pariññeyyo.

(Ga) "What is the one quality that should be fully understood? Tainted contact that is liable to clinging. This one quality should be fully understood."

(Gha) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo pahātabbo? Asmimāno. Ayaṃ eko dhammo pahātabbo.

(Gha) "What is the one quality that should be abandoned? The conceit 'I am.' This is the one quality that should be abandoned."

(Ṅa) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo hānabhāgiyo? Ayoniso manasikāro. Ayaṃ eko dhammo hānabhāgiyo.

(Ṅa) "What is the one quality that leads to decline? Unwise attention. This is the one quality that leads to decline."

(Ca) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo visesabhāgiyo? Yoniso manasikāro. Ayaṃ eko dhammo visesabhāgiyo.

(Ca) "What is the one quality that leads to distinction? Proper attention. This one quality leads to distinction."

(Cha) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo duppaṭivijjho? Ānantariko cetosamādhi. Ayaṃ eko dhammo duppaṭivijjho.

(Cha) "What is the one quality that is difficult to penetrate? Immediate samādhi of mind. This one quality is difficult to penetrate."

(Ja) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo uppādetabbo? Akuppaṃ ñāṇaṃ. Ayaṃ eko dhammo uppādetabbo.

(Ja) "What is the one quality that should be brought about? Unshakable knowledge. This one quality should be brought about."

(Jha) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo abhiññeyyo? Sabbe sattā āhāraṭṭhitikā. Ayaṃ eko dhammo abhiññeyyo.

(Jha) "What is the one quality that should be directly known? All beings are sustained by nutriment. This one quality should be directly known."

(Ña) ‘‘katamo eko dhammo sacchikātabbo? Akuppā cetovimutti. Ayaṃ eko dhammo sacchikātabbo.

(Ña) "What is the one quality that should be realized? Unshakable liberation of mind. This one quality should be realized."

‘‘Iti ime dasa dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these ten qualities are actual, factual, true, not otherwise, and rightly awakened by the Tathagata."

## Dve dhammā

### Two Qualities

352. ‘‘Dve dhammā bahukārā, dve dhammā bhāvetabbā, dve dhammā pariññeyyā, dve dhammā pahātabbā, dve dhammā hānabhāgiyā, dve dhammā visesabhāgiyā, dve dhammā duppaṭivijjhā, dve dhammā uppādetabbā, dve dhammā abhiññeyyā, dve dhammā sacchikātabbā.

352."Two qualities are very helpful, two qualities should be developed, two qualities should be fully understood, two qualities should be abandoned, two qualities partake of decline, two qualities partake of distinction, two qualities are difficult to penetrate, two qualities should be brought about, two qualities should be directly known, two qualities should be realized."

(Ka) ‘‘katame dve dhammā bahukārā? Sati ca sampajaññañca. Ime dve dhammā bahukārā.

(Ka) "What two qualities are very helpful? Mindfulness and clear comprehension. These two qualities are very helpful."

(Kha) ‘‘katame dve dhammā bhāvetabbā? Samatho ca vipassanā ca. Ime dve dhammā bhāvetabbā.

Which two qualities should be developed? Serenity and insight. These two qualities should be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katame dve dhammā pariññeyyā? Nāmañca rūpañca. Ime dve dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) "Which two qualities are to be fully understood? Name and form. These two qualities are to be fully understood."

(Gha) ‘‘katame dve dhammā pahātabbā? Avijjā ca bhavataṇhā ca. Ime dve dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) "Which two qualities should be abandoned? Ignorance and craving for existence. These two qualities should be abandoned."

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame dve dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Dovacassatā ca pāpamittatā ca. Ime dve dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) "Which are the two qualities that lead to decline? Being difficult to discipline and having bad friends. These two qualities lead to decline.

(Ca) ‘‘katame dve dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Sovacassatā ca kalyāṇamittatā ca. Ime dve dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) "What are the two qualities that lead to distinction? Receptiveness to instruction and good friendship. These two qualities lead to distinction."

(Cha) ‘‘katame dve dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Yo ca hetu yo ca paccayo sattānaṃ saṃkilesāya, yo ca hetu yo ca paccayo sattānaṃ visuddhiyā. Ime dve dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

(Cha) "What are the two qualities that are difficult to penetrate? The cause and condition for the defilement of beings, and the cause and condition for the purification of beings. These two qualities are difficult to penetrate."

(Ja) ‘‘katame dve dhammā uppādetabbā? Dve ñāṇāni – khaye ñāṇaṃ, anuppāde ñāṇaṃ. Ime dve dhammā uppādetabbā.

(Ja) "What are the two qualities that should be brought about? Two knowledges: the knowledge of destruction and the knowledge of non-arising. These two qualities should be brought about."

(Jha) ‘‘katame dve dhammā abhiññeyyā? Dve dhātuyo – saṅkhatā ca dhātu asaṅkhatā ca dhātu. Ime dve dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(Jha) "Which two qualities should be directly known? Two elements: the conditioned element and the unconditioned element. These two qualities should be directly known."

(Ña) ‘‘katame dve dhammā sacchikātabbā? Vijjā ca vimutti ca. Ime dve dhammā sacchikātabbā.

(Ña) "Which two qualities are to be realized? Knowledge and liberation. These two qualities are to be realized."

‘‘Iti ime vīsati dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these twenty qualities are actual, factual, true, not otherwise, and rightly awakened by the Tathagata."

## Tayo dhammā

### Three qualities.

353. ‘‘Tayo dhammā bahukārā, tayo dhammā bhāvetabbā…pe… tayo dhammā sacchikātabbā.

353."Three qualities are very helpful, three qualities are to be developed… three qualities are to be realized."

(Ka) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā bahukārā? Sappurisasaṃsevo, saddhammassavanaṃ, dhammānudhammappaṭipatti. Ime tayo dhammā bahukārā.

(Ka) "Which three qualities are very helpful? Association with good persons, listening to the true Dhamma, and practice in accordance with the Dhamma. These three qualities are very helpful."

(Kha) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā bhāvetabbā? Tayo samādhī – savitakko savicāro samādhi, avitakko vicāramatto samādhi, avitakko avicāro samādhi. Ime tayo dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) "Which three qualities are to be developed? These three samādhis: samādhi with applied thought and sustained thought, samādhi without applied thought but with sustained thought, and samādhi without applied thought or sustained thought. These three qualities are to be developed."

(Ga) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā pariññeyyā? Tisso vedanā – sukhā vedanā, dukkhā vedanā, adukkhamasukhā vedanā. Ime tayo dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) "Which three qualities are to be fully understood? The three feelings: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. These three qualities are to be fully understood."

(Gha) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā pahātabbā? Tisso taṇhā – kāmataṇhā, bhavataṇhā, vibhavataṇhā. Ime tayo dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) "Which three qualities are to be abandoned? The three cravings: craving for sensual pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for non-existence. These three qualities are to be abandoned."

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Tīṇi akusalamūlāni – lobho akusalamūlaṃ, doso akusalamūlaṃ, moho akusalamūlaṃ. Ime tayo dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) "Which three qualities are conducive to decline? The three unwholesome roots: greed is an unwholesome root, hatred is an unwholesome root, and delusion is an unwholesome root. These three qualities are conducive to decline."

(Ca) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Tīṇi kusalamūlāni – alobho kusalamūlaṃ, adoso kusalamūlaṃ, amoho kusalamūlaṃ. Ime tayo dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) "Which three qualities partake of distinction? The three wholesome roots: non-greed is a wholesome root, non-hatred is a wholesome root, and non-delusion is a wholesome root. These three qualities partake of distinction."

(Cha) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Tisso nissaraṇiyā dhātuyo – kāmānametaṃ nissaraṇaṃ yadidaṃ nekkhammaṃ, rūpānametaṃ nissaraṇaṃ yadidaṃ arūpaṃ, yaṃ kho pana kiñci bhūtaṃ saṅkhataṃ paṭiccasamuppannaṃ, nirodho tassa nissaraṇaṃ. Ime tayo dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

(Cha) "Which three qualities are hard to penetrate? The three elements of deliverance: deliverance from sensual pleasures is renunciation; deliverance from forms is the formless; and whatever is existent, conditioned, and dependently arisen, its cessation is deliverance. These three qualities are hard to penetrate."

(Ja) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā uppādetabbā? Tīṇi ñāṇāni – atītaṃse ñāṇaṃ, anāgataṃse ñāṇaṃ, paccuppannaṃse ñāṇaṃ. Ime tayo dhammā uppādetabbā.

(Ja) "Which three qualities are to be produced? The three knowledges: knowledge of the past, knowledge of the future, and knowledge of the present. These three qualities are to be produced."

(Jha) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā abhiññeyyā? Tisso dhātuyo – kāmadhātu, rūpadhātu, arūpadhātu. Ime tayo dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(Jha) "Which three qualities are to be fully known? The three realms: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm. These three qualities are to be fully known."

(Ña) ‘‘katame tayo dhammā sacchikātabbā? Tisso vijjā – pubbenivāsānussatiñāṇaṃ vijjā, sattānaṃ cutūpapāte ñāṇaṃ vijjā, āsavānaṃ khaye ñāṇaṃ vijjā. Ime tayo dhammā sacchikātabbā.

(Ña) "Which three qualities are to be realized? The three knowledges: the knowledge of the recollection of past lives, the knowledge of the passing away and reappearance of beings, and the knowledge of the destruction of the taints. These three qualities are to be realized."

‘‘Iti ime tiṃsa dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these thirty qualities are actual, factual, true, unfalsified, not otherwise, rightly fully awakened by the Tathagata."

## Cattāro dhammā

### Four qualities.

354. ‘‘Cattāro dhammā bahukārā, cattāro dhammā bhāvetabbā…pe… cattāro dhammā sacchikātabbā.

354."Four qualities are very helpful, four qualities are to be developed… four qualities are to be realized."

(Ka) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā bahukārā? Cattāri cakkāni – patirūpadesavāso, sappurisūpanissayo [sappurisupassayo (syā. kaṃ.)], attasammāpaṇidhi, pubbe ca katapuññatā. Ime cattāro dhammā bahukārā.

(Ka) "Which four qualities are very helpful? The four wheels: living in a suitable place, associating with good persons, setting oneself in the right direction, and having done merit in the past. These four qualities are very helpful."

(Kha) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā bhāvetabbā? Cattāro satipaṭṭhānā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu kāye kāyānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ. Vedanāsu…pe… citte… dhammesu dhammānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ. Ime cattāro dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) "Which four qualities are to be developed? The four foundations of mindfulness: Here, friends, a bhikkhu dwells contemplating the body as a body, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful, having removed covetousness and displeasure in regard to the world. He dwells contemplating feelings as feelings… mind as mind… phenomena as phenomena, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful, having removed covetousness and displeasure in regard to the world. These four qualities are to be developed."

(Ga) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā pariññeyyā? Cattāro āhārā – kabaḷīkāro [kavaḷīkāro (syā. kaṃ.)] āhāro oḷāriko vā sukhumo vā, phasso dutiyo, manosañcetanā tatiyā, viññāṇaṃ catutthaṃ. Ime cattāro dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) "Which four qualities are to be fully understood? The four kinds of nutriment: physical food, whether coarse or fine; contact as the second; mental volition as the third; and consciousness as the fourth. These four qualities are to be fully understood."

(Gha) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā pahātabbā? Cattāro oghā – kāmogho, bhavogho, diṭṭhogho, avijjogho. Ime cattāro dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) "Which four qualities are to be abandoned? The four floods: the flood of sensual pleasures, the flood of existence, the flood of views, and the flood of ignorance. These four qualities are to be abandoned."

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Cattāro yogā – kāmayogo, bhavayogo, diṭṭhiyogo, avijjāyogo. Ime cattāro dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) "Which four qualities are conducive to decline? The four yokes: the yoke of sensuality, the yoke of existence, the yoke of views, and the yoke of ignorance. These four qualities are conducive to decline."

(Ca) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Cattāro visaññogā – kāmayogavisaṃyogo, bhavayogavisaṃyogo, diṭṭhiyogavisaṃyogo, avijjāyogavisaṃyogo. Ime cattāro dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) "Which four qualities partake of distinction? The four disjunctions: disjunction from the yoke of sensuality, disjunction from the yoke of existence, disjunction from the yoke of views, and disjunction from the yoke of ignorance. These four qualities partake of distinction."

(Cha) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Cattāro samādhī – hānabhāgiyo samādhi, ṭhitibhāgiyo samādhi, visesabhāgiyo samādhi, nibbedhabhāgiyo samādhi. Ime cattāro dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

(Cha) "Which four qualities are hard to penetrate? These four samādhis: samādhi conducive to decline, samādhi conducive to stability, samādhi conducive to distinction, and samādhi conducive to penetration. These four qualities are hard to penetrate."

(Ja) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā uppādetabbā? Cattāri ñāṇāni – dhamme ñāṇaṃ, anvaye ñāṇaṃ, pariye ñāṇaṃ, sammutiyā ñāṇaṃ. Ime cattāro dhammā uppādetabbā.

(Ja) "Which four qualities are to be produced? The four knowledges: knowledge of the Dhamma, knowledge of inference, knowledge of discernment, and knowledge of convention. These four qualities are to be produced."

(Jha) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā abhiññeyyā? Cattāri ariyasaccāni – dukkhaṃ ariyasaccaṃ, dukkhasamudayaṃ [dukkhasamudayo (syā. kaṃ.)] ariyasaccaṃ, dukkhanirodhaṃ [dukkhanirodho (syā. kaṃ.)] ariyasaccaṃ, dukkhanirodhagāminī paṭipadā ariyasaccaṃ. Ime cattāro dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(Jha) "Which four qualities are to be fully known? The four noble truths: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. These four qualities are to be fully known."

(Ña) ‘‘katame cattāro dhammā sacchikātabbā? Cattāri sāmaññaphalāni – sotāpattiphalaṃ, sakadāgāmiphalaṃ, anāgāmiphalaṃ, arahattaphalaṃ. Ime cattāro dhammā sacchikātabbā.

(Ña) "Which four qualities are to be realized? The four fruits of recluseship: the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, and the fruit of arahantship. These four qualities are to be realized."

‘‘Iti ime cattārīsadhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these forty qualities are actual, factual, true, unfalsified, not otherwise, rightly fully awakened by the Tathagata."

## Pañca dhammā

### Five Qualities

355. ‘‘Pañca dhammā bahukārā…pe… pañca dhammā sacchikātabbā.

355.“Five qualities are very helpful… five qualities are to be realized.”

(Ka) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā bahukārā? Pañca padhāniyaṅgāni – idhāvuso, bhikkhu saddho hoti, saddahati tathāgatassa bodhiṃ – ‘itipi so bhagavā arahaṃ sammāsambuddho vijjācaraṇasampanno sugato lokavidū anuttaro purisadammasārathi satthā devamanussānaṃ buddho bhagavā’ti. Appābādho hoti appātaṅko samavepākiniyā gahaṇiyā samannāgato nātisītāya nāccuṇhāya majjhimāya padhānakkhamāya. Asaṭho hoti amāyāvī yathābhūtamattānaṃ āvīkattā satthari vā viññūsu vā sabrahmacārīsu. Āraddhavīriyo viharati akusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ pahānāya, kusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ upasampadāya, thāmavā daḷhaparakkamo anikkhittadhuro kusalesu dhammesu. Paññavā hoti udayatthagāminiyā paññāya samannāgato ariyāya nibbedhikāya sammā dukkhakkhayagāminiyā. Ime pañca dhammā bahukārā.

(Ka) “What are the five qualities that are very helpful? The five factors for striving: Here, friend, a bhikkhu is endowed with faith; he places faith in the Tathāgata’s enlightenment thus: ‘Indeed, the Blessed One is an Arahant, perfectly enlightened, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, well-gone, knower of the world, unsurpassed trainer of persons to be tamed, teacher of devas and humans, the Buddha, the Blessed One.’ He is seldom ill, rarely ailing, possessing a balanced digestive system, neither too cold nor too hot, but temperate and suitable for striving. He is honest and sincere, revealing himself as he actually is to the Teacher or to his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life. He dwells with aroused energy for abandoning unwholesome qualities and acquiring wholesome qualities; he is strong, firm in his effort, not shirking his duty in regard to wholesome qualities. And he is wise, possessing wisdom that leads to the rise and fall of phenomena, noble, penetrative, leading to the complete destruction of suffering. These are the five qualities that are very helpful.”

(Kha) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā bhāvetabbā? Pañcaṅgiko sammāsamādhi – pītipharaṇatā, sukhapharaṇatā, cetopharaṇatā, ālokapharaṇatā, paccavekkhaṇanimittaṃ [paccavekkhaṇānimittaṃ (syā. kaṃ.)]. Ime pañca dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) “What are the five qualities that are to be developed? The five-factored right samādhi: pervasion with rapture, pervasion with pleasure, pervasion with mind, pervasion with light, and the sign of reviewing. These are the five qualities that are to be developed.”

(Ga) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā pariññeyyā? Pañcupādānakkhandhā [seyyathīdaṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] – rūpupādānakkhandho, vedanupādānakkhandho, saññupādānakkhandho, saṅkhārupādānakkhandho viññāṇupādānakkhandho. Ime pañca dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) “What are the five qualities that are to be fully understood? The five aggregates subject to clinging: the form aggregate subject to clinging, the feeling aggregate subject to clinging, the perception aggregate subject to clinging, the mental formations aggregate subject to clinging, and the consciousness aggregate subject to clinging. These are the five qualities that are to be fully understood.”

(Gha) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā pahātabbā? Pañca nīvaraṇāni – kāmacchandanīvaraṇaṃ, byāpādanīvaraṇaṃ, thinamiddhanīvaraṇaṃ, uddhaccakukuccanīvaraṇaṃ, vicikicchānīvaraṇaṃ. Ime pañca dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) “What are the five qualities that are to be abandoned? The five hindrances: the hindrance of sensual desire, the hindrance of ill will, the hindrance of sloth and torpor, the hindrance of restlessness and remorse, and the hindrance of doubt. These are the five qualities that are to be abandoned.”

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Pañca cetokhilā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu satthari kaṅkhati vicikicchati nādhimuccati na sampasīdati. Yo so, āvuso, bhikkhu satthari kaṅkhati vicikicchati nādhimuccati na sampasīdati, tassa cittaṃ na namati ātappāya anuyogāya sātaccāya padhānāya. Yassa cittaṃ na namati ātappāya anuyogāya sātaccāya padhānāya. Ayaṃ paṭhamo cetokhilo. Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu dhamme kaṅkhati vicikicchati…pe… saṅghe kaṅkhati vicikicchati…pe… sikkhāya kaṅkhati vicikicchati…pe… sabrahmacārīsu kupito hoti anattamano āhatacitto khilajāto, yo so, āvuso, bhikkhu sabrahmacārīsu kupito hoti anattamano āhatacitto khilajāto, tassa cittaṃ na namati ātappāya anuyogāya sātaccāya padhānāya. Yassa cittaṃ na namati ātappāya anuyogāya sātaccāya padhānāya. Ayaṃ pañcamo cetokhilo. Ime pañca dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) “What are the five qualities that lead to decline? The five mental barrennesses: Here, friend, a bhikkhu doubts the Teacher, is uncertain, does not become confident, and does not gain serene faith. Because a bhikkhu doubts the Teacher, is uncertain, does not become confident, and does not gain serene faith, his mind does not incline to ardour, application, persistence, and exertion. Because his mind does not incline to ardour, application, persistence, and exertion, this is the first mental barrenness. Furthermore, friend, a bhikkhu doubts the Dhamma… doubts the Saṅgha… doubts the training… is angry and displeased with his fellow practitioners of the holy life, his mind is injured and obstructed. Because a bhikkhu is angry and displeased with his fellow practitioners of the holy life, his mind is injured and obstructed, his mind does not incline to ardour, application, persistence, and exertion. Because his mind does not incline to ardour, application, persistence, and exertion, this is the fifth mental barrenness. These are the five qualities that lead to decline.”

(Ca) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Pañcindriyāni – saddhindriyaṃ, vīriyindriyaṃ, satindriyaṃ, samādhindriyaṃ, paññindriyaṃ. Ime pañca dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) “What are the five qualities that lead to distinction? The five faculties: the faculty of faith, the faculty of energy, the faculty of mindfulness, the faculty of samādhi, and the faculty of wisdom. These are the five qualities that lead to distinction.”

(Cha) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Pañca nissaraṇiyā dhātuyo – idhāvuso, bhikkhuno kāme manasikaroto kāmesu cittaṃ na pakkhandati na pasīdati na santiṭṭhati na vimuccati. Nekkhammaṃ kho panassa manasikaroto nekkhamme cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati vimuccati. Tassa taṃ cittaṃ sugataṃ subhāvitaṃ suvuṭṭhitaṃ suvimuttaṃ visaṃyuttaṃ kāmehi. Ye ca kāmapaccayā uppajjanti āsavā vighātā pariḷāhā, mutto so tehi. Na so taṃ vedanaṃ vedeti. Idamakkhātaṃ kāmānaṃ nissaraṇaṃ.

(Cha) “What are the five qualities that are hard to penetrate? The five elements of escape: Here, friend, when a bhikkhu attends to sensual pleasures, his mind does not leap forward, does not become confident, does not settle, and is not liberated in regard to sensual pleasures. But when he attends to renunciation, his mind leaps forward, becomes confident, settles, and is liberated in regard to renunciation. His mind is thus well-gone, well-developed, well-established, well-liberated, and detached from sensual pleasures. And he is freed from the taints, torments, and feverishness that arise conditioned by sensual pleasures. He does not experience that feeling. This is declared to be the escape from sensual pleasures.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno byāpādaṃ manasikaroto byāpāde cittaṃ na pakkhandati na pasīdati na santiṭṭhati na vimuccati. Abyāpādaṃ kho panassa manasikaroto abyāpāde cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati vimuccati. Tassa taṃ cittaṃ sugataṃ subhāvitaṃ suvuṭṭhitaṃ suvimuttaṃ visaṃyuttaṃ byāpādena. Ye ca byāpādapaccayā uppajjanti āsavā vighātā pariḷāhā, mutto so tehi. Na so taṃ vedanaṃ vedeti. Idamakkhātaṃ byāpādassa nissaraṇaṃ.

“Furthermore, friend, when a bhikkhu attends to ill will, his mind does not leap forward, does not become confident, does not settle, and is not liberated in regard to ill will. But when he attends to non-ill will, his mind leaps forward, becomes confident, settles, and is liberated in regard to non-ill will. His mind is thus well-gone, well-developed, well-established, well-liberated, and detached from ill will. And he is freed from the taints, torments, and feverishness that arise conditioned by ill will. He does not experience that feeling. This is declared to be the escape from ill will.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno vihesaṃ manasikaroto vihesāya cittaṃ na pakkhandati na pasīdati na santiṭṭhati na vimuccati. Avihesaṃ kho panassa manasikaroto avihesāya cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati vimuccati. Tassa taṃ cittaṃ sugataṃ subhāvitaṃ suvuṭṭhitaṃ suvimuttaṃ visaṃyuttaṃ vihesāya. Ye ca vihesāpaccayā uppajjanti āsavā vighātā pariḷāhā, mutto so tehi. Na so taṃ vedanaṃ vedeti. Idamakkhātaṃ vihesāya nissaraṇaṃ.

“Furthermore, friend, when a bhikkhu attends to harming, his mind does not leap forward, does not become confident, does not settle, and is not liberated in regard to harming. But when he attends to harmlessness, his mind leaps forward, becomes confident, settles, and is liberated in regard to harmlessness. His mind is thus well-gone, well-developed, well-established, well-liberated, and detached from harming. And he is freed from the taints, torments, and feverishness that arise conditioned by harming. He does not experience that feeling. This is declared to be the escape from harming.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno rūpe manasikaroto rūpesu cittaṃ na pakkhandati na pasīdati na santiṭṭhati na vimuccati. Arūpaṃ kho panassa manasikaroto arūpe cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati vimuccati. Tassa taṃ cittaṃ sugataṃ subhāvitaṃ suvuṭṭhitaṃ suvimuttaṃ visaṃyuttaṃ rūpehi. Ye ca rūpapaccayā uppajjanti āsavā vighātā pariḷāhā, mutto so tehi. Na so taṃ vedanaṃ vedeti. Idamakkhātaṃ rūpānaṃ nissaraṇaṃ.

“Furthermore, friend, when a bhikkhu attends to forms, his mind does not leap forward, does not become confident, does not settle, and is not liberated in regard to forms. But when he attends to the formless, his mind leaps forward, becomes confident, settles, and is liberated in regard to the formless. His mind is thus well-gone, well-developed, well-established, well-liberated, and detached from forms. And he is freed from the taints, torments, and feverishness that arise conditioned by forms. He does not experience that feeling. This is declared to be the escape from forms.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno sakkāyaṃ manasikaroto sakkāye cittaṃ na pakkhandati na pasīdati na santiṭṭhati na vimuccati. Sakkāyanirodhaṃ kho panassa manasikaroto sakkāyanirodhe cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati vimuccati. Tassa taṃ cittaṃ sugataṃ subhāvitaṃ suvuṭṭhitaṃ suvimuttaṃ visaṃyuttaṃ sakkāyena. Ye ca sakkāyapaccayā uppajjanti āsavā vighātā pariḷāhā, mutto so tehi. Na so taṃ vedanaṃ vedeti. Idamakkhātaṃ sakkāyassa nissaraṇaṃ. Ime pañca dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

“Furthermore, friend, when a bhikkhu attends to self-identity, his mind does not leap forward, does not become confident, does not settle, and is not liberated in regard to self-identity. But when he attends to the cessation of self-identity, his mind leaps forward, becomes confident, settles, and is liberated in regard to the cessation of self-identity. His mind is thus well-gone, well-developed, well-established, well-liberated, and detached from self-identity. And he is freed from the taints, torments, and feverishness that arise conditioned by self-identity. He does not experience that feeling. This is declared to be the escape from self-identity. These are the five qualities that are hard to penetrate.”

(Ja) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā uppādetabbā? Pañca ñāṇiko sammāsamādhi – ‘ayaṃ samādhi paccuppannasukho ceva āyatiñca sukhavipāko’ti paccattaṃyeva ñāṇaṃ uppajjati. ‘Ayaṃ samādhi ariyo nirāmiso’ti paccattaññeva ñāṇaṃ uppajjati. ‘Ayaṃ samādhi akāpurisasevito’ti paccattaṃyeva ñāṇaṃ uppajjati. ‘Ayaṃ samādhi santo paṇīto paṭippassaddhaladdho ekodibhāvādhigato, na sasaṅkhāraniggayhavāritagato’ti [na ca sasaṅkhāraniggayha vāritavatoti (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), na sasaṅkhāraniggayhavārivāvato (ka.), na sasaṅkhāraniggayhavāriyādhigato (?)] paccattaṃyeva ñāṇaṃ uppajjati. ‘So kho panāhaṃ imaṃ samādhiṃ satova samāpajjāmi sato vuṭṭhahāmī’ti paccattaṃyeva ñāṇaṃ uppajjati. Ime pañca dhammā uppādetabbā.

(Ja) “What are the five qualities that are to be produced? The right samādhi with five knowledges: ‘This samādhi is presently pleasant and ripens as happiness in the future’—this knowledge arises in oneself. ‘This samādhi is noble and untainted’—this knowledge arises in oneself. ‘This samādhi is resorted to by superior persons’—this knowledge arises in oneself. ‘This samādhi is peaceful, sublime, attained through the calming of defilements, a state of unification of mind, and not attained by forceful restraint and suppression’—this knowledge arises in oneself. ‘I enter and emerge from this samādhi mindfully and with clear comprehension’—this knowledge arises in oneself. These are the five qualities that are to be produced.”

(Jha) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā abhiññeyyā? Pañca vimuttāyatanāni – idhāvuso, bhikkhuno satthā dhammaṃ deseti aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī. Yathā yathā, āvuso, bhikkhuno satthā dhammaṃ deseti, aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī, tathā tathā so [bhikkhu (syā. kaṃ.)] tasmiṃ dhamme atthappaṭisaṃvedī ca hoti dhammapaṭisaṃvedī ca. Tassa atthappaṭisaṃvedino dhammapaṭisaṃvedino pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Idaṃ paṭhamaṃ vimuttāyatanaṃ.

(Jha) "What are the five things to be directly known? The five bases for liberation. Here, friends, a teacher or a respected fellow monastic teaches the bhikkhu the Dhamma. In whatever way the teacher or respected fellow monastic teaches the bhikkhu the Dhamma, in that way he is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma in that teaching. For one who is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma, joy arises. Being joyful, rapture arises. When the mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil. With the body tranquil, one feels happiness. Being happy, the mind becomes collected. This is the first basis for liberation."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno na heva kho satthā dhammaṃ deseti, aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī, api ca kho yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena paresaṃ deseti yathā yathā, āvuso, bhikkhu yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena paresaṃ deseti. Tathā tathā so tasmiṃ dhamme atthappaṭisaṃvedī ca hoti dhammapaṭisaṃvedī ca. Tassa atthappaṭisaṃvedino dhammapaṭisaṃvedino pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Idaṃ dutiyaṃ vimuttāyatanaṃ.

"Furthermore, friends, neither does the Teacher teach the bhikkhu the Dhamma, nor does any respected fellow monastic. But rather, he teaches the Dhamma in detail to others as he has heard and learned it. In whatever way, friends, the bhikkhu teaches the Dhamma in detail to others as he has heard and learned it, in that way he is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma in that Dhamma. For one who is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma, joy arises. Being joyful, rapture arises. When the mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil. With the body tranquil, one feels happiness. Being happy, the mind becomes collected. This is the second basis for liberation."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno na heva kho satthā dhammaṃ deseti, aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena paresaṃ deseti. Api ca kho, yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena sajjhāyaṃ karoti. Yathā yathā, āvuso, bhikkhu yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena sajjhāyaṃ karoti tathā tathā so tasmiṃ dhamme atthappaṭisaṃvedī ca hoti dhammapaṭisaṃvedī ca. Tassa atthappaṭisaṃvedino dhammapaṭisaṃvedino pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Idaṃ tatiyaṃ vimuttāyatanaṃ.

"Furthermore, friends, neither does the Teacher teach the bhikkhu the Dhamma, nor does any respected fellow monastic, nor does he teach the Dhamma in detail to others as he has heard and learned it. But rather, he recites the Dhamma in detail as he has heard and learned it. In whatever way, friends, the bhikkhu recites the Dhamma in detail as he has heard and learned it, in that way he is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma in that Dhamma. For one who is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma, joy arises. Being joyful, rapture arises. When the mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil. With the body tranquil, one feels happiness. Being happy, the mind becomes collected. This is the third basis for liberation."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno na heva kho satthā dhammaṃ deseti, aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena paresaṃ deseti, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena sajjhāyaṃ karoti. Api ca kho, yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakketi anuvicāreti manasānupekkhati. Yathā yathā, āvuso, bhikkhu yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakketi anuvicāreti manasānupekkhati tathā tathā so tasmiṃ dhamme atthappaṭisaṃvedī ca hoti dhammapaṭisaṃvedī ca. Tassa atthappaṭisaṃvedino dhammapaṭisaṃvedino pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Idaṃ catutthaṃ vimuttāyatanaṃ.

"Furthermore, friends, neither does the Teacher teach the bhikkhu the Dhamma, nor does any respected fellow monastic, nor does he teach the Dhamma in detail to others as he has heard and learned it, nor does he recite the Dhamma in detail as he has heard and learned it. But rather, he mentally considers, examines, and contemplates the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it. In whatever way, friends, the bhikkhu mentally considers, examines, and contemplates the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it, in that way he is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma in that Dhamma. For one who is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma, joy arises. Being joyful, rapture arises. When the mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil. With the body tranquil, one feels happiness. Being happy, the mind becomes collected. This is the fourth basis for liberation."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno na heva kho satthā dhammaṃ deseti, aññataro vā garuṭṭhāniyo sabrahmacārī, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena paresaṃ deseti, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ vitthārena sajjhāyaṃ karoti, nāpi yathāsutaṃ yathāpariyattaṃ dhammaṃ cetasā anuvitakketi anuvicāreti manasānupekkhati; api ca khvassa aññataraṃ samādhinimittaṃ suggahitaṃ hoti sumanasikataṃ sūpadhāritaṃ suppaṭividdhaṃ paññāya. Yathā yathā, āvuso, bhikkhuno aññataraṃ samādhinimittaṃ suggahitaṃ hoti sumanasikataṃ sūpadhāritaṃ suppaṭividdhaṃ paññāya tathā tathā so tasmiṃ dhamme atthappaṭisaṃvedī ca hoti dhammappaṭisaṃvedī ca. Tassa atthappaṭisaṃvedino dhammappaṭisaṃvedino pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati. Idaṃ pañcamaṃ vimuttāyatanaṃ. Ime pañca dhammā abhiññeyyā.

"Furthermore, friends, neither does the Teacher teach the bhikkhu the Dhamma, nor does any respected fellow monastic, nor does he teach the Dhamma in detail to others as he has heard and learned it, nor does he recite the Dhamma in detail as he has heard and learned it, nor does he mentally consider, examine, and contemplate the Dhamma as he has heard and learned it. But rather, a certain object of samādhi is well grasped by him, well attended to, well retained, and well penetrated by wisdom. In whatever way, friends, a bhikkhu has a certain object of samādhi well grasped, well attended to, well retained, and well penetrated by wisdom, in that way he is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma in that Dhamma. For one who is sensitive to the meaning and sensitive to the Dhamma, joy arises. Being joyful, rapture arises. When the mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil. With the body tranquil, one feels happiness. Being happy, the mind becomes collected. This is the fifth basis for liberation. These five things are to be directly known."

(Ña) ‘‘katame pañca dhammā sacchikātabbā? Pañca dhammakkhandhā – sīlakkhandho, samādhikkhandho, paññākkhandho, vimuttikkhandho, vimuttiñāṇadassanakkhandho. Ime pañca dhammā sacchikātabbā.

(Ña) "What five things are to be realized? The five aggregates of qualities: the aggregate of virtue, the aggregate of samādhi, the aggregate of wisdom, the aggregate of liberation, and the aggregate of the knowledge and vision of liberation. These five things are to be realized."

‘‘Iti ime paññāsa dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these fifty things are real, actual, true, unfalsified, not otherwise, rightly awakened to by the Tathāgata."

## Cha dhammā

### Six Qualities

356. ‘‘Cha dhammā bahukārā…pe… cha dhammā sacchikātabbā.

356."Six qualities are very helpful... six qualities are to be realized.

(Ka) ‘‘katame cha dhammā bahukārā? Cha sāraṇīyā dhammā. Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno mettaṃ kāyakammaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti sabrahmacārīsu āvi ceva raho ca, ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

(A) "What six qualities are very helpful? The six qualities that inspire affection. Here, friends, a bhikkhu maintains a bodily action of loving-kindness toward his fellow bhikkhus, both in public and in private. This quality is inspiring affection, endearing, inspiring respect, conducive to cohesion, non-dispute, concord, and unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno mettaṃ vacīkammaṃ…pe… ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu's verbal action of loving-kindness... conducive to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno mettaṃ manokammaṃ…pe… ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu's mental action of loving-kindness... conducive to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu ye te lābhā dhammikā dhammaladdhā antamaso pattapariyāpannamattampi, tathārūpehi lābhehi appaṭivibhattabhogī hoti sīlavantehi sabrahmacārīhi sādhāraṇabhogī, ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo…pe… ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu shares in common with his virtuous fellow bhikkhus any lawful gains he has obtained by lawful means—even the mere contents of his alms bowl—without reservation. This quality is inspiring affection... conducive to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu, yāni tāni sīlāni akhaṇḍāni acchiddāni asabalāni akammāsāni bhujissāni viññuppasatthāni aparāmaṭṭhāni samādhisaṃvattanikāni, tathārūpesu sīlesu sīlasāmaññagato viharati sabrahmacārīhi āvi ceva raho ca, ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo…pe… ekībhāvāya saṃvattati.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu dwells in common with his fellow bhikkhus, both in public and in private, in regard to those virtues that are unbroken, flawless, spotless, unblotched, liberating, praised by the wise, ungrasped, and conducive to samādhi. This quality is inspiring affection... conducive to unity.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu yāyaṃ diṭṭhi ariyā niyyānikā niyyāti takkarassa sammā dukkhakkhayāya, tathārūpāya diṭṭhiyā diṭṭhi sāmaññagato viharati sabrahmacārīhi āvi ceva raho ca, ayampi dhammo sāraṇīyo piyakaraṇo garukaraṇo, saṅgahāya avivādāya sāmaggiyā ekībhāvāya saṃvattati. Ime cha dhammā bahukārā.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu dwells in common with his fellow bhikkhus, both in public and in private, in regard to that view which is noble, leading to liberation, leading one who practices it to the complete destruction of suffering. This quality is inspiring affection, endearing, inspiring respect, conducive to cohesion, non-dispute, concord, and unity. These six qualities are very helpful.

(Kha) ‘‘katame cha dhammā bhāvetabbā? Cha anussatiṭṭhānāni – buddhānussati, dhammānussati, saṅghānussati, sīlānussati, cāgānussati, devatānussati. Ime cha dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(B) "What six qualities are to be developed? The six recollections: recollection of the Buddha, recollection of the Dhamma, recollection of the Sangha, recollection of morality, recollection of generosity, and recollection of the deities. These six qualities are to be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katame cha dhammā pariññeyyā? Cha ajjhattikāni āyatanāni – cakkhāyatanaṃ, sotāyatanaṃ, ghānāyatanaṃ, jivhāyatanaṃ, kāyāyatanaṃ, manāyatanaṃ. Ime cha dhammā pariññeyyā.

(C) "What six qualities are to be fully understood? The six internal sense bases: the eye sense base, the ear sense base, the nose sense base, the tongue sense base, the body sense base, and the mind sense base. These six qualities are to be fully understood.

(Gha) ‘‘katame cha dhammā pahātabbā? Cha taṇhākāyā – rūpataṇhā, saddataṇhā, gandhataṇhā, rasataṇhā, phoṭṭhabbataṇhā, dhammataṇhā. Ime cha dhammā pahātabbā.

(D) "What six qualities are to be abandoned? The six bodies of craving: craving for forms, craving for sounds, craving for smells, craving for tastes, craving for tactile sensations, and craving for mental phenomena. These six qualities are to be abandoned.

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame cha dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Cha agāravā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu satthari agāravo viharati appatisso. Dhamme…pe… saṅghe… sikkhāya… appamāde… paṭisanthāre agāravo viharati appatisso. Ime cha dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(E) "What six qualities partake of decline? Six kinds of irreverence: Here, friends, a bhikkhu dwells irreverently and without deference toward the Teacher, toward the Dhamma... toward the Sangha... toward the training... toward diligence... and toward cordiality. These six qualities partake of decline.

(Ca) ‘‘katame cha dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Cha gāravā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu satthari sagāravo viharati sappatisso dhamme…pe… saṅghe… sikkhāya… appamāde… paṭisanthāre sagāravo viharati sappatisso. Ime cha dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(F) "What six qualities partake of distinction? Six kinds of reverence: Here, friends, a bhikkhu dwells with reverence and deference toward the Teacher, toward the Dhamma... toward the Sangha... toward the training... toward diligence... and toward cordiality. These six qualities partake of distinction.

(Cha) ‘‘katame cha dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Cha nissaraṇiyā dhātuyo – idhāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘mettā hi kho me, cetovimutti bhāvitā bahulīkatā yānīkatā vatthukatā anuṭṭhitā paricitā susamāraddhā, atha ca pana me byāpādo cittaṃ pariyādāya tiṭṭhatī’ti. So ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca, mā bhagavantaṃ abbhācikkhi. Na hi sādhu bhagavato abbhakkhānaṃ, na hi bhagavā evaṃ vadeyya. Aṭṭhānametaṃ āvuso anavakāso yaṃ mettāya cetovimuttiyā bhāvitāya bahulīkatāya yānīkatāya vatthukatāya anuṭṭhitāya paricitāya susamāraddhāya. Atha ca panassa byāpādo cittaṃ pariyādāya ṭhassatīti, netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati. Nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso, byāpādassa, yadidaṃ mettācetovimuttī’ti.

(G) "What six qualities are hard to penetrate? The six elements of escape: Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say, ‘I have developed the liberation of mind through loving-kindness, cultivated it, made it my vehicle, made it my basis, established it, practiced it, and well-undertaken it, and yet ill will still obsesses my mind.’ He should be told, ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One. It is not good to misrepresent the Blessed One, for the Blessed One would not say such a thing. It is impossible, friend, it cannot happen that one who has developed the liberation of mind through loving-kindness—cultivated it, made it one’s vehicle, made it one's basis, established it, practiced it, and well-undertaken it—would still have ill will obsessing the mind. There is no such possibility. For this, friend, is the escape from ill will: the liberation of mind through loving-kindness.’

‘‘Idha panāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘karuṇā hi kho me cetovimutti bhāvitā bahulīkatā yānīkatā vatthukatā anuṭṭhitā paricitā susamāraddhā. Atha ca pana me vihesā cittaṃ pariyādāya tiṭṭhatī’ti. So – ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo, ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca, mā bhagavantaṃ abbhācikkhi…pe… nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso, vihesāya, yadidaṃ karuṇācetovimuttī’ti.

Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say: ‘I have developed the liberation of mind through compassion, cultivated it, made it my vehicle, made it my basis, established it, practiced it, and well-undertaken it. Yet, violence still obsesses my mind.’ He should be told: ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One... For this, friend, is the escape from violence: the liberation of mind through compassion.’

‘‘Idha panāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘muditā hi kho me cetovimutti bhāvitā…pe… atha ca pana me arati cittaṃ pariyādāya tiṭṭhatī’ti. So – ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca…pe… nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso aratiyā, yadidaṃ muditācetovimuttī’ti.

Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say: ‘I have developed the liberation of mind through sympathetic joy... Yet, discontent still obsesses my mind.’ He should be told: ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One... For this, friend, is the escape from discontent: the liberation of mind through sympathetic joy.’

‘‘Idha panāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘upekkhā hi kho me cetovimutti bhāvitā…pe… atha ca pana me rāgo cittaṃ pariyādāya tiṭṭhatī’ti. So – ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca…pe… nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso, rāgassa yadidaṃ upekkhācetovimuttī’ti.

Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say: ‘I have developed the liberation of mind through equanimity... Yet, lust still obsesses my mind.’ He should be told: ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One... For this, friend, is the escape from lust: the liberation of mind through equanimity.’

‘‘Idha panāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘animittā hi kho me cetovimutti bhāvitā…pe… atha ca pana me nimittānusāri viññāṇaṃ hotī’ti. So – ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca…pe… nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso, sabbanimittānaṃ yadidaṃ animittā cetovimuttī’ti.

Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say: ‘I have developed the signless liberation of mind... Yet, my consciousness still follows after signs.’ He should be told: ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One... For this, friend, is the escape from all signs: the signless liberation of mind.’

‘‘Idha panāvuso, bhikkhu evaṃ vadeyya – ‘asmīti kho me vigataṃ, ayamahamasmīti na samanupassāmi, atha ca pana me vicikicchākathaṃkathāsallaṃ cittaṃ pariyādāya tiṭṭhatī’ti. So – ‘mā hevaṃ’ tissa vacanīyo ‘māyasmā evaṃ avaca, mā bhagavantaṃ abbhācikkhi, na hi sādhu bhagavato abbhakkhānaṃ, na hi bhagavā evaṃ vadeyya. Aṭṭhānametaṃ, āvuso, anavakāso yaṃ asmīti vigate ayamahamasmīti asamanupassato. Atha ca panassa vicikicchākathaṃkathāsallaṃ cittaṃ pariyādāya ṭhassati, netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati. Nissaraṇaṃ hetaṃ, āvuso, vicikicchākathaṃkathāsallassa, yadidaṃ asmimānasamugghāṭo’ti. Ime cha dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

Here, friends, a bhikkhu might say: ‘The notion "I am" has vanished for me; I do not regard "I am this." Yet, the dart of doubt and uncertainty still obsesses my mind.’ He should be told: ‘Do not say that! Do not misrepresent the Blessed One, for it is not good to misrepresent the Blessed One. The Blessed One would not say such a thing. It is impossible, friend, there is no possibility that when the notion "I am" has vanished and one does not regard "I am this," the dart of doubt and uncertainty would still obsess the mind—there is no such possibility. For this, friend, is the escape from the dart of doubt and uncertainty: the uprooting of the conceit "I am."’ These six qualities are hard to penetrate.

(Ja) ‘‘katame cha dhammā uppādetabbā? Cha satatavihārā. Idhāvuso, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā neva sumano hoti na dummano, upekkhako viharati sato sampajāno. Sotena saddaṃ sutvā…pe… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya neva sumano hoti na dummano, upekkhako viharati sato sampajāno. Ime cha dhammā uppādetabbā.

(H) "What six qualities are to be developed? The six constant abidings. Here, friends, a bhikkhu, seeing a form with the eye, is neither glad nor sad, but abides equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending. Hearing a sound with the ear... Smelling an odor with the nose... Tasting a flavor with the tongue... Feeling a tangible with the body... Cognizing a mental phenomenon with the mind, he is neither glad nor sad, but abides equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending. These six qualities are to be developed.

(Jha) ‘‘katame cha dhammā abhiññeyyā? Cha anuttariyāni – dassanānuttariyaṃ, savanānuttariyaṃ, lābhānuttariyaṃ, sikkhānuttariyaṃ, pāricariyānuttariyaṃ, anussatānuttariyaṃ. Ime cha dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(I) "What six qualities are to be directly known? The six unsurpassed states: the unsurpassed seeing, the unsurpassed hearing, the unsurpassed gain, the unsurpassed training, the unsurpassed service, and the unsurpassed recollection. These six qualities are to be directly known.

(Ña) ‘‘katame cha dhammā sacchikātabbā? Cha abhiññā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu anekavihitaṃ iddhividhaṃ paccanubhoti – ekopi hutvā bahudhā hoti, bahudhāpi hutvā eko hoti. Āvibhāvaṃ tirobhāvaṃ. Tirokuṭṭaṃ tiropākāraṃ tiropabbataṃ asajjamāno gacchati seyyathāpi ākāse. Pathaviyāpi ummujjanimujjaṃ karoti seyyathāpi udake. Udakepi abhijjamāne gacchati seyyathāpi pathaviyaṃ. Ākāsepi pallaṅkena kamati seyyathāpi pakkhī sakuṇo. Imepi candimasūriye evaṃmahiddhike evaṃmahānubhāve pāṇinā parāmasati parimajjati. Yāva brahmalokāpi kāyena vasaṃ vatteti.

(J) "What six qualities are to be realized? The six higher knowledges: Here, friends, a bhikkhu experiences various kinds of psychic power: being one, he becomes many; being many, he becomes one. He appears and vanishes. He goes unhindered through walls, ramparts, and mountains as if through space. He dives into and emerges from the earth as if it were water. He walks on water without breaking through as if it were earth. He travels through the sky cross-legged like a bird. He touches and strokes with his hand even the moon and sun, so mighty and powerful. He wields bodily mastery even as far as the Brahma-world.

‘‘Dibbāya sotadhātuyā visuddhāya atikkantamānusikāya ubho sadde suṇāti dibbe ca mānuse ca, ye dūre santike ca.

With the divine ear element, purified and surpassing the human, he hears both kinds of sounds, divine and human, those that are far and those that are near.

‘‘Parasattānaṃ parapuggalānaṃ cetasā ceto paricca pajānāti [jānāti (syā. kaṃ.)], sarāgaṃ vā cittaṃ sarāgaṃ cittanti pajānāti …pe… avimuttaṃ vā cittaṃ avimuttaṃ cittanti pajānāti.

He understands the minds of other beings, of other persons, having encompassed them with his own mind. He understands a mind with lust as ‘a mind with lust’… an unliberated mind as ‘an unliberated mind.’

‘‘So anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarati, seyyathidaṃ ekampi jātiṃ…pe… iti sākāraṃ sauddesaṃ anekavihitaṃ pubbenivāsaṃ anussarati.

He recollects his manifold past lives, that is, one birth… Thus with their aspects and details he recollects his manifold past lives.

‘‘Dibbena cakkhunā visuddhena atikkantamānusakena satte passati cavamāne upapajjamāne hīne paṇīte suvaṇṇe dubbaṇṇe sugate duggate yathākammūpage satte pajānāti …pe…

With the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, he sees beings passing away and reappearing, inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate, and he understands how beings fare according to their kamma…

‘‘Āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharati. Ime cha dhammā sacchikātabbā.

Through the destruction of the taints, he enters and dwells in the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, having realized it for himself with direct knowledge in this very life. These six qualities are to be realized.

‘‘Iti ime saṭṭhi dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

Thus, these sixty qualities are actual, true, real, not otherwise, not different, rightly realized by the Tathāgata.

## Satta dhammā

### Seven Qualities

357. ‘‘Satta dhammā bahukārā…pe… satta dhammā sacchikātabbā.

357.Seven qualities are very helpful… seven qualities are to be realized.

(Ka) ‘‘katame satta dhammā bahukārā? Satta ariyadhanāni – saddhādhanaṃ, sīladhanaṃ, hiridhanaṃ, ottappadhanaṃ, sutadhanaṃ, cāgadhanaṃ, paññādhanaṃ. Ime satta dhammā bahukārā.

(A) What seven qualities are very helpful? The seven noble treasures: the treasure of faith, the treasure of virtue, the treasure of moral shame, the treasure of moral dread, the treasure of learning, the treasure of generosity, and the treasure of wisdom. These seven qualities are very helpful.

(Kha) ‘‘katame satta dhammā bhāvetabbā? Satta sambojjhaṅgā – satisambojjhaṅgo, dhammavicayasambojjhaṅgo, vīriyasambojjhaṅgo, pītisambojjhaṅgo, passaddhisambojjhaṅgo, samādhisambojjhaṅgo, upekkhāsambojjhaṅgo. Ime satta dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(B) What seven qualities are to be developed? The seven factors of enlightenment: the mindfulness factor of enlightenment, the investigation of phenomena factor of enlightenment, the energy factor of enlightenment, the rapture factor of enlightenment, the tranquility factor of enlightenment, the samādhi factor of enlightenment, and the equanimity factor of enlightenment. These seven qualities are to be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katame satta dhammā pariññeyyā? Satta viññāṇaṭṭhitiyo – santāvuso, sattā nānattakāyā nānattasaññino, seyyathāpi manussā ekacce ca devā ekacce ca vinipātikā. Ayaṃ paṭhamā viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

(C) What seven qualities are to be fully understood? The seven stations of consciousness: There are, friends, beings with diverse bodies and diverse perceptions, such as humans, some gods, and some beings in the states of deprivation. This is the first station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā nānattakāyā ekattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā brahmakāyikā paṭhamābhinibbattā. Ayaṃ dutiyā viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

There are, friends, beings with diverse bodies and unified perceptions, such as the gods of Brahmā’s host who are newly reborn. This is the second station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā ekattakāyā nānattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā ābhassarā. Ayaṃ tatiyā viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

There are, friends, beings with unified bodies and diverse perceptions, such as the gods of Streaming Radiance. This is the third station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā ekattakāyā ekattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā subhakiṇhā. Ayaṃ catutthī viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

There are, friends, beings with unified bodies and unified perceptions, such as the gods of Refulgent Glory. This is the fourth station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā…pe… ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ pañcamī viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

There are, friends, beings who, by completely surmounting perceptions of form… perceiving, ‘Space is infinite,’ attain the base of the infinity of space. This is the fifth station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ chaṭṭhī viññāṇaṭṭhiti.

There are, friends, beings who, by completely surmounting the base of the infinity of space, perceiving, ‘Consciousness is infinite,’ attain the base of the infinity of consciousness. This is the sixth station of consciousness.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ sattamī viññāṇaṭṭhiti. Ime satta dhammā pariññeyyā.

There are, friends, beings who, by completely surmounting the base of the infinity of consciousness, perceiving, ‘There is nothing,’ attain the base of nothingness. This is the seventh station of consciousness. These seven qualities are to be fully understood.

(Gha) ‘‘katame satta dhammā pahātabbā? Sattānusayā – kāmarāgānusayo, paṭighānusayo, diṭṭhānusayo, vicikicchānusayo, mānānusayo, bhavarāgānusayo, avijjānusayo. Ime satta dhammā pahātabbā.

(D) What seven qualities are to be abandoned? The seven underlying tendencies: the underlying tendency to sensual desire, the underlying tendency to aversion, the underlying tendency to views, the underlying tendency to doubt, the underlying tendency to conceit, the underlying tendency to craving for existence, and the underlying tendency to ignorance. These seven qualities are to be abandoned.

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame satta dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Satta asaddhammā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu assaddho hoti, ahiriko hoti, anottappī hoti, appassuto hoti, kusīto hoti, muṭṭhassati hoti, duppañño hoti. Ime satta dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(E) What seven qualities lead to decline? The seven unwholesome qualities: Here, friends, a bhikkhu is faithless, shameless, reckless, unlearned, lazy, unmindful, and unwise. These seven qualities lead to decline.

(Ca) ‘‘katame satta dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Satta saddhammā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu saddho hoti, hirimā [hiriko (syā. kaṃ.)] hoti, ottappī hoti, bahussuto hoti, āraddhavīriyo hoti, upaṭṭhitassati hoti, paññavā hoti. Ime satta dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(F) What seven qualities lead to distinction? The seven wholesome qualities: Here, friends, a bhikkhu is faithful, conscientious, scrupulous, learned, energetic, mindful, and wise. These seven qualities lead to distinction.

(Cha) ‘‘katame satta dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Satta sappurisadhammā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu dhammaññū ca hoti atthaññū ca attaññū ca mattaññū ca kālaññū ca parisaññū ca puggalaññū ca. Ime satta dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

(G) What seven qualities are hard to penetrate? The seven qualities of a true person: Here, friends, a bhikkhu knows the Dhamma, knows the meaning, knows himself, knows moderation, knows the proper time, knows the assembly, and knows the individual. These seven qualities are hard to penetrate.

(Ja) ‘‘katame satta dhammā uppādetabbā? Satta saññā – aniccasaññā, anattasaññā, asubhasaññā, ādīnavasaññā, pahānasaññā, virāgasaññā, nirodhasaññā. Ime satta dhammā uppādetabbā.

(H) What seven qualities are to be produced? The seven perceptions: the perception of impermanence, the perception of non-self, the perception of unattractiveness, the perception of danger, the perception of abandonment, the perception of dispassion, and the perception of cessation. These seven qualities are to be produced.

(Jha) ‘‘katame satta dhammā abhiññeyyā? Satta niddasavatthūni – idhāvuso, bhikkhu sikkhāsamādāne tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca sikkhāsamādāne avigatapemo. Dhammanisantiyā tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca dhammanisantiyā avigatapemo. Icchāvinaye tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca icchāvinaye avigatapemo. Paṭisallāne tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca paṭisallāne avigatapemo. Vīriyāramme tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca vīriyāramme avigatapemo. Satinepakke tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca satinepakke avigatapemo. Diṭṭhipaṭivedhe tibbacchando hoti, āyatiñca diṭṭhipaṭivedhe avigatapemo. Ime satta dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(I) What seven qualities are to be directly known? The seven grounds for instruction: Here, friends, a bhikkhu has strong desire for undertaking the training and does not lose affection for undertaking the training in the future. He has strong desire for contentment with the Dhamma and does not lose affection for contentment with the Dhamma in the future. He has strong desire for the removal of wishes and does not lose affection for the removal of wishes in the future. He has strong desire for seclusion and does not lose affection for seclusion in the future. He has strong desire for the arousal of energy and does not lose affection for the arousal of energy in the future. He has strong desire for mindfulness and clear comprehension and does not lose affection for mindfulness and clear comprehension in the future. He has strong desire for the penetration of views and does not lose affection for the penetration of views in the future. These seven qualities are to be directly known.

(Ña) ‘‘katame satta dhammā sacchikātabbā? Satta khīṇāsavabalāni – idhāvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno aniccato sabbe saṅkhārā yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhā honti. Yaṃpāvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno aniccato sabbe saṅkhārā yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhā honti, idampi khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno balaṃ hoti, yaṃ balaṃ āgamma khīṇāsavo bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayaṃ paṭijānāti – ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

(J) What seven qualities are to be realized? The seven powers of one whose taints are destroyed: Here, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, all formations are well seen as impermanent with right wisdom, as they actually are. Because, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, all formations are well seen as impermanent with right wisdom, as they actually are, this is his power. Relying on this power, the bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed declares the destruction of the taints: ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno aṅgārakāsūpamā kāmā yathābhūtaṃ sammappaññāya sudiṭṭhā honti. Yaṃpāvuso…pe… ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, sensual pleasures are well seen as like a pit of glowing embers with right wisdom, as they actually are. Because, friends… ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno vivekaninnaṃ cittaṃ hoti vivekapoṇaṃ vivekapabbhāraṃ vivekaṭṭhaṃ nekkhammābhirataṃ byantībhūtaṃ sabbaso āsavaṭṭhāniyehi dhammehi. Yaṃpāvuso…pe… ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, his mind is inclined to seclusion, disposed to seclusion, tending to seclusion, fixed on seclusion, delighting in renunciation, and utterly vanished from all qualities that are grounds for taints. Because, friends… ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno cattāro satipaṭṭhānā bhāvitā honti subhāvitā. Yaṃpāvuso…pe… ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, the four establishments of mindfulness are cultivated and well cultivated. Because, friends… ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno pañcindriyāni bhāvitāni honti subhāvitāni. Yaṃpāvuso…pe… ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, the five faculties are cultivated and well cultivated. Because, friends… ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno satta bojjhaṅgā bhāvitā honti subhāvitā. Yaṃpāvuso…pe… ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, the seven factors of enlightenment are cultivated and well cultivated. Because, friends… ‘My taints are destroyed.’

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo bhāvito hoti subhāvito. Yaṃpāvuso, khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo bhāvito hoti subhāvito, idampi khīṇāsavassa bhikkhuno balaṃ hoti, yaṃ balaṃ āgamma khīṇāsavo bhikkhu āsavānaṃ khayaṃ paṭijānāti – ‘khīṇā me āsavā’ti. Ime satta dhammā sacchikātabbā.

Furthermore, friends, for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, the Noble Eightfold Path is developed and well developed. And friends, because the Noble Eightfold Path is developed and well developed for a bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, this too is a strength of the bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed, by relying on which strength the bhikkhu whose taints are destroyed declares the destruction of the taints: ‘My taints are destroyed.’ These seven qualities are to be realized.

‘‘Itime sattati dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

These seven qualities are factual, true, not unreal, not otherwise, rightly awakened to by the Tathagata.

Paṭhamabhāṇavāro niṭṭhito.

The first recitation portion is concluded.

## Aṭṭha dhammā

### Eight qualities

358. ‘‘Aṭṭha dhammā bahukārā…pe… aṭṭha dhammā sacchikātabbā.

358.“Eight qualities are very helpful… eight qualities are to be realized.”

(Ka) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā bahukārā? Aṭṭha hetū aṭṭha paccayā ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattanti. Katame aṭṭha? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu satthāraṃ [satthāraṃ vā (syā. ka.)] upanissāya viharati aññataraṃ vā garuṭṭhāniyaṃ sabrahmacāriṃ, yatthassa tibbaṃ hirottappaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti pemañca gāravo ca. Ayaṃ paṭhamo hetu paṭhamo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya. Paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

(Ka) “What are the eight qualities that are very helpful? Eight causes, eight conditions conduce to the gaining of wisdom that is fundamental to the holy life—wisdom not yet gained—and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection once it has been gained. What eight? Here, friends, a bhikkhu lives in dependence on the Teacher or on a fellow monastic he regards as worthy of respect, in whom he has a strong sense of shame and moral dread constantly present, along with affection and reverence. This is the first cause, the first condition that conduces to the gaining of wisdom that is fundamental to the holy life—wisdom not yet gained—and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection once it has been gained.

‘‘Taṃ kho pana satthāraṃ upanissāya viharati aññataraṃ vā garuṭṭhāniyaṃ sabrahmacāriṃ, yatthassa tibbaṃ hirottappaṃ paccupaṭṭhitaṃ hoti pemañca gāravo ca. Te kālena kālaṃ upasaṅkamitvā paripucchati paripañhati – ‘idaṃ, bhante, kathaṃ? Imassa ko attho’ti? Tassa te āyasmanto avivaṭañceva vivaranti, anuttānīkatañca uttānī [anuttānikatañca uttāniṃ (ka.)] karonti, anekavihitesu ca kaṅkhāṭṭhāniyesu dhammesu kaṅkhaṃ paṭivinodenti. Ayaṃ dutiyo hetu dutiyo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya, vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Living in dependence on the Teacher or on some other fellow bhikkhu who is in a position worthy of respect, in whom he maintains strong shame and moral dread, affection, and reverence, he approaches them from time to time to question and inquire: ‘How is this, venerable sir? What is the meaning of this?’ Those venerable ones then reveal to him what is unrevealed, clarify what is obscure, and dispel his doubt regarding various points of the Dhamma that cause doubt. This is the second cause, the second condition leading to the acquisition of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been acquired, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been acquired.

‘‘Taṃ kho pana dhammaṃ sutvā dvayena vūpakāsena sampādeti – kāyavūpakāsena ca cittavūpakāsena ca. Ayaṃ tatiyo hetu tatiyo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Having heard that Dhamma, he develops twofold seclusion: physical seclusion and mental seclusion. This is the third cause, the third condition that leads to the attainment of the wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been attained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been attained.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu sīlavā hoti, pātimokkhasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati ācāragocarasampanno, aṇumattesu vajjesu bhayadassāvī samādāya sikkhati sikkhāpadesu. Ayaṃ catuttho hetu catuttho paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is virtuous, he dwells restrained by the restraint of the Pātimokkha, possessing good conduct and range, seeing danger in the slightest faults, and training himself by undertaking the training rules. This is the fourth cause, the fourth condition that leads to the gaining of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been gained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been gained.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu bahussuto hoti sutadharo sutasannicayo. Ye te dhammā ādikalyāṇā majjhekalyāṇā pariyosānakalyāṇā sātthā sabyañjanā kevalaparipuṇṇaṃ parisuddhaṃ brahmacariyaṃ abhivadanti, tathārūpāssa dhammā bahussutā honti dhātā vacasā paricitā manasānupekkhitā diṭṭhiyā suppaṭividdhā. Ayaṃ pañcamo hetu pañcamo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is very learned, a preserver of what he has heard, an accumulator of what he has heard. Those teachings that are good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, with the meaning and phrasing, which proclaim the holy life that is perfectly complete and pure—such teachings are much heard by him, retained, recited verbally, investigated mentally, and penetrated well by view. This is the fifth cause, the fifth condition that conduces to the gaining of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been gained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been gained.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu āraddhavīriyo viharati akusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ pahānāya, kusalānaṃ dhammānaṃ upasampadāya, thāmavā daḷhaparakkamo anikkhittadhuro kusalesu dhammesu. Ayaṃ chaṭṭho hetu chaṭṭho paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu dwells with energy aroused for abandoning unwholesome qualities and acquiring wholesome qualities, firm, strong in effort, not neglecting the duty in regard to wholesome qualities. This is the sixth cause, the sixth condition that conduces to the gaining of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been gained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been gained.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu satimā hoti paramena satinepakkena samannāgato. Cirakatampi cirabhāsitampi saritā anussaritā. Ayaṃ sattamo hetu sattamo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is mindful, possessing supreme mindfulness and skill in mindfulness, remembering and recollecting what was done and said long ago. This is the seventh cause, the seventh condition that conduces to the gaining of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been gained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been gained.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu pañcasu upādānakkhandhesu, udayabbayānupassī viharati – ‘iti rūpaṃ iti rūpassa samudayo iti rūpassa atthaṅgamo; iti vedanā iti vedanāya samudayo iti vedanāya atthaṅgamo; iti saññā iti saññāya samudayo iti saññāya atthaṅgamo; iti saṅkhārā iti saṅkhārānaṃ samudayo iti saṅkhārānaṃ atthaṅgamo; iti viññāṇaṃ iti viññāṇassa samudayo iti viññāṇassa atthaṅgamo’ti. Ayaṃ aṭṭhamo hetu aṭṭhamo paccayo ādibrahmacariyikāya paññāya appaṭiladdhāya paṭilābhāya, paṭiladdhāya bhiyyobhāvāya vepullāya bhāvanāya pāripūriyā saṃvattati. Ime aṭṭha dhammā bahukārā.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu dwells contemplating the rise and fall of the five aggregates of clinging: ‘Such is form, such its origin, such its passing away; such is feeling, such its origin, such its passing away; such is perception, such its origin, such its passing away; such are formations, such their origin, such their passing away; such is consciousness, such its origin, such its passing away.’ This is the eighth cause, the eighth condition that conduces to the gaining of wisdom fundamental to the holy life, if it has not yet been gained, and to its increase, abundance, development, and perfection, if it has been gained. These eight qualities are very helpful.

(Kha) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā bhāvetabbā? Ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi, sammāsaṅkappo, sammāvācā, sammākammanto, sammāājīvo, sammāvāyāmo, sammāsati, sammāsamādhi. Ime aṭṭha dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) “What eight qualities should be developed? The Noble Eightfold Path, namely: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right samādhi. These eight qualities should be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā pariññeyyā? Aṭṭha lokadhammā – lābho ca, alābho ca, yaso ca, ayaso ca, nindā ca, pasaṃsā ca, sukhañca, dukkhañca. Ime aṭṭha dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) “What eight qualities are to be fully understood? The eight worldly conditions: gain and loss, fame and disrepute, censure and praise, pleasure and pain. These eight qualities are to be fully understood.”

(Gha) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā pahātabbā? Aṭṭha micchattā – micchādiṭṭhi, micchāsaṅkappo, micchāvācā, micchākammanto, micchāājīvo, micchāvāyāmo, micchāsati, micchāsamādhi. Ime aṭṭha dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) “What eight qualities should be abandoned? The eight wrongnesses: wrong view, wrong intention, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, wrong samādhi. These eight qualities should be abandoned.”

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Aṭṭha kusītavatthūni. Idhāvuso, bhikkhunā kammaṃ kātabbaṃ hoti, tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘kammaṃ kho me kātabbaṃ bhavissati, kammaṃ kho pana me karontassa kāyo kilamissati, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati, na vīriyaṃ ārabhati appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāya. Idaṃ paṭhamaṃ kusītavatthu.

(Ṅa) “What are the eight qualities that partake of decline? The eight grounds for laziness. Here, friends, a bhikkhu has work to do, and it occurs to him: ‘I have work to do, and doing it will tire my body. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down, and does not arouse energy for attaining the unattained, achieving the unachieved, and realizing the unrealized. This is the first ground for laziness.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā kammaṃ kataṃ hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho kammaṃ akāsiṃ, kammaṃ kho pana me karontassa kāyo kilanto, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati, na vīriyaṃ ārabhati…pe… idaṃ dutiyaṃ kusītavatthu.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu has done some work. Then it occurs to him: ‘I have done work, and my body is tired from doing work. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down, and does not arouse energy… This is the second ground for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā maggo gantabbo hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘maggo kho me gantabbo bhavissati, maggaṃ kho pana me gacchantassa kāyo kilamissati, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati, na vīriyaṃ ārabhati…pe… idaṃ tatiyaṃ kusītavatthu.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu has a road to travel. It occurs to him: ‘I have a road to travel, and traveling will tire my body. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down, and does not arouse energy… This is the third ground for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā maggo gato hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho maggaṃ agamāsiṃ, maggaṃ kho pana me gacchantassa kāyo kilanto, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati, na vīriyaṃ ārabhati…pe… idaṃ catutthaṃ kusītavatthu.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu has gone on a journey. It occurs to him: ‘I have gone on a journey, and traveling has tired my body. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down, and does not arouse energy… This is the fourth ground for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto na labhati lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto nālatthaṃ lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ, tassa me kāyo kilanto akammañño, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti…pe… idaṃ pañcamaṃ kusītavatthu.

Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu, while walking for alms in a village or town, does not obtain his fill of coarse or fine food. It occurs to him: ‘Indeed, while walking for alms in a village or town, I did not obtain my fill of coarse or fine food, so my body is tired and unfit for work. Suppose I lie down.’ … This is the fifth ground for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto labhati lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto alatthaṃ lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ, tassa me kāyo garuko akammañño, māsācitaṃ maññe, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati…pe… idaṃ chaṭṭhaṃ kusītavatthu.

Again, friends, a monk, while wandering for alms in a village or town, obtains enough coarse or fine food to satisfy him. It occurs to him: ‘I, wandering for alms in a village or town, have obtained enough coarse or fine food to satisfy me. My body is heavy, unfit for work, and I feel as if it is stuffed with beans. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down… This is the sixth basis for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno uppanno hoti appamattako ābādho, tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ appamattako ābādho atthi kappo nipajjituṃ, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati…pe… idaṃ sattamaṃ kusītavatthu.

Again, friends, a monk has a minor illness, and he thinks: ‘I have this minor illness. There is a suitable occasion for lying down. Come, let me lie down.’ So he lies down… This is the seventh basis for laziness.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gilānāvuṭṭhito hoti aciravuṭṭhito gelaññā. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gilānāvuṭṭhito aciravuṭṭhito gelaññā. Tassa me kāyo dubbalo akammañño, handāhaṃ nipajjāmī’ti. So nipajjati…pe… idaṃ aṭṭhamaṃ kusītavatthu. Ime aṭṭha dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

Again, friends, a monk has recovered from illness, not long recovered from his sickness. It occurs to him: ‘I have recovered from illness, not long recovered from my sickness. My body is weak and unfit for service. Suppose I lie down.’ So he lies down… This is the eighth basis for laziness. These eight things partake of decline.

(Ca) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Aṭṭha ārambhavatthūni. Idhāvuso, bhikkhunā kammaṃ kātabbaṃ hoti, tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘kammaṃ kho me kātabbaṃ bhavissati, kammaṃ kho pana me karontena na sukaraṃ buddhānaṃ sāsanaṃ manasikātuṃ, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāyā’ti. So vīriyaṃ ārabhati appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāya. Idaṃ paṭhamaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“And what are the eight qualities that lead to distinction? Eight grounds for effort. Here, friends, a monk has some work to do, and he thinks: ‘I will have to do this work, and while I am doing it, it will not be easy to attend to the Buddha’s teaching. Let me rouse my energy to attain what has not been attained, to realize what has not been realized, and to directly experience what has not been directly experienced.’ So he rouses his energy to attain what has not been attained, to realize what has not been realized, and to directly experience what has not been directly experienced. This is the first ground for effort.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā kammaṃ kataṃ hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho kammaṃ akāsiṃ, kammaṃ kho panāhaṃ karonto nāsakkhiṃ buddhānaṃ sāsanaṃ manasikātuṃ, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ dutiyaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk has done some work, and it occurs to him: ‘I did this work, and while doing it, I was unable to attend to the Buddha’s teaching. Come now, I will rouse up energy …’ This is the second ground for effort.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā maggo gantabbo hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘maggo kho me gantabbo bhavissati, maggaṃ kho pana me gacchantena na sukaraṃ buddhānaṃ sāsanaṃ manasikātuṃ, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ tatiyaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk has to go on a journey. He thinks: ‘I will have to go on a journey, and while going on the journey, it will not be easy to attend to the Buddha’s teaching. Let me rouse up energy…’ This is the third ground for effort.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhunā maggo gato hoti. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho maggaṃ agamāsiṃ, maggaṃ kho panāhaṃ gacchanto nāsakkhiṃ buddhānaṃ sāsanaṃ manasikātuṃ, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ catutthaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk has gone on a journey. He thinks: ‘I have gone on a journey, but while traveling, I was unable to attend to the Buddha’s teaching. Let me arouse energy….’ This is the fourth ground for effort.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto na labhati lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto nālatthaṃ lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ, tassa me kāyo lahuko kammañño, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ pañcamaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk, while wandering for alms in a village or town, does not obtain his fill of coarse or fine food. It occurs to him: ‘Wandering for alms in a village or town, I did not obtain my fill of coarse or fine food. Now my body is light and fit for work. Let me arouse energy …’ This is the fifth ground for effort.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto labhati lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gāmaṃ vā nigamaṃ vā piṇḍāya caranto alatthaṃ lūkhassa vā paṇītassa vā bhojanassa yāvadatthaṃ pāripūriṃ. Tassa me kāyo balavā kammañño, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ chaṭṭhaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk, while wandering for alms in a village or town, obtains enough coarse or fine food to fill himself. He thinks: ‘I, while wandering for alms in a village or town, obtained enough coarse or fine food to fill myself. Now my body is strong and fit for work. Let me arouse energy …’ This is the sixth ground for effort.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhuno uppanno hoti appamattako ābādho. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘uppanno kho me ayaṃ appamattako ābādho ṭhānaṃ kho panetaṃ vijjati, yaṃ me ābādho pavaḍḍheyya, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi…pe… idaṃ sattamaṃ ārambhavatthu.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk has a minor illness. He thinks: ‘This minor illness has arisen in me, and there is a possibility that my illness might worsen. Come, let me rouse energy…’ This is the seventh ground for effort.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu gilānā vuṭṭhito hoti aciravuṭṭhito gelaññā. Tassa evaṃ hoti – ‘ahaṃ kho gilānā vuṭṭhito aciravuṭṭhito gelaññā, ṭhānaṃ kho panetaṃ vijjati, yaṃ me ābādho paccudāvatteyya, handāhaṃ vīriyaṃ ārabhāmi appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāyā’ti. So vīriyaṃ ārabhati appattassa pattiyā anadhigatassa adhigamāya asacchikatassa sacchikiriyāya. Idaṃ aṭṭhamaṃ ārambhavatthu. Ime aṭṭha dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

“Furthermore, friends, a monk has recovered from illness, recently recovered from sickness. It occurs to him: ‘I have recovered from illness, recently recovered from sickness. It is possible that this ailment might recur. Come, let me arouse energy to attain what has not been attained, to realize what has not been realized, and to directly experience what has not been directly experienced.’ He arouses energy to attain what has not been attained, to realize what has not been realized, and to directly experience what has not been directly experienced. This is the eighth ground for effort. These eight things partake of distinction.”

(Cha) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Aṭṭha akkhaṇā asamayā brahmacariyavāsāya. Idhāvuso, tathāgato ca loke uppanno hoti arahaṃ sammāsambuddho, dhammo ca desiyati opasamiko parinibbāniko sambodhagāmī sugatappavedito. Ayañca puggalo nirayaṃ upapanno hoti. Ayaṃ paṭhamo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“(6) What are the eight conditions that are difficult to penetrate? Eight inopportune and unsuitable times for living the spiritual life. Here, friends, the Tathāgata has arisen in the world, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One, and the Dhamma is taught, leading to peace, to final liberation, leading to full enlightenment, proclaimed by the Well Gone One. But this person has been reborn in hell. This is the first inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, tathāgato ca loke uppanno hoti arahaṃ sammāsambuddho, dhammo ca desiyati opasamiko parinibbāniko sambodhagāmī sugatappavedito, ayañca puggalo tiracchānayoniṃ upapanno hoti. Ayaṃ dutiyo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“Furthermore, friends, a Tathāgata has arisen in the world, the Arahant, the Perfectly Self-Enlightened One, and the Dhamma is taught, leading to peace, to final liberation, leading to full enlightenment, proclaimed by the Well Gone One. But this person has been reborn in the animal realm. This is the second inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… pettivisayaṃ upapanno hoti. Ayaṃ tatiyo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“Furthermore, friends… this person is reborn in the realm of the departed spirits. This is the third inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… aññataraṃ dīghāyukaṃ devanikāyaṃ upapanno hoti. Ayaṃ catuttho akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

Furthermore, friends, someone is reborn in one of the long-lived deva realms. This is the fourth inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… paccantimesu janapadesu paccājāto hoti milakkhesu aviññātāresu, yattha natthi gati bhikkhūnaṃ bhikkhunīnaṃ upāsakānaṃ upāsikānaṃ. Ayaṃ pañcamo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“Furthermore, friends… this person is reborn in the borderlands, among barbarians, in an uncivilized region, where there is no access for monks, nuns, laymen, or laywomen. This is the fifth inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… ayañca puggalo majjhimesu janapadesu paccājāto hoti, so ca hoti micchādiṭṭhiko viparītadassano – ‘natthi dinnaṃ, natthi yiṭṭhaṃ, natthi hutaṃ, natthi sukatadukkaṭānaṃ kammānaṃ phalaṃ vipāko, natthi ayaṃ loko, natthi paro loko, natthi mātā, natthi pitā, natthi sattā opapātikā, natthi loke samaṇabrāhmaṇā sammaggatā sammāpaṭipannā ye imañca lokaṃ parañca lokaṃ sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā pavedentī’ti. Ayaṃ chaṭṭho akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“Furthermore, friends, this person is reborn in the central lands, but he holds wrong view and distorted vision: ‘There is no gift, no sacrifice, no offering; there is no fruit or result of good and bad deeds; there is no this world, no other world; there is no mother, no father; there are no beings who are reborn spontaneously; there are no ascetics or brahmins in the world who have attained the right path and practice rightly, who proclaim this world and the other world after realizing it by direct knowledge.’ This is the sixth inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… ayañca puggalo majjhimesu janapadesu paccājāto hoti, so ca hoti duppañño jaḷo eḷamūgo, nappaṭibalo subhāsitadubbhāsitānamatthamaññātuṃ. Ayaṃ sattamo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya.

“Furthermore, friends, this person is reborn in the central lands, but he is unwise, dull, and stammering, unable to understand the meaning of well-spoken and poorly spoken words. This is the seventh inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life.

‘‘Puna caparaṃ…pe… ayañca puggalo majjhimesu janapadesu paccājāto hoti, so ca hoti paññavā ajaḷo aneḷamūgo, paṭibalo subhāsitadubbhāsitānamatthamaññātuṃ. Ayaṃ aṭṭhamo akkhaṇo asamayo brahmacariyavāsāya. Ime aṭṭha dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

“Furthermore, friends… this person is reborn in the central lands, and is wise, not dull, not stammering, able to understand the meaning of well-spoken and poorly spoken words. This is the eighth inopportune and unsuitable time for living the spiritual life. These eight things are difficult to penetrate.”

(Ja) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā uppādetabbā? Aṭṭha mahāpurisavitakkā – appicchassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo mahicchassa. Santuṭṭhassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo asantuṭṭhassa. Pavivittassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo saṅgaṇikārāmassa. Āraddhavīriyassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo kusītassa. Upaṭṭhitasatissāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo muṭṭhassatissa. Samāhitassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo asamāhitassa. Paññavato [paññāvato (sī. pī.)] ayaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo duppaññassa. Nippapañcassāyaṃ dhammo, nāyaṃ dhammo papañcārāmassāti [nippapañcārāmassa ayaṃ dhammo nippapañcaratino, nāyaṃ dhammo papañcārāmassa papañcaratinoti (sī. syā. pī.) aṅguttarepi tatheva dissati. aṭṭhakathāṭīkā pana oloketabbā] ime aṭṭha dhammā uppādetabbā.

“What eight qualities should be developed? The eight thoughts of a great man: This Dhamma is for one of few desires, not for one of many desires. This Dhamma is for the contented, not for the discontented. This Dhamma is for the secluded, not for one who delights in company. This Dhamma is for the energetic, not for the lazy. This Dhamma is for one with established mindfulness, not for the unmindful. This Dhamma is for the collected, not for the uncollected. This Dhamma is for the wise, not for the unwise. This Dhamma is for one who avoids proliferation, not for one who delights in proliferation. These eight qualities should be developed.”

(Jha) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā abhiññeyyā? Aṭṭha abhibhāyatanāni – ajjhattaṃ rūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati parittāni suvaṇṇadubbaṇṇāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti – evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ paṭhamaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Which eight things should be fully understood? The eight bases of mastery: 1. One is inwardly aware of form, and sees outwardly forms that are limited, beautiful or ugly. Mastering them, one is aware: 'I know and see them.' This is the first base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ rūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati appamāṇāni suvaṇṇadubbaṇṇāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti – evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ dutiyaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

“One is inwardly aware of form and sees external forms as limitless, beautiful and ugly. Mastering them, one is aware: 'I know and see them.' This is the second base of mastery.”

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati parittāni suvaṇṇadubbaṇṇāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ tatiyaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Perceiving formlessness internally, one sees external forms that are limited, beautiful or ugly. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the third base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati appamāṇāni suvaṇṇadubbaṇṇāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ catutthaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Internally perceiving formlessness, one sees external forms that are measureless, beautiful or ugly. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the fourth base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati nīlāni nīlavaṇṇāni nīlanidassanāni nīlanibhāsāni. Seyyathāpi nāma umāpupphaṃ nīlaṃ nīlavaṇṇaṃ nīlanidassanaṃ nīlanibhāsaṃ. Seyyathā vā pana taṃ vatthaṃ bārāṇaseyyakaṃ ubhatobhāgavimaṭṭhaṃ nīlaṃ nīlavaṇṇaṃ nīlanidassanaṃ nīlanibhāsaṃ, evameva ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati nīlāni nīlavaṇṇāni nīlanidassanāni nīlanibhāsāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ pañcamaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are blue—blue in color, blue in appearance, blue in radiance. Just as a flax flower is blue, blue in color, blue in appearance, blue in radiance, or like a fine cloth from Benares, smoothed on both sides, is blue, blue in color, blue in appearance, blue in radiance—so too, internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are blue—blue in color, blue in appearance, blue in radiance. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the fifth base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati pītāni pītavaṇṇāni pītanidassanāni pītanibhāsāni. Seyyathāpi nāma kaṇikārapupphaṃ pītaṃ pītavaṇṇaṃ pītanidassanaṃ pītanibhāsaṃ. Seyyathā vā pana taṃ vatthaṃ bārāṇaseyyakaṃ ubhatobhāgavimaṭṭhaṃ pītaṃ pītavaṇṇaṃ pītanidassanaṃ pītanibhāsaṃ, evameva ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati pītāni pītavaṇṇāni pītanidassanāni pītanibhāsāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ chaṭṭhaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are yellow—yellow in color, yellow in appearance, yellow in radiance. Just as a kanikāra flower is yellow, yellow in color, yellow in appearance, yellow in radiance, or like a fine cloth from Benares, smoothed on both sides, is yellow, yellow in color, yellow in appearance, yellow in radiance—so too, internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are yellow—yellow in color, yellow in appearance, yellow in radiance. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the sixth base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati lohitakāni lohitakavaṇṇāni lohitakanidassanāni lohitakanibhāsāni. Seyyathāpi nāma bandhujīvakapupphaṃ lohitakaṃ lohitakavaṇṇaṃ lohitakanidassanaṃ lohitakanibhāsaṃ, seyyathā vā pana taṃ vatthaṃ bārāṇaseyyakaṃ ubhatobhāgavimaṭṭhaṃ lohitakaṃ lohitakavaṇṇaṃ lohitakanidassanaṃ lohitakanibhāsaṃ, evameva ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati lohitakāni lohitakavaṇṇāni lohitakanidassanāni lohitakanibhāsāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ sattamaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ.

Internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are red—red in color, red in appearance, red in radiance. Just as a bandhujīvaka flower is red, red in color, red in appearance, red in radiance, or like a fine cloth from Benares, smoothed on both sides, is red, red in color, red in appearance, red in radiance—so too, internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are red—red in color, red in appearance, red in radiance. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the seventh base of mastery.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati odātāni odātavaṇṇāni odātanidassanāni odātanibhāsāni. Seyyathāpi nāma osadhitārakā odātā odātavaṇṇā odātanidassanā odātanibhāsā, seyyathā vā pana taṃ vatthaṃ bārāṇaseyyakaṃ ubhatobhāgavimaṭṭhaṃ odātaṃ odātavaṇṇaṃ odātanidassanaṃ odātanibhāsaṃ, evameva ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati odātāni odātavaṇṇāni odātanidassanāni odātanibhāsāni, ‘tāni abhibhuyya jānāmi passāmī’ti evaṃsaññī hoti. Idaṃ aṭṭhamaṃ abhibhāyatanaṃ. Ime aṭṭha dhammā abhiññeyyā.

Internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are white—white in color, white in appearance, white in radiance. Just as the morning star is white, white in color, white in appearance, white in radiance, or like a fine cloth from Benares, smoothed on both sides, is white, white in color, white in appearance, white in radiance—so too, internally perceiving formless, one sees external forms that are white—white in color, white in appearance, white in radiance. ‘Having overcome them, I know and see,’ thus one perceives. This is the eighth base of mastery. These eight things are to be directly known.

(Ña) ‘‘katame aṭṭha dhammā sacchikātabbā? Aṭṭha vimokkhā – rūpī rūpāni passati. Ayaṃ paṭhamo vimokkho.

(Ña) What eight qualities are to be realized? The eight liberations: One possessing form sees forms. This is the first liberation.

‘‘Ajjhattaṃ arūpasaññī eko bahiddhā rūpāni passati. Ayaṃ dutiyo vimokkho.

Internally perceiving formlessness, one sees forms externally. This is the second liberation.

‘‘Subhanteva adhimutto hoti. Ayaṃ tatiyo vimokkho.

One is intent on the beautiful. This is the third liberation.

‘‘Sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ catuttho vimokkho.

With the complete surmounting of perceptions of form, with the disappearance of perceptions of sensory impact, with non-attention to perceptions of diversity, aware that ‘space is infinite,’ one enters and dwells in the dimension of infinite space. This is the fourth liberation.

‘‘Sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ pañcamo vimokkho.

Having entirely transcended the dimension of the infinitude of space, one enters and dwells in the dimension of the infinitude of consciousness, thinking: ‘Consciousness is infinite.’ This is the fifth liberation.

‘‘Sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ chaṭṭho vimokkho.

Having entirely transcended the dimension of the infinitude of consciousness, aware that ‘There is nothing,’ one enters and dwells in the dimension of nothingness. This is the sixth liberation.

‘‘Sabbaso ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ sattamo vimokkho.

Having entirely transcended the base of nothingness, one enters and dwells in the base of neither perception nor non-perception. This is the seventh liberation.

‘‘Sabbaso nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ayaṃ aṭṭhamo vimokkho. Ime aṭṭha dhammā sacchikātabbā.

Having entirely transcended the base of neither perception nor non-perception, one enters and dwells in the cessation of perception and feeling. This is the eighth liberation. These eight qualities are to be realized.

‘‘Iti ime asīti dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

Thus, these eighty things are actual, real, true, not false, not otherwise, rightly fully awakened by the Tathāgata.

## Nava dhammā

### Nine Things

359. ‘‘Nava dhammā bahukārā…pe… nava dhammā sacchikātabbā.

359.Nine things are helpful… nine things are to be realized.

(Ka) ‘‘katame nava dhammā bahukārā? Nava yonisomanasikāramūlakā dhammā, yonisomanasikaroto pāmojjaṃ jāyati, pamuditassa pīti jāyati, pītimanassa kāyo passambhati, passaddhakāyo sukhaṃ vedeti, sukhino cittaṃ samādhiyati, samāhite citte yathābhūtaṃ jānāti passati, yathābhūtaṃ jānaṃ passaṃ nibbindati, nibbindaṃ virajjati, virāgā vimuccati. Ime nava dhammā bahukārā.

(Ka) "What are the nine things that are greatly helpful? Nine things rooted in proper attention: for one who attends properly, joy arises; for one who is joyful, rapture arises; for one whose mind is rapturous, the body becomes tranquil; with a tranquil body, one feels happiness; with happiness, the mind becomes collected; with a collected mind, one knows and sees things as they actually are; knowing and seeing things as they actually are, one becomes disenchanted; through disenchantment, one becomes dispassionate; through dispassion, one is liberated. These are the nine things that are greatly helpful.

(Kha) ‘‘katame nava dhammā bhāvetabbā? Nava pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgāni – sīlavisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, cittavisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, diṭṭhivisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, kaṅkhāvitaraṇavisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, maggāmaggañāṇadassana – visuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, paṭipadāñāṇadassanavisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, ñāṇadassanavisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, paññāvisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ, vimuttivisuddhi pārisuddhipadhāniyaṅgaṃ. Ime nava dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) "What are the nine things to be developed? The nine factors that lead to purity: purity of virtue is a factor that leads to purity, purity of mind is a factor that leads to purity, purity of view is a factor that leads to purity, purity by overcoming doubt is a factor that leads to purity, purity of knowledge and vision of what is the path and what is not the path is a factor that leads to purity, purity of knowledge and vision of the way is a factor that leads to purity, purity of knowledge and vision is a factor that leads to purity, purity of wisdom is a factor that leads to purity, purity of liberation is a factor that leads to purity. These are the nine things to be developed.

(Ga) ‘‘katame nava dhammā pariññeyyā? Nava sattāvāsā – santāvuso, sattā nānattakāyā nānattasaññino, seyyathāpi manussā ekacce ca devā ekacce ca vinipātikā. Ayaṃ paṭhamo sattāvāso.

(Ga) "What are the nine things to be fully understood? The nine abodes of beings: There are, friends, beings with diverse bodies and diverse perceptions, such as human beings, some gods, and some beings in the states of deprivation. This is the first abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā nānattakāyā ekattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā brahmakāyikā paṭhamābhinibbattā. Ayaṃ dutiyo sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings with diverse bodies but unified perception, such as the gods of Brahmā’s Host who are newly reborn. This is the second abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā ekattakāyā nānattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā ābhassarā. Ayaṃ tatiyo sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings with unified bodies and diverse perceptions, such as the Ābhassara gods. This is the third abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā ekattakāyā ekattasaññino, seyyathāpi devā subhakiṇhā. Ayaṃ catuttho sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings with unified bodies and unified perceptions, such as the Subhakiṇha gods. This is the fourth abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā asaññino appaṭisaṃvedino, seyyathāpi devā asaññasattā. Ayaṃ pañcamo sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings who are unpercipient and do not experience anything, such as the Asaññasatta gods. This is the fifth abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā paṭighasaññānaṃ atthaṅgamā nānattasaññānaṃ amanasikārā ‘ananto ākāso’ti ākāsānañcāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ chaṭṭho sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings who, with the complete transcending of perceptions of form, with the passing away of perceptions of resistance, and not attending to perceptions of diversity, have attained the dimension of infinite space, [perceiving,] ‘Infinite space.’ This is the sixth abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ sattamo sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings who, having entirely transcended the dimension of infinite space, have attained the dimension of infinite consciousness, [perceiving,] ‘Infinite consciousness.’ This is the seventh abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ aṭṭhamo sattāvāso.

There are, friends, beings who, having entirely transcended the dimension of infinite consciousness, have attained the dimension of nothingness, [perceiving,] ‘There is nothing.’ This is the eighth abode of beings.

‘‘Santāvuso, sattā sabbaso ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma nevasaññānāsaññāyatanūpagā. Ayaṃ navamo sattāvāso. Ime nava dhammā pariññeyyā.

There are, friends, beings who, having entirely transcended the dimension of nothingness, have attained the dimension of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. This is the ninth abode of beings. These nine things are to be fully understood.

(Gha) ‘‘katame nava dhammā pahātabbā? Nava taṇhāmūlakā dhammā – taṇhaṃ paṭicca pariyesanā, pariyesanaṃ paṭicca lābho, lābhaṃ paṭicca vinicchayo, vinicchayaṃ paṭicca chandarāgo, chandarāgaṃ paṭicca ajjhosānaṃ, ajjhosānaṃ paṭicca pariggaho, pariggahaṃ paṭicca macchariyaṃ, macchariyaṃ paṭicca ārakkho, ārakkhādhikaraṇaṃ [ārakkhādhikaraṇaṃ paṭicca (syā. pī. ka.)] daṇḍādānasatthādānakalahaviggahavivādatuvaṃtuvaṃpesuññamusāvādā aneke pāpakā akusalā dhammā sambhavanti. Ime nava dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) "What are the nine things to be abandoned? Nine things rooted in craving: conditioned by craving, seeking arises; conditioned by seeking, gain arises; conditioned by gain, ascertainment arises; conditioned by ascertainment, desire and lust arise; conditioned by desire and lust, attachment arises; conditioned by attachment, possessiveness arises; conditioned by possessiveness, stinginess arises; conditioned by stinginess, guarding arises; and because of guarding, many evil, unwholesome states arise—taking up sticks and weapons, quarrels, disputes, arguments, contention, abusive speech, slander, and false speech. These are the nine things to be abandoned.

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame nava dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Nava āghātavatthūni – ‘anatthaṃ me acarī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati, ‘anatthaṃ me caratī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati, ‘anatthaṃ me carissatī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati; ‘piyassa me manāpassa anatthaṃ acarī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati…pe… ‘anatthaṃ caratī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati…pe… ‘anatthaṃ carissatī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati; ‘appiyassa me amanāpassa atthaṃ acarī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati…pe… ‘atthaṃ caratī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati…pe… ‘atthaṃ carissatī’ti āghātaṃ bandhati. Ime nava dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) "What are the nine things that partake of decline? Nine grounds for ill will: one binds ill will thinking, ‘They have done me harm’; one binds ill will thinking, ‘They are doing me harm’; one binds ill will thinking, ‘They will do me harm’; one binds ill will thinking, ‘They have done harm to someone dear and agreeable to me’… one binds ill will thinking, ‘They are doing harm to someone dear and agreeable to me’… one binds ill will thinking, ‘They will do harm to someone dear and agreeable to me’; one binds ill will thinking, ‘They have done good to someone undear and disagreeable to me’… one binds ill will thinking, ‘They are doing good to someone undear and disagreeable to me’… one binds ill will thinking, ‘They will do good to someone undear and disagreeable to me.’ These nine things partake of decline.

(Ca) ‘‘katame nava dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Nava āghātapaṭivinayā – ‘anatthaṃ me acari, taṃ kutettha labbhā’ti āghātaṃ paṭivineti; ‘anatthaṃ me carati, taṃ kutettha labbhā’ti āghātaṃ paṭivineti; ‘anatthaṃ me carissati, taṃ kutettha labbhā’ti āghātaṃ paṭivineti; ‘piyassa me manāpassa anatthaṃ acari…pe… anatthaṃ carati…pe… anatthaṃ carissati, taṃ kutettha labbhā’ti āghātaṃ paṭivineti; ‘appiyassa me amanāpassa atthaṃ acari…pe… atthaṃ carati…pe… atthaṃ carissati, taṃ kutettha labbhā’ti āghātaṃ paṭivineti. Ime nava dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) "What are the nine things that are conducive to distinction? Nine ways to remove ill will: one removes ill will thinking, ‘They have done me harm—how is it possible to obtain that?’; one removes ill will thinking, ‘They are doing me harm—how is it possible to obtain that?’; one removes ill will thinking, ‘They will do me harm—how is it possible to obtain that?’; one removes ill will thinking, ‘They have done harm to someone dear and agreeable to me’… one removes ill will thinking, ‘They are doing harm’… one removes ill will thinking, ‘They will do harm—how is it possible to obtain that?’; one removes ill will thinking, ‘They have done good to someone undear and disagreeable to me’… one removes ill will thinking, ‘They are doing good’… one removes ill will thinking, ‘They will do good—how is it possible to obtain that?’ These nine things are conducive to distinction.

(Cha) ‘‘katame nava dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Nava nānattā – dhātunānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati phassanānattaṃ, phassanānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati vedanānānattaṃ, vedanānānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati saññānānattaṃ, saññānānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati saṅkappanānattaṃ, saṅkappanānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati chandanānattaṃ, chandanānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati pariḷāhanānattaṃ, pariḷāhanānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati pariyesanānānattaṃ, pariyesanānānattaṃ paṭicca uppajjati lābhanānattaṃ. Ime nava dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

(Cha) "What are the nine things that are hard to penetrate? Nine diversities: dependent on diversity of elements, diverse contact arises; dependent on diversity of contact, diverse feeling arises; dependent on diversity of feeling, diverse perception arises; dependent on diversity of perception, diverse thought arises; dependent on diversity of thought, diverse desire arises; dependent on diversity of desire, diverse burning arises; dependent on diversity of burning, diverse seeking arises; dependent on diversity of seeking, diverse gain arises. These nine things are hard to penetrate.

(Ja) ‘‘katame nava dhammā uppādetabbā? Nava saññā – asubhasaññā, maraṇasaññā, āhārepaṭikūlasaññā, sabbalokeanabhiratisaññā [anabhiratasaññā (syā. ka.)], aniccasaññā, anicce dukkhasaññā, dukkhe anattasaññā, pahānasaññā, virāgasaññā. Ime nava dhammā uppādetabbā.

(Ja) "What are the nine things that should be developed? Nine perceptions: the perception of unattractiveness, the perception of death, the perception of revulsion towards food, the perception of non-delight in the entire world, the perception of impermanence, the perception of suffering in impermanence, the perception of no-self in suffering, the perception of abandoning, the perception of dispassion. These nine things should be developed.

(Jha) ‘‘katame nava dhammā abhiññeyyā? Nava anupubbavihārā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu vivicceva kāmehi vivicca akusalehi dhammehi savitakkaṃ savicāraṃ vivekajaṃ pītisukhaṃ paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Vitakkavicārānaṃ vūpasamā…pe… dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Pītiyā ca virāgā …pe… tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sukhassa ca pahānā…pe… catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sabbaso rūpasaññānaṃ samatikkamā…pe… ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sabbaso ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘anantaṃ viññāṇa’nti viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sabbaso viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samatikkamma ‘natthi kiñcī’ti ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sabbaso ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ upasampajja viharati. Sabbaso nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ samatikkamma saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ upasampajja viharati. Ime nava dhammā abhiññeyyā.

(Jha) "What are the nine things to be directly known? The nine progressive abidings: Here, friends, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unwholesome states, enters and dwells in the first jhāna, which is accompanied by thought and examination, born of seclusion, filled with rapture and joy. With the subsiding of thought and examination... he enters and dwells in the second jhāna. With the fading away of rapture... he enters and dwells in the third jhāna. With the abandoning of pleasure... he enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna. With the complete transcending of perceptions of form... he enters and dwells in the base of infinite space. With the complete transcending of the base of infinite space... he enters and dwells in the base of infinite consciousness. With the complete transcending of the base of infinite consciousness... he enters and dwells in the base of nothingness. With the complete transcending of the base of nothingness... he enters and dwells in the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. With the complete transcending of the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception... he enters and dwells in the cessation of perception and feeling. These are the nine things to be directly known."

(Ña) ‘‘katame nava dhammā sacchikātabbā? Nava anupubbanirodhā – paṭhamaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa kāmasaññā niruddhā hoti, dutiyaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa vitakkavicārā niruddhā honti, tatiyaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa pīti niruddhā hoti, catutthaṃ jhānaṃ samāpannassa assāsapassāssā niruddhā honti, ākāsānañcāyatanaṃ samāpannassa rūpasaññā niruddhā hoti, viññāṇañcāyatanaṃ samāpannassa ākāsānañcāyatanasaññā niruddhā hoti, ākiñcaññāyatanaṃ samāpannassa viññāṇañcāyatanasaññā niruddhā hoti, nevasaññānāsaññāyatanaṃ samāpannassa ākiñcaññāyatanasaññā niruddhā hoti, saññāvedayitanirodhaṃ samāpannassa saññā ca vedanā ca niruddhā honti. Ime nava dhammā sacchikātabbā.

(Ña) "What are the nine things to be realized? The nine successive cessations: For one who has attained the first jhāna, sensual perception ceases. For one who has attained the second jhāna, thought and examination cease. For one who has attained the third jhāna, rapture ceases. For one who has attained the fourth jhāna, in-breaths and out-breaths cease. For one who has attained the base of infinite space, perception of form ceases. For one who has attained the base of infinite consciousness, perception of the base of infinite space ceases. For one who has attained the base of nothingness, perception of the base of infinite consciousness ceases. For one who has attained the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, perception of the base of nothingness ceases. For one who has attained the cessation of perception and feeling, perception and feeling cease. These are the nine things to be realized."

‘‘Iti ime navuti dhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā.

"Thus, these ninety things are factual, true, real, accurate, not otherwise, rightly fully awakened to by the Tathāgata."

## Dasa dhammā

### Ten qualities

360. ‘‘Dasa dhammā bahukārā…pe… dasa dhammā sacchikātabbā.

360."Ten qualities are of great benefit... ten qualities are to be directly experienced."

(Ka) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā bahukārā? Dasa nāthakaraṇādhammā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu sīlavā hoti, pātimokkhasaṃvarasaṃvuto viharati ācāragocarasampanno, aṇumattesu vajjesu bhayadassāvī samādāya sikkhati sikkhāpadesu, yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu sīlavā hoti…pe… sikkhati sikkhāpadesu. Ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

(Ka) "What are the ten qualities of great benefit? The ten refuge-making qualities: Here, friends, a bhikkhu is virtuous, he dwells restrained by the Pātimokkha, accomplished in conduct and resort, seeing danger in the slightest faults, and he undertakes and trains in the training rules. That a bhikkhu is virtuous, he dwells restrained by the Pātimokkha, accomplished in conduct and resort, seeing danger in the slightest faults, and he undertakes and trains in the training rules—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu bahussuto …pe… diṭṭhiyā suppaṭividdhā, yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu bahussuto…pe… ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is learned, he has memorized the teachings, retained them, accumulated them, and examined them with wisdom, and has thoroughly penetrated [the Dhamma] by view. That a bhikkhu is learned, he has memorized the teachings, retained them, accumulated them, and examined them with wisdom, and has thoroughly penetrated [the Dhamma] by view—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu kalyāṇamitto hoti kalyāṇasahāyo kalyāṇasampavaṅko. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… kalyāṇasampavaṅko. Ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu has good friends, good companions, good comrades. That a bhikkhu has good friends, good companions, good comrades—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu suvaco hoti sovacassakaraṇehi dhammehi samannāgato, khamo padakkhiṇaggāhī anusāsaniṃ. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… anusāsaniṃ. Ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is easy to admonish, possessed of qualities conducive to being easily admonished, patient, and receptive to instruction. That a bhikkhu is easy to admonish, possessed of qualities conducive to being easily admonished, patient, and receptive to instruction—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu yāni tāni sabrahmacārīnaṃ uccāvacāni kiṃkaraṇīyāni tattha dakkho hoti analaso tatrupāyāya vīmaṃsāya samannāgato, alaṃ kātuṃ, alaṃ saṃvidhātuṃ. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… alaṃ saṃvidhātuṃ. Ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is skilled and tireless in whatever various duties his fellow practitioners have, he is endowed with the investigation and means to deal with them, being able to do them and arrange them properly. That a bhikkhu is skilled and tireless in whatever various duties his fellow practitioners have, he is endowed with the investigation and means to deal with them, being able to do them and arrange them properly—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu dhammakāmo hoti piyasamudāhāro abhidhamme abhivinaye uḷārapāmojjo. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… uḷārapāmojjo. Ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu loves the Dhamma, delights in pleasant conversation, and is filled with great joy in the Abhidhamma and the Abhivinaya. That a bhikkhu loves the Dhamma, delights in pleasant conversation, and is filled with great joy in the Abhidhamma and the Abhivinaya—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu santuṭṭho hoti itarītarehi cīvarapiṇḍapātasenāsanagilānappaccayabhesajjaparikkhārehi. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu …pe… ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is content with any robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicinal requisites for the sick. That a bhikkhu is content with any robes, almsfood, lodging, and medicinal requisites for the sick—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu āraddhavīriyo viharati…pe… kusalesu dhammesu. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu dwells with aroused energy for abandoning unwholesome qualities and acquiring wholesome qualities, steadfast, firm in exertion, not laying aside the burden in wholesome qualities. That a bhikkhu dwells with aroused energy for abandoning unwholesome qualities and acquiring wholesome qualities, steadfast, firm in exertion, not laying aside the burden in wholesome qualities—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu satimā hoti, paramena satinepakkena samannāgato, cirakatampi cirabhāsitampi saritā anussaritā. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is mindful, possessing supreme mindfulness and skill in mindfulness, remembering and recollecting what was done and said long ago. That a bhikkhu is mindful, possessing supreme mindfulness and skill in mindfulness, remembering and recollecting what was done and said long ago—this too is a refuge-making quality."

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, āvuso, bhikkhu paññavā hoti udayatthagāminiyā paññāya samannāgato, ariyāya nibbedhikāya sammā dukkhakkhayagāminiyā. Yaṃpāvuso, bhikkhu…pe… ayampi dhammo nāthakaraṇo. Ime dasa dhammā bahukārā.

"Furthermore, friends, a bhikkhu is wise, endowed with the wisdom that leads to the arising and vanishing (of phenomena), noble, penetrative, leading to the complete destruction of suffering. That a bhikkhu is wise, endowed with the wisdom that leads to the arising and vanishing (of phenomena), noble, penetrative, leading to the complete destruction of suffering—this too is a refuge-making quality. These ten qualities are of great benefit."

(Kha) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā bhāvetabbā? Dasa kasiṇāyatanāni – pathavīkasiṇameko sañjānāti uddhaṃ adho tiriyaṃ advayaṃ appamāṇaṃ. Āpokasiṇameko sañjānāti…pe… tejokasiṇameko sañjānāti… vāyokasiṇameko sañjānāti… nīlakasiṇameko sañjānāti… pītakasiṇameko sañjānāti… lohitakasiṇameko sañjānāti… odātakasiṇameko sañjānāti… ākāsakasiṇameko sañjānāti… viññāṇakasiṇameko sañjānāti uddhaṃ adho tiriyaṃ advayaṃ appamāṇaṃ. Ime dasa dhammā bhāvetabbā.

(Kha) "What ten things should be developed? The ten kasiṇa-spheres: One cognizes the earth kasiṇa as above, below, across, without duality, boundless. One cognizes the water kasiṇa ... the fire kasiṇa ... the air kasiṇa ... the blue kasiṇa ... the yellow kasiṇa ... the red kasiṇa ... the white kasiṇa ... the space kasiṇa ... One cognizes the consciousness kasiṇa as above, below, across, without duality, boundless. These ten things should be developed."

(Ga) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā pariññeyyā? Dasāyatanāni – cakkhāyatanaṃ, rūpāyatanaṃ, sotāyatanaṃ, saddāyatanaṃ, ghānāyatanaṃ, gandhāyatanaṃ, jivhāyatanaṃ, rasāyatanaṃ, kāyāyatanaṃ, phoṭṭhabbāyatanaṃ. Ime dasa dhammā pariññeyyā.

(Ga) "What ten things should be fully comprehended? The ten sense bases: the eye base, the form base, the ear base, the sound base, the nose base, the odour base, the tongue base, the taste base, the body base, the tangible base. These ten things should be fully comprehended."

(Gha) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā pahātabbā? Dasa micchattā – micchādiṭṭhi, micchāsaṅkappo, micchāvācā, micchākammanto, micchāājīvo, micchāvāyāmo, micchāsati, micchāsamādhi, micchāñāṇaṃ, micchāvimutti. Ime dasa dhammā pahātabbā.

(Gha) "What ten things should be abandoned? The ten wrongnesses: wrong view, wrong thought, wrong speech, wrong action, wrong livelihood, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, wrong samādhi, wrong knowledge, wrong liberation. These ten things should be abandoned."

(Ṅa) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā hānabhāgiyā? Dasa akusalakammapathā – pāṇātipāto, adinnādānaṃ, kāmesumicchācāro, musāvādo, pisuṇā vācā, pharusā vācā, samphappalāpo, abhijjhā, byāpādo, micchādiṭṭhi. Ime dasa dhammā hānabhāgiyā.

(Ṅa) "What are the ten qualities that partake of decline? The ten unwholesome courses of action: killing living beings, taking what is not given, sexual misconduct, false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, idle chatter, covetousness, ill will, wrong view. These ten qualities partake of decline."

(Ca) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā visesabhāgiyā? Dasa kusalakammapathā – pāṇātipātā veramaṇī, adinnādānā veramaṇī, kāmesumicchācārā veramaṇī, musāvādā veramaṇī, pisuṇāya vācāya veramaṇī, pharusāya vācāya veramaṇī, samphappalāpā veramaṇī, anabhijjhā, abyāpādo, sammādiṭṭhi. Ime dasa dhammā visesabhāgiyā.

(Ca) "What are the ten qualities that lead to distinction? The ten wholesome courses of action: abstaining from killing living beings, abstaining from taking what is not given, abstaining from sexual misconduct, abstaining from false speech, abstaining from malicious speech, abstaining from harsh speech, abstaining from idle chatter, non-covetousness, non-ill will, right view. These ten qualities lead to distinction."

(Cha) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā duppaṭivijjhā? Dasa ariyavāsā – idhāvuso, bhikkhu pañcaṅgavippahīno hoti, chaḷaṅgasamannāgato, ekārakkho, caturāpasseno, paṇunnapaccekasacco, samavayasaṭṭhesano, anāvilasaṅkappo, passaddhakāyasaṅkhāro, suvimuttacitto, suvimuttapañño.

(Cha) "What are the ten states difficult to penetrate? The ten noble abodes: here, friend, a bhikkhu has abandoned five factors, is endowed with six factors, has a single protection, has four supports, has discarded truths held individually, has a search that is diminished, has unclouded intentions, has calmed bodily fabrication, is well liberated in mind, and is well liberated in wisdom."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu pañcaṅgavippahīno hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno kāmacchando pahīno hoti, byāpādo pahīno hoti, thinamiddhaṃ pahīnaṃ hoti, uddhaccakukkuccaṃ pahīnaṃ hoti, vicikicchā pahīnā hoti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu pañcaṅgavippahīno hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has abandoned five factors? Here, friend, a bhikkhu has abandoned sensual desire, abandoned ill will, abandoned sloth and torpor, abandoned restlessness and remorse, and abandoned doubt. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu has abandoned five factors."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu chaḷaṅgasamannāgato hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā neva sumano hoti na dummano, upekkhako viharati sato sampajāno. Sotena saddaṃ sutvā…pe… ghānena gandhaṃ ghāyitvā… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā… kāyena phoṭṭhabbaṃ phusitvā… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya neva sumano hoti na dummano, upekkhako viharati sato sampajāno. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu chaḷaṅgasamannāgato hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who possesses six factors? Here, friend, a bhikkhu, having seen a form with the eye, is neither glad nor sad, but dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending. Having heard a sound with the ear… smelled an odor with the nose… tasted a flavor with the tongue… touched a tangible with the body… cognized a mental object with the mind, he is neither glad nor sad, but dwells equanimous, mindful, and clearly comprehending. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu possesses six factors."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu ekārakkho hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu satārakkhena cetasā samannāgato hoti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu ekārakkho hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has one guard? Here, friend, a bhikkhu is endowed with a mind guarded by mindfulness. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu has one guard."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu caturāpasseno hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu saṅkhāyekaṃ paṭisevati, saṅkhāyekaṃ adhivāseti, saṅkhāyekaṃ parivajjeti, saṅkhāyekaṃ vinodeti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu caturāpasseno hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has four supports? Here, friend, a bhikkhu uses something with reflection, endures something with reflection, avoids something with reflection, dispels something with reflection. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu is one who has four supports."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu paṇunnapaccekasacco hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno yāni tāni puthusamaṇabrāhmaṇānaṃ puthupaccekasaccāni, sabbāni tāni nunnāni honti paṇunnāni cattāni vantāni muttāni pahīnāni paṭinissaṭṭhāni. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu paṇunnapaccekasacco hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has refuted individual truths? Here, friend, whatever various individual truths of different ascetics and brahmins, all those are refuted, repelled, rejected, renounced, abandoned, and relinquished. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu has refuted individual truths."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu samavayasaṭṭhesano hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno kāmesanā pahīnā hoti, bhavesanā pahīnā hoti, brahmacariyesanā paṭippassaddhā. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu samavayasaṭṭhesano hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has exhausted all quests? Here, friend, a bhikkhu has abandoned the quest for sensual pleasures, abandoned the quest for existence, and calmed the quest for a holy life. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu has exhausted all quests."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu anāvilasaṅkappā hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno kāmasaṅkappo pahīno hoti, byāpādasaṅkappo pahīno hoti, vihiṃsāsaṅkappo pahīno hoti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu anāvilasaṅkappo hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one with unclouded thoughts? Here, friend, a bhikkhu has abandoned sensual thoughts, abandoned thoughts of ill-will, and abandoned thoughts of violence. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu is one with unclouded thoughts."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu passaddhakāyasaṅkhāro hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu sukhassa ca pahānā dukkhassa ca pahānā pubbeva somanassadomanassānaṃ atthaṅgamā adukkhamasukhaṃ upekkhāsatipārisuddhiṃ catutthaṃ jhānaṃ upasampajja viharati. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu passaddhakāyasaṅkhāro hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu one who has calmed bodily formation? Here, friend, a bhikkhu, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the previous disappearance of joy and sorrow, enters and dwells in the fourth jhāna, which is neither painful nor pleasant and possesses purity of mindfulness due to equanimity. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu has calmed bodily formation."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu suvimuttacitto hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhuno rāgā cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti, dosā cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti, mohā cittaṃ vimuttaṃ hoti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu suvimuttacitto hoti.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu well-liberated in mind? Here, friend, a bhikkhu's mind is liberated from lust, liberated from hatred, liberated from delusion. Thus, friend, a bhikkhu is well-liberated in mind."

‘‘Kathañcāvuso, bhikkhu suvimuttapañño hoti? Idhāvuso, bhikkhu ‘rāgo me pahīno ucchinnamūlo tālāvatthukato anabhāvaṃkato āyatiṃ anuppādadhammo’ti pajānāti. ‘Doso me pahīno…pe… āyatiṃ anuppādadhammo’ti pajānāti. ‘Moho me pahīno …pe… āyatiṃ anuppādadhammo’ti pajānāti. Evaṃ kho, āvuso, bhikkhu suvimuttapañño hoti. Ime dasa dhammā duppaṭivijjhā.

"And how, friend, is a bhikkhu liberated by wisdom? Here, friend, a bhikkhu understands: 'Greed has been abandoned by me, its root cut off, made like a palm stump, obliterated, so that it is no longer subject to future arising.' 'Ill-will has been abandoned by me... 'Delusion has been abandoned by me, its root cut off, made like a palm stump, obliterated, so that it is no longer subject to future arising.' Thus, friend, a bhikkhu is liberated by wisdom. These ten things are hard to penetrate."

(Ja) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā uppādetabbā? Dasa saññā – asubhasaññā, maraṇasaññā, āhārepaṭikūlasaññā, sabbalokeanabhiratisaññā, aniccasaññā, anicce dukkhasaññā, dukkhe anattasaññā, pahānasaññā, virāgasaññā, nirodhasaññā. Ime dasa dhammā uppādetabbā.

"What ten things should be developed? Ten perceptions: the perception of unattractiveness, the perception of death, the perception of revulsion towards food, the perception of non-delight in the entire world, the perception of impermanence, the perception of suffering in impermanence, the perception of not-self in suffering, the perception of abandoning, the perception of dispassion, the perception of cessation. These ten things should be developed."

(Jha) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā abhiññeyyā? Dasa nijjaravatthūni – sammādiṭṭhissa micchādiṭṭhi nijjiṇṇā hoti. Ye ca micchādiṭṭhipaccayā aneke pāpakā akusalā dhammā sambhavanti, te cassa nijjiṇṇā honti. Sammāsaṅkappassa micchāsaṅkappo…pe… sammāvācassa micchāvācā… sammākammantassa micchākammanto… sammāājīvassa micchāājīvo… sammāvāyāmassa micchāvāyāmo… sammāsatissa micchāsati… sammāsamādhissa micchāsamādhi… sammāñāṇassa micchāñāṇaṃ nijjiṇṇaṃ hoti. Sammāvimuttissa micchāvimutti nijjiṇṇā hoti. Ye ca micchāvimuttipaccayā aneke pāpakā akusalā dhammā sambhavanti, te cassa nijjiṇṇā honti. Ime dasa dhammā abhiññeyyā.

"What ten things should be fully understood? The ten grounds for wearing away: for one with right view, wrong view is worn away, and whatever numerous bad, unwholesome states arise dependent on wrong view, these too are worn away. For one with right intention, wrong intention is worn away... For one with right speech, wrong speech is worn away... For one with right action, wrong action is worn away... For one with right livelihood, wrong livelihood is worn away... For one with right effort, wrong effort is worn away... For one with right mindfulness, wrong mindfulness is worn away... For one with right samādhi, wrong samādhi is worn away... For one with right knowledge, wrong knowledge is worn away. For one with right liberation, wrong liberation is worn away, and whatever numerous bad, unwholesome states arise dependent on wrong liberation, these too are worn away. These are the ten things that should be fully understood."

(Ña) ‘‘katame dasa dhammā sacchikātabbā? Dasa asekkhā dhammā – asekkhā sammādiṭṭhi, asekkho sammāsaṅkappo, asekkhā sammāvācā, asekkho sammākammanto, asekkho sammāājīvo, asekkho sammāvāyāmo, asekkhā sammāsati, asekkho sammāsamādhi, asekkhaṃ sammāñāṇaṃ, asekkhā sammāvimutti. Ime dasa dhammā sacchikātabbā.

"What ten things are to be directly experienced? The ten qualities of one beyond training—the right view of one beyond training, the right thought of one beyond training, the right speech of one beyond training, the right action of one beyond training, the right livelihood of one beyond training, the right effort of one beyond training, the right mindfulness of one beyond training, the right samādhi of one beyond training, the right knowledge of one beyond training, and the right liberation of one beyond training. These ten things are to be directly experienced."

‘‘Iti ime satadhammā bhūtā tacchā tathā avitathā anaññathā sammā tathāgatena abhisambuddhā’’ti. Idamavocāyasmā sāriputto. Attamanā te bhikkhū āyasmato sāriputtassa bhāsitaṃ abhinandunti.

"Thus, these hundred qualities are factual, real, accurate, not false, not otherwise, rightly fully awakened to by the Tathagata." This was said by the Venerable Sariputta. The bhikkhus were gratified and delighted in the Venerable Sariputta's words.

Dasuttarasuttaṃ niṭṭhitaṃ ekādasamaṃ.

The Dasuttara Sutta is concluded. The eleventh.

Pāthikavaggo [pāṭikavaggo (sī. syā. pī.)] niṭṭhito.

The Pāthika Chapter is finished.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its summary:

Pāthiko ca [pāṭikañca (syā. kaṃ.)] udumbaraṃ [pāṭikodumbarīceva (sī. pī.)], cakkavatti aggaññakaṃ;Sampasādanapāsādaṃ [sampasādañca pāsādaṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)], mahāpurisalakkhaṇaṃ.

The Pāṭhika and the Udumbara, the Wheel-turning Monarch and the Aggañña; The Sampasādanapāsāda and the Marks of a Great Man.

Siṅgālāṭānāṭiyakaṃ, saṅgīti ca dasuttaraṃ;Ekādasahi suttehi, pāthikavaggoti vuccati.

The Sigālaka and the Āṭānāṭiya, the Saṅgīti, and the Dasuttara; With eleven suttas, it is called the Pāthika Vagga.

Pāthikavaggapāḷi niṭṭhitā.

The Chapter of Pāṭhika is concluded.

Tīhi vaggehi paṭimaṇḍito sakalo

Adorned with three sections, the entire collection.

Dīghanikāyo samatto.

The Collection of Long Discourses is complete.
