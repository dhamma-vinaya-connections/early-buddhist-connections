# SN 12.27 Paccayasuttaṃ

## SN 12.27 The Causes Discourse

27. Sāvatthiyaṃ viharati…pe… ‘‘avijjāpaccayā, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā; saṅkhārapaccayā viññāṇaṃ…pe… evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo hoti.

27.At Sāvatthī the Blessed One was dwelling… “Monks, with ignorance as condition, volitional formations arise; with volitional formations as condition, consciousness arises… Thus is the origin of this whole mass of suffering.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, jarāmaraṇaṃ? Yā tesaṃ tesaṃ sattānaṃ tamhi tamhi sattanikāye jarā jīraṇatā khaṇḍiccaṃ pāliccaṃ valittacatā āyuno saṃhāni indriyānaṃ paripāko – ayaṃ vuccati jarā. Yā tesaṃ tesaṃ sattānaṃ tamhā tamhā sattanikāyā cuti cavanatā bhedo antaradhānaṃ maccu maraṇaṃ kālakiriyā khandhānaṃ bhedo kaḷevarassa nikkhepo; idaṃ vuccati maraṇaṃ. Iti ayañca jarā idañca maraṇaṃ. Idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, jarāmaraṇaṃ. Jātisamudayā jarāmaraṇasamudayo; jātinirodhā jarāmaraṇanirodho. Ayameva ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo jarāmaraṇanirodhagāminī paṭipadā. Seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi, sammāsaṅkappo, sammāvācā, sammākammanto, sammāājīvo, sammāvāyāmo, sammāsati, sammāsamādhi.

“And what, monks, is aging-and-death? The aging, decrepitude, broken teeth, gray hair, wrinkled skin, decline of life, and weakening of the faculties of the various beings in the various orders of beings—this is called aging. The passing away, deceasing, dissolution, disappearance, dying, death, breaking up of the aggregates, and laying down of the body of the various beings in the various orders of beings—this is called death. Thus, this aging and this death are called aging-and-death, monks. With the arising of birth comes the arising of aging-and-death; with the cessation of birth comes the cessation of aging-and-death. This Noble Eightfold Path is the practice that leads to the cessation of aging-and-death, namely: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right samādhi.”

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, jāti…pe… katamo ca, bhikkhave, bhavo… katamañca, bhikkhave, upādānaṃ… katamā ca, bhikkhave, taṇhā… katamā ca, bhikkhave, vedanā… katamo ca, bhikkhave, phasso… katamañca, bhikkhave, saḷāyatanaṃ… katamañca, bhikkhave, nāmarūpaṃ… katamañca, bhikkhave, viññāṇaṃ…?

“And what, monks, is birth? … And what, monks, is becoming? … And what, monks, is clinging? … And what, monks, is craving? … And what, monks, is feeling? … And what, monks, is contact? … And what, monks, is the six sense bases? … And what, monks, is name-and-form? … And what, monks, is consciousness?

‘‘Katame ca, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā? Tayome, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā – kāyasaṅkhāro, vacīsaṅkhāro, cittasaṅkhāro. Ime vuccanti, bhikkhave, saṅkhārā. Avijjāsamudayā saṅkhārasamudayo; avijjānirodhā saṅkhāranirodho. Ayameva ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo saṅkhāranirodhagāminī paṭipadā. Seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi…pe… sammāsamādhi.

“And what, monks, are volitional formations? There are, monks, these three volitional formations: bodily volitional formation, verbal volitional formation, mental volitional formation. These, monks, are called volitional formations. With the arising of ignorance comes the arising of volitional formations; with the cessation of ignorance comes the cessation of volitional formations. This Noble Eightfold Path is the practice leading to the cessation of volitional formations, namely: right view… right samādhi.”

‘‘Yato kho, bhikkhave, ariyasāvako evaṃ paccayaṃ pajānāti, evaṃ paccayasamudayaṃ pajānāti, evaṃ paccayanirodhaṃ pajānāti, evaṃ paccayanirodhagāminiṃ paṭipadaṃ pajānāti. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ariyasāvako diṭṭhisampanno itipi, dassanasampanno itipi, āgato imaṃ saddhammaṃ itipi, passati imaṃ saddhammaṃ itipi, sekkhena ñāṇena samannāgato itipi, sekkhāya vijjāya samannāgato itipi, dhammasotaṃ samāpanno itipi, ariyo nibbedhikapañño itipi, amatadvāraṃ āhacca tiṭṭhati itipī’’ti. Sattamaṃ.

“When, monks, a noble disciple thus understands condition, thus understands the origin of condition, thus understands the cessation of condition, thus understands the way leading to the cessation of condition, this noble disciple is said to be: accomplished in view, accomplished in vision, one who has attained this true Dhamma, one who sees this true Dhamma, one who is endowed with the knowledge of a trainee, endowed with the wisdom of a trainee, one who has entered the stream of the Dhamma, a noble one with penetrative wisdom, one who stands at the door to the Deathless.”
