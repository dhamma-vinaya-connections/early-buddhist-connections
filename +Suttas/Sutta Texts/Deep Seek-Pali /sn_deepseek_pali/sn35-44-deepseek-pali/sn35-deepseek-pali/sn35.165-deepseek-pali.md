# SN 35.165 Dutiyasamuddasuttaṃ

## SN 35.165 The Second Discourse on the Ocean

229. ‘‘Samuddo, samuddo’ti, bhikkhave, assutavā puthujjano bhāsati. Neso, bhikkhave, ariyassa vinaye samuddo. Mahā eso, bhikkhave, udakarāsi mahāudakaṇṇavo. Santi, bhikkhave, cakkhuviññeyyā rūpā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ariyassa vinaye samuddo. Etthāyaṃ sadevako loko samārako sabrahmako sassamaṇabrāhmaṇī pajā sadevamanussā yebhuyyena samunnā tantākulakajātā kulagaṇṭhikajātā [guḷāguṇṭhikajātā (sī.), kulaguṇḍikajātā (syā. kaṃ.), guṇaguṇikajātā (pī.), kulāguṇḍikajātā (ka.)] muñjapabbajabhūtā, apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ saṃsāraṃ nātivattati…pe….

229."The ocean, the ocean," bhikkhus, the uninstructed ordinary person speaks. But that, bhikkhus, is not the ocean in the Noble Discipline. That, bhikkhus, is a great mass of water, a great ocean of water. There are, bhikkhus, forms cognizable by the eye—desirable, lovely, agreeable, pleasing, connected with sensuality, arousing lust. This, bhikkhus, is called the ocean in the Noble Discipline. Here, this world with its devas, Māras, and Brahmās, this generation with its ascetics and brahmins, its devas and humans, for the most part is immersed, tangled like a ball of string, knotted like a ball of thread, matted like muñja grass and reeds, and does not transcend the states of deprivation, the bad destinations, the lower worlds, saṃsāra...

‘‘Santi, bhikkhave, jivhāviññeyyā rasā…pe… santi, bhikkhave, manoviññeyyā dhammā iṭṭhā kantā manāpā piyarūpā kāmūpasaṃhitā rajanīyā. Ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ariyassa vinaye samuddo. Etthāyaṃ sadevako loko samārako sabrahmako sassamaṇabrāhmaṇī pajā sadevamanussā yebhuyyena samunnā tantākulakajātā kulagaṇṭhikajātā muñjapabbajabhūtā apāyaṃ duggatiṃ vinipātaṃ saṃsāraṃ nātivattatī’’ti.

"bhikkhus, there are tastes cognizable by the tongue... there are mental phenomena cognizable by the mind that are desirable, lovely, agreeable, pleasing, connected with sensuality, arousing lust. This, bhikkhus, is called the ocean in the Noble Discipline. Herein, this world with its devas, Māras, and Brahmās, this generation with its ascetics and brahmins, its devas and humans, is for the most part immersed, tangled like a ball of string, knotted like a ball of thread, matted like muñja grass and reeds, and does not transcend the states of deprivation, the bad destinations, the lower worlds, saṃsāra."

‘‘Yassa rāgo ca doso ca, avijjā ca virājitā;So imaṃ samuddaṃ sagāhaṃ sarakkhasaṃ, saūmibhayaṃ duttaraṃ accatari.

"One whose lust, hate, and ignorance are extinguished has crossed this ocean, with its sharks and monsters, its waves and terrors, so difficult to cross."

‘‘Saṅgātigo maccujaho nirupadhi, pahāsi dukkhaṃ apunabbhavāya;Atthaṅgato so na puneti [na pamāṇameti (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)], amohayī, maccurājanti brūmī’’ti. dutiyaṃ;

"Gone beyond all bonds, who has abandoned death, without acquisitions, He has abandoned suffering for no further becoming. Gone to rest, he does not return; he has confounded the king of death, so I say." Second.
