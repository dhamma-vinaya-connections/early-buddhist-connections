# SN 35.134 Atthinukhopariyāyasuttaṃ

## SN 35.134 The Discourse on the Way

153. ‘‘Atthi nu kho, bhikkhave, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya, aññatra ruciyā, aññatra anussavā, aññatra ākāraparivitakkā, aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākareyya – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti pajānāmī’’ti [pajānātīti (syā. kaṃ. pī. ka.)]? ‘‘Bhagavaṃmūlakā no, bhante, dhammā, bhagavaṃnettikā bhagavaṃpaṭisaraṇā. Sādhu vata, bhante, bhagavantaṃyeva paṭibhātu etassa bhāsitassa attho. Bhagavato sutvā bhikkhū dhāressantī’’ti. ‘‘Tena hi, bhikkhave, suṇātha, sādhukaṃ manasi karotha; bhāsissāmī’’ti. ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’ti kho te bhikkhū bhagavato paccassosuṃ. Bhagavā etadavoca – ‘‘atthi, bhikkhave, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya, aññatra ruciyā, aññatra anussavā, aññatra ākāraparivitakkā, aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākareyya – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti pajānāmī’’ti.

153.“Is there, bhikkhus, a way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, might declare: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being’?” “Venerable sir, our teachings have the Blessed One as their root, the Blessed One as their guide, the Blessed One as their recourse. It would be good if the Blessed One himself would illuminate the meaning of this statement. Having heard it from the Blessed One, the bhikkhus will remember it.” “Then listen, bhikkhus, and pay close attention. I will speak.” “Yes, venerable sir,” the bhikkhus replied. The Blessed One said: “There is, bhikkhus, a way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, might declare: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being.’ ”

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, pariyāyo, yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya…pe… aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākaroti – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyāti pajānāmī’ti? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, atthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti. Yaṃ taṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, atthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti. Api nume, bhikkhave, dhammā saddhāya vā veditabbā, ruciyā vā veditabbā, anussavena vā veditabbā, ākāraparivitakkena vā veditabbā, diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā vā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Nanume, bhikkhave, dhammā paññāya disvā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Ayaṃ kho, bhikkhave, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya, aññatra ruciyā, aññatra anussavā, aññatra ākāraparivitakkā, aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākaroti – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti pajānāmī’’ti…pe….

“And what, bhikkhus, is the way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, declares: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being’? Here, bhikkhus, a monk seeing a form with the eye, understands if there is lust, hatred, or delusion within: ‘There is lust, hatred, or delusion within me.’ Or if there is no lust, hatred, or delusion within, he understands: ‘There is no lust, hatred, or delusion within me.’ bhikkhus, these things that a monk understands—whether there is lust, hatred, or delusion within him when he sees a form with the eye, or whether there is none—should these things be known through faith? Should they be known through inclination? Should they be known through oral tradition? Should they be known through reasoned contemplation? Should they be known through acceptance of a speculative view?” “No, venerable sir.” “Should these things not be known by seeing them with wisdom?” “Yes, venerable sir.” “This, bhikkhus, is the way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, can declare: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being.’ ”

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhu jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ…pe… rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti. Yaṃ taṃ, bhikkhave, jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, atthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; api nume, bhikkhave, dhammā saddhāya vā veditabbā, ruciyā vā veditabbā, anussavena vā veditabbā, ākāraparivitakkena vā veditabbā, diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā vā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Nanume, bhikkhave, dhammā paññāya disvā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Ayampi kho, bhikkhave, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya, aññatra ruciyā, aññatra anussavā, aññatra ākāraparivitakkā, aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākaroti – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti pajānāmī’’ti [pajānātīti (syā. kaṃ. pī. ka.)] …pe….

“Furthermore, bhikkhus, a monk, having tasted a flavor with the tongue, understands: ‘If there is lust, hatred, or delusion within, I know there is lust, hatred, or delusion within me’; or ‘if there is no lust, hatred, or delusion within, I know there is no lust, hatred, or delusion within me.’ bhikkhus, these things that a monk understands—whether there is lust, hatred, or delusion within him when he tastes a flavor with the tongue, or whether there is none—should these things be known through faith? Should they be known through inclination? Should they be known through oral tradition? Should they be known through reasoned contemplation? Should they be known through acceptance of a speculative view?” “No, venerable sir.” “Should these things not be known by seeing them with wisdom?” “Yes, venerable sir.” “This, bhikkhus, is another way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, declares: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being.’ ” …

‘‘Puna caparaṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhu manasā dhammaṃ viññāya santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, atthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti. Yaṃ taṃ, bhikkhave, bhikkhu manasā dhammaṃ viññāya santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, atthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; asantaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, natthi me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti; api nume, bhikkhave, dhammā saddhāya vā veditabbā, ruciyā vā veditabbā, anussavena vā veditabbā, ākāraparivitakkena vā veditabbā, diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā vā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Nanume, bhikkhave, dhammā paññāya disvā veditabbā’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Ayampi kho, bhikkhave, pariyāyo yaṃ pariyāyaṃ āgamma bhikkhu aññatreva saddhāya, aññatra ruciyā, aññatra anussavā, aññatra ākāraparivitakkā, aññatra diṭṭhinijjhānakkhantiyā aññaṃ byākaroti – ‘khīṇā jāti, vusitaṃ brahmacariyaṃ, kataṃ karaṇīyaṃ, nāparaṃ itthattāyā’ti pajānāmī’’ti. Aṭṭhamaṃ.

“Furthermore, bhikkhus, a monk, having cognized a mental phenomenon with the mind, understands: ‘If there is lust, hatred, or delusion within, I know there is lust, hatred, or delusion within me’; or ‘if there is no lust, hatred, or delusion within, I know there is no lust, hatred, or delusion within me.’ bhikkhus, these things that a monk understands—whether there is lust, hatred, or delusion within him when he cognizes a mental phenomenon with the mind, or whether there is none—should these things be known through faith? Should they be known through inclination? Should they be known through oral tradition? Should they be known through reasoned contemplation? Should they be known through acceptance of a speculative view?” “No, venerable sir.” “Should these things not be known by seeing them with wisdom?” “Yes, venerable sir.” “This, bhikkhus, is another way by which a monk, quite apart from faith, quite apart from inclination, quite apart from oral tradition, quite apart from reasoned contemplation, quite apart from acceptance of a speculative view, declares: ‘Destroyed is birth, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further for this state of being.’ ” The eighth.
