# SN 35.180 Dukkhadhammasuttaṃ

## SN 35.180 The Discourse on Things Pertaining to Suffering

244. Yato kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbesaṃyeva dukkhadhammānaṃ samudayañca atthaṅgamañca yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti. Tathā kho panassa kāmā diṭṭhā honti, yathāssa kāme passato, yo kāmesu kāmacchando kāmasneho kāmamucchā kāmapariḷāho, so nānuseti. Tathā kho panassa cāro ca vihāro ca anubuddho hoti, yathā carantaṃ viharantaṃ abhijjhādomanassā pāpakā akusalā dhammā nānusenti.

244.“bhikkhus, when a monk understands as it actually is the origin and disappearance of all things pertaining to suffering, then sensual pleasures are seen by him as they actually are, in such a way that when he sees sensual pleasures, the desire, affection, infatuation, and fever for sensual pleasures do not lie latent in him. Moreover, his conduct and dwelling are understood in such a way that as he conducts himself and dwells, covetousness and displeasure—evil unwholesome states—do not flow in upon him.”

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbesaṃyeva dukkhadhammānaṃ samudayañca atthaṅgamañca yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti? ‘Iti rūpaṃ, iti rūpassa samudayo, iti rūpassa atthaṅgamo; iti vedanā… iti saññā… iti saṅkhārā… iti viññāṇaṃ, iti viññāṇassa samudayo, iti viññāṇassa atthaṅgamo’ti – evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu sabbesaṃyeva dukkhadhammānaṃ samudayañca atthaṅgamañca yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti.

“And how, bhikkhus, does a monk understand as it actually is the origin and disappearance of all things pertaining to suffering? ‘Such is form, such is the origin of form, such is the disappearance of form; such is feeling, such is the origin of feeling, such is the disappearance of feeling; such is perception, such is the origin of perception, such is the disappearance of perception; such are volitional formations, such is the origin of volitional formations, such is the disappearance of volitional formations; such is consciousness, such is the origin of consciousness, such is the disappearance of consciousness.’ It is in this way, bhikkhus, that a monk understands as it actually is the origin and disappearance of all things pertaining to suffering.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno kāmā diṭṭhā honti? Yathāssa kāme passato, yo kāmesu kāmacchando kāmasneho kāmamucchā kāmapariḷāho, so nānuseti. Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, aṅgārakāsu sādhikaporisā puṇṇā aṅgārānaṃ vītaccikānaṃ vītadhūmānaṃ. Atha puriso āgaccheyya jīvitukāmo amaritukāmo sukhakāmo dukkhapaṭikūlo. Tamenaṃ dve balavanto purisā nānābāhāsu gahetvā, taṃ aṅgārakāsuṃ upakaḍḍheyyuṃ. So iticīticeva kāyaṃ sannāmeyya. Taṃ kissa hetu? Ñāta [ñāṇaṃ (ka.)] ñhi, bhikkhave, tassa purisassa [purisassa hoti (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.), purisassa hetu hoti (ka.) ma. ni. 2.45] imaṃ cāhaṃ aṅgārakāsuṃ papatissāmi, tatonidānaṃ maraṇaṃ vā nigacchissāmi maraṇamattaṃ vā dukkhanti. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno aṅgārakāsūpamā kāmā diṭṭhā honti, yathāssa kāme passato, yo kāmesu kāmacchando kāmasneho kāmamucchā kāmapariḷāho, so nānuseti.

“And how, bhikkhus, are sensual pleasures seen by a monk? In such a way that when he sees sensual pleasures, the desire, affection, infatuation, and fever for sensual pleasures do not lie latent in him. Suppose, bhikkhus, there were a pit of glowing coals, more than a man’s height, full of coals without flame or smoke. Then a man would come along, desiring to live, not desiring to die, desiring happiness and averse to suffering. Then two strong men would seize him by the arms and drag him towards that pit of coals. He would twist and turn his body in every way. Why is that? Because that man knows: ‘If I fall into this pit of coals, then because of that I shall meet either death or deadly suffering.’ In the same way, bhikkhus, sensual pleasures are seen by a monk as like a pit of coals, in such a way that when he sees sensual pleasures, the desire, affection, infatuation, and fever for sensual pleasures do not lie latent in him.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno cāro ca vihāro ca anubuddho hoti, yathā carantaṃ viharantaṃ abhijjhādomanassā pāpakā akusalā dhammā nānussavanti [nānusenti (ka.)]? Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, puriso bahukaṇṭakaṃ dāyaṃ paviseyya. Tassa puratopi kaṇṭako, pacchatopi kaṇṭako, uttaratopi kaṇṭako, dakkhiṇatopi kaṇṭako, heṭṭhatopi kaṇṭako, uparitopi kaṇṭako. So satova abhikkameyya, satova paṭikkameyya – ‘mā maṃ kaṇṭako’ti. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, yaṃ loke piyarūpaṃ sātarūpaṃ, ayaṃ vuccati ariyassa vinaye kaṇṭako’’ti. Iti viditvā [kaṇḍako. taṃ kaṇḍakoti iti viditvā (sī.)] saṃvaro ca asaṃvaro ca veditabbo.

“And how, bhikkhus, are a monk’s conduct and dwelling understood, so that as he conducts himself and dwells, covetousness and displeasure—evil unwholesome states—do not flow in upon him? Suppose, bhikkhus, a man were to enter a thorny jungle thicket. There would be thorns in front of him, thorns behind him, thorns to the north, thorns to the south, thorns below him, thorns above him. He would advance mindfully, he would retreat mindfully, thinking: ‘May no thorn prick me!’ In the same way, bhikkhus, whatever in the world is agreeable and pleasing is called a thorn in the Noble One’s discipline.” Having understood this, restraint and non-restraint should be known.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, asaṃvaro hoti? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā piyarūpe rūpe adhimuccati, appiyarūpe rūpe byāpajjati, anupaṭṭhitakāyassati ca viharati parittacetaso, tañca cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ yathābhūtaṃ nappajānāti, yatthassa te uppannā pāpakā akusalā dhammā aparisesā nirujjhanti…pe… jivhāya rasaṃ sāyitvā…pe… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya piyarūpe dhamme adhimuccati, appiyarūpe dhamme byāpajjati, anupaṭṭhitakāyassati ca viharati parittacetaso, tañca cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ yathābhūtaṃ nappajānāti yatthassa te uppannā pāpakā akusalā dhammā aparisesā nirujjhanti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, asaṃvaro hoti.

“And how, bhikkhus, is there lack of restraint? Here, bhikkhus, a monk, seeing a form with the eye, becomes intent on agreeable forms and feels aversion towards disagreeable forms; and he dwells with mindfulness of the body unestablished and with a constricted mind. And he does not understand as it actually is the liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, whereby those evil unwholesome states that have arisen in him cease without remainder… having tasted a flavour with the tongue… having cognized a mental phenomenon with the mind, he becomes intent on agreeable mental phenomena and feels aversion towards disagreeable mental phenomena; and he dwells with mindfulness of the body unestablished and with a constricted mind. And he does not understand as it actually is the liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, whereby those evil unwholesome states that have arisen in him cease without remainder. It is in this way, bhikkhus, that there is lack of restraint.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, saṃvaro hoti? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cakkhunā rūpaṃ disvā piyarūpe rūpe nādhimuccati, appiyarūpe rūpe na byāpajjati, upaṭṭhitakāyassati ca viharati appamāṇacetaso, tañca cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti, yatthassa te uppannā pāpakā akusalā dhammā aparisesā nirujjhanti…pe… jivhā rasaṃ sāyitvā…pe… manasā dhammaṃ viññāya piyarūpe dhamme nādhimuccati, appiyarūpe dhamme na byāpajjati, upaṭṭhitakāyassati ca viharati appamāṇacetaso, tañca cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti, yatthassa te uppannā pāpakā akusalā dhammā aparisesā nirujjhanti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, saṃvaro hoti.

“And how, bhikkhus, is there restraint? Here, bhikkhus, a monk, seeing a form with the eye, does not become intent on agreeable forms, nor does he feel aversion towards disagreeable forms. He dwells with mindfulness of the body established and with an immeasurable mind. He understands as it actually is the liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, whereby those evil unwholesome states that have arisen in him cease without remainder… having tasted a flavour with the tongue… having cognized a mental phenomenon with the mind, he does not become intent on agreeable mental phenomena, nor does he feel aversion towards disagreeable mental phenomena. He dwells with mindfulness of the body established and with an immeasurable mind. He understands as it actually is the liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom, whereby those evil unwholesome states that have arisen in him cease without remainder. It is in this way, bhikkhus, that there is restraint.

‘‘Tassa ce, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno evaṃ carato evaṃ viharato kadāci karahaci satisammosā uppajjanti, pāpakā akusalā sarasaṅkappā saṃyojaniyā, dandho, bhikkhave, satuppādo. Atha kho naṃ khippameva pajahati vinodeti byantīkaroti anabhāvaṃ gameti.

“If, bhikkhus, while a monk is conducting himself and dwelling in this way, lapses of mindfulness occasionally arise—evil unwholesome thoughts connected with the fetters—then, slow, bhikkhus, is the arising of mindfulness. But he quickly abandons them, dispels them, puts an end to them, and makes them cease to be.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, puriso divasaṃsantatte [divasasantatte (sī.)] ayokaṭāhe dve vā tīṇi vā udakaphusitāni nipāteyya. Dandho, bhikkhave, udakaphusitānaṃ nipāto, atha kho naṃ khippameva parikkhayaṃ pariyādānaṃ gaccheyya. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, tassa ce bhikkhuno evaṃ carato, evaṃ viharato kadāci karahaci satisammosā uppajjanti pāpakā akusalā sarasaṅkappā saṃyojaniyā, dandho, bhikkhave, satuppādo. Atha kho naṃ khippameva pajahati vinodeti byantīkaroti anabhāvaṃ gameti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno cāro ca vihāro ca anubuddho hoti; yathā carantaṃ viharantaṃ abhijjhādomanassā pāpakā akusalā dhammā nānussavanti. Tañce, bhikkhave, bhikkhuṃ evaṃ carantaṃ evaṃ viharantaṃ rājāno vā rājamahāmattā vā mittā vā amaccā vā ñātī vā sālohitā vā, bhogehi abhihaṭṭhuṃ pavāreyyuṃ – ‘ehi [evaṃ (sī.)], bho purisa, kiṃ te ime kāsāvā anudahanti, kiṃ muṇḍo kapālamanucarasi, ehi hīnāyāvattitvā bhoge ca bhuñjassu, puññāni ca karohī’ti. So vata, bhikkhave, bhikkhu evaṃ caranto evaṃ viharanto sikkhaṃ paccakkhāya hīnāyāvattissatīti netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati.

Just as, bhikkhus, if a man were to drop two or three drops of water onto an iron pan heated all day long, slow, bhikkhus, is the falling of those drops, yet they would quickly vanish and come to an end. In the same way, bhikkhus, for a monk who is conducting himself and dwelling thus, even if occasionally, due to lapses in mindfulness, evil unwholesome thoughts connected with the fetters arise, slow, bhikkhus, is the arising of mindfulness. Yet he would quickly abandon them, dispel them, put an end to them, and make them cease to be. It is in this way, bhikkhus, that a monk’s conduct and dwelling are understood; in such a way that as he conducts himself and dwells, covetousness and displeasure—evil unwholesome states—do not flow in upon him. If, bhikkhus, kings or royal ministers, friends or ministers, relatives or blood relatives were to invite that monk—who is conducting himself and dwelling in this way—and offer him wealth, saying: ‘Come, good man, why do these saffron robes burn you? Why do you wander with a shaven head and a bowl? Come, return to the lower life, enjoy wealth, and make merit,’ it is impossible, bhikkhus, that that monk—who is conducting himself and dwelling in this way—would renounce the training and return to the lower life.

‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, gaṅgā nadī pācīnaninnā pācīnapoṇā pācīnapabbhārā. Atha mahājanakāyo āgaccheyya kuddāla-piṭakaṃ ādāya – ‘mayaṃ imaṃ gaṅgaṃ nadiṃ pacchāninnaṃ karissāma pacchāpoṇaṃ pacchāpabbhāra’nti. Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhikkhave, api nu kho so mahājanakāyo gaṅgaṃ nadiṃ pacchāninnaṃ kareyya pacchāpoṇaṃ pacchāpabbhāra’’nti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Taṃ kissa hetu’’? ‘‘Gaṅgā, bhante, nadī pācīnaninnā pācīnapoṇā pācīnapabbhārā; sā na sukarā pacchāninnā kātuṃ pacchāpoṇā pacchāpabbhārā. Yāvadeva ca pana so mahājanakāyo kilamathassa vighātassa bhāgī assā’’ti. ‘‘Evameva kho, bhikkhave, tañce bhikkhuṃ evaṃ carantaṃ evaṃ viharantaṃ rājāno vā rājamahāmattā vā mittā vā amaccā vā ñātī vā sālohitā vā bhogehi abhihaṭṭhuṃ pavāreyyuṃ – ‘ehi, bho purisa, kiṃ te ime kāsāvā anudahanti, kiṃ muṇḍo kapālamanucarasi, ehi hīnāyāvattitvā bhoge ca bhuñjassu, puññāni ca karohī’ti. So vata, bhikkhave, bhikkhu evaṃ caranto evaṃ viharanto sikkhaṃ paccakkhāya hīnāyāvattissatīti netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati. Taṃ kissa hetu? Yañhi taṃ, bhikkhave, cittaṃ dīgharattaṃ vivekaninnaṃ vivekapoṇaṃ vivekapabbhāraṃ, tathā [kañca (syā. kaṃ. ka.)] hīnāyāvattissatīti netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjatī’’ti. Sattamaṃ.

“Just as, bhikkhus, the Ganges river slopes, inclines, and tends to the east. Then a great crowd of people might come taking spades and baskets, thinking, ‘We will make this Ganges river slope to the west, incline to the west, tend to the west.’ What do you think, bhikkhus, would that great crowd of people be able to make the Ganges river slope, incline, and tend to the west?” “No, Venerable Sir.” “For what reason?” “Because, Venerable Sir, the Ganges river slopes, inclines, and tends to the east; it is not easy to make it slope, incline, and tend to the west. That great crowd of people would only reap fatigue and vexation.” “Just so, bhikkhus, if kings or royal ministers, friends or ministers, relatives or blood relatives were to invite a monk who is conducting himself and dwelling in such a way, and offer him wealth, saying, ‘Come, good man, why do these saffron robes burn you? Why do you wander with a shaven head and a bowl? Come, return to the lower life, enjoy wealth, and make merit.’ It is impossible, bhikkhus, that a monk conducting himself and dwelling thus would renounce the training and return to the lower life. For what reason? Because, bhikkhus, that mind has long sloped, inclined, and tended to seclusion. Therefore, it is impossible that he would return to the lower life.” The Seventh Discourse.
