# SN 36.23 Aññatarabhikkhusuttaṃ

## SN 36.23 A Certain Monk Sutta

271. Atha kho aññataro bhikkhu yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho so bhikkhu bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘katamā nu kho, bhante, vedanā, katamo vedanāsamudayo, katamā vedanāsamudayagāminī paṭipadā? Katamo vedanānirodho, katamā vedanānirodhagāminī paṭipadā? Ko vedanāya assādo, ko ādīnavo, kiṃ nissaraṇa’’nti?

271.Then a certain monk approached the Blessed One; having approached, he paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Sitting to one side, that monk said to the Blessed One: “What, venerable sir, is feeling? What is the origin of feeling? What is the path leading to the origin of feeling? What is the cessation of feeling? What is the path leading to the cessation of feeling? What is the gratification in feeling? What is the danger? What is the escape?”

‘‘Tisso imā, bhikkhu, vedanā – sukhā vedanā, dukkhā vedanā, adukkhamasukhā vedanā. Imā vuccanti, bhikkhu, vedanā. Phassasamudayā vedanāsamudayo. Taṇhā vedanāsamudayagāminī paṭipadā. Phassanirodhā vedanānirodho. Ayameva ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo vedanānirodhagāminī paṭipadā, seyyathidaṃ – sammādiṭṭhi…pe… sammāsamādhi. Yaṃ vedanaṃ paṭicca uppajjati sukhaṃ somanassaṃ, ayaṃ vedanāya assādo; yā vedanā aniccā dukkhā vipariṇāmadhammā, ayaṃ vedanāya ādīnavo; yo vedanāya chandarāgavinayo chandarāgappahānaṃ, idaṃ vedanāya nissaraṇa’’nti. Tatiyaṃ.

“Monk, there are these three feelings—pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. These are called feelings, monk. With the arising of contact comes the arising of feeling. Craving is the path leading to the arising of feeling. With the cessation of contact comes the cessation of feeling. This Noble Eightfold Path itself is the path leading to the cessation of feeling, that is—right view…pe… right samādhi. Whatever pleasure and joy arise dependent on feeling, this is the gratification in feeling. That feeling is impermanent, unsatisfactory, and subject to change—this is the danger in feeling. The subduing and abandoning of desire and lust for feeling—this is the escape from feeling.” The third.
