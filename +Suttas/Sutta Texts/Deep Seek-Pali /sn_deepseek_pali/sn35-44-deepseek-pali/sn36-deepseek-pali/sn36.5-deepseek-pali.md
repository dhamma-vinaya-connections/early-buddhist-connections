# SN 36.5 Daṭṭhabbasuttaṃ

## SN 36.5 The Sutta on What Should Be Seen

253. ‘‘Tisso imā, bhikkhave, vedanā. Katamā tisso? Sukhā vedanā, dukkhā vedanā, adukkhamasukhā vedanā. Sukhā, bhikkhave, vedanā dukkhato daṭṭhabbā, dukkhā vedanā sallato daṭṭhabbā, adukkhamasukhā vedanā aniccato daṭṭhabbā. Yato kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno sukhā vedanā dukkhato diṭṭhā hoti, dukkhā vedanā sallato diṭṭhā hoti, adukkhamasukhā vedanā aniccato diṭṭhā hoti – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ‘bhikkhu sammaddaso acchecchi taṇhaṃ, vivattayi saṃyojanaṃ, sammā mānābhisamayā antamakāsi dukkhassā’’’ti.

253.“bhikkhus, there are these three feelings. Which three? Pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling. Pleasant feeling, bhikkhus, should be seen as suffering; painful feeling should be seen as a dart; neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling should be seen as impermanent. When, bhikkhus, a monk has seen pleasant feeling as suffering, painful feeling as a dart, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling as impermanent—this is called, bhikkhus, ‘a monk who sees rightly, who has cut off craving, has burst the fetters, and by rightly comprehending conceit has made an end of suffering.’”

‘‘Yo sukhaṃ dukkhato adda, dukkhamaddakkhi sallato;Adukkhamasukhaṃ santaṃ, addakkhi naṃ aniccato.

He who sees pleasant [feeling] as suffering, sees painful [feeling] as a dart, and sees the peaceful neither-pleasant-nor-painful [feeling] as impermanent.

‘‘Sa ve sammaddaso bhikkhu, parijānāti vedanā;So vedanā pariññāya, diṭṭhe dhamme anāsavo;Kāyassa bhedā dhammaṭṭho, saṅkhyaṃ nopeti vedagū’’ti. pañcamaṃ;

That monk who sees rightly fully understands feelings; he, having fully understood feelings, is taintless in this very life. At the breaking up of the body, established in the Dhamma, the one who has reached the goal is not reckoned.” Fifth.
