# SN 36.3 Pahānasuttaṃ

## SN 36.3 The Discourse on Abandonment

251. ‘‘Tisso imā, bhikkhave, vedanā. Katamā tisso? Sukhā vedanā, dukkhā vedanā, adukkhamasukhā vedanā. Sukhāya, bhikkhave, vedanāya rāgānusayo pahātabbo, dukkhāya vedanāya paṭighānusayo pahātabbo, adukkhamasukhāya vedanāya avijjānusayo pahātabbo. Yato kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno sukhāya vedanāya rāgānusayo pahīno hoti, dukkhāya vedanāya paṭighānusayo pahīno hoti, adukkhamasukhāya vedanāya avijjānusayo pahīno hoti, ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ‘bhikkhu niranusayo sammaddaso acchecchi [acchejji (bahūsu)] taṇhaṃ, vivattayi [vāvattayi (sī.)] saṃyojanaṃ, sammā mānābhisamayā antamakāsi dukkhassā’’’ti.

251."bhikkhus, there are these three feelings. Which three? Pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. In pleasant feeling, the underlying tendency to lust should be abandoned. In painful feeling, the underlying tendency to aversion should be abandoned. In neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, the underlying tendency to ignorance should be abandoned. When, bhikkhus, a monk has abandoned the underlying tendency to lust in pleasant feeling, the underlying tendency to aversion in painful feeling, and the underlying tendency to ignorance in neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, then, bhikkhus, he is called a monk who is without underlying tendencies, one who sees rightly, who has cut off craving, has burst the fetters, and by rightly comprehending conceit has made an end of suffering."

‘‘Sukhaṃ vedayamānassa [vediyamānassa (sī. pī.)], vedanaṃ appajānato;So rāgānusayo hoti, anissaraṇadassino.

"When one experiences a pleasant feeling, not understanding that feeling, the underlying tendency to lust lies latent in one who does not see an escape."

‘‘Dukkhaṃ vedayamānassa, vedanaṃ appajānato;Paṭighānusayo hoti, anissaraṇadassino.

"When one experiences a painful feeling, not understanding that feeling, the underlying tendency to aversion lies latent in one who does not see an escape."

‘‘Adukkhamasukhaṃ santaṃ, bhūripaññena desitaṃ;Tañcāpi abhinandati, neva dukkhā pamuccati.

"Neither painful nor pleasant, peaceful, taught by the one with vast wisdom; yet even delighting in that, one is not freed from suffering."

‘‘Yato ca bhikkhu ātāpī, sampajaññaṃ na riñcati;Tato so vedanā sabbā, parijānāti paṇḍito.

"But when a monk is ardent and does not abandon clear comprehension, then that wise one fully understands all feelings."

‘‘So vedanā pariññāya, diṭṭhe dhamme anāsavo;Kāyassa bhedā dhammaṭṭho, saṅkhyaṃ nopeti vedagū’’ti. tatiyaṃ;

"Having fully understood feelings, taintless in this very life, at the breakup of the body, established in the Dhamma, the knower of feelings does not enter into reckoning." Third.
