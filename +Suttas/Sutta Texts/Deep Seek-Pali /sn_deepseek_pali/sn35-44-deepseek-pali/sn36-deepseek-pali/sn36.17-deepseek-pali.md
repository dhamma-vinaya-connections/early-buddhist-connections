265.Then several monks approached the Blessed One; having approached, they paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. Seated to one side, those monks said to the Blessed One: "What, venerable sir, is feeling? What is the origin of feeling? What is the cessation of feeling? What is the path leading to the cessation of feeling? What is the gratification in feeling? What is the danger? What is the escape?" "There are, monks, these three feelings: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. These are called, monks, feelings. With the arising of contact there is the arising of feeling; with the cessation of contact there is the cessation of feeling. This Noble Eightfold Path is the path leading to the cessation of feeling, that is: right view...right concentration. The pleasure and gladness that arise dependent on feeling: this is the gratification in feeling. That feeling is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change: this is the danger in feeling. The subduing of desire and lust, the abandoning of desire and lust for feeling: this is the escape from feeling."
