# SN 55.17 Dutiyamittāmaccasuttaṃ

## SN 55.17 The Second Discourse on Friends and Advisors

1013. ‘‘Ye te, bhikkhave, anukampeyyātha, ye ca sotabbaṃ maññeyyuṃ – mittā vā amaccā vā ñātī vā sālohitā vā – te, bhikkhave, catūsu sotāpattiyaṅgesu samādapetabbā, nivesetabbā, patiṭṭhāpetabbā. Katamesu catūsu? Buddhe aveccappasāde samādapetabbā, nivesetabbā, patiṭṭhāpetabbā – itipi so bhagavā…pe… satthā devamanussānaṃ buddho bhagavā’’ti.

1013."Monks, those for whom you have compassion, and those who think they should listen—whether friends, advisors, relatives, or family—they should be encouraged, settled, and established in the four factors of stream-entry. Which four? They should be encouraged, settled, and established in unwavering confidence in the Buddha: ‘Indeed, the Blessed One is…the Teacher of gods and humans, the Buddha, the Blessed One.’"

‘‘Siyā, bhikkhave, catunnaṃ mahābhūtānaṃ aññathattaṃ – pathavīdhātuyā, āpodhātuyā, tejodhātuyā, vāyodhātuyā – na tveva buddhe aveccappasādena samannāgatassa ariyasāvakassa siyā aññathattaṃ. Tatridaṃ aññathattaṃ – so vata buddhe aveccappasādena samannāgato ariyasāvako nirayaṃ vā tiracchānayoniṃ vā pettivisayaṃ vā upapajjissatī’’ti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati. ‘‘Dhamme…pe… saṅghe…pe… ariyakantesu sīlesu samādapetabbā, nivesetabbā, patiṭṭhāpetabbā akhaṇḍesu…pe… samādhisaṃvattanikesu. Siyā, bhikkhave, catunnaṃ mahābhūtānaṃ aññathattaṃ – pathavīdhātuyā, āpodhātuyā, tejodhātuyā, vāyodhātuyā – na tveva ariyakantehi sīlehi samannāgatassa ariyasāvakassa siyā aññathattaṃ. Tatridaṃ aññathattaṃ – so vata ariyakantehi sīlehi samannāgato ariyasāvako nirayaṃ vā tiracchānayoniṃ vā pettivisayaṃ vā upapajjissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati. Ye te, bhikkhave, anukampeyyātha, ye ca sotabbaṃ maññeyyuṃ – mittā vā amaccā vā ñātī vā sālohitā vā – te, bhikkhave, imesu catūsu sotāpattiyaṅgesu samādapetabbā, nivesetabbā, patiṭṭhāpetabbā’’ti. Sattamaṃ.

"Monks, there might be a change in the four great elements—the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element—but there can be no change in a noble disciple who possesses unwavering confidence in the Buddha. That is, there is no possibility that a noble disciple, possessing unwavering confidence in the Buddha, would be reborn in hell, in the animal realm, or in the realm of the departed spirits. This is not possible. Monks, there might be a change in the four great elements—the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element—but there can be no change in a noble disciple who possesses unwavering confidence in the Dhamma. That is, there is no possibility that a noble disciple, possessing unwavering confidence in the Dhamma, would be reborn in hell, in the animal realm, or in the realm of the departed spirits. This is not possible. Monks, there might be a change in the four great elements—the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element—but there can be no change in a noble disciple who possesses unwavering confidence in the Sangha. That is, there is no possibility that a noble disciple, possessing unwavering confidence in the Sangha, would be reborn in hell, in the animal realm, or in the realm of the departed spirits. This is not possible. Monks, there might be a change in the four great elements—the earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the air element—but there can be no change in a noble disciple who possesses virtues dear to the noble ones—unbroken... conducive to samādhi. That is, there is no possibility that a noble disciple, possessing virtues dear to the noble ones, would be reborn in hell, in the animal realm, or in the realm of the departed spirits. This is not possible. Monks, those for whom you have compassion, and who think it worth listening to—whether friends, advisors, relatives, or family—should be encouraged, settled, and established in these four factors of stream-entry.” The seventh.
