# SN 51.20 Vibhaṅgasuttaṃ

## SN 51.20 The Analysis Discourse

832. ‘‘Cattārome, bhikkhave, iddhipādā bhāvitā bahulīkatā mahapphalā honti mahānisaṃsā’’.

832.Monks, these four bases of spiritual power, when developed and cultivated, are of great fruit and great benefit.

‘‘Kathaṃ bhāvitā ca, bhikkhave, cattāro iddhipādā kathaṃ bahulīkatā mahapphalā honti mahānisaṃsā? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti – ‘iti me chando na ca atilīno bhavissati, na ca atippaggahito bhavissati, na ca ajjhattaṃ saṃkhitto bhavissati, na ca bahiddhā vikkhitto bhavissati’. Pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – ‘yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure; yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho; yathā divā tathā rattiṃ yathā rattiṃ tathā divā’. Iti vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti. Vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi…pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti – ‘iti me vīmaṃsā na ca atilīnā bhavissati, na ca atippaggahitā bhavissati, na ca ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittā bhavissati, na ca bahiddhā vikkhittā bhavissati’. Pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – ‘yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure; yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho; yathā divā tathā rattiṃ, yathā rattiṃ tathā divā’. Iti vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti.

And how, monks, when developed and cultivated, do the four bases of spiritual power become of great fruit and great benefit? Here, monks, a monk develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to desire and the volitional formations of striving, thinking: 'Thus my desire will be neither too sluggish nor too strained, and it will be neither constricted internally nor scattered externally.' And he dwells perceiving before and after: 'As before, so after; as after, so before; as below, so above; as above, so below; as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day.' Thus, with an open mind, unconfined and radiant, he develops his mind. He develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to energy, samādhi due to mind, and samādhi due to investigation and the volitional formations of striving, thinking: 'Thus my investigation will be neither too sluggish nor too strained, and it will be neither constricted internally nor scattered externally.' And he dwells perceiving before and after: 'As before, so after; as after, so before; as below, so above; as above, so below; as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day.' Thus, with an open mind, unconfined and radiant, he develops his mind.

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, atilīno chando? Yo, bhikkhave, chando kosajjasahagato kosajjasampayutto – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atilīno chando.

And what, monks, is desire that is overly slack? Whatever desire is accompanied by sloth, associated with sloth—this is called, monks, desire that is overly slack.

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, atippaggahito chando? Yo, bhikkhave, chando uddhaccasahagato uddhaccasampayutto – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atippaggahito chando.

And what, monks, is desire that is over-assertive? Whatever desire is accompanied by agitation, associated with agitation—this is called, monks, desire that is over-assertive.

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhitto chando? Yo, bhikkhave, chando thinamiddhasahagato thinamiddhasampayutto – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhitto chando.

And what, monks, is internally constricted desire? Monks, that desire which is accompanied by sloth and torpor, associated with sloth and torpor—this is called internally constricted desire.

‘‘Katamo ca, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhitto chando? Yo, bhikkhave, chando bahiddhā pañca kāmaguṇe ārabbha anuvikkhitto anuvisaṭo – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhitto chando.

And what, monks, is externally scattered desire? That desire, monks, which is scattered and spread outwards concerning the five strands of sensual pleasure—this is called externally scattered desire.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno pacchāpuresaññā suggahitā hoti sumanasikatā sūpadhāritā suppaṭividdhā paññāya. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure.

And how, monks, does a monk dwell with perception of before and after, so that as before, so after; as after, so before? Here, monks, a monk's perception of before and after is well grasped, well attended to, well pondered, and well penetrated by wisdom. Thus, monks, a monk dwells with perception of before and after, so that as before, so after; as after, so before.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho viharati? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu imameva kāyaṃ uddhaṃ pādatalā adho kesamatthakā tacapariyantaṃ pūraṃ nānappakārassa asucino paccavekkhati – ‘atthi imasmiṃ kāye kesā lomā nakhā dantā taco maṃsaṃ nhāru aṭṭhi aṭṭhimiñjaṃ vakkaṃ hadayaṃ yakanaṃ kilomakaṃ pihakaṃ papphāsaṃ antaṃ antaguṇaṃ udariyaṃ karīsaṃ pittaṃ semhaṃ pubbo lohitaṃ sedo medo assu vasā kheḷo siṅghāṇikā lasikā mutta’nti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho viharati.

And how, monks, does a monk dwell regarding what is below as above, and what is above as below? Here, monks, a monk reviews this very body from the soles of the feet upwards to the top of the hair, and downwards from the top of the hair to the soles of the feet, enclosed by skin and full of various kinds of impurities: 'In this body there are head hairs, body hairs, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, bone marrow, kidneys, heart, liver, midriff, spleen, lungs, intestines, intestinal mesentery, stomach contents, feces, bile, phlegm, pus, blood, sweat, fat, tears, grease, saliva, snot, synovial fluid, urine.' Thus, monks, a monk dwells regarding what is below as above, and what is above as below.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu yathā divā tathā rattiṃ, yathā rattiṃ tathā divā viharati? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu yehi ākārehi yehi liṅgehi yehi nimittehi divā chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, so tehi ākārehi tehi liṅgehi tehi nimittehi rattiṃ chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti; yehi vā pana ākārehi yehi liṅgehi yehi nimittehi rattiṃ chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, so tehi ākārehi tehi liṅgehi tehi nimittehi divā chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu yathā divā tathā rattiṃ, yathā rattiṃ tathā divā viharati.

And how, monks, does a monk dwell so that as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day? Here, monks, a monk develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to desire and the volitional formations of striving by day with the same features, signs, and indications with which he develops it by night; and by night with the same features, signs, and indications with which he develops it by day. Thus, monks, a monk dwells so that as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day.

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno ālokasaññā suggahitā hoti divāsaññā svādhiṭṭhitā. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti.

And how, monks, does a monk develop a mind that is open, unconfined, and radiant? Here, monks, a monk's perception of light is well grasped, and the perception of daytime is well established. Thus, monks, a monk develops a mind that is open, unconfined, and radiant.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, atilīnaṃ vīriyaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, vīriyaṃ kosajjasahagataṃ kosajjasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atilīnaṃ vīriyaṃ.

And what, monks, is overly lax energy? Monks, that energy which is associated with sloth, connected with sloth—this is called overly lax energy.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, atippaggahitaṃ vīriyaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, vīriyaṃ uddhaccasahagataṃ uddhaccasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atippaggahitaṃ vīriyaṃ.

And what, monks, is energy that is too forceful? Monks, that energy which is accompanied by agitation, connected with agitation—this is called energy that is too forceful.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittaṃ vīriyaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, vīriyaṃ thinamiddhasahagataṃ thinamiddhasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittaṃ vīriyaṃ.

And what, monks, is internal constricted energy? Whatever energy is accompanied by sloth and torpor, associated with sloth and torpor—this is called, monks, internal constricted energy.

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittaṃ vīriyaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, vīriyaṃ bahiddhā pañca kāmaguṇe ārabbha anuvikkhittaṃ anuvisaṭaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittaṃ vīriyaṃ…pe….

And what, monks, is externally scattered energy? It is the energy, monks, that is scattered and spread externally, concerning the five strands of sensual pleasure. This is called, monks, externally scattered energy…

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhuno ālokasaññā suggahitā hoti divāsaññā svādhiṭṭhitā. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti.

“And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu develop a mind that is open, unconfined, and luminous? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has well-grasped the perception of light, and the perception of daytime is well-established. Thus, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu develops a mind that is open, unconfined, and luminous.”

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, atilīnaṃ cittaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, cittaṃ kosajjasahagataṃ kosajjasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atilīnaṃ cittaṃ.

“And what, bhikkhus, is an overly sluggish mind? That mind, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by laziness, conjoined with laziness—this is called, bhikkhus, an overly sluggish mind.”

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, atippaggahitaṃ cittaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, cittaṃ uddhaccasahagataṃ uddhaccasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atippaggahitaṃ cittaṃ.

“And what, bhikkhus, is an over-exerted mind? That mind, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by restlessness, conjoined with restlessness—this is called, bhikkhus, an over-exerted mind.”

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittaṃ cittaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, cittaṃ thinamiddhasahagataṃ thinamiddhasampayuttaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittaṃ cittaṃ.

“And what, bhikkhus, is the internally constricted mind? That mind, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by sloth and torpor, conjoined with sloth and torpor—this is called, bhikkhus, the internally constricted mind.”

‘‘Katamañca, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittaṃ cittaṃ? Yaṃ, bhikkhave, cittaṃ bahiddhā pañca kāmaguṇe ārabbha anuvikkhittaṃ anuvisaṭaṃ – idaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittaṃ cittaṃ…pe… evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti.

“And what, bhikkhus, is an externally scattered mind? That mind, bhikkhus, which is scattered and dispersed externally, focusing on the five cords of sensual pleasure—this is called, bhikkhus, an externally scattered mind… Thus, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu develops a mind that is open, unconfined, and luminous.”

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, atilīnā vīmaṃsā? Yā, bhikkhave, vīmaṃsā kosajjasahagatā kosajjasampayuttā – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atilīnā vīmaṃsā.

“And what, bhikkhus, is excessively sluggish investigation? That investigation, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by laziness, conjoined with laziness—this is called, bhikkhus, excessively sluggish investigation.”

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, atippaggahitā vīmaṃsā? Yā, bhikkhave, vīmaṃsā uddhaccasahagatā uddhaccasampayuttā – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, atippaggahitā vīmaṃsā.

“And what, bhikkhus, is over-exerted investigation? That investigation, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by restlessness, conjoined with restlessness—this is called, bhikkhus, over-exerted investigation.”

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittā vīmaṃsā? Yā, bhikkhave, vīmaṃsā thinamiddhasahagatā thinamiddhasampayuttā – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittā vīmaṃsā.

“And what, bhikkhus, is the internally constricted investigation? That investigation, bhikkhus, which is accompanied by sloth and torpor, conjoined with sloth and torpor—this is called, bhikkhus, the internally constricted investigation.”

‘‘Katamā ca, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittā vīmaṃsā? Yā, bhikkhave, vīmaṃsā bahiddhā pañca kāmaguṇe ārabbha anuvikkhittā anuvisaṭā – ayaṃ vuccati, bhikkhave, bahiddhā vikkhittā vīmaṃsā…pe… evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti. Evaṃ bhāvitā kho, bhikkhave, cattāro iddhipādā evaṃ bahulīkatā mahapphalā honti mahānisaṃsā.

“And what, bhikkhus, is externally scattered investigation? That investigation, bhikkhus, which is scattered and dispersed externally, focusing on the five cords of sensual pleasure—this is called, bhikkhus, externally scattered investigation. … Thus, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu develops a mind that is open, unconfined, and luminous. When the four bases of spiritual power are thus developed and cultivated, they are of great fruit and great benefit.”

‘‘Evaṃ bhāvitesu kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu catūsu iddhipādesu evaṃ bahulīkatesu, anekavihitaṃ iddhividhaṃ paccanubhoti – ekopi hutvā bahudhā hoti, bahudhāpi hutvā eko hoti…pe… yāva brahmalokāpi kāyena vasaṃ vatteti. Evaṃ bhāvitesu kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu catūsu iddhipādesu evaṃ bahulīkatesu, āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharatī’’ti. Dasamaṃ.

“Bhikkhus, when these four bases of spiritual power are thus developed and cultivated, a bhikkhu experiences various kinds of spiritual power: having been one, he becomes many; having been many, he becomes one… he wields bodily mastery even as far as the Brahmā world. When these four bases of spiritual power are thus developed and cultivated, a bhikkhu, through the destruction of the taints, by realizing for himself with direct knowledge, enters and dwells in the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom in this very life.” The tenth.

Pāsādakampanavaggo dutiyo.

The Pāsādakampana Chapter, the Second.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Pubbaṃ mahapphalaṃ chandaṃ, moggallānañca uṇṇābhaṃ;Dve samaṇabrāhmaṇā bhikkhu, desanā vibhaṅgena cāti.

First, the Great Fruit, Aspiration, Moggallāna, and Uṇṇābha; Two Ascetics and Brahmins, a Bhikkhu, and the Exposition with Analysis.
