# SN 51.32 Tathāgatasuttaṃ

## SN 51.32 The Tathāgata Sutta

844. Tatra kho bhagavā bhikkhū āmantesi – ‘‘taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhikkhave, katamesaṃ dhammānaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā tathāgato evaṃmahiddhiko evaṃmahānubhāvo’’ti? Bhagavaṃmūlakā no, bhante, dhammā…pe… ‘‘catunnaṃ kho, bhikkhave, iddhipādānaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā tathāgato evaṃmahiddhiko evaṃmahānubhāvo’’.

844.There the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus: “Monks, what do you think: by developing and cultivating which qualities is the Tathāgata of such great power and might?” “Our teachings, Bhante, are rooted in the Blessed One, guided by the Blessed One…” “Monks, it is by developing and cultivating the four bases of psychic power that the Tathāgata is of such great power and might.”

‘‘Katamesaṃ catunnaṃ? Idha, bhikkhave, tathāgato chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti – ‘iti me chando na ca atilīno bhavissati, na ca atippaggahito bhavissati, na ca ajjhattaṃ saṃkhitto bhavissati, na ca bahiddhā vikkhitto bhavissati’. Pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – ‘yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure; yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho; yathā divā tathā rattiṃ, yathā rattiṃ tathā divā’. Iti vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti. Vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi…pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti – ‘iti me vīmaṃsā na ca atilīnā bhavissati, na ca atipaggahitā bhavissati, na ca ajjhattaṃ saṃkhittā bhavissati, na ca bahiddhā vikkhittā bhavissati’. Pacchāpuresaññī ca viharati – ‘yathā pure tathā pacchā, yathā pacchā tathā pure; yathā adho tathā uddhaṃ, yathā uddhaṃ tathā adho; yathā divā tathā rattiṃ, yathā rattiṃ tathā divā’. Iti vivaṭena cetasā apariyonaddhena sappabhāsaṃ cittaṃ bhāveti. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, catunnaṃ iddhipādānaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā tathāgato evaṃmahiddhiko evaṃmahānubhāvo.

“Which four? Here, monks, the Tathāgata develops the ![[Iddhipadas]] that possesses samādhi of energy accompanied by the volitional formations of striving, thinking: ‘Thus my energy will be neither too slack nor too tense, neither inwardly constricted nor outwardly scattered.’ He dwells perceiving the before and after: ‘As before, so after; as after, so before; as below, so above; as above, so below; as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day.’ Thus, with an open, unconfined mind, he develops a luminous mind. He develops the ![[Iddhipadas]] that possesses samādhi of investigation accompanied by the volitional formations of striving, thinking: ‘Thus my investigation will be neither too slack nor too tense, neither inwardly constricted nor outwardly scattered.’ He dwells perceiving the before and after: ‘As before, so after; as after, so before; as below, so above; as above, so below; as by day, so by night; as by night, so by day.’ Thus, with an open, unconfined mind, he develops a luminous mind. It is by developing and cultivating these four bases of psychic power, monks, that the Tathāgata is of such great power and might.”

‘‘Imesañca pana, bhikkhave, catunnaṃ iddhipādānaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā tathāgato anekavihitaṃ iddhividhaṃ paccanubhoti – ekopi hutvā bahudhā hoti…pe… yāva brahmalokāpi kāyena vasaṃ vatteti. Imesañca pana, bhikkhave, catunnaṃ iddhipādānaṃ bhāvitattā bahulīkatattā tathāgato āsavānaṃ khayā anāsavaṃ cetovimuttiṃ paññāvimuttiṃ diṭṭheva dhamme sayaṃ abhiññā sacchikatvā upasampajja viharatī’’ti. Dvādasamaṃ.

“Moreover, monks, by developing and cultivating these four bases of psychic power, the Tathāgata experiences various kinds of psychic power: being one, he becomes many… he wields bodily mastery as far as the Brahmā world. And moreover, monks, by developing and cultivating these four bases of psychic power, the Tathāgata, through the destruction of the taints, having realized it for himself with direct knowledge, enters and dwells in the taintless liberation of mind, liberation by wisdom, in this very life.”

(Chapi abhiññāyo vitthāretabbā).

(The six kinds of direct knowledge should be elaborated.)

Ayoguḷavaggo tatiyo.

The Chapter on the Iron Ball is the third.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its summary:

Maggo ayoguḷo bhikkhu, suddhikañcāpi dve phalā;Dve cānandā duve bhikkhū, moggallāno tathāgatoti.

The Path, the Iron Ball, the Bhikkhu, and also two Pure Fruits; Two Ānandas, two Bhikkhus, Moggallāna, and the Tathāgata.

4. Gaṅgāpeyyālavaggo

4.The Chapter on the Ganges Repetition Series

## 1-12. Gaṅgānadīādisuttadvādasakaṃ

### 1-12. The Twelve Discourses Beginning with the River Ganges

845-856. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, gaṅgā nadī pācīnaninnā pācīnapoṇā pācīnapabbhārā; evameva kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro iddhipāde bhāvento cattāro iddhipāde bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro. Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro iddhipāde bhāvento cattāro iddhipāde bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi…pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti.

845-856. "Just as, bhikkhus, the river Ganges slopes, tends, and inclines to the east, so too, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu who develops and cultivates the four bases of spiritual power slopes, tends, and inclines to Nibbāna. And how, bhikkhus, does a bhikkhu who develops and cultivates the four bases of spiritual power slope, tend, and incline to Nibbāna? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to desire and the volitional formations of exertion. He develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to energy... the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to mind... He develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to investigation and the volitional formations of exertion.

‘‘Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro iddhipāde bhāvento cattāro iddhipāde bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro’’ti. Dvādasamaṃ.

"Thus, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu who develops and cultivates the four bases of spiritual power slopes, tends, and inclines to Nibbāna." The Twelfth.

Gaṅgāpeyyālavaggo catuttho.

The Chapter on the Ganges Repetition Series, the Fourth.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Cha pācīnato ninnā, cha ninnā ca samuddato;Dvete cha dvādasa honti, vaggo tena pavuccatīti.

Six sloping to the east, and six sloping to the sea; These two sixes make twelve, thus is the chapter called.

Appamādavaggo vitthāretabbo.

The Chapter on Heedfulness should be expanded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Tathāgataṃ padaṃ kūṭaṃ, mūlaṃ sāro ca vassikaṃ;Rājā candimasūriyā, vatthena dasamaṃ padanti.

The Tathāgata, the Footprint, the Peak, the Root, the Heartwood, and the Jasmine; The King, the Moon, and the Sun, with Cloth as the tenth item.

Balakaraṇīyavaggo vitthāretabbo.

The Chapter on What is to be Done by Strength should be expanded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Balaṃ bījañca nāgo ca, rukkho kumbhena sūkiyā;Ākāsena ca dve meghā, nāvā āgantukā nadīti.

Strength, the Seed, and the Elephant, the Tree, the Pot, and the Winnower; The Sky and the Two Clouds, the Boat, the Newcomer, and the River.

Esanāvaggo vitthāretabbo.

The Chapter on Searches should be expanded.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Esanā vidhā āsavo, bhavo ca dukkhatā tisso;Khilaṃ malañca nīgho ca, vedanā taṇhā tasinā cāti.

Searches, Modes, Taints, Becoming, and the Three Kinds of Suffering; Barrenness, Stain, Distress, Feeling, Craving, and Thirst.

8. Oghavaggo

8.The Chapter on Floods

## 1-10. Oghādisuttadasakaṃ

### 1-10. The Ten Discourses Beginning with Floods.

889-898. ‘‘Pañcimāni, bhikkhave, uddhambhāgiyāni saṃyojanāni. Katamāni pañca? Rūparāgo, arūparāgo, māno, uddhaccaṃ, avijjā – imāni kho, bhikkhave, pañcuddhambhāgiyāni saṃyojanāni. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, pañcannaṃ uddhambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ abhiññāya pariññāya parikkhayāya pahānāya cattāro iddhipādā bhāvetabbā. Katame cattāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi …pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, pañcannaṃ uddhambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ abhiññāya pariññāya parikkhayāya pahānāya ime cattāro iddhipādā bhāvetabbā’’ti.

889-898. "Bhikkhus, there are these five higher fetters. Which five? Lust for form, lust for the formless, conceit, restlessness, and ignorance. These, bhikkhus, are the five higher fetters. For the direct knowledge, full understanding, utter destruction, and abandoning of these five higher fetters, the four bases of spiritual power should be developed. Which four? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to desire and the volitional formations of exertion. He develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to energy... the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to mind... He develops the basis of spiritual power endowed with samādhi due to investigation and the volitional formations of exertion. For the direct knowledge, full understanding, utter destruction, and abandoning of these five higher fetters, these four bases of spiritual power should be developed."

(Yathā maggasaṃyuttaṃ tathā vitthāretabbaṃ).

(As with the Connected Discourses on the Path, so should it be expanded.)

Oghavaggo aṭṭhamo.

The Chapter on Floods, the Eighth.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Ogho yogo upādānaṃ, ganthā anusayena ca;Kāmaguṇā nīvaraṇā, khandhā oruddhambhāgiyāti.

Floods, Yokes, Clinging, Bonds, and Latent Tendencies; Strands of Sensual Pleasure, Hindrances, Aggregates, and Lower Fetters.

Iddhipādasaṃyuttaṃ sattamaṃ.

The Connected Discourses on the Bases of Spiritual Power, the Seventh.
