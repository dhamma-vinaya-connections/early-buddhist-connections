# SN 51.4 Nibbidāsuttaṃ

## SN 51.4 The Discourse on Disenchantment

816. ‘‘Cattārome, bhikkhave, iddhipādā bhāvitā bahulīkatā ekantanibbidāya virāgāya nirodhāya upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattanti. Katame cattāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi…pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti. Ime kho, bhikkhave, cattāro iddhipādā bhāvitā bahulīkatā ekantanibbidāya virāgāya nirodhāya upasamāya abhiññāya sambodhāya nibbānāya saṃvattantī’’ti. Catutthaṃ.

816.“Monks, these four bases for spiritual power, when developed and cultivated, lead to utter disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to full enlightenment, to Nibbāna. Which four? Here, monks, a bhikkhu develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi of zeal, accompanied by volitional formations of striving. He develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi of energy, accompanied by volitional formations of striving. He develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi of mind, accompanied by volitional formations of striving. He develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi of investigation, accompanied by volitional formations of striving. These, monks, are the four bases for spiritual power that, when developed and cultivated, lead to utter disenchantment, to dispassion, to cessation, to peace, to direct knowledge, to full enlightenment, to Nibbāna.” The fourth.
