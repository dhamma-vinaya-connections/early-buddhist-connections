# SN 51.15 Uṇṇābhabrāhmaṇasuttaṃ

## SN 51.15 The Discourse to Uṇṇābha the Brahmin

827. Evaṃ me sutaṃ – ekaṃ samayaṃ āyasmā ānando kosambiyaṃ viharati ghositārāme. Atha kho uṇṇābho brāhmaṇo yenāyasmā ānando tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā āyasmatā ānandena saddhiṃ sammodi. Sammodanīyaṃ kathaṃ sāraṇīyaṃ vītisāretvā ekamantaṃ nisīdi. Ekamantaṃ nisinno kho uṇṇābho brāhmaṇo āyasmantaṃ ānandaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘kimatthiyaṃ nu kho, bho ānanda, samaṇe gotame brahmacariyaṃ vussatī’’ti? ‘‘Chandappahānatthaṃ kho, brāhmaṇa, bhagavati brahmacariyaṃ vussatī’’ti.

827.Thus have I heard. At one time the Venerable Ānanda was dwelling at Kosambī in Ghosita’s Park. Then the brahmin Uṇṇābha approached the Venerable Ānanda. Having approached, he exchanged greetings with the Venerable Ānanda. When they had exchanged courteous greetings and amiable talk, he sat down to one side. When he had sat down to one side, the brahmin Uṇṇābha said to the Venerable Ānanda: “For what purpose, Master Ānanda, is the holy life lived under the ascetic Gotama?” “For the purpose of abandoning desire, brahmin, the holy life is lived under the Blessed One.”

‘‘Atthi pana, bho ānanda, maggo atthi paṭipadā etassa chandassa pahānāyā’’ti? ‘‘Atthi kho, brāhmaṇa, maggo atthi paṭipadā etassa chandassa pahānāyā’’ti.

“Is there, Master Ānanda, a path, a practice for abandoning this desire?” “There is, brahmin, a path, a practice for abandoning this desire.”

‘‘Katamo pana, bho ānanda, maggo katamā paṭipadā etassa chandassa pahānāyā’’ti? ‘‘Idha, brāhmaṇa, bhikkhu chandasamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti, vīriyasamādhi…pe… cittasamādhi…pe… vīmaṃsāsamādhippadhānasaṅkhārasamannāgataṃ iddhipādaṃ bhāveti – ayaṃ kho, brāhmaṇa, maggo ayaṃ paṭipadā etassa chandassa pahānāyā’’ti.

“And what, Master Ānanda, is the path, what is the practice for abandoning this desire?” “Here, brahmin, a bhikkhu develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi due to desire and the volitional formations of striving. He develops the basis for spiritual power that possesses samādhi due to energy… samādhi due to mind… samādhi due to investigation and the volitional formations of striving. This, brahmin, is the path, this is the practice for abandoning this desire.”

‘‘Evaṃ sante, bho ānanda, santakaṃ hoti no asantakaṃ. Chandeneva chandaṃ pajahissatīti – netaṃ ṭhānaṃ vijjati’’. ‘‘Tena hi, brāhmaṇa, taññevettha paṭipucchissāmi. Yathā te khameyya tathā taṃ byākareyyāsi. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, brāhmaṇa, ahosi te pubbe chando ‘ārāmaṃ gamissāmī’ti? Tassa te ārāmagatassa yo tajjo chando so paṭippassaddho’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’. ‘‘Ahosi te pubbe vīriyaṃ ‘ārāmaṃ gamissāmī’ti? Tassa te ārāmagatassa yaṃ tajjaṃ vīriyaṃ taṃ paṭippassaddha’’nti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’. ‘‘Ahosi te pubbe cittaṃ ‘ārāmaṃ gamissāmī’ti? Tassa te ārāmagatassa yaṃ tajjaṃ cittaṃ taṃ paṭippassaddha’’nti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’. ‘‘Ahosi te pubbe vīmaṃsā ‘ārāmaṃ gamissāmī’ti? Tassa te ārāmagatassa yā tajjā vīmaṃsā sā paṭippassaddhā’’ti? ‘‘Evaṃ, bho’’.

“If that is so, Master Ānanda, then it is something that is possessed, not something that is not possessed. That desire should abandon desire—this is not possible.” “Well then, brahmin, I shall question you in return about this. Answer as you think fit. What do you think, brahmin? Did you previously have the desire: ‘I shall go to the park’? When you had gone to the park, did that desire of yours subside?” “Yes, Master.” “Did you previously have the effort: ‘I shall go to the park’? When you had gone to the park, did that effort of yours subside?” “Yes, Master.” “Did you previously have the thought: ‘I shall go to the park’? When you had gone to the park, did that thought of yours subside?” “Yes, Master.” “Did you previously have the investigation: ‘I shall go to the park’? When you had gone to the park, did that investigation of yours subside?” “Yes, Master.”

‘‘Evameva kho, brāhmaṇa, yo so bhikkhu arahaṃ khīṇāsavo vusitavā katakaraṇīyo ohitabhāro anuppattasadattho parikkhīṇabhavasaṃyojano sammadaññā vimutto, tassa yo pubbe chando ahosi arahattappattiyā, arahattappatte [arahatte patte (sī. syā. kaṃ.)] yo tajjo chando so paṭippassaddho; yaṃ pubbe vīriyaṃ ahosi arahattappattiyā, arahattappatte yaṃ tajjaṃ vīriyaṃ taṃ paṭippassaddhaṃ; yaṃ pubbe cittaṃ ahosi arahattappattiyā, arahattappatte yaṃ tajjaṃ cittaṃ taṃ paṭippassaddhaṃ; yā pubbe vīmaṃsā ahosi arahattappattiyā, arahattappatte yā tajjā vīmaṃsā sā paṭippassaddhā. Taṃ kiṃ maññasi, brāhmaṇa, iti evaṃ sante, santakaṃ vā hoti no asantakaṃ vā’’ti?

“Just as, brahmin, for a bhikkhu who is an arahant, one whose taints are destroyed, who has lived the holy life, done what had to be done, laid down the burden, reached his own goal, utterly destroyed the fetters of existence, and is liberated by right final knowledge—for him, the desire that was previously present for the attainment of arahantship, upon the attainment of arahantship that desire subsides; the effort that was previously present for the attainment of arahantship, upon the attainment of arahantship that effort subsides; the thought that was previously present for the attainment of arahantship, upon the attainment of arahantship that thought subsides; the investigation that was previously present for the attainment of arahantship, upon the attainment of arahantship that investigation subsides. What do you think, brahmin? In such a case, is it something that is possessed, or not something that is possessed?”

‘‘Addhā, bho ānanda, evaṃ sante, santakaṃ hoti no asantakaṃ. Abhikkantaṃ, bho ānanda, abhikkantaṃ, bho ānanda! Seyyathāpi, bho ānanda, nikkujjitaṃ vā ukkujjeyya, paṭicchannaṃ vā vivareyya, mūḷhassa vā maggaṃ ācikkheyya, andhakāre vā telapajjotaṃ dhāreyya – cakkhumanto rūpāni dakkhantīti; evamevaṃ bhotā ānandena anekapariyāyena dhammo pakāsito. Esāhaṃ, bho ānanda, taṃ bhavantaṃ gotamaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi dhammañca bhikkhusaṅghañca. Upāsakaṃ maṃ bhavaṃ ānando dhāretu ajjatagge pāṇupetaṃ saraṇaṃ gata’’nti. Pañcamaṃ.

“Certainly, Master Ānanda, in such a case, it is something that is possessed, not something that is not possessed. Magnificent, Master Ānanda! Magnificent, Master Ānanda! Just as if one were to set upright what had been overturned, or to reveal what was hidden, or to show the way to one who was lost, or to carry a lamp into the darkness so that those with eyes could see forms—in the same way has Master Ānanda made the Dhamma clear in many ways. I go for refuge to that Blessed Gotama, to the Dhamma, and to the Saṅgha of bhikkhus. May Master Ānanda remember me as a lay follower who has gone for refuge from today onwards for as long as life lasts.” The Fifth.
