# SN 47.50 Āsavasuttaṃ

## SN 47.50 The Discourse on the Taints

416. ‘‘Tayome, bhikkhave āsavā. Katame tayo? Kāmāsavo, bhavāsavo, avijjāsavo – ime kho, bhikkhave, tayo āsavā. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, tiṇṇannaṃ āsavānaṃ pahānāya cattāro satipaṭṭhānā bhāvetabbā.

416.“Monks, there are these three taints. Which three? The taint of sensual desire, the taint of becoming, and the taint of ignorance. These, monks, are the three taints. For the abandoning of these three taints, the four foundations of mindfulness should be developed.”

‘‘Katame cattāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu kāye kāyānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; vedanāsu…pe… citte…pe… dhammesu dhammānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, tiṇṇannaṃ āsavānaṃ pahānāya ime cattāro satipaṭṭhānā bhāvetabbā’’ti. Dasamaṃ.

“What four? Here, monks, a bhikkhu ![[Satipatthanas]]. He ![[Satipatthanas]]. For the abandoning of these three taints, these four foundations of mindfulness should be developed.” This is the tenth discourse.

Amatavaggo pañcamo.

The Chapter on the Deathless, the Fifth.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Amataṃ samudayo maggo, sati kusalarāsi ca;Pātimokkhaṃ duccaritaṃ, mittavedanā āsavena cāti.

Deathless, Origin, Path, Mindfulness, Heap of wholesome states; The Pātimokkha, Misconduct, Friend, Feeling, and the Taints.

6. Gaṅgāpeyyālavaggo

6.The Chapter on the Ganges Repetition Section

## 1-12. Gaṅgānadīādisuttadvādasakaṃ

### 1–12. The Group of Twelve Discourses Beginning with the Ganges River

417-428. ‘‘Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, gaṅgā nadī pācīnaninnā pācīnapoṇā pācīnapabbhārā; evameva kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bhāvento cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro.

417–428. “Just as, monks, the Ganges River inclines to the east, slopes to the east, and tends to the east; in the same way, monks, a bhikkhu who develops and cultivates the four foundations of mindfulness inclines toward Nibbāna, slopes toward Nibbāna, and tends toward Nibbāna.”

‘‘Kathañca, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bhāvento cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu kāye kāyānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; vedanāsu …pe… citte …pe… dhammesu dhammānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ. Evaṃ kho, bhikkhave, bhikkhu cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bhāvento cattāro satipaṭṭhāne bahulīkaronto nibbānaninno hoti nibbānapoṇo nibbānapabbhāro’’ti vitthāretabbaṃ.

“And how, monks, does a bhikkhu, developing and cultivating the four foundations of mindfulness, incline toward Nibbāna, slope toward Nibbāna, and tend toward Nibbāna? Here, monks, a bhikkhu ![[Satipatthanas]]. He ![[Satipatthanas]]. It is in this way, monks, that a bhikkhu, developing and cultivating the four foundations of mindfulness, inclines toward Nibbāna, slopes toward Nibbāna, and tends toward Nibbāna.” This should be expanded.

Gaṅgāpeyyālavaggo chaṭṭho.

The Chapter on the Ganges Repetition Section, the Sixth.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Cha pācīnato ninnā, cha ninnā ca samuddato;Ete dve cha dvādasa honti, vaggo tena pavuccatīti.

Six inclining to the east, and six inclining to the ocean; these two sixes make twelve, thus the chapter is called.

7. Appamādavaggo

7.The Chapter on Heedfulness

## 1-10. Tathāgatādisuttadasakaṃ

### 1–10. The Decad of Discourses Beginning with the Tathāgata

429-438. Yāvatā, bhikkhave, sattā apadā vā dvipadā vā catuppadā vā bahuppadā vāti vitthāretabbaṃ.

429–438. “Monks, as far as there are beings, whether footless, two-footed, four-footed, or many-footed—this should be expanded.”

Appamādavaggo sattamo.

The Chapter on Heedfulness, the Seventh.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Tathāgataṃ padaṃ kūṭaṃ, mūlaṃ sāro ca vassikaṃ;Rājā candimasūriyā, vatthena dasamaṃ padanti.

The Tathāgata, the Footprint, the Peak, the Root, the Essence, and the Rain-cloth; The King, the Moon, and the Sun—the tenth is declared to be the Rain-cloth.

8. Balakaraṇīyavaggo

8.The Chapter on What Requires Strength

## 1-12. Balādisuttadvādasakaṃ

### 1–12. The Group of Twelve Discourses Beginning with Strength

439-450. Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, ye keci balakaraṇīyā kammantā karīyantīti vitthāretabbaṃ.

439–450. “Just as, monks, whatever tasks requiring strength are done—this should be expanded.”

Balakaraṇīyavaggo aṭṭhamo.

The Chapter on What Requires Strength, the Eighth.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Balaṃ bījañca nāgo ca, rukkho kumbhena sūkiyā;Ākāsena ca dve meghā, nāvā āgantukā nadīti.

Strength, Seed, Elephant, Tree, Pot, Needle, Sky, Two Clouds, Boat, Visitor, River.

9. Esanāvaggo

9.The Division on Seeking

## 1-10. Esanādisuttadasakaṃ

### 1–10. The Decad on Discourses Beginning with Seeking

451-460. Tisso imā, bhikkhave, esanā. Katamā tisso? Kāmesanā, bhavesanā, brahmacariyesanāti vitthāretabbaṃ.

451–460. “Bhikkhus, there are these three kinds of seeking. Which three? Seeking sensual pleasures, seeking existence, and seeking the holy life.” This should be elaborated.

Esanāvaggo navamo.

The Ninth Division is on Seeking.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Esanā vidhā āsavo, bhavo ca dukkhatā tisso;Khilaṃ malañca nīgho ca, vedanā taṇhā tasināya cāti.

Seekings, modes, taints, existence, and the three kinds of suffering; Barrenness, stains, afflictions, feelings, craving, and thirst.

10. Oghavaggo

10.The Division on Floods

## 1-10. Uddhambhāgiyādisuttadasakaṃ

### 1–10. The Decad on Discourses Beginning with the Higher Fetters

461-470. ‘‘Pañcimāni, bhikkhave, uddhambhāgiyāni saṃyojanāni. Katamāni pañca? Rūparāgo, arūparāgo, māno, uddhaccaṃ, avijjā – imāni kho, bhikkhave, pañcuddhambhāgiyāni saṃyojanāni. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, pañcannaṃ uddhambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ abhiññāya pariññāya parikkhayāya pahānāya cattāro satipaṭṭhānā bhāvetabbā.

461–470. “Bhikkhus, there are these five higher fetters. Which five? Lust for form, lust for the formless, conceit, restlessness, and ignorance. These, bhikkhus, are the five higher fetters. For the direct knowledge, full understanding, utter destruction, and abandonment of these five higher fetters, the four foundations of mindfulness should be developed.

‘‘Katame cattāro? Idha, bhikkhave, bhikkhu kāye kāyānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ; vedanāsu…pe… citte…pe… dhammesu dhammānupassī viharati ātāpī sampajāno satimā, vineyya loke abhijjhādomanassaṃ. Imesaṃ kho, bhikkhave, pañcannaṃ uddhambhāgiyānaṃ saṃyojanānaṃ abhiññāya pariññāya parikkhayāya pahānāya ime cattāro satipaṭṭhānā bhāvetabbā’’ti.

“Which four? Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu ![[Satipatthanas]]. He ![[Satipatthanas]]. For the direct knowledge, full understanding, utter destruction, and abandonment of these five higher fetters, these four foundations of mindfulness should be developed.”

(Yathā maggasaṃyuttaṃ tathā satipaṭṭhānasaṃyuttaṃ vitthāretabbaṃ).

(As with the Connected Discourses on the Path, so the Connected Discourses on the Foundations of Mindfulness should be elaborated.)

Oghavaggo dasamo.

The Tenth Division is on Floods.

Tassuddānaṃ –

Its Summary:

Ogho yogo upādānaṃ, ganthā anusayena ca;Kāmaguṇā nīvaraṇā, khandhā oruddhambhāgiyāti.

Floods, yokes, clinging, bonds, and latent tendencies; Sensual pleasures, hindrances, aggregates, and lower fetters.

Satipaṭṭhānasaṃyuttaṃ tatiyaṃ.

The Connected Discourses on the Foundations of Mindfulness, the Third.
