# SN 22.80 Piṇḍolyasuttaṃ

## SN 22.80 The Piṇḍolya Sutta

80. Ekaṃ samayaṃ bhagavā sakkesu viharati kapilavatthusmiṃ nigrodhārāme. Atha kho bhagavā kismiñcideva pakaraṇe bhikkhusaṅghaṃ paṇāmetvā pubbaṇhasamayaṃ nivāsetvā pattacīvaramādāya kapilavatthuṃ piṇḍāya pāvisi. Kapilavatthusmiṃ piṇḍāya caritvā pacchābhattaṃ piṇḍapātapaṭikkanto yena mahāvanaṃ tenupasaṅkami divāvihārāya. Mahāvanaṃ ajjhogāhetvā beluvalaṭṭhikāya mūle divāvihāraṃ nisīdi.

80.On one occasion the Blessed One was dwelling among the Sakyans at Kapilavatthu in the Nigrodha Park. Then the Blessed One, having dismissed the community of monks for some reason, dressed in the morning, took his bowl and robe, and entered Kapilavatthu for alms. Having wandered for alms in Kapilavatthu, after the meal, on returning from the alms round, he approached the Great Wood for the day's retreat. Having entered the Great Wood, he sat down at the foot of a beluva shoot for the day's retreat.

Atha kho bhagavato rahogatassa paṭisallīnassa evaṃ cetaso parivitakko udapādi – ‘‘mayā kho bhikkhusaṅgho pabāḷho. Santettha bhikkhū navā acirapabbajitā adhunāgatā imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ. Tesaṃ mamaṃ apassantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Seyyathāpi nāma vacchassa taruṇassa mātaraṃ apassantassa siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo, evameva santettha bhikkhū navā acirapabbajitā adhunāgatā imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ tesaṃ mamaṃ apassantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Seyyathāpi nāma bījānaṃ taruṇānaṃ udakaṃ alabhantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo, evameva santettha…pe… tesaṃ mamaṃ alabhantānaṃ dassanāya siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Yaṃnūnāhaṃ yatheva mayā pubbe bhikkhusaṅgho anuggahito, evameva etarahi anuggaṇheyyaṃ bhikkhusaṅgha’’nti.

Then, while the Blessed One was alone in seclusion, this consideration arose in his mind: “The community of monks has been dismissed by me. There are monks here who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline. If they do not see me, there might be a change or a transformation. Just as a young calf, not seeing its mother, might change or transform, so too there are monks here who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline; if they do not see me, there might be a change or a transformation. Just as young seeds, not obtaining water, might change or transform, so too there are monks here who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline; if they do not obtain the opportunity to see me, there might be a change or a transformation. Why don’t I now support the community of monks just as I supported the community of monks before?”

Atha kho brahmā sahampati bhagavato cetasā cetoparivitakkamaññāya – seyyathāpi nāma balavā puriso samiñjitaṃ [sammiñjitaṃ (sī. syā. kaṃ. pī.)] vā bāhaṃ pasāreyya pasāritaṃ vā bāhaṃ samiñjeyya evameva – brahmaloke antarahito bhagavato purato pāturahosi. Atha kho brahmā sahampati ekaṃsaṃ uttarāsaṅgaṃ karitvā yena bhagavā tenañjaliṃ paṇāmetvā bhagavantaṃ etadavoca – ‘‘evametaṃ, bhagavā; evametaṃ, sugata! Bhagavato, bhante, bhikkhusaṅgho pabāḷho. Santettha bhikkhū navā acirapabbajitā adhunāgatā imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ. Tesaṃ bhagavantaṃ apassantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Seyyathāpi nāma vacchassa taruṇassa mātaraṃ apassantassa siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo, evameva santettha bhikkhū navā acirapabbajitā adhunāgatā imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ tesaṃ bhagavantaṃ apassantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Seyyathāpi nāma bījānaṃ taruṇānaṃ udakaṃ alabhantānaṃ siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo, evameva santettha bhikkhū navā acirapabbajitā adhunāgatā imaṃ dhammavinayaṃ, tesaṃ bhagavantaṃ alabhantānaṃ dassanāya siyā aññathattaṃ siyā vipariṇāmo. Abhinandatu, bhante, bhagavā bhikkhusaṅghaṃ; abhivadatu, bhante, bhagavā bhikkhusaṅghaṃ. Yatheva bhagavatā pubbe bhikkhusaṅgho anuggahito, evameva etarahi anuggaṇhātu bhikkhusaṅgha’’nti.

Then Brahmā Sahampati, knowing with his mind the thought in the Blessed One’s mind—just as a strong man might extend his bent arm or bend his extended arm—disappeared from the Brahmā world and reappeared before the Blessed One. Then Brahmā Sahampati arranged his upper robe over one shoulder, extended his joined hands in reverential salutation toward the Blessed One, and said to him: “So it is, Blessed One! So it is, Fortunate One! The Blessed One’s community of monks has been dismissed, venerable sir. There are here monks who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline. If they do not see the Blessed One, there might be a change or a transformation. Just as a young calf, not seeing its mother, might change or transform, so too there are here monks who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline; if they do not see the Blessed One, there might be a change or a transformation. Just as young seeds, not obtaining water, might change or transform, so too there are here monks who are new, recently gone forth, newly come to this Dhamma and Discipline; if they do not get to see the Blessed One, there might be a change or a transformation. Let the Blessed One delight in the community of monks, venerable sir! Let the Blessed One address the community of monks! Just as the Blessed One formerly favored the community of monks, so too let him favor the community of monks now.”

Adhivāsesi bhagavā tuṇhībhāvena. Atha kho brahmā sahampati bhagavato adhivāsanaṃ viditvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā padakkhiṇaṃ katvā tatthevantaradhāyi.

The Blessed One consented by his silence. Then Brahmā Sahampati, knowing the Blessed One's consent, bowed to him, circumambulated him, and disappeared right there.

Atha kho bhagavā sāyanhasamayaṃ paṭisallānā vuṭṭhito yena nigrodhārāmo tenupasaṅkami; upasaṅkamitvā paññatte āsane nisīdi. Nisajja kho bhagavā tathārūpaṃ iddhābhisaṅkhāraṃ abhisaṅkhāsi [abhisaṅkhāresi (syā. kaṃ.), abhisaṅkhāyi (pī.), abhisaṅkharoti (ka.)] yathā te bhikkhū (ekadvīhikāya sārajjamānarūpā yenāhaṃ [yena bhagavā (?)] tenupasaṅkameyyuṃ. Tepi bhikkhū ) [( ) sī. syā. kaṃ. potthakesu natthi] ekadvīhikāya sārajjamānarūpā yena bhagavā tenupasaṅkamiṃsu; upasaṅkamitvā bhagavantaṃ abhivādetvā ekamantaṃ nisīdiṃsu. Ekamantaṃ nisinne kho te bhikkhū bhagavā etadavoca –

Then, in the evening, the Blessed One arose from seclusion and went to the Nigrodha Grove. Having arrived, he sat down on the prepared seat. Having sat down, the Blessed One performed such a psychic feat that those monks would come to him one or two at a time, with a sense of shame and fear. And those monks, one or two at a time, with a sense of shame and fear, went to the Blessed One. Having approached, they paid homage to the Blessed One and sat down to one side. As they were sitting to one side, the Blessed One said to them:

‘‘Antamidaṃ, bhikkhave, jīvikānaṃ yadidaṃ piṇḍolyaṃ. Abhisāpoyaṃ, bhikkhave, lokasmiṃ piṇḍolo vicarasi pattapāṇīti. Tañca kho etaṃ, bhikkhave, kulaputtā upenti atthavasikā, atthavasaṃ paṭicca; neva rājābhinītā, na corābhinītā, na iṇaṭṭā, na bhayaṭṭā, na ājīvikāpakatā; api ca kho otiṇṇāmha jātiyā jarāya maraṇena sokehi paridevehi dukkhehi domanassehi upāyāsehi dukkhotiṇṇā dukkhaparetā appeva nāma imassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa antakiriyā paññāyethāti.

“Monks, this is the lowest of livelihoods, that is, alms-seeking. Monks, in the world, this is a curse: ‘He wanders for alms, bowl in hand.’ And yet, monks, noble sons take this up for a purpose, because of a purpose; not compelled by kings, not compelled by thieves, not because of debt, not because of fear, not because of loss of livelihood, but rather: ‘We are overwhelmed by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair. We are afflicted by suffering, overcome by suffering. Perhaps an end to this entire mass of suffering may be discerned.’”

‘‘Evaṃ pabbajito cāyaṃ, bhikkhave, kulaputto. So ca hoti abhijjhālu kāmesu tibbasārāgo byāpannacitto paduṭṭhamanasaṅkappo muṭṭhassati asampajāno asamāhito vibbhantacitto pākatindriyo. Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, chavālātaṃ ubhatopadittaṃ majjhe gūthagataṃ, neva gāme kaṭṭhatthaṃ pharati, nāraññe kaṭṭhatthaṃ pharati. Tathūpamāhaṃ, bhikkhave, imaṃ puggalaṃ vadāmi gihibhogā ca parihīno, sāmaññatthañca na paripūreti.

“Thus gone forth, this noble son, monks, is covetous, intensely passionate in sensual pleasures, with a malevolent mind, with corrupt thoughts, unmindful, without clear comprehension, uncollected, with a bewildered mind, and with ungoverned senses. Just as, monks, a firebrand burning at both ends, smeared with dung in the middle, serves no purpose as firewood either in the village or in the forest—so too, monks, I describe this person as one who has declined from household enjoyments and does not fulfill the purpose of the ascetic life.

‘‘Tayome, bhikkhave, akusalavitakkā – kāmavitakko, byāpādavitakko, vihiṃsāvitakko. Ime ca bhikkhave, tayo akusalavitakkā kva aparisesā nirujjhanti? Catūsu vā satipaṭṭhānesu suppatiṭṭhitacittassa viharato animittaṃ vā samādhiṃ bhāvayato. Yāvañcidaṃ, bhikkhave, alameva animitto samādhi bhāvetuṃ. Animitto, bhikkhave, samādhi bhāvito bahulīkato mahapphalo hoti mahānisaṃso.

“Monks, there are these three unwholesome thoughts: sensual thought, ill-will thought, and cruel thought. And where do these three unwholesome thoughts cease without remainder? In one who dwells with a mind well-established in the four foundations of mindfulness, or who develops signless samādhi. Indeed, monks, it is quite enough to develop signless samādhi. Signless samādhi, when developed and cultivated, is of great fruit and great benefit.”

‘‘Dvemā, bhikkhave, diṭṭhiyo – bhavadiṭṭhi ca vibhavadiṭṭhi ca. Tatra kho, bhikkhave, sutavā ariyasāvako iti paṭisañcikkhati – ‘atthi nu kho taṃ kiñci lokasmiṃ yamahaṃ upādiyamāno na vajjavā assa’nti? So evaṃ pajānāti – ‘natthi nu kho taṃ kiñci lokasmiṃ yamahaṃ upādiyamāno na vajjavā assaṃ. Ahañhi rūpaññeva upādiyamāno upādiyeyyaṃ vedanaññeva… saññaññeva… saṅkhāreyeva viññāṇaññeva upādiyamāno upādiyeyyaṃ. Tassa me assa [ayaṃ (ka.)] upādānapaccayā bhavo; bhavapaccayā jāti; jātipaccayā jarāmaraṇaṃ sokaparidevadukkhadomanassupāyāsā sambhaveyyuṃ. Evametassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa samudayo assā’’’ti.

“Monks, there are these two views: the view of existence and the view of annihilation. Here, monks, a learned noble disciple reflects thus: ‘Is there anything in the world that, by clinging to which, I would not be blameworthy?’ He understands thus: ‘There is nothing in the world that, by clinging to which, I would not be blameworthy. For if I were to cling to form, or feeling, or perception, or volitional formations, or consciousness, for me, due to clinging as condition, there would be becoming; due to becoming as condition, birth; due to birth as condition, aging and death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and despair would come to be. Thus, there would be the arising of this entire mass of suffering.’”

‘‘Taṃ kiṃ maññatha, bhikkhave, rūpaṃ niccaṃ vā aniccaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Aniccaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vā taṃ sukhaṃ vā’’ti? ‘‘Dukkhaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Yaṃ panāniccaṃ dukkhaṃ vipariṇāmadhammaṃ kallaṃ nu taṃ samanupassituṃ – ‘etaṃ mama, esohamasmi, eso me attā’’’ti? ‘‘No hetaṃ, bhante’’. ‘‘Vedanā… saññā… saṅkhārā… viññāṇaṃ…pe… tasmātiha, bhikkhave, evaṃ passaṃ… nāparaṃ itthattāyāti pajānātī’’ti. Aṭṭhamaṃ.

“What do you think, monks? Is form permanent or impermanent?” “Impermanent, venerable sir.” “And what is impermanent, is that suffering or happiness?” “Suffering, venerable sir.” “And what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change, is it fit to be regarded thus: ‘This is mine, this I am, this is my self’?” “No, venerable sir.” “Feeling… perception… volitional formations… consciousness… Therefore, monks, seeing thus… he understands: ‘There is no more coming to any state of being.’”
