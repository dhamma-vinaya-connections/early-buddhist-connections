# Thig 15. Cattālīsanipāto
## 15. The Chapter of Fifteens

15.The Chapter of the Forties
### 1. Isidāsītherīgāthā
1.The Verses of the Elder Isidāsī
402.
Nagaramhi kusumanāme, pāṭaliputtamhi pathaviyā maṇḍe;Sakyakulakulīnāyo, dve bhikkhuniyo hi guṇavatiyo. In the city named Kusuma, Pāṭaliputta, the ornament of the earth, there were two virtuous bhikkhunīs from a noble Sakyan family.


403.
Isidāsī tattha ekā, dutiyā bodhīti sīlasampannā ca;Jhānajjhāyanaratāyo, bahussutāyo dhutakilesāyo. One was Isidāsī, the other Bodhī, both accomplished in virtue. They delighted in jhāna and study, were very learned, and had shaken off their defilements.


404.
Tā piṇḍāya caritvā, bhattatthaṃ [bhattattaṃ (sī.)] kariya dhotapattāyo;Rahitamhi sukhanisinnā, imā girā abbhudīresuṃ.

Having wandered for alms, finished their meal, and washed their bowls, they sat comfortably in seclusion and uttered these words.

405.
‘‘Pāsādikāsi ayye, isidāsi vayopi te aparihīno;Kiṃ disvāna byālikaṃ, athāsi nekkhammamanuyuttā’’. “Venerable Isidāsī, you are lovely, and your youth has not yet faded. What misfortune did you see that led you to devote yourself to renunciation?”


406.
Evamanuyuñjiyamānā sā, rahite dhammadesanākusalā;Isidāsī vacanamabravi, ‘‘suṇa bodhi yathāmhi pabbajitā. Thus questioned in seclusion, Isidāsī, who was skilled in teaching the Dhamma, spoke these words: “Listen, Bodhi, to how I went forth.


407.
‘‘Ujjeniyā puravare, mayhaṃ pitā sīlasaṃvuto seṭṭhi;Tassamhi ekadhītā, piyā manāpā ca dayitā ca. “In the excellent city of Ujjenī, my father was a merchant, virtuous and restrained. I was his only daughter, dear, pleasing, and beloved.


408.
‘‘Atha me sāketato varakā, āgacchumuttamakulīnā;Seṭṭhī pahūtaratano, tassa mamaṃ suṇhamadāsi tāto. “Then from Sāketa came a suitor from a preeminent family, a merchant of great wealth. To his son my father gave me as a daughter-in-law.


409.
‘‘Sassuyā sassurassa ca, sāyaṃ pātaṃ paṇāmamupagamma;Sirasā karomi pāde, vandāmi yathāmhi anusiṭṭhā.

“Morning and evening, I would approach my mother-in-law and father-in-law. As I had been instructed, I would pay homage, bowing my head to their feet.

410.
‘‘Yā mayhaṃ sāmikassa, bhaginiyo bhātuno parijano vā;Tamekavarakampi disvā, ubbiggā āsanaṃ demi.

“Upon seeing any of my husband's sisters, brothers, or attendants, I would anxiously rise and offer them my seat.

411.
‘‘Annena ca pānena ca, khajjena ca yañca tattha sannihitaṃ;Chādemi upanayāmi ca, demi ca yaṃ yassa patirūpaṃ.

“With food and drink, with hard food and whatever else was available, I would provide and serve, giving to each what was appropriate.

412.
‘‘Kālena upaṭṭhahitvā [uṭṭhahitvā (syā. ka.), upaṭṭhahituṃ (?)], gharaṃ samupagamāmi ummāre;Dhovantī hatthapāde, pañjalikā sāmikamupemi.

“Rising at the proper time to attend to my duties, I would wash my hands and feet. Then, with palms joined in reverence, I would approach my husband.

413.
‘‘Kocchaṃ pasādaṃ añjaniñca, ādāsakañca gaṇhitvā;Parikammakārikā viya, sayameva patiṃ vibhūsemi.

“Taking a comb, cosmetics, eye-ointment, and a mirror, I myself would adorn my husband as if I were a handmaiden.

414.
‘‘Sayameva odanaṃ sādhayāmi, sayameva bhājanaṃ dhovantī;Mātāva ekaputtakaṃ, tathā [tadā (sī.)] bhattāraṃ paricarāmi.

“I myself cooked the rice; I myself washed the dishes. I served my husband just as a mother serves her only child.

415.
‘‘Evaṃ maṃ bhattikataṃ, anurattaṃ kārikaṃ nihatamānaṃ;Uṭṭhāyikaṃ [uṭṭhāhikaṃ (ka.)] analasaṃ, sīlavatiṃ dussate bhattā.

“Though I was so devoted and affectionate, a dutiful worker with pride subdued, energetic, diligent, and virtuous, my husband despised me.

416.
‘‘So mātarañca pitarañca, bhaṇati ‘āpucchahaṃ gamissāmi;Isidāsiyā na saha vacchaṃ, ekāgārehaṃ [ekagharepa’haṃ (?)] saha vatthuṃ’. “He said to his mother and father: ‘I will take my leave and go. I will not live with Isidāsī or dwell together with her in the same house.’


417.
‘‘‘Mā evaṃ putta avaca, isidāsī paṇḍitā paribyattā;Uṭṭhāyikā analasā, kiṃ tuyhaṃ na rocate putta’. “‘Do not speak thus, son. Isidāsī is wise and competent, energetic and diligent. What about her does not please you, son?’


418.
‘‘‘Na ca me hiṃsati kiñci, na cahaṃ isidāsiyā saha vacchaṃ;Dessāva me alaṃ me, apucchāhaṃ [āpucchāhaṃ (syā.), āpucchahaṃ-nāpucchahaṃ (?)] gamissāmi’. “‘She does not harm me in any way, but I will not live with Isidāsī. She is hateful to me, I have had enough. I will take my leave and go.’


419.
‘‘Tassa vacanaṃ suṇitvā, sassu sasuro ca maṃ apucchiṃsu;‘Kissa [kiṃsa (?)] tayā aparaddhaṃ, bhaṇa vissaṭṭhā yathābhūtaṃ’.

“Hearing his words, my mother-in-law and father-in-law asked me: ‘In what have you done wrong? Speak freely and tell the truth.’

420.
‘‘‘Napihaṃ aparajjhaṃ kiñci, napi hiṃsemi na bhaṇāmi dubbacanaṃ;Kiṃ sakkā kātuyye, yaṃ maṃ viddessate bhattā’.

“‘I have done no wrong at all. I do not harm him or speak harsh words. What can be done, venerable lady, when my husband despises me?’

421.
‘‘Te maṃ pitugharaṃ paṭinayiṃsu, vimanā dukhena adhibhūtā;‘Puttamanurakkhamānā, jitāmhase rūpiniṃ lakkhiṃ’.

“They sent me back to my father’s house, dejected and overwhelmed with sorrow. They said: ‘Protecting our son, we have been defeated and lost a veritable goddess of fortune.’

422.
‘‘Atha maṃ adāsi tāto, aḍḍhassa gharamhi dutiyakulikassa;Tato upaḍḍhasuṅkena, yena maṃ vindatha seṭṭhi.

“Then my father gave me to a man from another wealthy family for half the bride-price that the first merchant had paid.

423.
‘‘Tassapi gharamhi māsaṃ, avasiṃ atha sopi maṃ paṭiccharayi [paṭicchasi (sī. ka.), paṭicchati (syā.), paṭiccharati (ka.)];Dāsīva upaṭṭhahantiṃ, adūsikaṃ sīlasampannaṃ.

“I lived in his house for a month, attending on him like a slave-girl, faultless and accomplished in virtue, but he too sent me away.

424.
‘‘Bhikkhāya ca vicarantaṃ, damakaṃ dantaṃ me pitā bhaṇati;‘Hohisi [sohisi (sabbattha)] me jāmātā, nikkhipa poṭṭhiñca [pontiṃ (sī. syā.)] ghaṭikañca’.

“My father saw a disciplined man, a tamer of the untamed, wandering for alms, and said to him: ‘You shall be my son-in-law. Put aside your bag and your water-pot.’

425.
‘‘Sopi vasitvā pakkhaṃ [pakkamatha (sī.)], atha tātaṃ bhaṇati ‘dehi me poṭṭhiṃ;Ghaṭikañca mallakañca, punapi bhikkhaṃ carissāmi’.

“After living there for a fortnight, he too said to my father: ‘Give me my bag, my water-pot, and my bowl. I will go wandering for alms again.’

426.
‘‘Atha naṃ bhaṇatī tāto, ammā sabbo ca me ñātigaṇavaggo;‘Kiṃ te na kīrati idha, bhaṇa khippaṃ taṃ te karihi’ti.

“Then my father and mother and all my relatives said to him: ‘What is not being done for you here? Speak quickly, and we will do it for you.’

427.
‘‘Evaṃ bhaṇito bhaṇati, ‘yadi me attā sakkoti alaṃ mayhaṃ;Isidāsiyā na saha vacchaṃ, ekagharehaṃ saha vatthuṃ’. “Thus addressed, he said: ‘I am quite able to support myself. I will not live with Isidāsī or dwell together with her in one house.’


428.
‘‘Vissajjito gato so, ahampi ekākinī vicintemi;‘Āpucchitūna gacchaṃ, marituye [maritāye (sī.), marituṃ (syā.)] vā pabbajissaṃ vā’.

“Dismissed, he departed. Left alone, I reflected: ‘I shall ask for permission to go—either to die or to go forth.’

429.
‘‘Atha ayyā jinadattā, āgacchī gocarāya caramānā;Tātakulaṃ vinayadharī, bahussutā sīlasampannā. “Then the venerable Jinadattā, a preserver of the Vinaya, very learned and accomplished in virtue, came to my father’s house while wandering for alms.


430.
‘‘Taṃ disvāna amhākaṃ, uṭṭhāyāsanaṃ tassā paññāpayiṃ;Nisinnāya ca pāde, vanditvā bhojanamadāsiṃ.

“Seeing her, I rose from my seat and prepared one for her. When she was seated, I bowed to her feet and offered her food.

431.
‘‘Annena ca pānena ca, khajjena ca yañca tattha sannihitaṃ;Santappayitvā avacaṃ, ‘ayye icchāmi pabbajituṃ’.

“Having satisfied her with food and drink, with hard food and whatever else was available, I said: ‘Venerable lady, I wish to go forth.’

432.
‘‘Atha maṃ bhaṇatī tāto, ‘idheva puttaka [puttike (syā. ka.)] carāhi tvaṃ dhammaṃ;Annena ca pānena ca, tappaya samaṇe dvijātī ca’.

“Then my father said to me: ‘Dear child, practice the Dhamma right here. Gratify ascetics and brahmins with food and drink.’

433.
‘‘Athahaṃ bhaṇāmi tātaṃ, rodantī añjaliṃ paṇāmetvā;‘Pāpañhi mayā pakataṃ, kammaṃ taṃ nijjaressāmi’.

“Then, weeping, with hands joined in reverence, I said to my father: ‘Indeed, an evil kamma has been done by me. I shall wear it out.’

434.
‘‘Atha maṃ bhaṇatī tāto, ‘pāpuṇa bodhiñca aggadhammañca;Nibbānañca labhassu, yaṃ sacchikarī dvipadaseṭṭho’. “Then my father said to me: ‘May you attain awakening and the supreme Dhamma. May you realize Nibbāna, which the Best of Bipeds himself realized.’


435.
‘‘Mātāpitū abhivādayitvā, sabbañca ñātigaṇavaggaṃ;Sattāhaṃ pabbajitā, tisso vijjā aphassayiṃ.

“Having paid homage to my mother and father and to my whole circle of relatives, within seven days of going forth I attained the three true knowledges.

436.
‘‘Jānāmi attano satta, jātiyo yassayaṃ phalavipāko;Taṃ tava ācikkhissaṃ, taṃ ekamanā nisāmehi.

“I know my own seven past lives, of which this is the fruit and result. I shall declare that to you; listen with a collected mind.

437.
‘‘Nagaramhi erakacche [erakakacche (syā. ka.)], suvaṇṇakāro ahaṃ pahūtadhano;Yobbanamadena matto so, paradāraṃ asevihaṃ.

“In the city of Erakaccha, I was a wealthy goldsmith. Intoxicated with the pride of youth, I had affairs with the wives of others.

438.
‘‘Sohaṃ tato cavitvā, nirayamhi apaccisaṃ ciraṃ;Pakko tato ca uṭṭhahitvā, makkaṭiyā kucchimokkamiṃ.

“Passing away from there, I was cooked in hell for a long time. Rising from there, I was conceived in the womb of a female monkey.

439.
‘‘Sattāhajātakaṃ maṃ, mahākapi yūthapo nillacchesi;Tassetaṃ kammaphalaṃ, yathāpi gantvāna paradāraṃ.

“When I was seven days old, the great monkey who was leader of the troop castrated me. This was the fruit of that kamma, for having gone with another’s wife.

440.
‘‘Sohaṃ tato cavitvā, kālaṃ karitvā sindhavāraññe;Kāṇāya ca khañjāya ca, eḷakiyā kucchimokkamiṃ.

“Passing away from there and dying in the Sindhava forest, I was conceived in the womb of a one-eyed and lame she-goat.

441.
‘‘Dvādasa vassāni ahaṃ, nillacchito dārake parivahitvā;Kimināvaṭṭo akallo, yathāpi gantvāna paradāraṃ.

“For twelve years, castrated, I carried children about. Infested with worms and infirm—this was for having gone with another’s wife.

442.
‘‘Sohaṃ tato cavitvā, govāṇijakassa gāviyā jāto;Vaccho lākhātambo, nillacchito dvādase māse.

“Passing away from there, I was born as a lac-red calf to a cow of a cattle-trader. I was castrated in the twelfth month.

443.
‘‘Voḍhūna [te puna (syā. ka.), vodhuna (ka. aṭṭha.)] naṅgalamahaṃ, sakaṭañca dhārayāmi;Andhovaṭṭo akallo, yathāpi gantvāna paradāraṃ.

“I pulled the plough and drew the cart. Blind and infirm—this was for having gone with another’s wife.

444.
‘‘Sohaṃ tato cavitvā, vīthiyā dāsiyā ghare jāto;Neva mahilā na puriso, yathāpi gantvāna paradāraṃ.

“Passing away from there, I was born in the house of a street-slave, as one who was neither female nor male—this was for having gone with another’s wife.

445.
‘‘Tiṃsativassamhi mato, sākaṭikakulamhi dārikā jātā;Kapaṇamhi appabhoge, dhanika [aṇika (aṭṭha.), taṃsaṃvaṇṇanāyampi atthayutti gavesitabbā] purisapātabahulamhi.

“At thirty years of age I died and was born a girl in a carter’s family—a wretched family with little property, beset by moneylenders and full of drunkards.

446.
‘‘Taṃ maṃ tato satthavāho, ussannāya vipulāya vaḍḍhiyā;Okaḍḍhati vilapantiṃ, acchinditvā kulagharasmā.

“Then a caravan leader, to whom a huge debt with high interest was owed, snatched me from my family home and dragged me away while I was weeping.

447.
‘‘Atha soḷasame vasse, disvā maṃ pattayobbanaṃ kaññaṃ;Orundhatassa putto, giridāso nāma nāmena. “Then in my sixteenth year, a man named Giridāsa, the son of a householder, saw that I was a maiden who had come of age.


448.
‘‘Tassapi aññā bhariyā, sīlavatī guṇavatī yasavatī ca;Anurattā [anuvattā (ka.)] bhattāraṃ, tassāhaṃ [tassa taṃ (?)] viddesanamakāsiṃ.

“He had another wife, who was virtuous, of good character, and renowned, and she was devoted to her husband. I stirred up hatred against her.

449.
‘‘Tassetaṃ kammaphalaṃ, yaṃ maṃ apakīritūna gacchanti;Dāsīva upaṭṭhahantiṃ, tassapi anto kato mayā’’ti.

“This is the fruit of that kamma: that my husbands reject me and leave, even though I serve them like a slave-girl. That kamma was my bringing an end to my co-wife.” … Isidāsī therī…. ... The Elder Isidāsī ... Cattālīsanipāto niṭṭhito. The Chapter of the Forties is finished.
