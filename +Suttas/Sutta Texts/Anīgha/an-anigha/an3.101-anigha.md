# AN 3.101 - A Panner

â€œBhikkhus, gold has coarse defilements: sand, soil, and gravel. A panner or his apprentice pours it into a pan, where he washes, rinses, and cleans it. When that has been eliminated, there are medium defilements in the gold: fine grit and coarse sand. The panner washes it again. When that has been eliminated, there are fine defilements in the gold: fine sand and black grime. The panner washes it again. When that has been eliminated, only gold dust is left. A goldsmith or his apprentice places the gold in a crucible where he blows, melts, and smelts it. Still the gold is not settled and the dross is not entirely gone. It is not pliable, workable, or radiant, but is brittle and not completely ready for working. But the goldsmith continues blowing, melting, and smelting it. The gold becomes pliable, workable, and radiant, not brittle, and ready to be worked. Then the goldsmith can successfully create any kind of ornament he wants, whether a bracelet, earrings, a necklace, or a golden garland.

In the same way, a bhikkhu who is committed to the higher mind has coarse defilements: misconduct by body, speech, and thought. A sincere, capable bhikkhu gives these up, dispels, eliminates, and obliterates them. 

When they have been given up and eliminated, there are middling defilements: sensual, averse, or cruel thoughts. A sincere, capable bhikkhu gives these up, dispels, eliminates, and obliterates them. 

When they have been given up and eliminated, there are fine defilements: thoughts of family, country, and reputation. A sincere, capable bhikkhu gives these up, dispels, eliminates, and obliterates them.

When they have been given up and eliminated, only thoughts about the Dhamma remain.  That composure is not peaceful or sublime or calm or unified, but is held in place by active repression.

But there comes a time when that mind is established, settled, unified, and composed internally. That composure is peaceful and sublime and calm and unified, not held in place by active repression. By directing his mind towards it, he is able to realize whatever phenomenon is to be realized by comprehension, provided there is the \[required\] extent of recollectedness (_sati_).
